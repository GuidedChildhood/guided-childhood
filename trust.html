T — Timing

Right device.
Right age.
Not all or nothing.

Timing is not about being strict. It is about understanding what the research says about developmental readiness, and making an active choice rather than a passive one. Most parents do not decide when their child gets a smartphone. It just happens. The research suggests that is the problem.

“The earlier a child received their first smartphone, the worse their mental wellbeing as an adult. The trajectory is dose-dependent: getting a phone at 6 produces worse outcomes than at 8, worse than at 10, worse than at 12.”

Sapien Labs, Global Mind Project — N=27,969 young adults across 85 countries
61%
Higher risk of depression with 4+ hours of daily screen time
2026 study — N=50,231 children and adolescents aged 6 to 17
167%
Rise in suicide rate for teenage girls from 2010 to 2020 in the US
Haidt & colleagues — Clinical Psychological Science — N=506,820
2012
The year teen mental health began declining simultaneously across the US, UK, Canada and Australia
Twenge, Joiner, Rogers & Martin (2018); replicated across 36 countries

The debate in this field is not whether to act. It is about the size of the effect. Jean Twenge (San Diego State University, N=506,820) identifies 1 to 2 hours per day as the research-based threshold below which risk is low. Above 4 hours, depression risk jumps 61%. Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski at Oxford found that screen time explains 0.4% of wellbeing variance in isolation — but their own later work acknowledged substantially stronger effects in girls and in vulnerable groups. The question is not whether the risk is real. The question is what age your child is, and whether the timing of introduction is working for your family.

“No smartphone until at least age 14. No social media until 16. These are not parenting preferences. They are the thresholds the research data points toward.”

Jonathan Haidt, NYU Stern — The Anxious Generation (2024)

Note: Haidt’s causal claims remain scientifically contested. The correlation between smartphone introduction and mental health decline is well-established. Causation has not been proven in a randomised trial.

Three actions based on the evidence
01
Delay the smartphone introduction. A shared family tablet before a personal device is a meaningful buffer. Most research on harm clusters around personal smartphone ownership, not shared screen use. Ages 12 to 13 is the evidence-backed minimum, not the ideal.
02
Track total recreational hours, not just platforms. The dose-response relationship runs on total daily screen time. Two hours of YouTube plus one hour of gaming plus 45 minutes of TikTok is not three separate activities. It is 3 hours 45 minutes.
03
Start the conversation at 8 or 9. By 13, habits are set and the social pressure to conform is overwhelming. The timing conversation is much easier at 9 than at 12.
R — Relationships

Connection
is the
strongest shield.

Connection is not a soft idea. It is the most consistently evidenced protective factor against online harm across every major review. A rule without a relationship is just a fight. The research does not say warm parents have fewer rules. It says warm parents have children who come to them when something goes wrong.

“Children of Mentors — parents who actively guide rather than restrict or permit — showed the best digital outcomes and the least problematic behaviour online. The Mentor profile maps almost exactly to Baumrind’s authoritative parent: high warmth, high expectation, high communication.”

Alexandra Samuel — Survey of 10,000+ North American families — The Atlantic
40,587
Participants in the largest meta-analysis on parenting style and problematic internet use
Journal of Behavioral Addictions (2023) — 35 studies, 171 effect sizes
#1
Active parental mediation ranked as the single most effective protective factor against online harm
Meta-analysis — 88 studies, 2008 to 2024 — University of Hong Kong
86%
The rate at which relationship “masters” respond to emotional bids from their partner or child (vs 33% for “disasters”)
John Gottman — University of Washington Love Lab — longitudinal research

Diana Baumrind (UC Berkeley) identified three parenting styles over decades of research: authoritative (high warmth and high structure), authoritarian (high structure, low warmth), and permissive (high warmth, low structure). Every significant study on parenting style and digital outcomes has found the same pattern: authoritative parents produce the best outcomes, authoritarian parents see children use devices as emotional escape routes, and permissive parents see the highest rates of problematic use. The ABCD Study (NIH, N=8,096 adolescents) found that bedroom device access was the single most influential parenting variable — but it is the relationship quality that determines whether that conversation can happen at all.

John Gottman’s research on emotional bids — the small moments when children (and partners) reach out for connection — found that how you respond to these moments in ordinary daily life determines whether your child comes to you when something difficult happens online. The “turning toward” habit is built in hundreds of small moments that have nothing to do with screens.

Three actions based on the evidence
01
Co-use before solo use, at every age. Watching together first is not just about safety. It builds the shared language to talk about what they are seeing. Research from UCL (N=1,288) shows active co-use produces measurably better outcomes than passive co-presence. Your presence matters. Your engagement matters more.
02
When something goes wrong online, your first response matters more than your rule. Curiosity before judgment keeps the disclosure channel open. “Tell me what happened” before “Why did you do that” is the research-supported approach in every study on adolescent disclosure.
03
Move from restricting to mentoring as your child ages. The evidence consistently shows restrictive monitoring loses effectiveness with older teens and damages the relationship. The Gottman Institute frames this as a shift from supervisor to consultant — staying involved without staying in control.
U — Upstream

Design the
environment.
Not the rules.

The apps your child uses are not neutral. They were built by the world’s best engineers using the same variable reward mechanisms as slot machines. Willpower is not a strategy against that. The research says the same thing Wendy Wood at USC says about all habits: 43% of daily behaviour fires automatically from context, not conscious choice. Change the context.

“I invented infinite scroll. I’m sorry. What we are looking at is 200,000 human lifetimes wasted every single day. And I can’t stop using it myself. That tells you everything about willpower as a strategy.”

Aza Raskin — Inventor of Infinite Scroll, Co-founder Center for Humane Technology — testified Meta trial, New Mexico, 2025

How the platforms actually work

Variable reward
The slot machine mechanic
B.F. Skinner showed in the 1950s that unpredictable rewards produce the highest and most persistent response rates of any reinforcement schedule. A pigeon pecked 87,000 times in 14 hours under this schedule. Pull-to-refresh is the lever. The next post is the unpredictable reward.
Dopamine anticipation
Wanting, not getting
Kent Berridge at the University of Michigan showed that dopamine surges in anticipation of a reward, not upon receiving it. Platforms are designed to sustain anticipation. The checking behaviour is the mechanism, not the content. Anna Lembke at Stanford calls this a chronic dopamine deficit state.
Social validation loops
Likes activate reward circuits
UCLA fMRI research (Sherman et al. 2016, N=32, ages 13 to 21) showed that receiving likes activates the nucleus accumbens — the same reward centre triggered by food, social connection, and drugs. The effect was strongest in adolescents. Absence of expected likes activated brain regions associated with physical pain.
Disappearing content
FOMO by design
Stories, Snaps, and streaks are not design accidents. They exploit fear of missing out (first documented by Przybylski et al. 2013) to create urgency and compulsive checking. Adolescents are neurologically more susceptible to this mechanism than adults because their social reward system matures earlier than their impulse control system.
38
Minutes less daily screen time from switching the phone to grayscale mode. Three independent studies, central finding around 38 to 40 minutes per day. The mechanism: colour is a trigger. Remove the trigger, weaken the automatic response.
Holte & Ferraro 2020 (N=161) — Dekker & Baumgartner 2024 (N=84) — Social Science Journal / Mobile Media & Communication
20.6
Fewer minutes of sleep per night when a child sleeps near a small screen. This is the cost of the phone being in the bedroom. Not the usage — just the presence.
Falbe et al. — Pediatrics (Harvard) — N=2,048 fourth and seventh graders
Three actions based on the evidence
01
Create a family charging station outside all bedrooms. This applies to parents too. BJ Fogg’s behaviour model and Wendy Wood’s habit research both point to the same mechanism: if the phone is not in the room, the automatic reach cannot fire. Physical separation is the evidence-based intervention, not screen time settings.
02
Design device-free zones before you design device-free times. The kitchen table, the car, the first 30 minutes after school. Zones work because they interrupt the context cue. Wood’s research shows we are most likely to change habits during transitions — new term, new year, new device — those are the leverage points.
03
Do not rely on screen time apps alone. 97% of digital wellbeing apps have no research backing (systematic review, PMC 2022). The apps with the best evidence for reducing use are friction-based tools that make the phone mildly annoying to open — not hard lockouts, which children bypass within minutes.
S — Sleep

The highest
impact action
in the system.

The bedroom phone rule is not a screen time rule. It is a health rule. Sleep is when the developing brain consolidates everything it learned during the day, secretes growth hormone, regulates emotional responses, and builds immune capacity. The data on what screen use in the bedroom does to all of this is unambiguous.

“Reading on an iPad for four hours before bed suppressed melatonin by 55% and delayed its onset by more than 1.5 hours compared to reading a printed book. Participants slept less, had reduced REM sleep, and were impaired the next morning even after eight hours in bed.”

Chang, Aeschbach, Duffy & Czeisler — Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (2015) — Harvard and Brigham & Women’s Hospital
2.7×
More melatonin suppression in children than adults under the same evening light exposure
Lee et al. (2018) — Physiological Reports — N=22 children, N=20 adults
77%
Of US high school students do not get 8 hours of sleep on school nights
CDC Youth Risk Behaviour Surveillance System (2023)
76%
More likely to be overweight or obese with the shortest sleep duration vs. longest
Cappuccio et al. — Sleep (meta-analysis, 2018)

Mary Carskadon at Brown University spent decades establishing that the adolescent phase delay — the biological drive to sleep later — is real and driven by puberty, not laziness. During puberty, the rate at which sleep pressure builds slows from a time constant of 8.9 hours in pre-pubescent children to 15.4 hours in post-pubescent teenagers. The melatonin signal shifts later at every Tanner stage. This is not a choice your teenager is making. It is a biological fact.

The practical implication: a teenager who cannot fall asleep before 11pm is not being defiant. Their melatonin onset has not arrived yet. Screens in the bedroom delay it further, and the melatonin suppression in children is 2.7 times more powerful than in adults under the same light (Lee et al. 2018). Even 5 lux — barely any light at all — suppresses preschoolers’ melatonin by 77.5% (Hartstein et al. 2022, University of Colorado Boulder).

“70 to 80% of growth hormone is secreted during slow-wave sleep, predominantly in the first sleep cycle of the night. This is the stage device use most disrupts. Chronic sleep deprivation during adolescence — the most critical window for brain maturation — can be a tipping point toward lifelong psychiatric illness.”

Matthew Walker — Professor of Neuroscience, UC Berkeley — Why We Sleep (2017)
Three actions based on the evidence
01
All devices charge outside all bedrooms. Not just children’s bedrooms. A 2025 University of Geneva study (N=329) found that only the strictest rules — phone out of bedroom AND no evening use — produced significantly longer sleep duration. Partial rules produced no significant benefit.
02
Devices off 90 minutes before bed. The Chang et al. PNAS study used four hours of evening screen use. The minimum evidence base supports 90 minutes as the practical threshold. The blue light wavelength most responsible is around 480nm — the dominant wavelength of most phone and tablet screens.
03
20 to 30 minutes of outdoor light within the first hour of waking. Morning light is what anchors the circadian clock. Research published in Frontiers in Neuroscience (2020) showed that 2.5 hours of weekend morning bright light advanced teenagers’ melatonin onset by a full hour. Even overcast UK daylight delivers 2,000 to 10,000 lux. Indoor lighting delivers 200 to 500.
Bedroom temperature: 16 to 18°C (NHS UK recommendation). The body needs to drop its core temperature by 1 to 2°C to initiate sleep. A cooler room supports this.

The Sleep Protocol

Evidence-based by age band — NHS, AAP, National Sleep Foundation and the latest peer-reviewed research. Select your child’s age to see their full protocol.

Recommended sleep
10 to 13
hours per night including any nap — NHS and National Sleep Foundation
Most 4-year-olds are dropping their nap. If no daytime nap, move bedtime earlier by 30 to 45 minutes. Watch for the late-afternoon “second wind” — that wired, silly energy is cortisol masking exhaustion.
Device off time
90 min before bed
No screens in bedrooms at any time
Preschoolers’ melatonin suppression is extreme: even 5 lux (a phone screen face-down) suppresses melatonin by 77.5% (Hartstein et al. 2022). A TV in the corner of the room causes measurable sleep disruption even when the child is not watching it.
Bedtime ritual (3 steps)
  • Bath or warm wash (drops core body temperature, signals sleep time)
  • One book together in a dim, warm room (not overhead light)
  • Consistent final words — same short phrase every night — this becomes the sleep trigger
Warning signs of poor sleep
  • Hyperactivity or “silliness” at bedtime — the cortisol second wind
  • Meltdowns disproportionate to the trigger, especially between 4pm and 6pm
  • Falling asleep in the car or pushchair unexpectedly during the day
Three parent tasks
  • No screen in the bedroom — including a television. This is the single most effective structural intervention for this age group.
  • Bedtime ritual begins 45 minutes before lights out, screens off at the start of the ritual not the end.
  • Consistent wake time matters as much as bedtime. Letting a tired child sleep past 7:30am on weekends pushes their next night’s bedtime later. Same wake time, 7 days a week.
Recommended sleep
9 to 11
hours per night — NHS and National Sleep Foundation
NHS guidance runs more granularly: 9 years old needs around 10 hours, 6 to 7 year olds need closer to 10 hours 30 to 11 hours. The AAP gives a 9 to 12 hour range for ages 6 to 12. Aim for the higher end if your child is hard to wake in the mornings.
Device off time
60 min before bed
No devices in the bedroom — including tablets, handheld games
Children’s melatonin suppression from blue light is 2.7 times greater than adults (Lee et al. 2018). The 60-minute rule is the practical minimum. 90 minutes gives a more complete melatonin recovery before sleep onset.
Bedtime ritual (3 steps)
  • Screens off at 7:30pm — then a quiet activity of their choice: drawing, Lego, a book
  • Teeth, dim light, 10 minutes of reading or stories together
  • Lights out at 8pm to 8:30pm for most 6 to 9 year olds aiming for 10 to 11 hours
Warning signs of poor sleep
  • Poor impulse control, noncompliance, aggression — often misread as behavioural issues
  • Recurring headaches or stomach aches without clear cause
  • Difficulty retaining what they learned at school the day before
Three parent tasks
  • Screen-free bedroom enforced from this age. If a TV is in the room, move it now — this is the easiest and highest-impact structural change.
  • Consistent bedtime and wake time 7 days a week. Weekend lie-ins past one hour of the school wake time create “social jetlag” that accumulates across the week.
  • Physical activity before 6pm helps sleep quality. Research shows 3+ sessions of moderate activity per week significantly improves sleep efficiency in school-age children.
Recommended sleep
9 to 11
hours per night — NHS and National Sleep Foundation
Some children this age begin the early stages of the pubertal circadian shift. They may start wanting to stay up later and finding it harder to wake. This is biological, not behavioural. It will intensify at 12 to 13. Establish the bedroom rule now, before it becomes a battle.
Device off time
60 to 90 min before bed
Phone charges outside bedroom from this age
This is often the age at which children first get a personal device. Introduce the charging station habit from day one. It is far easier to establish it as the norm now than to remove it from the bedroom at 13 when social pressure is at its peak.
Bedtime ritual (3 steps)
  • Device handover to parent at agreed time — make this a habit, not a fight
  • Independent wind-down: reading, drawing, journalling — something they choose
  • Lights out by 9pm to 9:30pm for most 10 to 11 year olds
Warning signs of poor sleep
  • Sneaking devices after bedtime — particularly common at this age
  • Exhaustion mid-week despite early bedtimes — check for night-time device use
  • Significant mood shifts between Sunday night and Monday morning
Three parent tasks
  • Start the handover habit now. A shared family charging station makes it feel normal rather than punitive. The rule is about sleep, not about trust.
  • Teach the science honestly: “Your brain is doing more growing right now than at almost any other point in your life. Sleep is when it does most of the work. That’s the reason for this rule.”
  • Watch the weekend drift. Late weekend nights create a sleep deficit that compounds through the school week. More than a 1-hour difference between weekend and school wake times is worth addressing.
Recommended sleep
8 to 10
hours per night — AAP and National Sleep Foundation
Puberty triggers a genuine biological delay in the circadian clock. Mary Carskadon (Brown University) showed that by Tanner stage 5, sleep pressure accumulates nearly twice as slowly as in pre-pubescent children. A 12 to 13 year old who cannot fall asleep before 11pm is not being difficult. Their melatonin has not arrived yet.
Device off time
90 min before bed
Phone out of bedroom — non-negotiable at this age
A 2025 University of Geneva study (N=329) found that only the strictest parental rules — phone out of bedroom AND no evening use — produced significantly longer sleep duration. Partial rules (phone in bedroom on silent, time limits only) produced no significant sleep improvement.
Bedtime ritual (3 steps)
  • 9:30pm phone handover to charging station — agreed time, consistent every night
  • Personal time without screens until they feel sleepy — reading, music, drawing
  • Aim for lights out at 10pm to 10:30pm, giving 8 to 9 hours before a 6:30am school wake
Warning signs of poor sleep
  • Consistently alert and cheerful at 11pm, impossible to wake at 7am — this is the biological phase delay, not attitude
  • Sleeping significantly later on weekends — more than 2 hours difference is social jetlag
  • Emotional fragility or irritability that seems disproportionate, especially in the mornings
Three parent tasks
  • Phone out of bedroom is the non-negotiable at this age. Social pressure to stay connected overnight peaks here. The rule needs to be structural (charging station elsewhere) not volitional (asking them not to).
  • Make it about sleep, not punishment. “Your melatonin arrives about two hours later than mine does right now. The phone delays it another hour and a half. That is three hours of sleep quality you are losing.” The science is genuinely persuasive to curious 12 to 13 year olds.
  • Watch for energy drink use. The AAP recommends no caffeine for under-12s and a maximum of 100mg per day for 13 to 18 year olds. One energy drink often exceeds that entire limit. Caffeine consumed at 3pm is still active at 8pm to 10pm, delaying sleep onset on an already-delayed clock.
Recommended sleep
8 to 10
hours per night — AAP and National Sleep Foundation
In the 2024 NSF Sleep in America Poll, teenagers were nearly half as likely to get the recommended 8 to 10 hours on school nights as on weekends. Sleep debt is cumulative: a week of 6-hour nights cannot be recovered over the weekend. Every hour lost impairs the memory consolidation for everything learned that week (Walker, UC Berkeley).
Device off time
90 min before bed
Phone outside bedroom — negotiated but enforced
Autonomy matters at this age. Covert removal backfires. The evidence-based approach is to negotiate the rule together and involve them in setting it. A co-created rule that a 15-year-old owns is more effective than an imposed rule they resent and route around.
Bedtime ritual (3 steps)
  • Agreed phone handover time (10pm or earlier) — their input, your agreement, consistent
  • Wind-down of their choice: music, reading, a podcast — without a screen in hand
  • Lights out by 10:30pm to 11pm for most 14 to 16 year olds with a 7am school start
Warning signs of poor sleep
  • Falling asleep in the first two periods of school — the most common and underreported sign
  • Emotional dysregulation that seems sudden or out of character — sleep deprivation increases amygdala reactivity by up to 60% (Walker, UC Berkeley)
  • Declining school performance despite apparent effort — working memory and executive function are the first casualties of sleep debt
Three parent tasks
  • Negotiate the bedtime rule together — involve them in setting it, show them the science. Matthew Walker’s explanation of memory consolidation during sleep is genuinely persuasive to teenagers preparing for GCSEs who care about their results.
  • No energy drinks. 100mg caffeine per day maximum (AAP for teens). One can of many energy drinks exceeds that limit. Cut off all caffeine by 2pm for a 10pm bedtime — the half-life is 4 to 6 hours.
  • 30 to 45 minutes of outdoor morning light at weekends. Research shows this can advance teenagers’ melatonin onset by a full hour over several weeks, making it easier to sleep and wake at school-compatible times (Frontiers in Neuroscience, 2020).
T — Transparency

Openness
over
secrecy.

The question is not whether your child will encounter something difficult online. They will. 73% of UK 11 to 17 year olds encountered harmful content in a four-week period (Ofcom 2025). The question is whether they will come to you when it happens. That is determined by openness, not by the tightness of the rule.

“Teens are not reckless online because they are ignorant. They are navigating very real social pressures with a tool they were given without a manual. What they need from parents is not surveillance. It is a trusted adult they are not afraid to come to.”

Emily Weinstein and Carrie James — Behind Their Screens (2022) — MIT Press — Harvard Graduate School of Education
73%
Of UK 11 to 17 year olds encountered harmful content online in a four-week period
Ofcom Children’s Media Literacy Report 2025 — N=3,383 children
21%
Of UK parents have never spoken to their children about online privacy
ICO “One Click Too Many” research — April 2026 — N=1,000 UK parents
89%
Rise in online grooming offences in the UK since 2017 to 18 — 7,062 offences in 2023 to 24
NSPCC (2024) — 40% occurred on Snapchat — 81% of victims are girls

A 2025 meta-analysis of 88 studies (University of Hong Kong) ranked parental protective factors in order of evidence strength. Active mediation — open conversations about what your child sees online — was the single most robustly evidenced factor. Technical controls ranked seventh. Parental warmth and relationship quality ranked second. The Oxford Internet Institute’s rapid evidence review of parental control apps concluded that controls “lose effectiveness in the eyes of older teens” and that overuse of surveillance “undermines parent-child relationships and children’s sense of autonomy.”

Sonia Livingstone at LSE and the EU Kids Online project (N=6,400 across 8 European countries) found that restriction of peer-to-peer online interactions was protective against specific risks, but that active co-engagement — talking about what children see, explaining how platforms work, and being genuinely curious about their online life rather than suspicious of it — was the mechanism that kept children disclosing when something went wrong.

53% of teachers report that children make safeguarding disclosures following online safety awareness activities at school (UK Safer Internet Centre). The disclosure channel opens when children feel safe. What makes them feel safe is a trusted adult who responds with curiosity before judgment.

Three actions based on the evidence
01
Know the five platforms your child uses. Not to monitor, but to be able to talk about them without sounding like you have never opened one. A parent who has tried TikTok for five minutes can have a completely different conversation about it than one who has not.
02
Build the disclosure habit early with low-stakes questions. “What is the funniest thing you saw online today?” at 8 years old is the question that keeps the channel open for harder questions at 14. The research on adolescent disclosure consistently shows that children go to the adults who have consistently shown curiosity, not alarm.
03
Teach how the platforms work, not just what they are. A child who understands that TikTok’s algorithm is designed to keep them watching, not to serve their interests, has a fundamentally different relationship with the app. Digital literacy is a protective factor: children with higher digital literacy show lower cyberbullying involvement and better online outcomes (Nature, 2025).
Quick assessment

Three questions that tell you where to start

These questions come from validated clinical screening tools used by sleep and child health specialists. They are not a diagnosis. They are a starting point.

01
Does your child seem refreshed when they wake up, or do they struggle to get up most mornings?
A child who is consistently difficult to rouse, groggy for an hour or more, or frequently says they are tired in the morning is not getting enough restorative sleep — regardless of how many hours they appear to be in bed. If the answer is “struggle,” start with S.
02
Has their mood, behaviour, or school performance changed noticeably in the past four to six weeks without an obvious reason?
Unexplained irritability, emotional outbursts disproportionate to situations, declining school effort, or social withdrawal that has appeared gradually over weeks frequently traces back to a sleep deficit, a relationship strain, or an online experience they have not disclosed. This question points to R and T.
03
Is there more than a two-hour difference between when your child wakes up on a school day versus a weekend?
This gap — called social jetlag — is a measurable indicator of circadian misalignment. A difference of more than 2 hours signals chronic sleep debt and a body clock significantly out of sync with their school schedule. This is a U and S issue.
