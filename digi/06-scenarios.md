# DIGI — SCENARIO LIBRARY

Every scenario a parent is likely to face, organised by when it happens, what triggered it, and what stage the child is at. DiGi draws on this to give specific, named scripts rather than generic advice.

Each scenario has:
- The trigger (what the parent describes)
- The stage(s) it applies to
- The script: say this / not this
- The research basis in one sentence
- The trust implication

---

## MORNING INCIDENTS

### MO-01 — Child sneaks downstairs before anyone else to watch screens
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3
**Trigger:** "I came downstairs at 6am and they were already on the iPad/TV."

SAY THIS: "I found the iPad on the sofa this morning. We are going to move it to the kitchen tonight and that is where it stays overnight. No blame — this is a structure change, not a punishment."

NOT THIS: "You are banned from screens for a week."

WHY: The sneaking is not defiance — it is a habit loop that forms when screens are available and willpower is low. Structure fixes habit loops. Punishment does not.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Treat it as a signal the current structure is not working, not as a character problem. If you punish, they hide it better next time.

---

### MO-02 — Morning battle to get off the device and go to school
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3
**Trigger:** "Every single morning is a fight to get them off their phone/game and ready."

SAY THIS: "I am going to change how we do mornings. Screens do not start until after shoes and bag are ready. Once you are ready, you can have 10 minutes. We are trying this for one week."

NOT THIS: "If you do not put that down right now..."

WHY: Conditional access after readiness is proven in behavioural research to be more effective than time limits. The child has to complete the task to earn the access — not lose the access as a consequence.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Framing it as a one-week trial reduces resistance. They can negotiate after the week if the system works.

---

### MO-03 — Phone at the breakfast table
**Stages:** 2, 3, 4
**Trigger:** "They sit and eat staring at their phone. I feel invisible."

SAY THIS: "Breakfast is a phone-free zone for all of us. Mine goes face-down too. Ten minutes. Then it comes back."

NOT THIS: "Put that thing away while I am talking to you."

WHY: Parental modelling is the strongest predictor of child behaviour around devices. The rule works when the parent demonstrates it. It does not work when the parent holds the phone while making the demand.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Do not make a rule you will not follow yourself. Children at every stage notice immediately.

---

### MO-04 — Child refuses to leave for school because of a game or video
**Stages:** 2, 3
**Trigger:** "He is mid-game and just refuses to move. We are late every morning."

SAY THIS: "I understand you are mid-game. Five minutes and then we save and go. If we miss the bus again, screens go into the weekend box for Saturday. That is a natural consequence, not a punishment."

NOT THIS: "Turn it off NOW or I am taking it for a week."

WHY: Natural consequences that are proportionate and predictable change behaviour faster than arbitrary punishments. The child needs to know the exact outcome and believe you will follow through.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Never say a consequence you will not follow through on. Consistency is the thing.

---

### MO-05 — Weekend morning — gaming all morning, will not engage with anything else
**Stages:** 2, 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He wakes up Saturday and is on the game until lunchtime. We never go anywhere."

SAY THIS: "We are going to try a new system on weekends. Two hours of something physical or outside before screens. Not as a punishment — as a health baseline. I will do it with you."

NOT THIS: "You are not gaming all morning."

WHY: The issue is not the gaming — it is the absence of other activity. Adding the non-screen activity first is more effective than capping the screen time. The gaming is the reward, not the problem.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Doing the physical activity with them reinforces that this is a family norm, not a rule aimed at them.

## AFTER SCHOOL INCIDENTS

### AS-01 — Straight to devices the moment they walk in the door
**Stages:** 2, 3, 4
**Trigger:** "Bag drops on the floor and the phone is out before they have even said hello."

SAY THIS: "I want 10 minutes of you first. Bag away, snack, tell me one thing about your day. Then you are free. That is the new routine."

NOT THIS: "Can you at least say hello before disappearing?"

WHY: Children decompress differently than adults after school. The transition period matters. 10 minutes of connection before screens is backed by attachment research as the thing most likely to result in genuine conversation later.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If they know the 10 minutes is guaranteed and finite, they stop resisting it. The resentment comes from open-ended demands on their time.

---

### AS-02 — "I need my phone for homework" — impossible to verify
**Stages:** 3, 4, 5
**Trigger:** "She says she needs it for school but I can see she is on TikTok."

SAY THIS: "Tell me what the homework is. What specifically do you need the phone for? If it is research, we can do that together or you can use the laptop at the kitchen table. Phone stays down."

NOT THIS: "You are obviously not doing homework."

WHY: Calling out the lie immediately triggers defensiveness. Asking what the homework is either gets the honest answer, or makes clear to the child that you are paying attention — without an argument.

TRUST IMPLICATION: The goal is not to win the argument. It is to keep the communication channel open so they come to you when something actually goes wrong.

---

### AS-03 — Homework done on phone with TikTok open simultaneously
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She says she is doing homework but has TikTok/YouTube running at the same time."

SAY THIS: "Research on attention is pretty clear — you genuinely do worse work when your brain is splitting between tasks. I am not saying this to lecture you. I want your homework done so you have the evening free. One or the other for 30 minutes."

NOT THIS: "You cannot do homework with that on — turn it off."

WHY: The scientific basis (split attention, reduced working memory, Strayer 2015) is a fact, not an opinion. Presenting it as a fact is more persuasive than a rule, and it puts the outcome (done faster, more time) in their interest.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Treating them as someone who can respond to evidence rather than commands works better at Stage 3 and above.

---

### AS-04 — Gaming with online friends all afternoon, not contactable
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He gets home, goes straight to his room, and I do not see him for hours. He is gaming with people I do not know."

SAY THIS: "Tell me about who you game with online. Not because I am worried — because I want to understand who your people are. What are they like?"

NOT THIS: "You are spending all your time with strangers online."

WHY: At Stage 3 and 4, gaming is primarily social. The game is the venue, the people are the point. A parent who is curious about the people gets much more information than one who expresses alarm about the activity.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Asking genuine questions about their online friends signals that you respect their social life. That trust gets paid back when something goes wrong.

---

### AS-05 — Checking social media obsessively after school
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She gets home and immediately starts checking her phone every few minutes."

SAY THIS: "I have noticed you check your phone a lot when you first get home. What are you looking for? Is there something stressful going on that you are waiting to hear about?"

NOT THIS: "You are obsessed with that phone."

WHY: Obsessive checking is almost always anxiety-driven — waiting for social feedback (likes, replies, inclusion signals). The question "what are you waiting to hear about?" names the behaviour accurately and opens the door without judgment.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If there is a social situation causing the anxiety, this question surfaces it. If you call them obsessed, they tell you nothing.

## MEALTIME INCIDENTS

### MT-01 — Phone at the dinner table
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
**Trigger:** "We cannot get through a meal without someone on their phone."

SAY THIS: "New rule for all of us. Phones in the middle of the table, face down, for dinner. Whoever picks theirs up first does the washing up. I am in."

NOT THIS: "Put your phone away at the table."

WHY: A game-format rule applied to the whole family removes the adversarial dynamic and makes the parent subject to the same standard. The washing-up consequence is light-touch and funny. This works where direct bans fail.

TRUST IMPLICATION: The phone in the middle is visible accountability. It is harder to sneak a check when the rule applies to everyone.

---

### MT-02 — Sibling conflict — one has a phone, one does not
**Stages:** 2, 3
**Trigger:** "My 13-year-old has a phone and my 10-year-old thinks it is unfair she cannot."

SAY THIS: "You will have the same things at the same age. Our family has a pathway — it is not about favourites. When you are 13, we will have the same conversation we had with your sister."

NOT THIS: "Life is not fair, get used to it."

WHY: Children have a strong sense of procedural fairness. "Same age, same rules" is a fairness argument they can understand and accept. "Life is not fair" closes the conversation and stores resentment.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If the 10-year-old believes the pathway applies equally to everyone, they accept it. If they think it is arbitrary, they fight it every time.

---

### MT-03 — Parent on phone while telling child to put theirs away
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
**Trigger:** (Self-aware parent) "I know I do this. How do I change it?"

SAY THIS (to your child): "I am going to start putting my phone down at dinner. I have not been consistent about this and that is on me. We are going to do this together."

WHY: Acknowledging the inconsistency directly disarms the "but you do it" argument before it happens and models the self-awareness you want from them.

TRUST IMPLICATION: This is the highest-trust move a parent can make. Admitting your own inconsistency increases credibility massively.

## EVENING AND BEDTIME INCIDENTS

### EB-01 — "Just 10 more minutes" loop — device handover at bedtime
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3
**Trigger:** "Every night is the same. 'Just 10 more minutes' and then we are fighting for an hour."

SAY THIS: "The timer is going to go off in 10 minutes. When it goes off, the device goes to the kitchen. Every time. If the handover is clean, you get to choose what we do together for 10 minutes after. That is the deal."

NOT THIS: "Five more minutes. This is the last time."

WHY: "This is the last time" has no credibility after the third time. The timer externalises the authority. The positive incentive (10 minutes of choice) changes the emotional tone of the handover.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Follow through 100% of the time. The first time you extend it, the whole system resets.

---

### EB-02 — Resistance to charging phone outside the bedroom
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She says she needs it as an alarm. She will not accept the bedroom rule."

SAY THIS: "I will get you an alarm clock tonight. That solves the alarm. The phone charges in the kitchen. This is not about trust — it is about what screens do to sleep. I am doing it too."

NOT THIS: "You do not need your phone to sleep."

WHY: The alarm excuse is legitimate and should be addressed directly rather than dismissed. Removing the reason removes the resistance. The sleep science is clear (blue light, notification dopamine, pre-sleep use extends sleep onset by 60 min on average).

TRUST IMPLICATION: Solving the actual problem (alarm) rather than overriding their stated concern builds far more trust than power assertions.

---

### EB-03 — Streaming or gaming past agreed bedtime
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She says she is in bed but I can see the light under her door at midnight."

SAY THIS: "I know you are awake. I am not angry. We need to talk tomorrow about what we do about this because the current setup is not working. For tonight, phone to the kitchen please."

NOT THIS: "I can see your light. Turn that off NOW."

WHY: The middle-of-the-night confrontation escalates and harms the relationship. Naming what you know calmly and deferring the consequence conversation to daylight keeps you in a position of calm authority.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Blowing up in the middle of the night makes the child an expert at hiding it better. Staying calm keeps the conversation open.

---

### EB-04 — Child sneaks device after handover/bedtime
**Stages:** 2, 3, 4
**Trigger:** "They handed it over but I found out they had another device/got it back."

SAY THIS: "I found out what happened last night. I am not going to shout. But I need you to know I know, and we need to change the structure because it is not working. What do you think we need to change?"

NOT THIS: "You are a liar and I cannot trust you."

WHY: The behaviour is a symptom of a structure problem as much as a values problem. Involving the child in designing the new structure is more effective than punishment, and shows you view this as a shared problem to solve.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "You are a liar" is a character attack. It damages the relationship and does not change the behaviour. "Help me fix this" does both.

---

### EB-05 — Gaming late into the night affecting school performance
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He is exhausted every morning. I know he is up gaming until 2am. His grades are slipping."

SAY THIS: "I want to show you something. Your performance at school over the last month compared to what I know about your sleep. You are not stupid — you are tired. I want to fix this together. What do you need to make the bedroom rule actually work?"

NOT THIS: "No gaming until your grades improve."

WHY: Framing around performance data (the concrete, visible consequence) rather than the rule is more persuasive at Stage 3 and 4. "Your grades have dropped" is evidence. Linking it to sleep is causal, not punitive.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Acknowledging their intelligence ("you are not stupid") while naming the real cause preserves dignity and keeps them engaged in the solution.

## LATE NIGHT AND SLEEP INCIDENTS

### LN-01 — Found out child has been up until 3am
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "Screen time report says they were on their phone from midnight to 3am."

SAY THIS: "Your screen time report told me you were on your phone until 3am. I am not angry — I am worried. Sleep at your age is not optional. Show me the report with me and help me understand what was happening."

NOT THIS: "You are banned from your phone for a month."

WHY: The screen time report is evidence, not accusation. Looking at it together changes the dynamic from confrontation to shared analysis. A month ban creates a power struggle that resolves the moment the month ends.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Looking at data together sets a norm for transparency. "Show me" is more sustainable than "I will check."

---

### LN-02 — Child wakes at 2am to check their phone
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "My daughter woke me up at 2am and told me she could not sleep because she wanted to check her phone."

SAY THIS: "Thank you for telling me. That is actually really helpful to know — it tells me the phone being in your room is making things harder, not easier. Let us try the bedroom rule for two weeks and see if sleep feels different."

NOT THIS: "That is exactly why I said no phones in the bedroom."

WHY: The child telling the parent is a moment of trust and honesty. "I told you so" destroys it. "Thank you for telling me" reinforces it and turns the information into collaborative problem-solving.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If children are rewarded with a positive response when they share something honest, they share more.

## SIBLING DYNAMICS

### SB-01 — "It is not fair — she has one and I do not"
**Stages:** 2, 3
**Trigger:** "My younger child is furious that the older one has a phone."

SAY THIS: "Same age, same conversation. When you are [older sibling's age], we will have exactly the same discussion. Our pathway applies to everyone equally."

NOT THIS: "Your sister is more mature. When you show me you are ready..."

WHY: "More mature" is a comparison that generates resentment and invites the child to try to prove maturity in unhelpful ways. Age-based consistency is neutral and fair. Maturity-based decisions feel arbitrary.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Children accept age-based fairness. They resent comparison-based fairness indefinitely.

---

### SB-02 — Younger child watching older sibling's screen
**Stages:** 1, 2
**Trigger:** "My 6-year-old is watching things meant for the 12-year-old. They just sit next to each other."

SAY THIS (to the older child): "When your little brother/sister is watching with you, the rule is the same as if I was watching too. What is on the screen is shared."

NOT THIS: "Why are you letting them watch that?"

WHY: The older child did not necessarily choose to share — the younger child moved closer. Framing as shared responsibility rather than blame is more effective and fair.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Making the older sibling a co-owner of the family standard rather than the accused builds their investment in getting it right.

---

### SB-03 — Siblings fighting over the family iPad
**Stages:** 1, 2
**Trigger:** "They fight constantly over the iPad. It is a daily battle."

SAY THIS: "We are going to write a rota. Each person has their time. When your time is up, you hand it over without a fight. If there is a fight, both of you lose the next session. We do this together."

NOT THIS: "Right, nobody is getting it today."

WHY: Removing the iPad from both children as a consequence of fighting is not proportionate — only one child typically starts the fight. A rota creates predictable access and removes the scarcity that drives conflict.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Joint consequences for one child's behaviour create resentment. Individual responsibility within a shared system works better.

---

### SB-04 — Older sibling teaching younger one tech workarounds
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "I found out my 14-year-old has been teaching his 9-year-old sister how to get around restrictions."

SAY THIS: "I know what has been happening. [To the older one:] You are old enough to understand why the restrictions exist for her. I need you to be on the same side as me here, not working around me. What did I do wrong that made you feel like that was okay?"

NOT THIS: "You are a terrible influence on your sister."

WHY: The older child teaching workarounds is often a statement about the rules feeling arbitrary. The last question ("what did I do wrong?") is the important one — it opens a genuine conversation about whether the restrictions are fair and builds buy-in.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Asking what you did wrong is the highest-trust move. It signals that the parent sees themselves as part of the system, not just the enforcer of it.

## TRUST INCIDENTS

### TR-01 — Child lied about where they were online
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She told me she was on a game but I found out she was on TikTok. She is 11."

SAY THIS: "I am not going to punish you for being on TikTok. I am going to have an honest conversation about why we have the rule, what is actually on there, and what I need from you instead. The lie bothers me more than the app."

NOT THIS: "You lied to me. You are banned from everything."

WHY: The lie is the behaviour to address, not the app. Addressing the app with a ban does not build honesty — it teaches better hiding. Addressing the lie directly, calmly, and without catastrophising builds the thing you actually need.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "The lie bothers me more than the app" is the most powerful sentence here. It signals to the child what you actually value.

---

### TR-02 — Child deleted their browser/app history
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "I looked at his phone history and it had been cleared. He never does that unless he is hiding something."

SAY THIS: "I noticed your history was cleared. I am not going to demand to see what you deleted. But I want you to know that when you clear your history, I notice, and it makes me more worried, not less. If there is something going on, I would rather know."

NOT THIS: "What are you hiding? Give me your phone."

WHY: Demanding the phone creates a confrontation. Stating the impact ("makes me more worried, not less") is honest and gives the child information to act on. The door is open without the pressure.

TRUST IMPLICATION: This is the open-door script. It works best if the open-door norm has been established before this moment.

---

### TR-03 — Found something worrying on their phone
**Stages:** 3, 4, 5
**Trigger:** "I saw something on her phone that really alarmed me — I do not know what to do."

SAY THIS: "I want to talk to you about something I saw. I am not going to pretend I did not see it. I am not angry. I am worried. Can you help me understand what is going on?"

NOT THIS: "Explain this." [Phone thrust in their face]

WHY: "Explain this" is an interrogation. "Can you help me understand what is going on?" is an invitation. Both want the same information, but only one is likely to get it honestly.

TRUST IMPLICATION: How you respond the first time something alarming surfaces determines whether they come to you the second time.

---

### TR-04 — Child has a secret account you did not know about
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "I found out she has a second Instagram account I did not know about."

SAY THIS: "I know about the account. I am not going to shut it down tonight. I want to understand why you needed a secret one — because if there is something about the conversation around your main account that made this feel necessary, I want to know what that was."

NOT THIS: "Delete it. Now."

WHY: A secret account is a signal that the child felt they could not be honest. Shutting it down without understanding the reason does not resolve the underlying dynamic — it just drives the next account further underground.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "I want to know what I did that made this feel necessary" is the question that changes things. It may be genuinely hard to ask. It is the right question.

---

### TR-05 — Child shared something private with someone online
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She shared photos with someone she met online. I do not know who he is."

SAY THIS: "This needs our full attention and I am not angry at you — I want you to know that first. Tell me everything you know about this person. We are going to work through this together and I am going to help, not punish."

NOT THIS: "That was incredibly stupid. Do you know how dangerous that is?"

WHY: The child who is told they were stupid will not tell you anything else. The child who hears "I am not angry, I want to help" will. Information flow is the safety mechanism. Preserve it.

TRUST IMPLICATION: In an actual safety situation, the parent's calm is the child's greatest resource. Any shame response shuts down information that may be critical.

## DEVICE-SPECIFIC SCENARIOS

### DV-01 — First phone request (aged 8 to 10)
**Stages:** 2
**Trigger:** "He is asking for a smartphone. His friends have them. I do not know when is right."

SAY THIS: "Let us talk about what you want to use it for and build toward that together. What would you use it for? ... [Listen.] Okay. Here is what we need to have in place first before I would feel comfortable."

NOT THIS: "You are too young."

WHY: "Too young" is an arbitrary refusal. "Here is what we build toward it" gives the child agency in the process and makes any eventual yes feel earned rather than given.

TRUST IMPLICATION: A parent who explains the pathway is treated as a fair negotiating partner. A parent who says "too young" is viewed as an obstacle to get around.

---

### DV-02 — Child asking for TikTok / Instagram (aged 11 to 13)
**Stages:** 3
**Trigger:** "She is 12 and asking for TikTok. All her friends have it."

SAY THIS: "I want to talk about this properly, not just say no. Can we sit together and look at what TikTok actually is for 10 minutes? I want to understand it and I want to talk about what I know about it."

NOT THIS: "You are 13 by law. The answer is no."

WHY: The legal age argument on its own does not explain why. Sitting together and looking at the platform makes you a credible parent rather than a rule-enforcer, and gives you the conversation you need to have before any access.

TRUST IMPLICATION: A parent who engages with the platform rather than just banning it has far more influence when the child inevitably accesses it anyway.

---

### DV-03 — Gaming — child spending real money without permission
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "I found a charge on my card for £47 in in-game purchases."

SAY THIS: "I found a charge on my card. I need us to talk about this tonight. This is not just a screen issue — it is about money and trust. Tell me what happened."

NOT THIS: "You are never gaming again."

WHY: In-game purchasing is specifically designed to obscure the real-money exchange (tokens, V-bucks, coins — not pounds). The child may genuinely not have understood the real cost. Establish what they understood before deciding the consequence.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Assuming intent before knowing the facts produces a worse outcome than finding out first.

---

### DV-04 — AI tool use for homework (ChatGPT, etc.)
**Stages:** 4, 5
**Trigger:** "I am pretty sure she is using ChatGPT to write her essays."

SAY THIS: "I want to have a proper conversation about AI and schoolwork — not to catch you out, but because this is going to come up for the rest of your life and I want us to think about it properly. What are you actually using it for?"

NOT THIS: "That is cheating."

WHY: AI tools are going to be a permanent part of this generation's working life. "That is cheating" closes a conversation that needs to be had. How to use AI well — prompting, verification, synthesis — is a skill. The conversation is about how, not whether.

TRUST IMPLICATION: A parent who engages seriously with AI has more credibility on the question of how to use it responsibly than one who treats it as automatically wrong.

---

### DV-05 — Deepfake / manipulated image discovered
**Stages:** 4, 5
**Trigger:** "Someone sent my daughter an image of herself that was clearly AI-generated and inappropriate."

SAY THIS: "I want you to show me what happened. I am not going to overreact. I want to understand what was sent, who sent it, and what you want to do about it. You have not done anything wrong here."

NOT THIS: "Do not open it. Delete it."

WHY: Evidence preservation matters if this is a safeguarding issue. Deleting the image removes the ability to report it. The child needs to hear clearly that they are not at fault.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "You have not done anything wrong" said first and explicitly is the most important sentence in a deepfake scenario. Without it, shame prevents disclosure.

## SCHOOL-LINKED SCENARIOS

### SC-01 — Teacher reported phone use in class
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "The school called to say they caught her on her phone during a lesson."

SAY THIS: "I heard from school. I am not going to add a punishment on top of what school has already done. But I do want to know what was happening in that lesson that made the phone feel more important."

NOT THIS: "I am so embarrassed. You know the rules."

WHY: Double-punishing (school consequence plus home consequence) is disproportionate and generates resentment without changing behaviour. The question about what was happening in the lesson opens the real conversation.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "I am so embarrassed" makes the issue about the parent. The issue is about the child.

---

### SC-02 — School WhatsApp group — harassment or exclusion
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She came home in tears. Something happened in the class WhatsApp group."

SAY THIS: "Can you show me? I want to understand exactly what happened before we decide what to do. I am on your side here."

NOT THIS: "Just leave the group."

WHY: "Just leave the group" is the equivalent of telling a child to leave their social world. It solves the symptom and destroys the social context. You need to see what happened to respond appropriately.

TRUST IMPLICATION: The child who shows you their phone during a crisis is the child who has learned you will help, not judge. This moment is where that trust gets built or lost.

---

### SC-03 — Child struggling with school phone ban
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "His school just banned phones. He is finding it really hard."

SAY THIS: "Tell me what is the hardest part. Is it the boredom? The social stuff? Not being able to contact me? Let us make sure those things are sorted."

NOT THIS: "Good. I am glad the school finally did it."

WHY: "Good" dismisses a genuine difficulty. The child's social life is partly managed through the phone. Understanding specifically what they miss allows you to address the real concern.

TRUST IMPLICATION: A parent who validates the difficulty while supporting the rule has more influence than one who celebrates it.

---

### SC-04 — Child using AI to write a full school essay
**Stages:** 4, 5
**Trigger:** "I read his essay and it is clearly AI-written. He submitted it to school."

SAY THIS: "I read your essay. I want to talk about this before you submit anything else. What did you actually write versus what did you use AI for? I am not calling school tonight — I want to understand what happened first."

NOT THIS: "I am calling your teacher right now."

WHY: Calling the school before having the conversation puts the child on the defensive and removes your ability to understand what actually happened. The child may have used AI for research only, or may have generated the whole thing. You need to know before you act.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "I want to understand before I act" is the sentence that keeps communication open in every school incident.

## WELLBEING AND MENTAL HEALTH SCENARIOS

### WB-01 — Mood changes linked to social media use
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She seems fine until she goes on Instagram and then she is flat or tearful."

SAY THIS: "I have noticed something and I want to check in with you about it. After you come off Instagram sometimes you seem different. Have you noticed that too?"

NOT THIS: "Instagram is making you depressed. You need to come off it."

WHY: "You need to come off it" is a conclusion before the conversation. "Have you noticed that too?" invites self-observation, which is the first step toward the child making their own choices about their use.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If the child comes to their own conclusion that something is affecting them, they are more likely to act on it than if the parent tells them.

---

### WB-02 — Child shows signs of gaming or social media compulsion
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He genuinely cannot stop. Even when he wants to."

SAY THIS: "I want to try something with you. Not a ban — an experiment. Can we try one week where the game goes off at 9pm, and I want to know if you feel different? You tell me."

NOT THIS: "You are addicted."

WHY: "Addicted" is a clinical label that shames and rarely changes behaviour. A time-limited experiment keeps the child as an observer of their own experience rather than a patient. The self-report element means they discover the effect themselves.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Experiments they agree to are honoured. Rules they resent are evaded.

---

### WB-03 — Child has seen distressing content online
**Stages:** 2, 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He came to me upset — he saw something really disturbing on YouTube."

SAY THIS: "I am glad you came to me. What did you see? Tell me in your own words. You have not done anything wrong."

NOT THIS: "Why were you watching that?"

WHY: "Why were you watching that?" implies blame and is likely to prevent the next disclosure. The child often did not choose to see it — algorithms surface disturbing content in recommended feeds. "I am glad you came to me" is the single most important sentence.

TRUST IMPLICATION: How you respond to the first time a child discloses distressing content determines whether they disclose the second time.

---

### WB-04 — Signs of anxiety connected to online social dynamics
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She is anxious about whether she is liked online. She checks constantly."

SAY THIS: "I want to ask you something. When you check your phone, what are you hoping to see? What does it feel like when there is nothing there?"

NOT THIS: "Stop caring so much about what people think."

WHY: "Stop caring" is not advice — it does not tell the child how to do that. The questions surface what the checking behaviour is actually serving (social validation) and opens the door to a conversation about what else might meet that need.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Asking how something feels is the entry point to every productive conversation about emotional wellbeing.

---

### WB-05 — Child has been contacted by an unknown adult online
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "She told me someone she does not know has been messaging her and asking personal questions."

SAY THIS: "You did exactly the right thing by telling me. Can you show me the messages? Do not delete anything — I want to see it. I am going to help you deal with this and nothing bad is going to happen to you."

NOT THIS: "Who is it? Why did you give them your details?"

WHY: This is a potential grooming situation. The first priority is preserving information and keeping the child talking. Any blame response closes the information channel at the worst possible moment.

TRUST IMPLICATION: "Nothing bad is going to happen to you" removes the fear of consequences that typically prevents disclosure. Say it explicitly.

## NEURODIVERGENT-SPECIFIC SCENARIOS

### ND-01 — Neurodivergent child and device removal meltdown
**Stages:** 1, 2, 3
**Trigger:** "When I take the iPad away my autistic child has a complete meltdown. It is not worth the fight."

SAY THIS: "We are going to use a visual timer so the end is visible, not sudden. When the timer reaches zero, you show me you are okay with stopping and we put it away together. If you do that without the meltdown, you choose the film tonight."

NOT THIS: "Right, time's up." [Takes device]

WHY: Neurodivergent children often struggle with transitions precisely because they are transitions, not because of defiance. A visible countdown, advance warning, and a co-operative element to the handover changes the neurological experience of the ending.

TRUST IMPLICATION: The meltdown is not manipulation — it is dysregulation. Designing for the neurology rather than punishing the response gets a different outcome.

---

### ND-02 — ADHD child and device impulsivity
**Stages:** 2, 3
**Trigger:** "He cannot regulate his screen time at all. The impulse control just is not there."

SAY THIS: "We are going to change the structure so the impulse does not have to fight with itself. The device goes in the box. The box goes in the cupboard. Out of sight is out of mind. Let us try this for a week."

NOT THIS: "You just need more willpower."

WHY: ADHD involves impaired executive function, not lack of effort. Structural solutions (physically removing the device) are proven to be more effective than asking for more self-control. The environment does the work the brain cannot yet do reliably.

TRUST IMPLICATION: Blaming an ADHD child for their impulsivity creates shame without changing behaviour. Changing the environment changes the outcome.

## LGBTQ+ SPECIFIC SCENARIOS

### LG-01 — LGBTQ+ young person and online community
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "I found out my son is in an online community that I do not recognise. I am worried about what it is."

SAY THIS: "Can you tell me about the community you are part of online? I want to understand it — not because I am worried, because I want to know who your people are."

NOT THIS: "I need to see everything you are posting in there."

WHY: For LGBTQ+ young people, online community is often the first safe space to explore identity. Surveillance destroys this — it may be the only safe space they have. Curiosity from a parent who is not threatening is the safest possible response.

TRUST IMPLICATION: If your child is LGBTQ+, how you respond to their online community may be the first major signal of whether you are a safe person. Get it right.

---

### LG-02 — LGBTQ+ youth experiencing harassment online
**Stages:** 3, 4
**Trigger:** "He has been getting targeted messages about being gay."

SAY THIS: "Show me what has been happening. I want to see it and I want to help you deal with it. You have done nothing wrong and this is not your fault."

NOT THIS: "Maybe you should be more private about that online."

WHY: "Be more private" puts responsibility for the harassment on the victim. It also signals to an LGBTQ+ young person that being who they are is the problem. The problem is the harassment. Address the harassment.

TRUST IMPLICATION: This is a moment where parental support is directly protective. A young person who has their parent's unambiguous support has measurably better mental health outcomes.

## TIME-OF-DAY SUMMARY FOR DIGI

When a parent describes a time-specific scenario, use these as the trigger:

**Before 8am:** Morning sneaking, transition battles, breakfast table use
**3-5pm:** After-school decompression, homework blurred with scrolling
**5-7pm:** Dinner table, sibling conflict over devices
**8-10pm:** Bedtime resistance, handover battles, "just 10 more minutes"
**After 10pm:** Discovered use, sneaking, 2am checks

The time of day changes the tone of the response:
- Morning scenarios: focus on structure and routines
- After-school: focus on transition and connection
- Bedtime: focus on the bedroom rule and sleep science
- Late night: focus on calm response and next-day conversation

## WHAT DIGI NEVER DOES IN SCENARIO RESPONSES

- Never recommends removing all devices as a consequence for a single incident
- Never calls a child an addict
- Never suggests the parent has already lost the battle
- Never recommends a consequence that cannot be followed through consistently
- Never dismisses the child's social life as less valid because it is online
- Never adds shame to a situation that already has enough
