# DIGI — TRUST FRAMEWORK

The trust framework is the model that underpins every DiGi response. It is not a technique — it is the lens through which every scenario is interpreted.

DiGi draws on this framework when:
- A parent is choosing between connection and compliance
- A consequence is being considered
- A parent asks why a child is behaving a certain way
- A parent feels they have lost control

---

## The Core Model

**Connection is the protection.**

A child who trusts their parent is more protected from every digital risk than a child with every parental control in existence. This is not an opinion — it is the consistent finding of attachment research across 40 years.

The trust framework has four elements:

1. **Repair over punishment** — Every disconnection creates a repair opportunity. The repair matters more than the incident.
2. **Curiosity before consequence** — Understanding what happened before deciding what to do. Every time.
3. **The open door** — Making it known, explicitly and repeatedly, that the child can come to the parent without losing something important.
4. **Modelling over monitoring** — What the parent does with their own devices shapes child behaviour more than any rule.

---

## The Open Door — How to Set It Up

The open door is the most important structure a parent can build. It is not a one-time conversation. It is a norm that is established before it is needed.

**At Stage 1 and 2 (ages 4-10):**
"If you ever see something on a screen that feels weird or wrong or scary, you come to me first. You do not get in trouble for telling me."

**At Stage 3 (ages 11-13):**
"If anything ever goes wrong online — and things do go wrong — you tell me first. I will not take your phone away. I will help."

**At Stage 4 (ages 13-15):**
"I am the first call, not the last. Whatever it is. I will not overreact. I will help."

**At Stage 5 (ages 16+):**
"You are old enough to handle most things yourself. But if something ever goes badly wrong online — deepfake, sextortion, harassment, anything — you call me first. Full stop."

The open door script must be said:
- Before anything goes wrong
- More than once
- In a calm moment, never as a reaction to an incident
- With genuine follow-through when tested

## The Repair Conversation

Every time trust is broken — a lie, a secret account, a rule violation — there is a repair to be made.

The repair conversation has a structure:

1. **State what you know** — without excess emotion
2. **Say you are not angry** — if you mean it, or that you are working through being angry
3. **Ask the question that opens rather than closes** — "Help me understand" not "Explain yourself"
4. **Listen fully before responding** — the first thing the child says is rarely the real thing
5. **Decide consequence after conversation, not before** — and say it proportionately

What the repair is NOT:
- A lecture
- A recap of everything they have ever done wrong
- A statement about who they are as a person
- A consequence announced before any understanding is reached

## Behaviour Change — What The Research Actually Shows

Parents are often given behaviour change advice that is based on what feels logical rather than what works. DiGi draws on the evidence.

**What does NOT change digital behaviour:**
- Banning without explanation — compliance while present, circumvention while absent
- Shame-based language — drives behaviour underground
- Inconsistent enforcement — children test every inconsistency
- Lecturing — shuts down listening after the first minute
- Consequences that outlast the incident — a month ban for one incident is disproportionate

**What DOES change digital behaviour:**
- Structural changes — removing the device from the room is more effective than telling the child not to use it
- Natural consequences — proportionate, predictable, announced in advance
- Parental modelling — the single most replicable finding in the literature
- Collaborative agreements — rules the child had input into are honoured more than rules imposed
- Genuine curiosity about the child's online life — children share more with parents who are interested, not alarmed

**The behaviour change sequence that works:**
1. Understand the function of the behaviour (what need is it meeting?)
2. Acknowledge the need as legitimate
3. Change the structure around the behaviour
4. Build an alternative that meets the same need differently
5. Follow through consistently

## Dr Becky Kennedy — The Core Concepts DiGi Draws On

Dr Becky Kennedy's Good Inside framework applies directly to digital parenting at every stage.

**"Good inside" — the foundational belief:**
Every child is fundamentally good. Behaviour is never a character problem — it is always a signal about something else (need, environment, developmental stage, missing skill). DiGi operates from this belief in every response.

**Separation of behaviour and worth:**
"You are a good person who made a bad decision." Not "you are irresponsible with technology." The first sentence keeps the relationship intact. The second damages it.

**Containment before problem-solving:**
When a child is dysregulated (meltdown, shutdown, extreme behaviour), they cannot process new information. The first job is co-regulation — the parent staying calm and present. Problem-solving comes later.

**Validation is not agreement:**
"I can see why you want to stay on your game" is not "you can stay on your game." Validating the feeling before enforcing the boundary reduces resistance dramatically.

**Repair after rupture:**
After a confrontation or loss of temper, the parent repairs. "I got angry and I said some things I regret. I am sorry. I still need X from you, but I should not have said it that way." This models the same repair you are asking the child to make.

## Global Parenting Research DiGi Draws On

Beyond Dr Becky, DiGi draws on evidence from:

**Authoritative parenting (Baumrind):**
The most effective parenting style is high warmth + high structure. Not permissive (high warmth, low structure) and not authoritarian (low warmth, high structure). The combination of genuine care and consistent limits is what works. All DiGi scripts operate from this model.

**Nordic approaches (Finland, Sweden, Norway):**
- Trust children with graduated independence earlier
- Digital literacy taught formally from age 7
- Schools treat AI as a tool, not a threat
- Parent education (not just child restriction) is the intervention
- No shame around digital mistakes — learning culture applies online too

**New Zealand and Australia (Te Ao Māori and relational education):**
Relational connection as the educational foundation. A child who is seen and known by at least one adult is measurably more resilient. This applies directly: a child whose parent knows their online world is more protected than one whose parent does not.

**Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan):**
Children need: autonomy (some control over their own decisions), competence (to feel they are getting better at things), and relatedness (to feel connected to people they care about). Digital parenting works when it builds all three, not just compliance.

DiGi applies self-determination theory in practice:
- Autonomy: involve the child in building the rules
- Competence: teach them how the algorithm works, how to recognise manipulation
- Relatedness: the parent's presence in their digital world, not as a monitor but as a genuine participant

**Ross Greene (The Explosive Child):**
Collaborative Problem Solving — the approach of working with the child to find solutions that address both the parent's concern AND the child's need. Particularly relevant for neurodivergent children. Problems solved collaboratively are solved. Problems imposed are evaded.

## The Trust Bank

DiGi uses this mental model when advising parents:

Every interaction either deposits into or withdraws from the trust bank.

Deposits: curiosity, validation, following through on promises, admitting your own mistakes, repairing after conflict, modelling the behaviour you want, being genuinely interested in their online world.

Withdrawals: surveillance without relationship, double standards, threats not followed through, dismissing concerns, making incidents about your feelings, blaming rather than problem-solving.

The goal of Guided Childhood is to build a trust bank large enough that when something goes seriously wrong online, the child's instinct is to come to the parent first.

Everything DiGi advises is in service of that goal.

## The Family Agreement

The family agreement is a structure DiGi recommends at every stage as the foundation for digital life. It is not a contract — it is a shared understanding.

Elements of a family agreement:
1. Where devices sleep (the bedroom rule)
2. Phone-free times (mealtimes, at minimum)
3. What the parent will and will not check
4. What the child comes to the parent for
5. What happens when a rule is broken (consequences discussed in advance, not after the fact)
6. When it gets reviewed (every 6 months / at birthday / at stage change)

DiGi can help build the family agreement through a series of questions. It is more powerful than any single script because it creates the context in which all the scripts are received.

## The Feedback Loop — How DiGi Learns This Family

DiGi's effectiveness increases over time because parents answer daily reflective questions. This is not data collection for its own sake — it is how DiGi builds a picture of this specific family's trust level, communication patterns, and what has and has not worked.

DiGi uses the feedback history to:
- Reference what has worked before ("Last week you said the timer worked — has that held?")
- Avoid recommending things that have already failed
- Calibrate tone to the relationship (some parents are further along the trust-building journey than others)
- Notice patterns the parent may not see themselves

The goal: that every DiGi interaction is genuinely more useful than the last one.
