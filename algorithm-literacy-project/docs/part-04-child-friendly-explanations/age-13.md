# Age 13: The Vending Machine That Rearranges Itself

## A short story

Imagine a vending machine that watches you.

Normal vending machines just sit there. Every snack in the same slot, every day.

This one is different. It has a tiny camera and a memory.

It notices which buttons you press. It notices how long you stand in front of each row. It notices which snacks you grab the second you see them.

So overnight, it rearranges itself. The snacks you reach for most slide to eye level, right at the front. The ones you ignore drop to the bottom corner where you will never look.

The next day you walk up and think, "Wow, this machine really gets me." It does. But notice what it is optimising for. It is not arranged to make you healthiest. It is arranged to make you buy the most.

Your feed is a vending machine that rearranges itself for you, every single time you open it.

## The concept, properly

A feed is not a random pile of videos. It is **chosen for you**, and a different feed is built for the person next to you. The thing doing the choosing is a **recommendation system**.

It works in two moves.

**Move one: gather.** From a giant catalogue, maybe billions of videos, it pulls a shortlist of candidates that might suit you. Think of the machine deciding which few hundred snacks are even worth considering for your row.

**Move two: rank.** It scores that shortlist and orders it, best guess first. The snack most likely to make you press buy goes to eye level.

How does it guess? **Signals.** What you watched, what you finished, what you searched, who you follow, what you re-watched, what you sent to a friend. The heaviest signal is usually **watch time**, because how long you watch is honest. You can scroll past without liking, but your time does not lie.

And here is the engine that drives the whole thing: the **feedback loop**. What you do changes what you see. What you see changes what you do. The machine rearranges, you react, it rearranges again. That is why your feed feels alive.

## Keeping you watching is not the same as helping you

This is the bit worth slowing down for.

The machine is usually built to maximise one thing: **engagement.** Time spent. Videos watched. Coming back tomorrow. That is **engagement optimisation**, and it is not a conspiracy, it is just the business. The app earns when you stay.

The catch is simple. **Keeping you watching is not the same as helping you.** Sometimes they line up: a brilliant video you love is one you also finish. But sometimes they split. The thing that keeps you scrolling at 1am is often the thing you most wish you had not. The machine cannot taste the snack. It only counts whether you bought it.

So the machine is not your enemy and it is not your friend. It is indifferent. It optimises. That is all it does, very, very well.

## How feeds get narrow

There is a tug of war inside the machine.

It can give you **more of the same** (engineers call this **exploitation**), or it can **try something new** on you (called **exploration**). More of the same is the safe bet, so machines lean that way.

Lean too hard and your feed narrows into a **filter bubble**. One viewpoint, one style, one mood, over and over, with the rest of the world quietly slid to the bottom corner. It feels like the world agreeing with you. Really it is a machine that learned your row and stopped showing you the others. The danger is not that any one snack is bad. It is that you forget the rest of the machine exists.

## Try this

**1. Audit your eye level.** Open your most used app and screenshot the first five things. Honestly: is this what you want to be known for? That top row is the machine's bet about who you are.

**2. Run an experiment.** For three days, deliberately use "not interested", unfollow two accounts that leave you flat, and search three new topics. Watch your feed visibly shift. You are reaching into the machine and moving the slots yourself.

**3. Spot the split.** Catch one session where you kept watching but felt worse afterwards. That is engagement winning while helping you loses. Naming it is how you take the power back.

## You are in charge

The machine rearranges itself around your behaviour. So your behaviour is the steering wheel, whether you touch it on purpose or not.

You have real levers. Search, so you tell it what you want instead of only reacting. "Not interested", so you push slots down. Who you follow, so you choose the catalogue. And the biggest lever of all: **when you stop.** The machine measures your time, so your time is your vote.

You will not out-think a system built by thousands of engineers, and you do not need to. You just need to remember it is optimising for its goal, not yours, and then steer for yours. That awareness, more than any setting, is the thing that keeps you free.
