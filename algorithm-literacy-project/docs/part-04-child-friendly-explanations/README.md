# Part 4: Child-Friendly Explanations

How recommendation systems work, told seven ways: for ages 7, 9, 11, 13 and 16, and for parents and teachers.

## Guiding philosophy: literacy, not fear

A child who understands how a feed is built is calmer, not more anxious. So everything here is written to teach, not to scare.

We follow three rules.

1. **Systems, not villains.** Apps are built to keep people watching because that is how they make money. That is an incentive, not a plot. We explain the machine, we do not demonise the people.

2. **Power, not panic.** Every file ends the same way: you are in charge. A child should leave feeling capable, not frightened. The point is agency.

3. **True, but right-sized.** We never lie to make it simpler. A seven year old gets a smaller, true picture. A sixteen year old gets close to the real engineering. Nobody has to unlearn what we taught them earlier.

Each level uses one running analogy so the idea sticks, and each builds the same core picture: a feed is chosen for you, the app learns from what you do (especially how long you watch), it gathers options then ranks them, what you do changes what comes next, it is often optimising to keep you watching, feeds can narrow, and you can steer your own feed on purpose.

## The files

- **age-07.md**: The robot waiter who keeps bringing the snacks you smile at. Very short sentences, one big idea: the app is watching to please you.
- **age-09.md**: The puppy you accidentally train. Signals and the feedback loop, in terms of rewarding behaviour you did not mean to reward.
- **age-11.md**: The DJ reading the room. Candidate generation and ranking, plus playing it safe versus trying something new.
- **age-13.md**: The vending machine that rearranges itself. Engagement optimisation and the filter bubble, with the difference between keeping you watching and helping you.
- **age-16.md**: Close to the real engineering: retrieval, ranking, objective functions, exploration versus exploitation, and how to audit your own feed.
- **for-parents.md**: A calm, practical guide: what the signals are, how to talk about it at home, family activities, and what is and is not worth worrying about.
- **for-teachers.md**: Ready-to-use classroom framing, links to the Part 9 curriculum, three discussion prompts, and common misconceptions to correct.

## A note on the analogies

The analogies are doors, not the whole house. Each one is true enough to walk through and is then handed up to the next age as something a bit more accurate. By age 16 the analogy quietly steps aside and the real terms (retrieval, ranking, objective function, exploration) take its place.
