# For Parents

A calm, practical guide to what a recommendation feed is, how it learns about your child, and how to talk about it at home without turning it into a fight or a fright.

## Start here: the mindset that works

The instinct, when you read a scary headline, is to treat the app as a villain and your child as a victim. Understandable, but it does not help, and children can smell panic. The mindset that actually works is calmer and more accurate.

A feed is a machine built to keep people watching, because that is how the business earns. That is an incentive, not a conspiracy. Your job is not to frighten your child away from it. Your job is to help them understand it, so they can use it on purpose rather than be used by it. We call this literacy, not fear, and it is the single most protective thing you can offer, more durable than any setting or ban, because it travels with them onto every new app and every friend's phone.

## What is actually happening in the feed

Three things, and they are not mysterious.

**1. The feed is chosen, not random.** Your child's feed and yours are different by design. A recommendation system assembles a personalised stream for each user. Nothing in it arrived by chance.

**2. It learns from behaviour, through "signals".** It does not need your child to like, comment or fill in a survey. It watches what they do. The signals it weighs most heavily are usually:
- **Watch time / completion:** how long they watch, and whether they finish. This is the big one, because it is honest and abundant. A child can scroll past without "liking" anything, yet still train the feed simply by lingering.
- **Searches:** what they actively look for.
- **Follows and subscriptions:** who they have opted into.
- **Re-watches, shares, saves:** stronger interest signals.
- **Speed of skipping:** how fast they swipe away tells the system what to drop.

**3. It runs a feedback loop.** What your child does changes what they see, which changes what they do next. After a few days this loop makes a feed feel uncannily "them". The same loop is why a feed can drift somewhere unhelpful surprisingly fast.

Two more ideas worth holding. The system gathers a shortlist of candidate videos, then ranks them best-guess-first ("gather, then rank"). And it usually optimises for **engagement**, which means **keeping them watching is not the same as helping them.** Often those align. Sometimes they do not, and that gap is where your attention belongs.

## How to talk to your child about it

A few principles that keep the conversation open rather than shutting it down.

- **Be curious, not accusatory.** "Show me your feed, I want to see what it thinks you like" lands very differently from "Give me your phone." Sit beside them, not across from them.
- **Hand them the controls, do not just take them away.** The goal is a child who can steer, not one who waits for you to police. Restriction without understanding tends to collapse the moment you are not in the room.
- **Normalise the pull.** Say plainly that the app is designed by experts to be hard to put down, and that feeling stuck is the design working, not a personal failing. This removes shame, and shame is what stops kids talking to you.
- **Name the split out loud.** Teach one sentence they can keep for life: "Am I enjoying this, or just stuck?" That single distinction, fun versus stuck, does more than any parental control.
- **Match the depth to the age.** A seven year old needs "the robot waiter brings snacks you smile at". A teenager can handle "it is optimising for your watch time, not your wellbeing". The age files in this folder are written to be read together.

## Family activities

**The feed swap (15 minutes).** Each family member shows their feed and explains why they think it looks like that. Children are often startled by how different yours is, and it makes "the feed is chosen for you" concrete and a bit funny.

**The five-screenshot audit.** Have your child screenshot the first five things in their most-used app. Talk about it without judgement: is this what they want to be known for? Is it making them feel good, or just keeping them there?

**Train it together, on purpose.** Pick something genuinely good they care about. Search it, watch a few right through, follow a couple of quality accounts, and use "not interested" on the dross. Watch the feed visibly shift over a few days. Nothing teaches "you have power here" like watching the machine respond to a deliberate hand.

**The stopping game.** Agree that closing the app while you still slightly want to keep going is a small win, not a punishment, because your time is the thing the app is measuring. Make stopping feel like strength, not deprivation.

## What to worry about, and what not to

**Worth your attention:**
- **Narrowing.** Feeds can tighten into a "filter bubble", showing one mood, view or body-image ideal on repeat. The risk is rarely a single video; it is the loss of range and the sense that this slice is the whole world. Widen it deliberately and often.
- **The genuine engagement/wellbeing split.** Late-night doom-scrolling, content that leaves them flat or anxious yet glued, comparison spirals. This is engagement winning while your child loses, and it is the real thing to watch for.
- **Sleep and displacement.** Often the clearest, most measurable harm is not the content at all but what the scrolling pushed out: sleep, movement, boredom, face-to-face time.

**Worth keeping in proportion:**
- **"The algorithm is brainwashing my child."** It is an optimiser, not a hypnotist. It nudges and amplifies; it does not override a child who understands it. Understanding is the antidote, and you are reading it.
- **The idea that one bad video ruins everything.** The mechanism that matters is the loop and the narrowing over time, not any single item. This should make you calmer, not less vigilant: you are watching a trend, not policing every frame.
- **Total avoidance as the goal.** For most families, fluent and deliberate use beats a ban that ends the day they find a way around it. Bans teach nothing portable; literacy does.

## The science in one paragraph

A recommendation feed is a personalised ranking system. From a catalogue too large to score in full, it retrieves a shortlist of candidate items (candidate generation), then a ranking model predicts how likely your child is to engage with each one (watching, finishing, liking, returning) and orders them by that prediction. The dominant signal is usually watch time, because it is abundant and hard to fake. Crucially, the system optimises an objective function built around engagement, which is a proxy for value, not value itself: most of the time they align, but when they diverge the system still maximises engagement. Every interaction feeds back as training data, creating a loop that personalises quickly and can narrow into a filter bubble. None of this requires malice; it is incentives and optimisation doing exactly what they were built to do, which is precisely why understanding beats fearing it.
