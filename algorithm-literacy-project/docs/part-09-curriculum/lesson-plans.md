# Lesson Plans

Nine full lesson plans spanning Key Stage 1 to Key Stage 4. Each one is self-contained. Read the linked Part 4 file the night before, print the named worksheet, and you are ready.

Every plan follows the same shape: title, Key Stage and age, duration, objectives, materials, starter, main activity, plenary, differentiation, and a link to a Part 8 simulation or a Part 4 concept. Timings are guides, not gospel.

A house rule for all eight: stay neutral about platforms. Say "an app" or "a feed", not a brand name. Name behaviours, not companies.

## Lesson 1 (KS1): What is a recommendation?

**Key Stage / age:** KS1, Years 1 to 2, ages 5 to 7
**Duration:** 40 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-07.md` (the robot waiter). Pairs with Worksheet A.

### Learning objectives
- I can say that a feed is chosen for me and is different for different people.
- I can say that when I look at something for longer, more of it comes.

### Materials
- A soft toy or a printed picture of the DiGi Squad robot (DiGi Junior) to be the "robot waiter".
- A tray and ten picture cards of snacks or toys (cut from Worksheet A).
- Two children to be "customers".

### Starter (8 minutes)
Sit the class in a circle. Hold up the robot. "This is a little robot waiter. Its whole job is to bring you things you like. It watches your face. If you smile, it brings more of that. If you frown, it brings less." Do a quick demo: show a card, ask the class to smile or frown, and "the robot" brings more smiling cards. Big idea planted: the robot is watching.

### Main activity (20 minutes)
Play **The Robot Waiter** (full rules in `games-and-experiments.md`). Two children sit as customers. You are the robot. Bring a snack card to each. Whoever smiles gets more of that kind. Within a few rounds, the two children have completely different trays. Stop and hold both trays up. "Same robot. Same snacks at the start. But look. Two different trays. Why?" Draw out: because they smiled at different things. That is a recommendation. It was chosen for you.

### Plenary (8 minutes)
One sentence each, going round: "My robot would bring me lots of ____ because I smile at ____." Close with the Part 4 line, in your own warm voice: "The robot is not naughty. It just wants to please you. And you can always ask it for something different."

### Differentiation
- **Support:** give the child two cards and let them point to the one they like; the adult narrates the rule.
- **Stretch:** ask "what if you smiled at something just by accident? What would the robot think?" This plants the age 9 idea early.

## Lesson 2 (KS1 to lower KS2): Asking for something different

**Key Stage / age:** end of KS1 into Year 3, ages 6 to 8
**Duration:** 40 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-07.md` and `age-09.md`. Pairs with Worksheet A.

### Learning objectives
- I know that a feed can fill up with lots of the same thing.
- I know I can ask for, or look for, something different to change it.

### Materials
- The snack or toy cards from Lesson 1.
- A paper plate per child labelled "my plate".
- Coloured pencils.

### Starter (7 minutes)
"Yesterday the robot learned what you smile at. But what if your whole plate ends up the same? All cake, no fruit, no anything else." Show a plate piled with one card repeated eight times. "Is that a good plate? What is missing?" Let them notice the sameness. Name it gently: the plate got narrow.

### Main activity (22 minutes)
Each child draws their "too samey" plate (four of the same thing). Then they do one **steering action**: they draw or choose two brand new different things and add them. Discuss: "What did you do to change your plate? You asked for something new. You can do that with a real feed too. You look for something different on purpose." This is the first taste of agency.

### Plenary (8 minutes)
Thumbs up or down game. You describe a plate ("all the same forever", "lots of different fun things", "one favourite plus some new tries"). Children vote. Land the message: a good plate has your favourites and some new things.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** pre-drawn cards to glue rather than draw.
- **Stretch:** "How would the robot know you wanted something new? What would you have to do?" (look longer at the new thing).

## Lesson 3 (KS2): Training the puppy: feedback loops

**Key Stage / age:** KS2, Years 3 to 4, ages 7 to 9
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-09.md` (the puppy). Pairs with Worksheet B.

### Learning objectives
- I can explain a feedback loop: what I do changes what I see, and what I see changes what I do.
- I can explain that watching to the end is a "treat" the app counts, even when I did not mean it.

### Materials
- The Part 4 puppy story (read it aloud, it is short).
- Worksheet B printed per child.
- Sticky notes.

### Starter (8 minutes)
Read the Maya and Biscuit story from `age-09.md`. Ask: "Did Maya mean to teach Biscuit to bark?" No. "But she did. How?" Every bark got a treat. Write on the board: **a treat teaches, even by accident.**

### Main activity (25 minutes)
Run **Feed the Algorithm** (card game, rules in `games-and-experiments.md`) in small groups, or do the unplugged version: each child is a "feed". They get a stack of mixed video cards. The rule: any card you stop and look at for a slow count of three, you keep, and the dealer hands you two more of the same kind. After three rounds, groups compare hands. They will be lopsided. Draw the loop on the board as a circle: *I watch → it's a signal → more of that comes → I watch more.* Then the key twist: "Some of you stopped on a card you did not even like, just because it was loud. What did the feed learn?" That you wanted more loud cards. The accidental treat.

### Plenary (10 minutes, includes Worksheet B)
Worksheet B asks children to circle which actions are "treats" (watching to the end, rewatching, searching) and which are not (scrolling past fast). Then one sticky note each: "One thing I will train my feed for on purpose is ____."

### Differentiation
- **Support:** work the loop as a physical circle, children passing a beanbag round to feel "round and round".
- **Stretch:** "Can you train it for two different things at once? What happens?" Introduces competing signals.

## Lesson 4 (upper KS2): Build a paper algorithm

**Key Stage / age:** KS2, Years 5 to 6, ages 9 to 11
**Duration:** 55 minutes (or a double)
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-11.md` (the DJ) and Part 5 (the recommendation engine). Pairs with Worksheet C.

### Learning objectives
- I can describe the two moves a feed makes: gather a shortlist, then rank it.
- I can rank a shortlist by hand using simple signal scores and explain my order.

### Materials
- Worksheet C printed per pair (a deck of ten "video cards", each with three numbers: a watch-time score, a likes score, and a "new to you" tag).
- A class scoring rule written on the board.
- Optional: a computer to run `python code/part-05-recommendation-engine/engine.py` at the end.

### Starter (8 minutes)
"A DJ reads the room. They do not play a random song. They watch the dance floor and pick what keeps people moving." Ask the class how a DJ decides the next track. Translate: a feed does the same in two moves. **Gather** a shortlist (which songs are even worth considering), then **rank** them (which one first).

### Main activity (30 minutes)
In pairs, students are the algorithm. Step one, gather: from ten video cards, throw out any tagged "not for this person" so a shortlist remains. Step two, rank: apply the board rule, for example *score = (watch-time x 2) + likes, and add 1 if it is "new to you".* They calculate each card's score and lay them out in ranked order. Then the discussion that makes it click: "Change the rule. What if watch-time counted x5? Re-rank. What moved to the top?" They feel, with their own hands, that the rule decides the feed.

If you have a screen, finish by running the Part 5 engine. Change the same weighting in the live code and show the order shift, mirroring what they just did on paper. Paper first, code second: the code now confirms something they already understand.

### Plenary (10 minutes, includes Worksheet C answer review)
Each pair reports their top card and why. Then the big question: "Whoever writes the rule, controls the feed. Who writes the rule in real life?" (The company.) Land it without villainising: that is just how it is built, and knowing it is power.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** give a two-number scoring rule and pre-sorted shortlist.
- **Stretch:** invent your own scoring rule that you think would be *kinder*, and justify it. This previews KS4 Unit 4.4.

## Lesson 5 (upper KS2): Escaping the bubble

**Key Stage / age:** KS2, Year 6, ages 10 to 11
**Duration:** 50 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-11.md` and Part 8 `echo_chamber`. Pairs with Worksheet C and the "Bubble or Balanced" game.

### Learning objectives
- I can explain how a feed can narrow until I only see one kind of thing.
- I can name two actions that widen a feed back out.

### Materials
- Part 8 simulation `echo_chamber` on a projected screen, OR the unplugged **Bubble or Balanced** sorting game (rules in `games-and-experiments.md`) as the fallback.
- Worksheet C (back page: the bubble diagram).

### Starter (8 minutes)
"Imagine your feed is a swimming pool. It starts with every kind of water. But every time you splash in one corner, more water gathers there, until you are stuck in one corner and forget the rest of the pool exists." That corner is a bubble. Ask: "Has anyone ever felt like they kept seeing the same one thing over and over?"

### Main activity (25 minutes)
**Plugged in:** run `echo_chamber`. Start it balanced. Set a viewer who slightly prefers one kind of content. Run the steps and project the feed narrowing. Pause it. "What would un-narrow it?" Let students call out a change (introduce variety, drop the preference strength), apply it, and watch the bubble loosen.

**Unplugged fallback:** play Bubble or Balanced. Each group sorts a stack of feed cards. The rule rewards picking similar cards, and groups visibly drift into one topic. Then they get three "wildcard" actions (search something new, follow someone different, watch a thing all the way through on purpose) and must rebalance.

### Plenary (10 minutes)
Two columns on the board: "narrows my feed" and "widens my feed". Class fills them. Close on the Part 4 truth: a bubble is not a punishment, it is just what happens if you only ever splash in one corner, and you can swim out any time.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** give the three widening actions as cards to place, not to recall.
- **Stretch:** "Is a bubble always bad? When might a narrow feed actually be fine?" (when you are deliberately learning one skill). Nuance, not absolutes.

## Lesson 6 (KS3): Watch time is a vote

**Key Stage / age:** KS3, Years 7 to 8, ages 11 to 13
**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-13.md` (the vending machine) and Part 3 (behaviour mapping). Pairs with Worksheet D.

### Learning objectives
- I can explain why watch time is the heaviest signal, and why it is "honest" even when I never tap like.
- I can sort my own actions into stronger and weaker signals.

### Materials
- Worksheet D printed per student.
- Board space for a "signal strength" line.

### Starter (10 minutes)
"You can lie with a like. You can like something to be polite, or never bother. But your time does not lie. If you watched it to the end, you watched it to the end." Pose the hook: "Which tells an app more about you, a single tap, or forty seconds of your attention?" Take a vote, hold the disagreement, do not resolve it yet.

### Main activity (30 minutes)
Students rank a list of actions from weakest to strongest signal (Worksheet D): scrolling past fast, a quick like, a comment, watching halfway, watching to the end, rewatching, sharing to a friend, searching for more. They place each on a "signal strength" line and justify the top three. Then connect to Part 3: explicit signals (you chose to do it, like a like) versus implicit signals (you did it without deciding to, like lingering). The punchline of the vending machine analogy: the machine watches how long you stand in front of each row, not just which button you press. Standing there is a vote you did not know you were casting.

### Plenary (15 minutes, includes Worksheet D)
Worksheet D closing task: "Describe one time you 'voted' for something you did not actually want more of." Share a few. The aim is a small jolt of recognition, not guilt.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** sort five actions, not eight, with pre-written justification stems ("This is strong because...").
- **Stretch:** "An app cannot read your mind, only your behaviour. What does it get wrong about you?" Introduces the gap between revealed and reflective preference (Part 7).

## Lesson 7 (KS3): Outrage travels fast

**Key Stage / age:** KS3, Years 8 to 9, ages 12 to 14
**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-13.md` and Part 8 `virality`. Pairs with Worksheet E. **Read the safeguarding note in `teacher-guides.md` first.**

### Learning objectives
- I can explain why content that provokes a strong reaction tends to spread further.
- I can separate "this is true" from "this is spreading", and check before sharing.

### Materials
- Part 8 simulation `virality` projected, OR the unplugged **Signal Detectives** activity (rules in `games-and-experiments.md`).
- Worksheet E.

### Starter (10 minutes)
"Two posts. One is calm and useful. One makes you angry. Which gets more comments, more shares, more time?" Most classes say the angry one. "Why?" Because reactions are engagement, and engagement is the signal the feed rewards. Be careful and neutral here: the feed is not choosing to make you angry, it is choosing what spreads, and angry things happen to spread.

### Main activity (28 minutes)
**Plugged in:** run `virality`. Seed two items, one neutral, one high-reaction. Run the spread step by step. Project the high-reaction item outrunning the calm one. Pause: "Nobody decided this. It fell out of the rule. The rule rewards reactions." Then the intervention: dampen the reaction weighting in the sim and re-run. The gap shrinks. Design choices change outcomes.

**Unplugged fallback:** Signal Detectives. Students get a set of headline cards and predict which will "travel". They tally reactions. The most reaction-provoking cards win the spread, every time.

Then pivot to defence: the **check before you share** habit. Three questions on the board: Is it true? Who gains if I share this? Am I sharing because it is right, or because I am fired up? This is where it links to ordinary media literacy and to Zara the Truth Finder's "think before tapping".

### Plenary (15 minutes, includes Worksheet E)
Worksheet E gives three real-feeling posts. Students apply the three check questions to each and decide: share, pause, or do not share. Share reasoning, not just verdicts.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** the three check questions as a printed bookmark to use, not memorise.
- **Stretch:** "If outrage spreads and a company earns from spread, what stops them turning it down?" Links straight to Part 7 commercial viability.

### Safeguarding note
Use invented, low-stakes example posts. Do not surface real divisive political or distressing content in class. The point is the mechanism, not the material. See `teacher-guides.md`.

## Lesson 8 (KS3): Audit your own feed

**Key Stage / age:** KS3, Year 9, ages 13 to 14
**Duration:** 60 minutes, plus an optional private home experiment
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-13.md` (the vending machine) and `age-16.md` (the audit levers). Pairs with Worksheet G and **The Mystery Feed** game (rules in `games-and-experiments.md`). This is the KS3 capstone: it assumes the signal work of Lesson 6 and the spread work of Lesson 7. **Read the safeguarding note below and in `teacher-guides.md` first.**

### Learning objectives
- I can read a feed backwards: from what it serves, work out the signals that trained it.
- I can judge a feed against a human question: is this serving this person, or just holding them?
- I can name the three reset actions and turn them into a specific plan.

### Materials
- One **Mystery Feed pack** per group of three or four (full contents and rules in `games-and-experiments.md`): twelve invented feed cards for one persona, a goal card, and a sealed history card. All invented, all platform-neutral. **Make one group's pack the success story persona** (see the game rules): a feed already trained into an asset, so the audit is not only a hunt for problems.
- Worksheet G printed per student.
- Board space for the three reset actions.
- No devices. This lesson is deliberately unplugged: nobody opens a real feed in class.

### Starter (10 minutes)
Recall the vending machine from `age-13.md`: it watches you and rearranges overnight, your row sliding to eye level. Then write up the line this lesson hangs on: **the top of a feed is the machine's bet about who you are.** Read out the first five items of an invented feed (the sample on Worksheet G works). Ask: "What is the machine betting about this person? What do you think they did to train it?" Take guesses, confirm nothing yet. Then tell them what they just did: they read a feed backwards to the signals behind it. That skill has a name, an audit, and by the end of the hour they will be good at it.

### Main activity (35 minutes)
Three phases: audit, diagnose, reset. Worksheet G walks the same three.

1. **Audit (12 min).** Groups take their Mystery Feed pack and study the twelve cards. For each dominant card type, the detective question from Lesson 6: what signal put this here? Watched to the end? Rewatched? Searched? Followed? Lingered by accident? They write the machine's bet in one sentence: "The machine thinks this person is ____." Then they open the sealed history card and check their deduction against the signals the persona actually sent. The gap between the two is where the accidental treats live.

2. **Diagnose (10 min).** Read the persona's goal card aloud ("I want to get better at drawing", "I want to get to sleep earlier"). Sort the twelve cards into two piles against that goal: **serving them** or **just holding them**. This is the age 13 split made physical: keeping you watching is not the same as helping you, and now the split is sitting on the table in two piles they made themselves. The success story group will find their piles lopsided the good way. Surface that loudly: this persona trained their feed on purpose and it became an asset, proof that the audit sometimes ends in "keep going, this is working", not always in a telling off.

3. **Reset (13 min).** Groups write the persona a reset prescription on Worksheet G, one line per reset action. Keep the wording exactly as the parent workshops use it, so home and school agree:
   - **Clear the bad signals.** Stop lingering on what you do not want. Use "not interested". Unfollow what leaves you flat.
   - **Search and finish good content.** Train it on purpose. Watch time and search are the strongest levers you have, so point them at what you actually want more of.
   - **Follow variety.** Force the exploration the machine under-prioritises. One deliberate out-of-lane follow or finish keeps the bubble loose.

   Push for specifics. "Search three drawing tutorials and finish them" is a prescription. "Use it less" is not.

### The home experiment (optional and private)
The back page of Worksheet G is a take-home version of the same audit for their own feed, if they have one. Set it as an invitation, never as homework to hand in: nobody collects it, nobody shares it, and any recommender counts, a video app, a music app, a games store, a shopping site. Three days, three moves: look at your eye level and ask if it is what you want to be known for, run the three reset actions, look again and see what shifted. Doing it alongside a parent is worth suggesting out loud: Workshop 2 in `parent-workshops.md` teaches parents the same three reset actions and how to co-view without inspecting, so a family that wants to can run the audit side by side, each on their own feed. For a class that can take it, add the `age-16.md` framing: change exactly one behaviour and watch retrieval and ranking respond.

### Plenary (15 minutes)
Each group presents their persona's prescription in sixty seconds: the machine's bet, the split, the three resets. Then close on the Year 9 sentence from the outcomes ladder, which is this whole lesson in one line: "I checked what mine was feeding me, cleared the bad signals, and trained it on what I want." Land the final word as power, not panic: you do not write the rule, but every signal the machine reads comes from you, so the wheel was in your hands all along.

### Where the skill goes next (one minute, worth saying every time)
Name the transfer before they leave the room. The moves they just practised, read a system's objective, send deliberate signals, judge the output against your own goal, are exactly how people direct AI tools: briefing an assistant clearly, checking an answer instead of swallowing it, steering an AI agent through a task, and noticing when "the tool is holding my attention" has replaced "the tool is helping me learn". Some people their age have trained a feed around a craft until it taught them, then used AI tools to build and sell what they made. Keep it invented and platform-neutral like everything else, but say it plainly: this lesson was not only about protecting yourself from a machine. It was the first lesson in running one.

### Differentiation
- **Support:** provide pre-written signal cards to match against feed cards instead of free deduction, and prescription sentence starters ("Stop lingering on...", "Search and finish...", "Follow one...").
- **Stretch:** rank the reset actions by strength using the `age-16.md` levers (watch time and search beat "not interested", and why). Then the sharpest question in the file: the machine measures when you stop, so which signal do you control completely?

### Safeguarding note
Nobody opens, shows, or reports on a real feed in class, ever. The audit skill is taught on invented personas precisely so that no child's actual feed becomes classroom material. Under current UK policy many Year 9 pupils will have no social media feeds at all: teach the skill as applying to any recommender, never assume access, and never imply anyone should get around a restriction. The home page of Worksheet G is private and optional. If the lesson prompts a disclosure about something a child has been served, follow your school's safeguarding policy. Frame every recognition as power, never as a failing. See `teacher-guides.md`.

## Lesson 9 (KS4): Design a kinder feed

**Key Stage / age:** KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
**Duration:** 90 minutes (one double, or two singles)
**Links to:** Part 4 `age-16.md`, Part 6 (alternative algorithms), Part 7 (can we change the algorithms), Part 8 `learning`. Pairs with Worksheet F and the end-of-unit project in `assessment.md`.

### Learning objectives
- I can describe the objective function: the thing a feed is told to maximise.
- I can propose an alternative objective and explain its trade-off, including how it could be gamed.

### Starter (12 minutes)
Recap the real pipeline from `age-16.md`: retrieval (pull a shortlist from billions), ranking (score and order), all in service of an **objective function**, usually a blend of engagement signals. Write the key sentence on the board: **change the objective, and you change the feed.** Then the hard question: "If you could set the objective for the feed of every fourteen year old in this country, what would you set it to?"

### Main activity (55 minutes)
Three phases.

1. **Pick an outcome (15 min).** Students choose a human outcome to optimise for, drawing from Part 7's twelve (sleep, learning, family time, physical activity, empathy, reduced anxiety...). Using the Part 7 summary table, they note its scores: evidence, measurability, feasibility, commercial viability. They are choosing with their eyes open.
2. **Design the metric (20 min).** What signal would the feed actually read to know it is working? This is the hard part, and the point. For "better sleep" they might use time-of-day patterns. For "learning" a quiz score. Push them: most good outcomes are hard to measure cheaply, which is exactly why feeds default to engagement.
3. **Break your own design (20 min).** Goodhart's law made concrete: "How would a clever person, or the system itself, game your metric and hit the number without the good thing happening?" If they optimise quiz scores, the feed floods easy quizzes. This is the lesson's intellectual heart. A design you cannot break is a design you have not understood. If a screen is free, run Part 8 `learning` to show an optimised-for-learning feed and where it bends.

### Plenary (20 min, sets up the project)
Each group pitches their kinder feed in sixty seconds: the objective, the metric, and the one way it could be gamed. The class votes not on "nicest idea" but on "most honest about its trade-off". This reframes the whole topic: there is no free lunch, only better-understood trade-offs. Hand out the end-of-unit project brief ("Design your ideal feed", rubric in `assessment.md`).

### Differentiation
- **Support:** provide a chosen outcome and a starter metric; focus the student entirely on the "how could it be gamed" step.
- **Stretch:** "Who should get to choose the objective for a child's feed, and how would a young person consent?" This is Part 7's moral question, and there is no clean answer, which is the point.
