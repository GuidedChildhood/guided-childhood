# Animation Briefs

Briefs for five short explainer animations, one per rung of the spiral. Each gives the concept, target age, script and storyboard beats, key message, and suggested length. They tie to the DiGi Squad characters (Oliver, Zara, Sofia, DiGi Junior) where it helps, but each stays generic enough to reuse without the characters.

Production house rules, applied to all five:
- Stay neutral about platforms. Show a generic feed, never a real brand or logo.
- End every animation on agency, never on fear. The last beat is always "you are in charge".
- No dashes in on-screen copy. Plain, warm narration.
- British English in any visible text.
- Keep one analogy per film. Do not mix the puppy and the vending machine in the same short.

## Brief 1: The Robot Waiter

**Concept:** a feed is chosen for you, and looking longer brings more of the same.
**Target age:** KS1, ages 5 to 7.
**Suggested length:** 60 to 75 seconds.
**DiGi Squad tie-in:** DiGi Junior hosts, bouncing in to introduce the robot waiter. Natural fit, since DiGi Junior is already the friendly child-facing robot.

**Key message:** the app brings you what you smile at. You can always ask for something different.

**Script and storyboard beats:**
1. DiGi Junior waves. "BEEP BOOP! Meet the robot waiter." A small round robot wheels a trolley of snack cards.
2. Two children sit at a table. The robot shows each a snack. One smiles at cake, one smiles at fruit.
3. The robot zooms off and comes back, piling more cake on one tray, more fruit on the other. Split screen, two different trays.
4. Freeze. DiGi Junior: "Same robot. Two different plates. It brought what they smiled at."
5. One child's plate is now ALL cake and they look bored. They tap the robot and point at something new. The robot brings a fresh kind.
6. Final beat: both children smiling at varied trays. DiGi Junior: "Your feed is your waiter. You can always ask for something new. You are the boss." Hold on the words "you are the boss".

## Brief 2: The Puppy You Accidentally Train

**Concept:** the feedback loop, and that watching to the end is an accidental treat.
**Target age:** lower KS2, ages 7 to 9.
**Suggested length:** 90 seconds.
**DiGi Squad tie-in:** Oliver narrates, using his coaching energy ("training" is on-brand for him). The puppy can be a squad mascot pup.

**Key message:** you train your feed without meaning to. Watching to the end is a treat. You can train it on purpose instead.

**Script and storyboard beats:**
1. A puppy, Biscuit, barks at the door. A child laughs and tosses a treat. Caption: "She did not mean to teach him anything."
2. Biscuit barks again, and again. More treats. Oliver, sideline-coach voice: "Every treat taught him. He is just doing what got rewarded."
3. Cut to a phone feed. The child lingers on a loud, silly video without really meaning to. A treat icon pops out of the screen.
4. The feed fills with more loud silly videos. Show the loop as a running circle: watch, signal, more of it, watch.
5. Oliver: "Watching to the end is a treat, even by accident. The feed thinks, more of this please."
6. Turn: the child deliberately searches football skills, watches a few all the way. The feed shifts toward football. "Train it on purpose. You are the trainer, not the puppy." End on the child and Biscuit, both happy.

## Brief 3: The DJ Reading the Room

**Concept:** gather then rank (retrieval and ranking), and steering out of a bubble.
**Target age:** upper KS2, ages 9 to 11.
**Suggested length:** 90 to 110 seconds.
**DiGi Squad tie-in:** generic DJ character, with Zara appearing as the "detective" who works out the trick. Light tie, fully reusable without her.

**Key message:** a feed gathers a shortlist then ranks it. If it narrows, you can steer it back out.

**Script and storyboard beats:**
1. A DJ at a party watches the dance floor. "A DJ does not play a random song. They read the room."
2. Move one, gather: the DJ flips through a huge crate and pulls a small shortlist of records that might suit the crowd. Caption: "Gather a shortlist."
3. Move two, rank: the DJ lines those records up, best guess first, and drops the top one. Caption: "Rank them, best first."
4. The crowd loves one style, so the DJ keeps picking it, until the whole dance floor is stuck on one beat and looking bored. The room has narrowed. A bubble forms visually around the dance floor.
5. Zara, detective hat on: "Spotted it. The room went samey. Time to steer." She requests a different track.
6. The DJ widens the set, the floor fills again. End: "Your feed gathers then ranks, just for you. If it gets samey, ask for something new. You can steer the room."

## Brief 4: The Vending Machine That Rearranges Itself

**Concept:** engagement optimisation, and the difference between holding your attention and helping you.
**Target age:** KS3, ages 11 to 14.
**Suggested length:** 110 to 120 seconds.
**DiGi Squad tie-in:** keep this one largely character-light for an older feel. Optional brief Zara cameo for the "check before you share" tag. Reusable as a standalone.

**Key message:** the feed is built to keep you watching, not to help you. Knowing that is power.

**Script and storyboard beats:**
1. A normal vending machine, every snack in a fixed slot. "Most machines just sit there."
2. This one has a tiny camera and a memory. It notices which rows a teenager lingers in front of, which buttons they press.
3. Overnight, it rearranges. The snacks they reach for slide to eye level at the front. The ignored ones drop to the bottom corner.
4. The teen returns. "Wow, this machine really gets me." Narrator, gently: "It does. But notice what it is arranged for. Not your health. The most presses."
5. Two labels rise over the machine: "holds your attention" versus "helps you". Show they are not the same.
6. Quick aside on spread: an angry, loud snack wrapper gets grabbed and shared fast; a calm useful one sits still. "Reactions travel. Travelling is not the same as being true." Optional Zara tag: "Check before you share."
7. End: the teen pauses, chooses on purpose, and picks something they actually wanted. "It is built to keep you watching. You get to decide what you do with that. You are in charge."

## Brief 5: Design a Kinder Feed

**Concept:** the objective function, and that change the objective and you change the feed, at a cost.
**Target age:** KS4, ages 14 to 16.
**Suggested length:** 120 to 150 seconds.
**DiGi Squad tie-in:** DiGi (the guide, not Junior) narrates calmly, suited to the older, more reflective tone. Fully reusable without the character.

**Key message:** a feed maximises an objective. You can aim it at kinder goals, but the kind ones are hard to measure and tend to cost engagement. It is a real, unsolved problem worth caring about.

**Script and storyboard beats:**
1. A control panel labelled "objective" with a single dial currently set to "watch time". The feed below it fills with endless clips. DiGi: "Every feed maximises something. Usually, this."
2. A hand turns the dial to "better sleep". The feed changes: it winds down at night, eases off near bedtime. "Change the objective, change the feed."
3. The catch appears. A magnifying glass tries to measure "better sleep" and struggles. Caption: "But how does it know it is working?" Show a noisy, hard-to-read signal.
4. Goodhart beat: the system games its own metric. Dial set to "quiz scores", and the feed floods trivially easy quizzes to hit the number while no real learning happens. "A metric you can game is a metric that lies."
5. The trade-off beat: a balance scale, "kinder feed" on one side, "engagement" on the other. The kinder side weighs less in the short term. "Most kinder goals cost engagement, which is why almost no one has shipped one."
6. End, honest and hopeful: a young designer at the panel, thoughtful. DiGi: "It is hard, not impossible. Some good versions have been tried. The people who understand the trade-offs are the ones who could build something better. That could be you." Hold on the dial, now showing many possible labels.
