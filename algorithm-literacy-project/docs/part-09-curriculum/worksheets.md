# Worksheets

Seven printable worksheets, varied by Key Stage. Plain markdown, so they print or photocopy cleanly. Teacher answer notes are marked clearly and are for you, not the pupil, so cut them off before copying, or copy only the pupil section above the line.

All examples are invented and platform-neutral. Children write about "a feed" or "an app", never a brand.

---

## Worksheet A (KS1): My Robot Waiter

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

A little robot waiter brings you things you like. It watches your face. When you smile, it brings more.

**1. Colour in the foods your robot would bring you.** Draw a smile next to the ones you like and a straight line next to the ones you do not.

(Teacher: provide six picture boxes here, or use the snack cards from Lesson 1. Pizza, apple, cake, broccoli, ice cream, banana.)

**2. Draw your plate.** Draw three things your robot keeps bringing you.

[ big empty plate box ]

**3. The tricky bit.** Your robot brought you something boring because you looked at it by accident. Tick what you should do.

- [ ] Smile at it so it brings more
- [ ] Look away and ask for something different

**4. Finish the sentence.** "If I want something new, I can ____________________."

> **Teacher answer note.** Q3, second box (look away / ask for different). Q4, accept anything about asking, searching, or looking for new things. The aim is the idea that the child can steer, not a perfect sentence.

## Worksheet B (lower KS2): Treats and the Training Loop

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

Maya trained her puppy Biscuit by accident. Every bark got a treat, so Biscuit barked more. Your feed learns the same way. Watching something is a treat. It says "more of this please".

**1. Circle the actions that are TREATS for your feed (they say "more please").**

watching to the very end   ·   scrolling past quickly   ·   rewatching a video   ·   searching for more of something   ·   glancing for half a second   ·   sending it to a friend

**2. Draw the loop.** Fill in the four boxes going round the circle.

```
   I watch something  →  it becomes a __________
          ↑                          ↓
   I watch it more   ←   more of __________ comes
```

**3. The accidental treat.** Write about one time you watched something you did not really want, just because it was there.

____________________________________________

**4. Train on purpose.** Name one good thing you want MORE of in your feed, and one action you will do to train it.

I want more: ____________________
I will: ____________________

> **Teacher answer note.** Q1 treats: watching to the end, rewatching, searching, sending to a friend. Not treats: scrolling past, glancing half a second. Q2: "a signal" and "that thing / the same kind". Q4: any deliberate action (search, watch fully, follow) counts.

## Worksheet C (upper KS2): Be the Algorithm

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

A feed makes two moves. First it **gathers** a shortlist. Then it **ranks** it, best guess first.

**1. Gather.** Cross out the two cards that are tagged "not for this person". The rest are the shortlist.

| Card | Watch-time | Likes | New to you? | Tagged not-for-you? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A football skills | 8 | 5 | no | no |
| B baking fails | 6 | 9 | yes | no |
| C car review | 3 | 2 | no | YES |
| D dog tricks | 9 | 4 | yes | no |
| E maths trick | 2 | 1 | no | YES |
| F dance clip | 7 | 8 | no | no |

**2. Rank.** Score each remaining card with this rule: **score = (watch-time x 2) + likes, then +1 if new to you.** Write the score, then list the cards in order, highest first.

A: ____   B: ____   D: ____   F: ____

My ranked feed (top to bottom): ____________________

**3. Change the rule.** New rule: **score = watch-time only.** Which card is now top? ____  Did the order change? ____

**4. Think.** In one sentence, who decides your feed: the cards, or the person who writes the rule?

____________________________________________

> **Teacher answer note.** Q1: cross out C and E. Q2 scores with rule (wt x2 + likes +1 if new): A = 16+5 = 21; B = 12+9+1 = 22; D = 18+4+1 = 23; F = 14+8 = 22. Order: D (23), B (22), F (22), A (21). B and F tie at 22, accept either order, or discuss tie-breaks. Q3: with watch-time only, D wins (9). Order changes. Q4: the person who writes the rule. This is the headline.

## Worksheet D (KS3): Watch Time Is a Vote

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

You can lie with a like. You cannot lie with your time. If you watched it to the end, you watched it to the end. The biggest signal a feed reads is usually how long you watch.

**1. Rank these actions from WEAKEST signal (1) to STRONGEST signal (8).**

___ a quick like
___ scrolling past in under a second
___ watching to the very end
___ a thoughtful comment
___ rewatching three times
___ watching halfway then leaving
___ searching for more of this
___ sharing it to a friend

**2. Explicit or implicit?** Mark each E (you chose to do it) or I (you did it without deciding).

like ___   lingering on a video ___   following someone ___   watching to the end ___   commenting ___   rewatching ___

**3. Explain.** Why is watching to the end a "more honest" signal than a like? Two sentences.

____________________________________________

**4. The jolt.** Describe one time you "voted" for something by lingering on it, even though you did not actually want more of it.

____________________________________________

> **Teacher answer note.** Q1 is for reasoning, not one correct order, but a sensible ranking runs: scrolling past (1, weakest), quick like (2), watching halfway (3), comment (4), watching to end (5), searching (6), rewatching three times (7), sharing (8). Defend, do not police. Q2: like E, lingering I, following E, watching to end I, commenting E, rewatching I. Q3: time is behaviour you did not perform for show; a like can be polite or careless. Q4: any honest example; frame as power, never shame.

## Worksheet E (KS3): Check Before You Share

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

Content that makes people react strongly tends to travel further. Travelling far is not the same as being true. Before you share, ask three questions: **Is it true? Who gains if I share this? Am I sharing because it is right, or because I am fired up?**

For each invented post below, apply the three questions and decide: **Share / Pause / Do not share.**

**Post 1.** "SHOCKING: this one food is secretly poisoning your whole class! Share before they delete it!"
- Is it true? ____  Who gains? ____  Fired up? ____  Decision: ____

**Post 2.** "Reminder from the school: sports day moved to Friday. Check the newsletter."
- Is it true? ____  Who gains? ____  Fired up? ____  Decision: ____

**Post 3.** "Everyone who disagrees with me is an idiot. Like if you agree!!!"
- Is it true? ____  Who gains? ____  Fired up? ____  Decision: ____

**Reflect.** Which post was built to make you feel something rather than to inform you? How could you tell?

____________________________________________

> **Teacher answer note.** Post 1: classic outrage bait, urgency ("before they delete"), no source. Do not share, verify first. Post 2: low-drama, checkable, useful. Reasonable to share after a quick check of the newsletter. Post 3: pure reaction-fishing, "like if you agree". Do not share. Reflect: posts 1 and 3 are built for feeling; tells include urgency, insults, demands to share, and no checkable source.

## Worksheet F (KS4): Design a Kinder Feed

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

A feed maximises an **objective function**, usually a blend of engagement signals. Change the objective and you change the feed. But the kinder the goal, the harder it is to measure, and the easier it is to game.

**1. Pick a human outcome** for your feed to optimise (for example: sleep, learning, family time, physical activity, reduced anxiety, empathy).

My outcome: ____________________

**2. Using the Part 7 summary table, record its honest scores.**

Evidence base: ____  Measurability: ____  Feasibility: ____  Commercial viability: ____

**3. Design the metric.** What signal would the feed actually read to know it is working? Be specific. ("Feel happier" is not a metric. "Self-reported mood at 8pm" is.)

____________________________________________

**4. Break your own design (the most important box).** How would a clever person, or the system itself, hit your metric without the good thing actually happening?

____________________________________________

**5. The trade-off.** Most kinder objectives reduce short-term engagement. Why does that make them hard for an advertising-funded company to ship?

____________________________________________

**6. The moral question.** Who should get to choose the objective for a child's feed, and how would a young person agree to it?

____________________________________________

> **Teacher answer note.** There is no single right answer, this is evaluate-and-create level. Strong responses: Q2 honestly transcribes Part 7 scores (sleep, learning, physical activity, family time score better; empathy, confidence, wellbeing score worse). Q3 names a concrete, observable signal and admits its noise. Q4 shows real Goodhart thinking (optimise quiz scores, feed floods trivially easy quizzes; optimise sleep, feed just nags at bedtime). Q5 connects to the advertising business model and lost engagement. Q6 wrestles honestly with consent and who decides, with no tidy answer. Reward honesty about trade-offs over slick utopian pitches.

## Worksheet G (KS3): Audit the Feed

*Name: ____________________    Class: __________*

A feed can be read backwards. What it serves is a record of the signals someone sent, including the accidental ones. Today you audit a mystery feed. The back page is a private version for your own, only if you choose.

**Sample feed for the starter (teacher reads aloud):** a gaming clip watched to the end · another clip from the same game · a third, longer one · a prank video · a phone unboxing. *(What is the machine betting? Probably: searched the game once, finished everything it sent, lingered on the prank by accident, and the unboxing is the machine exploring.)*

**1. The detective work.** Look at your group's twelve feed cards. Pick the three kinds of card the feed serves most. For each, name the signal you think put it there: watched to the end / rewatched / searched / followed / lingered by accident.

| What the feed keeps serving | The signal that probably trained it |

**2. The machine's bet.** One sentence: "The machine thinks this person is ____________________."

**3. Open the history card.** Which of your guesses were right? Which card in the feed came from an accidental signal?

**4. Serving or holding?** Read the persona's goal card, then sort all twelve cards against it.

Cards serving their goal: ____   Cards just holding their attention: ____

**5. The reset prescription.** Write the persona one specific instruction for each reset action. Be concrete: "search three drawing tutorials and finish them" is a prescription, "use it less" is not.

Clear the bad signals: ____________________________

**The private page: your own audit. Optional. Take it home. Nobody collects this.**

If you use anything with a feed (videos, music, games, shopping), try this over three days. If you want company, invite a parent or carer to run it alongside you, each of you on your own feed. They audit theirs, you audit yours, and you compare notes only if you both want to. Nobody grabs anybody's phone.

**Day 1, eye level.** Open your most used app and look at the first five things. That is the machine's bet about you. Is it what you want to be known for? You do not have to write anything down.

**Day 2, reset.** Run your own prescription: clear the bad signals (use "not interested", unfollow two things that leave you flat), search and finish three things you actually want more of, follow one thing outside your usual lane.

**Day 3, look again.** What shifted? What does that tell you about whose hands are on the wheel?

**And one more thing.** The skill you just used, working out what a system is optimising for and steering it toward your goal instead, is the same skill people use to get good work out of AI tools. You will use it for the rest of your life. This was practice.

> **Teacher answer note.** Q1 to Q3 mark against the pack's history card, but reward reasoned wrong guesses: the skill being taught is inference from behaviour, and the machine itself guesses wrong the same way. Q4 counts vary by pack; what matters is that the split exists and students can defend their sort. Q5: a strong prescription is specific and reaches for the strong signals (search, watch time), not just avoidance. Q6 is the positivity check: accept any concrete capability (a craft, a language, a sport skill, a school subject), and push back gently on vague answers like "be happier"; a trained feed teaches things, name the thing. The private page is never collected, never discussed unless a pupil volunteers, and never set as assessed homework. That deal is what makes it safe, keep it.
