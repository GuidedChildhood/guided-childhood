# Parent Workshops

Two ready-to-run 60 minute workshops for parents and carers. Each one has a facilitator script outline, a slide outline, and take-home actions. Run them in a school hall, a library, or online. No tech knowledge needed to facilitate. Read Part 4 `for-parents.md` once before you run either.

A note on tone, carried from the whole programme: **power, not panic.** Parents arrive worried. They should leave calmer and more capable, not more frightened. You are handing them understanding and a few small habits, not a list of things to fear.

---

## Workshop 1: How your child's feed actually works

**Length:** 60 minutes
**Audience:** parents and carers of children roughly 7 to 14
**Aim:** parents leave able to explain a feed to their child and start one good conversation at home.

### Slide outline

1. **Title.** How your child's feed actually works. One promise: you will leave able to explain this to your child tonight.
2. **The myth.** "It is random / it is spying / it is evil." We will replace all three.
3. **It is chosen for you.** Two phones, same app, two totally different feeds. Why? Because each was built for that person.
4. **The puppy.** Maya trains Biscuit by accident. Your child trains their feed the same way. (Borrowed from Part 4 age 9.)
5. **Watch time is a vote.** The biggest signal is how long you watch. Time is honest even when no one taps like.
6. **The loop.** What you do changes what you see changes what you do. Round and round.
7. **Bubbles.** Why a feed narrows, and that it is normal, not a punishment.
8. **You can steer it.** Three actions that widen a feed. Your child has more control than they think.
9. **Systems, not villains.** It is built to hold attention because that is the business. Knowing the machine is the power.
10. **Tonight.** The one conversation to have, and three take-home actions.

### Facilitator script outline

**0 to 8 min, Welcome and the myth.** Warm open. Ask the room, by show of hands: who thinks a feed is random? Who thinks it is spying? Who thinks it is designed to addict their child? Note the hands, do not correct yet. "By the end we will have a truer, calmer picture of all three."

**8 to 20 min, It is chosen for you.** Walk slides 3 to 5. The killer demonstration: ask two parents to describe the last few things their own feed showed them. They will be wildly different. "Same app. That is your proof it is chosen, not random." Introduce the puppy. Land watch time as the honest signal.

**20 to 35 min, The loop and the bubble.** Slides 6 and 7. Draw the loop as a circle on a flip chart. Then the bubble: "If they only ever splash in one corner of the pool, the feed gathers there." Reassure: this is normal mechanics, not a sign anything has gone wrong with their child.

**35 to 48 min, You can steer it.** Slide 8. This is the turn from worry to agency. The three widening actions: search something new on purpose, follow someone different, finish good content all the way through. "Your child is the trainer. Right now they are training by accident. You can help them train on purpose."

**48 to 58 min, The conversation.** Slide 9 and 10. Model the conversation live: not "get off your phone", but "show me your feed, what is it bringing you lately, did you mean to train it for that?" Curiosity, not inspection. Hand out the take-home card.

**58 to 60 min, Close.** Revisit the three myths from the start. Random, no, chosen. Spying, no, reading behaviour. Evil, no, optimising for attention. "You now know more about how that feed works than most adults. Go and pass it on."

### Take-home actions

1. **The "show me your feed" chat.** Once this week, sit with your child and ask them to scroll their feed and narrate it. Be curious, not corrective. You are learning together.
2. **Find one accidental treat.** Together, spot one thing the feed keeps showing that your child does not actually want. Talk about what signal trained it.
3. **Train it on purpose once.** Help your child pick one good thing they want more of and deliberately search, watch fully, and follow around it. Check back in a few days to see the feed shift.

Optional further reading on the take-home card: Part 4 `for-parents.md`.

## Workshop 2: Steering, bubbles, and the kinder-feed question

**Length:** 60 minutes
**Audience:** parents and carers of children roughly 12 to 16, and parents who attended Workshop 1
**Aim:** parents leave able to support a feed audit, handle the "outrage spreads" conversation, and discuss whether feeds could be built kinder, without doom.

### Slide outline

1. **Title.** Steering, bubbles, and the kinder-feed question.
2. **Recap in one line.** A feed is chosen, learns from behaviour, especially time, and you can steer it.
3. **The audit.** What it means to check your own feed, and the three reset actions.
4. **Co-viewing.** The five rules for sitting alongside: they hold the device, your feed first, curious questions, amnesty, short and regular.
5. **Why outrage travels.** Reaction is engagement, engagement is rewarded, so reaction-provoking content spreads. Mechanism, not malice.
6. **Check before you share.** The three questions: is it true, who gains, am I sharing because it is right or because I am fired up.
7. **The honest part.** Could feeds be built to help sleep, learning, family time instead? Sometimes, at a cost. (Part 7.)
8. **What is and is not worth worrying about.** A calm triage.
9. **Tonight.** The audit-together action, the family agreement idea, and the skill they are really building.

### Facilitator script outline

**0 to 6 min, Welcome and recap.** Quick recap for newcomers (slide 2). Set the frame: tonight is less "how it works" and more "what we do about it", including the honest limits.

**6 to 22 min, The audit, and how to co-view.** Slides 3 and 4. Explain a feed audit in plain terms: looking at what your feed has been feeding you, and deciding if you are happy with it. Walk the three reset actions teenagers learn in class (clear the bad signals by not lingering, search and finish good content, follow variety). The parent's job is not to do the audit for them but to ask the questions alongside. This mirrors the KS3 "Audit your own feed" lesson, so home and school agree.

Then teach co-viewing as a technique, because "sit with them" fails without the how. Five rules, worth a slide of their own:

1. **They hold the device.** The child drives, scrolls, and decides what to show. The moment you take the phone, the audit becomes an inspection and the door closes.
2. **Your feed first.** Open your own feed and narrate it honestly, accidental treats included. Going first buys you the right to ask about theirs, and models that nobody's feed is a report card.
3. **Curious questions, not verdicts.** "Why do you think it showed you that?" and "did you mean to train it for that?" work. "That is rubbish, why do you watch this?" ends the conversation for a year.
4. **Amnesty on what you see.** Decide before you sit down: nothing seen during a co-view gets punished. A child who expects consequences curates, and you learn nothing. (Genuine safety concerns still follow your normal family and safeguarding routes; amnesty is for taste, cringe, and time-wasting, not danger.)
5. **Short and regular beats one big audit.** Ten minutes monthly keeps the door open. A two-hour annual inspection slams it.

Close the segment on the upside, not the threat: a co-view is also where you will spot what the feed is teaching them well. Tell one success story, invented or your own: the child whose feed became a drawing tutor, the teenager who trained everything toward a language before a family trip. Half of co-viewing is catching them steering well and saying so.

**22 to 38 min, Outrage and sharing.** Slides 5 and 6. Carefully and neutrally: reaction-provoking content spreads because reactions are the signal. Not a plot, a mechanism. Then the family-safe defence everyone can use, the three check-before-you-share questions. Practise on one invented, low-stakes example so parents feel the move.

**38 to 50 min, The kinder-feed question.** Slide 7. The grown-up payoff. "Could the feed be built to help your child sleep or learn instead of just watch?" Honest answer from Part 7: partially, for some outcomes, at a real cost, and the kind things are hard to measure and tend to reduce engagement, which is why no one has shipped it wholesale. This respects parents' intelligence and arms them against both naive hope and pure doom.

**50 to 58 min, Worry triage.** Slide 8. A short, calm list. Worth attention: sleep disruption, a feed narrowing into something distressing, sharing without checking. Less worth panic: that the feed is "chosen" (that is normal), that your child's feed differs from yours (also normal), that they enjoy it (enjoyment is not the enemy, accidental training is the thing to notice).

**58 to 60 min, Close and take-home.** Hand out the card. "You are not trying to win against the machine. You are helping your child become its trainer instead of its puppy." And one forward-looking line, because it reframes the whole evening from defence to investment: the steering skill they are building on feeds is the same one they will use on AI tools for the rest of their working lives. Knowing what a system is optimising for, briefing it deliberately, and judging its output against your own goal is how people get good work out of AI assistants and agents, and some young people are already building small businesses on exactly that fluency. Your child is not just staying safe. They are training early for the most useful skill of their generation.

### Take-home actions

1. **Audit together, once.** Sit with your child and look honestly at what their feed has been serving, using the five co-viewing rules: they hold the device, your feed first, curious questions, amnesty, keep it short. Pick one thing to reset. Do it with them, not to them.
2. **Adopt the three share questions as a family.** Put them on the fridge: is it true, who gains, am I sharing because it is right or because I am fired up.
3. **Have the kinder-feed conversation.** Ask your teenager: "If you could set the feed to help you with one thing, what would it be, and how would the app even know it was working?" It is the same question they tackle in the KS4 lesson, and it is a brilliant dinner-table debate.
4. **Catch them steering well.** Once this month, notice something their feed is teaching them, a skill, a craft, an interest going deep, and say so out loud. Steering well should get more airtime at home than drifting gets.

## Running notes for facilitators

- **Flip chart beats slides** for the loop and the bubble. Drawing it live lands better than a polished graphic.
- **Use the room.** The two-parents-compare-your-feeds demo in Workshop 1 is the single most convincing moment. Do not skip it.
- **Hold the worry, then turn it.** Let parents voice fear early, then spend the back half on agency. A workshop that only validates fear has failed.
- **Stay neutral on brands and on screen-time limits.** Those are family decisions. You teach the mechanism and the habits.
- **If a parent discloses a serious concern about their child**, follow your school or setting's safeguarding route. Do not try to counsel on the spot.
