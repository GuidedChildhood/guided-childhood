# Part 10, Theme 6: Prosocial and Community

*Ideas 126 to 150. Recommendation systems usually rank for individual engagement, which can quietly corrode the social fabric: rewarding outrage over kindness, strangers over friends, the global over the local. This theme asks what a feed optimised for connection, kindness, and community would surface instead. The most credible idea here, bridging-based ranking, is already partly [ESTABLISHED] in research and limited deployment; most others are [SPECULATIVE].*

Feasibility labels follow the repository convention: **High**, **Medium**, **Low**, **Speculative**. See [`EPISTEMICS.md`](../../EPISTEMICS.md).

---

### 126. Bridging-Based Ranking

**How it would work.** Content is ranked higher when it earns positive engagement from people who usually disagree, rather than when it inflames one side, surfacing what brings groups together over what divides them. [INFERENCE]; bridging algorithms are partly [ESTABLISHED] (Ovadya and Thorburn; deployed in community-notes-style systems).

**Problem it solves.** Standard ranking rewards divisive content because conflict drives engagement. Bridging rewards common ground.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Bridging signals are computable where diverse audience data exists.

**Potential risks.** Can be gamed; "agreement across groups" is not always the same as "good".

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 127. Kindness Boosts

**How it would work.** Content and interactions that are demonstrably kind, supportive, or encouraging get a gentle ranking boost, nudging the feed's culture toward warmth without banning anything. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Kindness is invisible to engagement metrics; boosting it tilts the feed's norms toward generosity.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Reliably detecting genuine kindness (versus performed kindness) is hard.

**Potential risks.** Performative kindness gets gamed; whose definition of kind counts is contestable.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 128. Local Community Surfacing

**How it would work.** The feed reserves space for genuinely local content (a nearby club, a community event, a local maker) so a child sees their actual neighbourhood, not only the global viral churn. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds are relentlessly global; local life, where a child actually lives, is invisible.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Location-aware surfacing is feasible; quality and safety of local content varies.

**Potential risks.** Location data is sensitive for children; must be coarse and protected.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 129. Friendship-Strengthening Features

**How it would work.** The feed prioritises content from and about a child's real friends, and nudges small acts of connection ("haven't heard from Sam in a while"), optimising for relationships over reach. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds drown close friends under influencers and strangers, weakening the bonds that matter most.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Identifying close ties is feasible; nudging well is the design work.

**Potential risks.** Can feel manipulative; social pressure if overdone.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 130. The Collaboration Finder

**How it would work.** When children share an interest and complementary skills, the system suggests safe, supervised ways to make something together, recommending collaboration rather than solo consumption. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds keep children consuming alone; collaboration builds both skills and friendships.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Safe child collaboration is a hard safeguarding problem.

**Potential risks.** High child-safety stakes for any connection feature.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 131. Gratitude and Appreciation Loops

**How it would work.** The feed makes it easy and visible to thank a creator or friend whose content helped, surfacing appreciation back to them, building a culture of acknowledgement. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Engagement is taking; the feed rarely supports giving. Appreciation loops add reciprocity.

**Technical feasibility.** High.

**Potential risks.** Appreciation can be gamed for clout; keep it genuine and low-stakes.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 132. The Helper Feed

**How it would work.** A mode that surfaces opportunities for a child to help others appropriate to their age (answer a peer's question, contribute to a community project), recommending small prosocial actions. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds offer no path to contribution; a helper feed makes helping easy to find and do.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Matching safe, age-appropriate helping opportunities is hard.

**Potential risks.** Exploitation of children's effort; safety of any interaction.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 133. Conflict Cool-Down

**How it would work.** When an interaction is heating toward conflict, the feed and interface slow down, suggest a pause, and offer perspective before things escalate, designing for de-escalation. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds accelerate pile-ons and arguments; a cool-down interrupts the spiral.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Detecting escalating conflict in real time is hard.

**Potential risks.** Misjudged interventions feel patronising; can be evaded.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 134. The Common Ground Finder

**How it would work.** Across a contentious topic, the feed deliberately surfaces the things most people across the divide actually agree on, modelling that disagreement is rarely total. [SPECULATIVE]; related to bridging work.

**Problem it solves.** Feeds amplify the edges of every debate; common ground gets buried.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Identifying genuine cross-group agreement is hard.

**Potential risks.** False balance; can paper over real and important disagreement.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 135. Reciprocity-Aware Recommendations

**How it would work.** The feed favours communities and creators that engage *back* with their audience over one-way broadcast, surfacing spaces where a child can actually be heard, not just talked at. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Parasocial broadcast feeds offer no reciprocity; favouring two-way spaces supports real belonging.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Reciprocity is measurable from interaction patterns.

**Potential risks.** Could disadvantage large creators unfairly; reciprocity can be faked.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 136. The Encouragement Engine

**How it would work.** When a child shares their own creation, the system ensures it reaches a small, kind audience likely to encourage rather than ignore or mock, protecting the vulnerable act of putting work out. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** First creative posts often meet silence or cruelty, which kills the impulse; a kind first audience nurtures it.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Curating a reliably kind audience is hard.

**Potential risks.** Artificial encouragement can mislead about real reception.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 137. Civic Windows for Older Children

**How it would work.** For teenagers, the feed surfaces age-appropriate, balanced civic content (local issues, ways to get involved) framed for understanding and participation rather than outrage. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Teens meet civic life mainly through its most inflammatory feed moments; a calmer window supports real citizenship.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Balanced civic content selection is contested and hard.

**Potential risks.** Charges of bias; "balanced" is itself contested.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 138. The Pay-It-Forward Feed

**How it would work.** When a child benefits from someone's help or content, the feed gently offers a way to pass the kindness on to another, designing for a chain of generosity. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Goodwill stops at the recipient; a pay-it-forward nudge propagates it.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Can feel coercive; must stay optional and light.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 139. Group Belonging Over Individual Virality

**How it would work.** The feed favours content that strengthens a small community a child belongs to (a club, a class, a fandom done well) over content optimised purely for individual viral reach. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Virality rewards the individual at the community's expense; this rebalances toward belonging.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Measuring community health is hard.

**Potential risks.** Insular communities can also be unhealthy; needs care.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 140. The Mediator Mode

**How it would work.** In group spaces, the system can surface neutral framings and shared facts when a discussion fractures, acting like a calm classmate who reminds everyone what they have in common. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Group discussions among children can spiral; a light mediator helps them self-correct.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Real-time mediation is hard and high-stakes.

**Potential risks.** Heavy-handed mediation suppresses legitimate disagreement.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 141. Local Hero Spotlights

**How it would work.** The feed regularly highlights ordinary people doing good in the child's own community, countering the dominance of distant celebrity with reachable, relatable role models. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds offer only unreachable stars as models; local heroes are both inspiring and attainable.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Sourcing genuine local stories is the work.

**Potential risks.** Location and identity privacy; sourcing quality.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 142. The Anti-Pile-On Brake

**How it would work.** When many users converge to criticise one person, especially a child, the system dampens the cascade and protects the target rather than amplifying the dogpile for engagement. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds mechanically amplify pile-ons, which can devastate a child; a brake breaks the cascade.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium. Detecting a coordinated surge is feasible.

**Potential risks.** Could suppress legitimate accountability; the line is hard to draw.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 143. Shared Goal Feeds

**How it would work.** A community working toward a shared goal (a fundraiser, a clean-up, a group project) gets a feed that surfaces progress and contributions, designing recommendation around collective achievement. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feeds rarely support collective action; a shared-goal feed coordinates and motivates it.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Pressure to contribute; goals can be co-opted.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 144. The Welcome Committee

**How it would work.** When a child joins a new community, the feed prioritises content that helps them belong (norms, friendly faces, how things work here) rather than dropping them into the deep end of whatever is trending. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** New members face a cold, confusing space; a welcoming feed eases entry and reduces early harm.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Onboarding can over-shape a child's view of a community.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 145. Empathy Perspective Shifts

**How it would work.** Occasionally the feed offers a respectful glimpse into a life very different from the child's own, framed for understanding rather than spectacle, building the muscle of seeing through others' eyes. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Personalised feeds mirror the self back endlessly; deliberate perspective shifts grow empathy.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Empathy is hard to target without tokenism (Part 7 rates it Low).

**Potential risks.** Othering or voyeurism if done badly; respect is essential.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 146. Cooperative Challenges

**How it would work.** The feed offers challenges that can only be completed *together* (a group of friends each contributing a part), recommending cooperation as the path to the reward instead of competition. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Feed mechanics are competitive and solo; cooperative challenges build teamwork and bonds.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Exclusion of children without a group; coordination overhead.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 147. The Appreciation Wall

**How it would work.** A shared space where a community collects and resurfaces the kind, helpful, and creative things its members have done, the feed keeping the community's best self visible to itself. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Communities forget their good moments and remember the conflicts; a wall keeps generosity salient.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Curation bias; can feel forced.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 148. Friend-Echo Reduction

**How it would work.** Even within a friend group, the feed gently ensures a child still hears a range of voices and is not sealed inside an ever-narrowing clique, balancing belonging with breadth. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Friend-based feeds can become their own tight echo chamber; gentle breadth keeps it healthy.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Tension with the friendship-strengthening aim; needs balance.

**Educational value.** Medium.

---

### 149. Real-World Meetup Bridges

**How it would work.** For interests with safe, supervised offline outlets (a coding club, a sports group), the feed surfaces the real-world version, treating screen interest as a door to in-person community. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Online interest rarely converts to offline belonging; a bridge helps it cross over.

**Technical feasibility.** Low. Vetting real-world opportunities for child safety is hard.

**Potential risks.** Safety of any offline referral is paramount; needs rigorous vetting.

**Educational value.** High.

---

### 150. The Generosity Ledger (Personal)

**How it would work.** A private record of the kind and helpful things a child has done online, reflected back to build a self-image as someone who contributes, not only consumes. [SPECULATIVE].

**Problem it solves.** Children get constant feedback on consumption and none on contribution; a generosity ledger rebalances their self-image.

**Technical feasibility.** Medium.

**Potential risks.** Could gamify kindness into points-chasing; keep it gentle and private.

**Educational value.** High.
