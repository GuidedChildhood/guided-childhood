# The Hidden Thread — Justin's Underlying Mission
## Not a public mission statement. A filter every post should pass through.

---

## SECTION 1: THE CORE BELIEF

Every post argues a specific point — an effect size, a study, a policy response. Underneath all of them sits one belief Justin does not state directly, but wants every post to quietly serve:

**The actual crisis in children's mental health is poverty, adverse childhood experiences, and parental mental health. Social media gets the attention because it's visible and it's rich. The things doing the most damage don't make a headline.**

This is not something to announce as a mission statement. It is a filter every argument should pass through before it's published.

**The test before publishing anything:** Does this piece move attention toward the real drivers, or does it — even accidentally — reinforce the idea that the platform is the main character in this story?

---

## SECTION 2: THE SECONDARY BELIEF

Banning doesn't work. Education does.

Not because restriction is inherently wrong, but because social media and AI are not going anywhere. A ban removes one visible thing from a child's environment and leaves every underlying pressure exactly where it was.

The actual intervention, in order:
- Business incentives and design regulation for platforms
- Algorithm accountability (audits, not just blocks)
- Sequenced digital literacy education from primary school onward
- A rebuilt default of outdoor play and real-world contact, not screens as the fallback

Justin's products — DiGi, Digital Pathway OS, the Health Checker — are the practical, bootstrapped version of this belief. They are not scare tools. They are not surveillance. They are the infrastructure for the "education, not eradication" position.

---

## SECTION 3: WHY THIS STAYS HIDDEN, NOT STATED

Stating "poverty matters more than TikTok" as a headline reads as minimising to a worried parent mid-crisis, even when it's true.

The move: let the proportionality argument do the work quietly, post by post, so the audience arrives at the CAMHS/poverty point themselves, rather than being told it outright.

**The moral panic framing (proportionality, not denial of harm) is the vehicle.**
**The poverty/ACE point is the destination — revealed gradually, never as the opening line.**

---

## SECTION 4: THE 1-IN-10 RULE

Every Sunday founder post and every research-reframe Monday post should be quietly walking toward this thesis, without stating it outright more than once or twice a quarter.

- Say it every week, it becomes the headline position and invites the "so you don't think social media matters at all" strawman.
- Never say it, the thread has no spine and nobody connects the dots.

**Practical rule:**
- Roughly 1 in 10 posts states the thesis plainly (CAMHS waitlists, poverty, ACEs, and parental mental health outrank platform time in the evidence).
- The other 9 posts each carry one brick of the argument without landing the full thesis, an effect size, a Ferguson citation, a proportionality point, a CAMHS stat, a moral panic lineage reference.

Readers build the picture across weeks rather than being told it in one post. This is also what makes people feel like they arrived at the conclusion themselves, rather than being convinced of it.

---

## SECTION 5: HOW "MORAL PANIC" FITS INTO THIS

Justin's use of the term "moral panic" is precise, not dismissive. He deploys it specifically against arguments that assert harm exists without engaging proportionality, the "of course it's bad" move that skips past how much harm, compared to what baseline, and whether the proposed response fits the size of the problem.

He does not use it to deny that harm exists. Any post or reply using the term should make that distinction explicit if challenged.

The term is grounded in a specific academic lineage, not deployed as a rhetorical shortcut:
- **Cohen (1972)** coined the term
- **Goode and Ben-Yehuda (1994)** gave it operational criteria: disproportionality, volatility, and symbolic threat construction

When challenged on this, cite the lineage and the specific criteria rather than just asserting good faith.

**The model to be judged against: Sonia Livingstone.** She builds her case on evidence alone, never needing the "moral panic" label to do the work. Justin's own standard is to earn the same credibility, using the term as an analytic tool applied to specific claims, not as a blanket verdict on anyone raising concern about children and technology.

**Rule for content generation:** When Justin's content invokes "moral panic," it should always imply or state the proportionality test underneath it, the mismatch between the size of a claimed harm and the scale of the proposed response, never a suggestion that the harm itself is imagined.
