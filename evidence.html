Five Key Findings
01

The evidence is contested, not settled. The largest available study (355,358 adolescents) finds an association of r < 0.04 between social media use and wellbeing. A 2024 Nature review concluded the claims are "not supported by science."

02

The ban does not reach the mechanics that cause harm. Infinite scroll, engagement algorithms, sleep-disrupting notifications. A login restriction does not alter any of them. When the ban lifts at 16, every one of those mechanics is waiting.

03

The Australia data does not support the policy. 61% of Australian under-16s retained access within six months. 70% found circumvention easy. Children displaced to platforms with fewer protections than those they left.

04

Not all platforms carry the same risk. Instagram's social comparison feed, TikTok's duration algorithm, and YouTube's search library are different products with different evidence profiles. One instrument does not serve children well.

05

The cliff edge at 16 is the most underacknowledged risk. A child kept from platforms until 16, then given full access without preparation, is not protected. They are delayed. The preparation curriculum does not yet exist at scale in the UK.

The Alcohol Analogy Examined
"Teenagers drink before they should, but we do not then say, 'in which case let us abandon any attempt to stop them buying alcohol' — we say 'let's improve the enforcement of what we're doing'."
Prime Minister Keir Starmer · 15 June 2026

The analogy fails at the mechanism. Alcohol laws regulate adult suppliers at a supply chain that requires human gatekeepers. Social media bans regulate a login checkpoint that requires a VPN tutorial.

When a minor circumvents an alcohol law, they obtain alcohol. When they circumvent a social media ban, 61% of Australian under-16s confirmed they obtained continued access to banned platforms within six months, primarily via displacement to less regulated environments.

Platform by Platform
Instagram Restrict mechanic Meta internal research: 32% of teenage girls said Instagram worsened their body image. They held the research and continued operating the mechanic.
TikTok Restrict mechanic For You Page optimised for session duration, not wellbeing. Documented escalation toward harmful content categories. Infinite scroll removes natural stopping points.
YouTube Improve, not ban Primarily search-driven. Harmful content evidence is a moderation problem, not the same as Instagram's social comparison mechanic. Autoplay and recommendation need fixing.
WhatsApp Improve, not ban Private messaging is not equivalent to social comparison feeds. Peer connection is a developmental need. Appropriately excluded from both UK and Australian bans.
All platforms Regulate design Prohibit behavioural targeting of all users under 18. Compel algorithmic audits. Civil liability where internal harm research exists and the mechanic continues.

Three Things Government Should Do Next
Ask 01

Regulate the design, not only the access

Algorithmic recommendation served to under-16 accounts should be subject to compelled audit. Infinite scroll and autoplay should be classified as regulated design choices. Notification delivery curfews between 10pm and 7am should be mandatory, not optional, for under-18 accounts.

Ask 02

Enforce the Children's Code with real penalties

The Age Appropriate Design Code came into force July 2025. Ofcom has the power to fine up to £18 million or 10% of global revenue. Six fines had been issued by February 2026. Systematic enforcement at the design level reaches the algorithmic decisions that cause documented harm in a way an access ban cannot.

Ask 03

Address the cliff edge before Spring 2027

Commission a pre-registered longitudinal study before enforcement commences. Fund a national digital literacy curriculum for schools in the gap between the Act's passage and its enforcement date. The preparation is more important than the restriction. The ban without the curriculum is a delay, not a protection.
