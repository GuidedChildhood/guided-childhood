Dr Amy Orben's work at Cambridge's MRC Cognition and Brain Sciences Unit identified something every secondary school pastoral lead should know:

There is a developmental sensitivity window for digital harm.

For girls: ages 11–13.
For boys: ages 14–15.

Social media exposure during these windows shows the strongest association with reduced life satisfaction.

Professor Candace Odgers at UC Irvine adds the critical nuance: effects depend on vulnerability and environment, not just the device. Structure is protective.

Professor Andrew Przybylski at the Oxford Internet Institute describes the Goldilocks Effect: moderate, age-appropriate use isn't inherently harmful. Too much, too early, structured wrong — that's where risk concentrates.

Professor Sonia Livingstone at LSE ties it together: children need skills and agency, not just restrictions.

What this means for schools: it's not about banning phones. It's about building structure around the specific year groups where harm is most likely to compound.

That structure exists. It's called the Guided Digital Pathway.
