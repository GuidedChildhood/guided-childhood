# The Positive Canon — researchers and evidence Guided Childhood relies on
Verified 3 July 2026. Framing rule: small average effects, concentrated risks, regulate design. Never claim "no harm." Cite Ferguson as one credentialed voice, never sole source.

## The researchers
- **Candice Odgers** (UC Irvine): Nature 2024 review: the "rewiring" claim is "not supported by science"; blaming social media distracts from real causes (economic hardship in the bottom 20%, opioid crisis, discrimination). Policy: invest in the adults around children (US counselor ratio 1 to 500), fund digital literacy and support, tax platforms to pay; "bans let companies off the hook" (NPR June 2026, TED talk).
- **Amy Orben** (Cambridge): with Przybylski 2019 (Nature Human Behaviour, n=355,358): technology explains at most 0.4% of adolescent wellbeing variance. "Sisyphean Cycle of Technology Panics" (Perspect Psychol Sci 2020): four stage panic pattern repeats across novels, radio, comics, TV, video games; science restarts from zero each time. On bans (Science Media Centre): broad agreement we must do more for children online; no strong evidence bans achieve it; safety by design regulation is the ask.
- **Andrew Przybylski** (Oxford) and **Matti Vuorre**: Royal Society Open Science 2023: 946,798 people, 72 countries, 2008 to 2019, no evidence Facebook spread damaged wellbeing, slightly positive if anything (caution: adult skewed, correlational). Goldilocks (Psych Science 2017, n=120,115): moderate use may be advantageous; declines only past inflection points (weekday smartphone about 1h57m; descriptive, not prescriptive).
- **Chris Ferguson** (Stetson): meta analysis 2021/22 (33 studies): pooled social media effects on youth internalising below practical significance r=.10. Video games and aggression 2015 (101 studies): r=.06. 2020 reanalysis of APA violent media task force: negligible behavioural effects. 2025 preregistered meta analysis of 27 reduction experiments: null average effect. CAUTION: the 2025 meta analysis is heavily contested by Haidt's team (alleged calculation errors); never sole source.

## The benefits evidence
- Common Sense/Hopelab "A Double-Edged Sword" 2024 (NORC, n=1,274, ages 14 to 22): 74% of LGBTQ+ youth vs 52% of straight/cis peers say social media important for feeling less alone; 66% with depressive symptoms vs 48% without say the same. Always quote both sides: the same groups face more harms (6 in 10 LGBTQ+ affected by negative news vs 43%).
- UNICEF Innocenti 2017 (Kardefelt-Winther): moderate use evidence review endorsing benefits.
- Roozenbeek et al., Science Advances 2022: inoculation videos teaching manipulation techniques improved detection in 6 RCTs (n=6,464) and a 5.4M user YouTube field test (+5% recognition at $0.05/view). Caution: adults; outcome is recognition, not wellbeing. Say promising, not proven.
- SMART Schools, Lancet Regional Health Europe 2025 (1,227 pupils, 30 English schools): no wellbeing difference between restrictive and permissive phone policy schools; more total phone time correlated worse. Reading: bans are insufficient, not phones harmless.

## Contested points to handle carefully
- The 0.4%/potatoes framing: Twenge and Haidt argue social media and girls specifically shows r=.15 to .20; acknowledge the debate is live.
- Goldilocks inflection points are not prescriptions.
- 72 country study cannot prove safety for 13 year olds specifically.
