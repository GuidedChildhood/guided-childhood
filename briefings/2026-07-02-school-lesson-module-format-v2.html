How should you format the school lesson modules?

You asked how to format lesson modules so schools adopt them and actually teach them. Six lenses researched it: a PSHE lead, a pedagogy researcher, a skeptic, an economist, a resource format historian, and the pupil in the room.

60 Second Summary

The format that gets taught, rather than shelved, is designed for the least confident person in the building: a never trained form tutor with somewhere between 15 and 60 minutes. Over half of secondary teachers name confidence as the main barrier to PSHE, a third have never been trained, and Ofsted found untrained deliverers in half of schools. Every UK scheme that became a default (Jigsaw, SCARF, Oak's lesson unit) shipped complete, editable, scripted lessons a non specialist could deliver cold. Every framework without a lesson layer died.

The pedagogy evidence points the same way with one twist: scripted must not mean lecture. Interactive discussion based delivery beats teacher talk by roughly five to one on behaviour outcomes, one off assemblies and scare tactics reliably fail, and the flagship Google digital citizenship curriculum improved knowledge but changed zero behaviour in its only randomised trial. So the winning unit is scripted discussion: scenario cards, debate prompts and rehearsed skills, with the sensitive wording already written for the nervous teacher.

The economics seal it. Slides plus notes cost a fraction of filmed content to produce, and only text formats can absorb the July 2025 RSHE guidance (compulsory September 2026) and the ban stages with edits rather than reshoots. Oak now gives away a free RSHE curriculum, so the sellable layer is not lessons: it is compliance mapping, assessment evidence, DSL reporting and parent transparency. Price the whole school licence between £300 and £800, under the threshold a PSHE lead can sign off alone.

Key Findings

Design for the never trained form tutor, or the module never gets taught.

Reliability 9/10

55% of secondary teachers cite confidence and expertise as the main PSHE barrier, 31% delivering it have never had any training, and Ofsted found untrained RSHE deliverers in around half of schools visited. Real delivery slots are often 15 to 30 minute tutor times. The unit that survives contact with a school: a full hour lesson plus a short tutor time variant, editable slides with a scripted teacher voice track, anticipated questions answered in the notes, and SEND adapted outcomes built in rather than bolted on (the sector framework sets programme level adaptation, so per lesson notes are the differentiator, not the requirement).

Supported: Teacher, Skeptic, Economist, Historian
Sources: S1, S2, S3, S4

Sequenced and spaced beats one off, every time it has been measured.

Reliability 9/10

The 213 study SEL meta analysis found programmes work when they are sequenced, active, focused and explicit. Spaced delivery beats massed delivery (d = 0.54). Pupils who get regular online safety lessons rate them far more useful than those who get assemblies, and official UK guidance explicitly lists drop down days and one off assemblies as markers of ineffective provision. Weekly spiral modules, each lesson building on the last, are the only defensible architecture, which is exactly what your five stage pathway already is.

Supported: Academic, Pupil, Teacher
Sources: S5, S6, S7, S8

Scripted discussion, not scripted lecture. The interaction is the active ingredient.

Reliability 8/10

Interactive school prevention programmes cut target behaviours roughly 21% against 4% for lecture formats. But the teachers delivering PSHE are exactly the ones who fear open discussion. The resolution: script the discussion itself. Scenario cards using fictional characters (the distancing technique the PSHE Association requires anyway), structured debate prompts, and skill rehearsal with the wording pre written. A pedagogically weaker lesson that runs itself beats a stronger one needing 40 minutes of prep, so build the interaction inside the script.

Supported: Academic, Teacher · Challenged: Pupil notes discussion formats fail if the teacher visibly cannot facilitate
Sources: S6, S9

Knowledge transfer is not behaviour change. Nobody in this market has proven the latter.

Reliability 8/10

The only cluster randomised trial of Google's flagship digital citizenship curriculum (1,072 pupils) improved knowledge and confidence but changed no measured behaviour. The leading research group concluded youth internet safety education has proliferated with almost no rigorous evaluation. And more components made media literacy interventions worse, not better. This is both a warning (do not overclaim outcomes) and the single biggest opening: nobody has a rigorously evaluated UK offer. Bake baseline and endpoint measurement into the format and you can be first.

Supported: Academic, Skeptic
Sources: S10, S11, S12

The Oak lesson unit is the format standard. Oak itself is now your free competitor.

Reliability 8/10

Oak's four artefact unit (editable slides, starter quiz, exit quiz, worksheet) is what "school ready" means to English teachers, and its users report saving around 4 hours a week. But only 11 to 13% of teachers actually use Oak resources, its remit is under judicial review, and it has launched a free RSHE curriculum aligned to the September 2026 guidance, in your exact category. Copy the unit shape because teachers recognise it. Do not lean on "Oak aligned" as the pitch, and do not try to out free a state funded competitor.

Supported: Historian, Teacher · Challenged: Skeptic, on both Oak's reach claims and the badge's value
Sources: S13, S14, S15, S16

Sell what free content cannot do: evidence, reporting and compliance mapping.

Reliability 8/10

Free sets the price floor: Oak's RSHE curriculum, Twinkl giving every UK primary a year of Natterhub, ThinkUKnow, Be Internet Legends. Meanwhile 76% of primaries and 94% of secondaries cannot afford next year's costs, and what schools demonstrably buy is risk reduction: safeguarding evidence for Ofsted, DSL reporting, progression tracking, and mapping to the statutory guidance. The lessons are the delivery vehicle; the paid product is the evidence trail around them. Whole school licence at £300 to £800 sits inside the low value procurement threshold a PSHE lead can sign off without governors.

Supported: Economist, Skeptic, Historian
Sources: S15, S17, S18, S19

Regulation will keep changing. Only editable text formats survive that cheaply.

Reliability 8/10

The July 2025 RSHE guidance (compulsory 1 September 2026) forces every provider to reissue materials, and the ban stages will do it again through 2027. Slides and notes absorb that with text edits; filmed lessons need reshoots; interactive formats cost up to an order of magnitude more per finished hour. The same guidance also requires parents to be able to view all materials, which kills anything locked inside an app login, the way whiteboard flipcharts died with their hardware. Open, exportable, inspectable files are both the cheap option and the compliant one. Your scripts in the database with the law flag column are already the right architecture.

Supported: Economist, Historian, Teacher
Sources: S20, S21, S22

Pupils will arrive knowing the ban leaks. Format ban content as honest debate.

Reliability 7/10

In Australia, 61% of banned age children still had access months after their ban, and pupils openly swap workarounds. Yet two thirds of UK young people backed restricting at least some platforms in the national consultation. So the room is split, informed and allergic to being lied to. Any lesson framed as "the ban has fixed this" is discredited on contact. Format ban lessons as structured debate plus the autonomy frame that works with adolescents: the platforms are engineered to exploit you, and seeing through that is power. Never scare tactics, which at best do nothing and at worst backfire with the highest risk pupils.

Supported: Pupil, Academic
Sources: S23, S24, S25

Bought does not mean taught. Design the pilot to produce usage evidence.

Reliability 6/10

Two thirds of school software licences go unused, and edtech pilots routinely succeed technically while failing institutionally. But edtech trials that are structured convert around 24.8% to paid, and the most trusted purchase input is a recommendation from a teacher in the buyer's own network. So the pilot format matters as much as the lesson format: a named champion, usage visible to the DSL, a baseline and endpoint measure, and a case study the head can forward. The pilot's product is the story another school believes.

Supported: Economist · Challenged: Skeptic, US shelfware data may overstate the UK problem

Six perspectives

Lens Panels

The Teacher

Pick up and teach, but still editable

I have 20 minutes, no training, and a room that knows these platforms better than me. Give me the slides, the exact words for the hard moments, the answers to the questions I dread, and the SEND notes on the same page.

The Academic

Sequenced, active, focused, explicit

One offs and fear fail. Knowledge gains without behaviour change are the norm, not the win. Space the lessons, rehearse the skills, revisit the baseline, and measure. Nobody in the UK has done this rigorously yet. Be first.

The Skeptic

Purchased is not taught, and free is your rival

Two thirds of school software goes unused, Oak gives your category away, and no format has proven behaviour change. Sell the evidence trail and the workload saved, because the lessons themselves now cost zero next door.

The Economist

£300 to £800, zero prep, text first

Price under the sign off threshold. Every prep hour you remove is worth £30 to the school. And build in text, because you alone must absorb every guidance change between now and 2027 without a film crew.

The Historian

Complete schemes survive, frameworks die

Jigsaw and SCARF won by shipping everything, weekly, scripted. SEAL reached 90% of schools and evaporated because it was a philosophy without lessons. And platform locked content is always one policy change from worthless.

The Pupil

Stop repeating yourself and do not lie to me

I have had the stranger danger assembly nine times. I know the ban leaks because my cousin in Sydney is still on TikTok. Give me real scenarios, let us argue about it, and treat me like I am capable of seeing through the platforms myself.

So what do we do

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • Schools Ship the recognisable unit: editable slides, scripted teacher notes, starter and exit quiz, worksheet. Full hour version plus a 15 minute tutor time variant of every lesson. Export to PowerPoint and PDF so nothing is locked behind a login, which the 2026 guidance effectively requires anyway.
  • Digi Squad Teo, Olga, Alma and DiGi Junior are your distancing technique. The PSHE Association requires scenarios about fictional characters rather than pupils' own lives. You already have the characters. Every scenario card, debate prompt and script runs through them, which no free competitor can copy.
  • Product The paid layer is the evidence trail, not the lessons. Baseline and endpoint measures per module (the wellbeing tracker already exists), progression reporting for the PSHE lead, a DSL dashboard view, and mapping to the September 2026 RSHE guidance. That is what a school with no money still buys.
  • Ban readiness Format ban lessons as debate with the autonomy frame. Two thirds of pupils back some restriction, and all of them know it leaks. "The platforms are engineered to hold you, here is how to see it" fits non negotiable rule one: never allow or deny, always a calibrated pathway. The social media law flag already drives this content without a rewrite.
  • Pricing Whole school licence, £300 to £800 by size, free structured pilot. Pilot design: named champion teacher, usage visible weekly, baseline and endpoint measured, ending in a named case study the head forwards to another head. Budget for roughly one in four pilots converting.
  • Positioning Become the first rigorously evaluated UK offer. The evidence vacuum is documented in the literature. Publish your measurement framework, run the evaluation through the pilots, and every future school post, tender and journalist conversation inherits it.
Honest limits

Assumptions and the Missing Lens

This briefing rests on

The shelfware and pilot conversion figures are US datasets applied to the UK. The behaviour change evidence base is imported from SEL and drug prevention research because no UK online safety RCT exists. Vendor school counts (Jigsaw, SCARF, Oak) are self reported and likely inflated. And the pedagogy findings assume lessons are delivered roughly as designed, which the delivery reality data itself undermines.

The missing seventh lens

The parent. The 2025 guidance makes every slide parent inspectable, parents increasingly drive school reputation on exactly this topic, and your parent platform means a school module could hand each family a linked home conversation. Nobody researched the parent as the module's second audience and your bridge from school sales to parent subscriptions. That is a V3 worth running.
