The algorithm: the science, the history, and your child's mind

You asked for the science of the recommendation algorithm, the history of how it came about, what drove it, and how it hooks into human behaviour. Six lenses researched it independently, and they disagree in exactly the places that matter. This is the material for your scientific LinkedIn post and newsletter.

60 Second Summary

The recommendation algorithm was not discovered. It was assembled, step by dated step, out of behavioural science that predates the internet. Skinner showed in the 1950s that unpredictable rewards produce the most compulsive behaviour ever measured in a lab. Schultz showed in 1997 that dopamine neurons fire for surprise, not pleasure. A Stanford lab spent the 2000s teaching designers to combine those findings with well timed triggers; its students went on to build Instagram, and its orbit produced the industry's habit playbook. The feed you scroll is that science, aimed by machine learning at whatever keeps you there longest.

The money explains the aim. Time on feed is revenue: an internal Meta email values a 13 year old at roughly $270 of lifetime revenue, a peer reviewed model puts US ad revenue from under 18s at about $11 billion a year, and a randomised experiment in Science showed chronological feeds cut time on platform, which is why no platform volunteers one. The people who built the Like button, infinite scroll and the social validation loop have all said, on the record, that they understood the psychology they were exploiting.

Then the honesty that will make your post credible: the population level evidence that algorithms cause the teen mental health crisis is weak, and the famous "1 in 3 teen girls" statistic is a subgroup misread. What the evidence does support is sharper and scarier: feeds measurably spiral vulnerable children into harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate, a UK coroner has ruled algorithmically recommended content contributed to a child's death, and children themselves describe the defining experience as awareness without control. They know the machine is engineered to hold them. They cannot steer it. That gap is exactly where a calibrated pathway lives.

Key Findings

The mechanism is dated, named, peer reviewed science.

Reliability 9/10

Ferster and Skinner (1957): variable ratio reinforcement, reward after an unpredictable number of actions, produces the highest and most extinction resistant response rates of any schedule. Schultz, Dayan and Montague (Science, 1997): dopamine neurons encode reward prediction error, firing for unpredicted rewards and going quiet for expected ones, so unpredictability itself is the active ingredient. Fogg (Stanford, model published 2009): behaviour happens when motivation, ability and a well timed trigger converge, which is what a notification is. Pull to refresh and the unpredictable like count are these three findings, shipped.

Supported: Academic, Historian, Clinician · Challenged: Skeptic, on pop dopamine framing only
Sources: A1, A2, A3

The feed has a build history, and its builders confessed.

Reliability 9/10

GroupLens builds one of the first automated collaborative filtering systems, for Usenet (1994). Amazon industrialises it (paper 2003). Facebook launches the algorithmic News Feed (5 September 2006) and hundreds of thousands protest within days (estimates run to a million), then stay. The Like button ships (9 February 2009). EdgeRank's human readable formula is replaced by machine learning with roughly 100,000 weighting factors (by 2013). YouTube switches its target from clicks to watch time (October 2012). TikTok's For You page completes the arc (2018): a feed built on your behaviour, not your friends. Then the confessions: Sean Parker ("exploiting a vulnerability in human psychology... we did it anyway... God only knows what it's doing to our children's brains", 2017), Justin Rosenstein on Likes as "bright dings of pseudo pleasure" (2017), Aza Raskin on his infinite scroll as "behavioural cocaine" (2018), Chamath Palihapitiya on "dopamine driven feedback loops... destroying how society works" (2017).

Supported: Historian, Economist, Academic · Challenged: Skeptic notes insider regret is design intent evidence, not harm evidence
Sources: A4, A5, A6, A7, A8, A9, A10

The economics made engagement optimisation inevitable.

Reliability 8/10

Herbert Simon named it in 1971: a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention. Attention converts to revenue at published rates (Meta earned roughly $233 of revenue per North American user in 2024), an internal Meta email surfaced in the 2023 attorneys general lawsuit values a 13 year old at roughly $270 of lifetime revenue, and a peer reviewed model estimates US platforms earned about $11 billion from under 18s in 2022, with Snapchat and TikTok drawing a third or more of their US ad revenue from minors. The control experiment exists: in the landmark Science study, users switched to chronological feeds spent substantially less time on platform. The engagement ranked feed is the profitable feed, by the companies' own numbers. TikTok's internal research even quantified onset: a lasting usage habit forms after roughly 260 videos, under 35 minutes.

Supported: Economist, Historian
Sources: A11, A12, A13, A14, A15, A16

Children inherited machinery never calibrated for them.

Reliability 8/10

Every system in the build history was designed and tuned on adult engagement data. Meanwhile the adolescent brain is structurally more sensitive to exactly what feeds serve: in Sherman's fMRI work, teens viewing their own photos with many likes showed heightened activation in the brain's reward hub, and were more likely to like content simply because it was already popular. Developmental reviews (Somerville, Blakemore, Crone) converge: peer approval sensitivity peaks in adolescence while the prefrontal control system is still maturing. The mismatch is the vulnerability, and possible sensitivity windows around ages 11 to 13 for girls and 14 to 15 for boys mean population averages hide the exposed groups.

Supported: Academic, Clinician · Challenged: Skeptic notes subgroup findings cut both ways
Sources: A17, A18, A19

The harm evidence splits in two, and your post must respect the split.

Reliability 9/10

Strong: design intent (the confessions, the internal documents), and amplification at the vulnerable tail. A UK coroner ruled in 2022 that algorithmically recommended content contributed to the death of 14 year old Molly Russell, the only finding in this field with legal weight; audits found feeds of teens signalling distress fill with harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate, within hours. Weak: population level causation. Digital technology use explains at most 0.4% of variance in adolescent wellbeing, six week deactivation experiments improve mood by only 0.04 to 0.06 standard deviations, the strongest causal study predates recommendation algorithms entirely, and switching users to chronological feeds did not improve wellbeing or reduce polarisation. The scientifically honest claim: the machinery demonstrably exploits attention, and it demonstrably concentrates harm on already struggling children, but "algorithms caused the teen mental health crisis" outruns the data.

Supported: Skeptic, Academic, Clinician converge on the split
Sources: A20, A21, A22, A23, A24, A25

Retire the "1 in 3 teen girls" statistic.

Reliability 8/10

The most quoted number from the Facebook Files is a misread. The internal slide said Instagram made body image worse for 1 in 3 of the teen girls who already felt bad about their bodies, a subgroup, not all teen girls, and within that same subgroup 22% said Instagram made them feel better. The survey behind it had around 2,500 respondents, the qualitative decks were small, and none of it was peer reviewed either way; Slate's own framing is that the findings stay damning despite the subgroup caveat. Using the misread version in a post invites a fact check that discredits everything around it. The replacement facts are stronger anyway: Meta's researchers documented the problem and the company kept optimising, and 60% of youth with depressive symptoms report feed content making them feel worse about their looks against 25% of those without.

Supported: Skeptic · Challenged: Clinician and Economist quoted the popular version
Sources: A20, A26

Kids know, and kids cannot steer. Awareness without control.

Reliability 9/10

63% of UK 8 to 17s know platforms use algorithms to direct content at them, rising to 81% of 16 to 17s, with a class gap (75% awareness in the wealthiest households against about 60% elsewhere). Harm arrives unbidden: of the nearly three quarters of 11 to 17s who saw harmful content, 35% met it while scrolling and 1% by searching. Teens build folk theories and actively try to retrain their feeds, and the research shows it mostly fails: one stray search can dominate a feed for weeks, and "not interested" buttons feel dead. Their own verdict is turning fast: 48% of teens now say social media is mostly negative for people their age, up from 32% three years earlier, and young people quoted the spiral precisely: "even if you don't like it, it gets bumped to you... it delayed my recovery."

Supported: The Child, Clinician, Academic
Sources: A27, A28, A29, A30, A31, A32

Regulation has shifted from screen time to design, and so should you.

Reliability 8/10

The clinical and regulatory world has stopped asking "how long" and started asking "what does the feed do". The American Academy of Pediatrics says the ecosystem "can no longer be thought of as screen time for families to manage". WHO data shows 11% of adolescents across 44 countries meet problematic use criteria, up from 7% in 2018. Ofcom's children's codes (in force July 2025) legally require recommenders to filter suicide, self harm and eating disorder content out of children's feeds, and the EU has preliminarily found TikTok's addictive design in breach of the DSA, having already forced TikTok Lite's rewards scheme off the market, the first proof that regulating design, not content, moves platforms. Note before quoting: the EU finding is preliminary.

Supported: Clinician, Economist
Sources: A33, A34, A35, A36

What actually helps families is narrow, specific and doable.

Reliability 7/10

A randomised trial found capping social media at one hour a day for three weeks improved appearance and weight esteem in distressed young people: reduction works, abstinence is not required. Phones out of the bedroom remains the highest value single move (sleep is the most measurable and reversible harm pathway, and 50% of teen girls say social media costs them sleep). NICE now instructs clinicians to ask about the feed as part of self harm assessment. And the frame that lands with adolescents is autonomy, not protection: the platforms are engineered to hold you, and seeing the machinery is power.

Supported: Clinician, The Child, Academic
Sources: A29, A37, A38, A39

Six perspectives

Lens Panels

The Clinician

The only legal finding of fact in this field

A coroner ruled algorithmic recommendation contributed to Molly Russell's death. Averages are the wrong statistic for a clinic: the 11% with compulsive use and the distressed child whose feed converges on harm are my patients. Sleep is where I start with every family.

The Academic

Separate the mechanism from the outcome

The machinery is proven: variable reward, prediction error, engineered triggers, aimed at a developmentally hypersensitive reward system. Aggregate harm is small and contested. A scientifically honest post makes both statements in the same breath.

The Skeptic

Never claim the algorithm is the proven culprit

Technology use explains at most 0.4% of wellbeing variance. The feed swap experiment did not help anyone. The famous teen girls statistic is a misread. Say design exploits attention, say harm concentrates on the vulnerable, and stop there, or a researcher will take the post apart.

The Economist

A 13 year old is worth $270. Everything follows.

Attention is revenue, children are inventory, chronological feeds lose money, and fines to date are rounding errors. Only design regulation has ever moved a platform. You cannot therapy your way out of an incentive.

The Historian

Assembled step by step, regretted on the record

1994 collaborative filtering, 2006 the ranked feed, 2009 the Like, 2012 watch time, 2018 the interest graph. The people who built the compulsive parts have named what they did. And nothing in the panic archive resembles a machine that learns each child individually.

The Child

I know it's engineered. I still can't steer it.

Most of us know the feed is built to hold us. One sad video and my whole feed is Sadtok for weeks, whatever buttons I press. Harm finds me while I scroll; I almost never go looking. Don't teach me the feed is curated. I know. Give me control, or teach me where the exits are.

So what do we do

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • Marketing Your scientific LinkedIn post writes itself from the split. Hook with the build history and the $270 email, walk the mechanism chain with named researchers and dates, then do the thing nobody else does: state honestly that population harm evidence is small while vulnerable tail amplification is proven. The honesty is the differentiator, and it is fact check proof.
  • Curriculum Teach awareness without control as the lesson frame. Kids already know feeds are curated, so a lesson announcing that is dead on arrival. The gap is control and the business model behind it. Use the autonomy reframe the evidence supports: the platforms are engineered to hold you, and seeing the machinery is power. This slots straight into the algorithm literacy project and Stages 3 and 4.
  • Scripts Ship a Sadtok script. The spiral has a name in kids' own words. A parent script for "what does your feed think of you at the moment", a feed reset ritual done together, and phones out of the bedroom as the one non negotiable. Reduction, not abstinence: the trial evidence supports an hour cap, which is a calibrated pathway, not an allow or deny.
  • Ban readiness The ban removes platforms, not mechanics. Variable reward and engineered triggers live on in gaming and in whatever fills the vacuum, and Australia shows the leak. Your line for the next nine months: the ban buys time, it does not retrain anyone. That is why a pathway matters, and it is honest enough to survive contact with pupils who know the workarounds.
  • DiGi Give DiGi the split brain. When parents ask "is the algorithm harming my child", DiGi should hold both truths: the design exploits attention by intent, the average child is probably fine, and the struggling child's feed makes struggling worse. Then route to the sleep question first, because it is the most measurable and most reversible pathway.
Honest limits

Assumptions and the Missing Lens

This briefing rests on

The amplification audits (Amnesty, CCDH, 5Rights) use simulated accounts and are not peer reviewed, though their findings are consistent across organisations and years. The internal company documents are leaked or litigated material, strong on intent, unaudited on method. The EU's addictive design finding is preliminary. And nearly all the causal experiments study adults or all ages platforms, because nobody has yet run a randomised trial isolating recommendation algorithms in children, which is the largest single hole in the field.

The missing seventh lens

The engineer. Nobody in this briefing has actually built a ranking system. An engineer's lens would answer the questions the others cannot: why "not interested" signals barely work, what controls are technically feasible tomorrow, and what a child safe recommender would actually optimise. That lens would turn your critique into a proposal, and it is a V3 worth running before the school version teaches this.
