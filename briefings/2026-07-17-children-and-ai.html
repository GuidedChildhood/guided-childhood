Guided Childhood Research Briefing

Children and AI

The next debate, not the last one. What generative AI, AI companions, AI in school and AI toys actually do to children, weighed on the newest 2025 and 2026 evidence, and why a calibrated developmental pathway beats both the panic and the ban.

60 Second Summary

AI has already arrived in childhood, quietly and at scale. Half of UK children aged eight to seventeen have used a generative AI tool, and in the US 72 percent of teens have tried an AI companion and 52 percent use one regularly. This is not a future problem to prepare for. It is the room your child is already in.

The single most useful move, and the one almost no headline makes, is to split the harm into two piles. One pile is real, counted, and getting worse fast: AI made child sexual abuse imagery and deepfake nudify abuse. The Internet Watch Foundation assessed 8,029 AI abuse images in a single year, and AI abuse videos jumped from 13 to 3,443, with girls 94 percent of victims. Here the alarm is fully earned. The other pile is softer and hotly contested: the brain rot and companion addiction claims, whose studies turn out to share the exact same weaknesses as the AI tutor miracle claims. Be severe on the first pile. Be calm and sceptical about the second. That posture is the whole brand.

On learning, the evidence lands almost perfectly on your thesis. The same generative AI roughly doubled learning in a Harvard trial and cut retained knowledge by about a quarter in a four million student dataset. Design and developmental fit decided which. An AI that makes the child struggle productively helps. An AI that vends the answer offloads the thinking. And the offload cost scales with age, largest for teenagers and undetectable in Year 5, which argues for a staged pathway, not a flat rule.

On companions, the honest reading is neither ban nor shrug. Most teens keep perspective, 80 percent still spend more time with real friends and 67 percent find the bot less satisfying than a person. But a real minority do not: a third find the AI as satisfying as a friend, and a randomised trial shows heavy use causes loneliness and dependence, concentrated in the lonely, anxious and neurodivergent child, the exact child on the two year CAMHS waiting list that now holds 550,610 children in England. The bot is filling a service vacuum, not winning a fair fight.

Ask the children and they settle it. Only 15 percent think a ban would make them safer, 69 percent would rather know a space is safe, and 62 percent say a ban would feel like punishment. They are asking for safer design and to be taught, not to be shut out. The experts agree: safety has to be built into the product, not outsourced to an exhausted parent, and AI is a tool, not a companion. That is the calibrated pathway, and the evidence now carries it end to end.

The clear answer

Do not ban the AI and do not wave it through. Separate the severe evidenced harm from the diffuse panic, centre the activity and the child not the device, build safety in by design, and teach a staged, age matched pathway so that by sixteen a young person can steer AI instead of being steered by it. Neither the panic nor the ban fits the evidence. The pathway does.

Key Findings

AI made child abuse imagery is the one harm where alarm is fully earned, and it is escalating fast

Reliability 9/10

The Internet Watch Foundation assessed 8,029 AI generated child sexual abuse images and videos in 2025, with AI abuse videos rising from 13 in 2024 to 3,443 in 2025, and girls making up 94 percent of victims. Open models can clone a specific child from around twenty photos in roughly fifteen minutes. Unlike the contested cognition claims, this rests on counts of criminal material, not surveys, and the cause is direct: a nudify tool produces the image. This is a supply and tooling harm, and it deserves the severe end of the response.

Supported: Skeptic, Designer, Clinician·Challenged: none on the counts
Sources: Internet Watch Foundation 2025; Stanford AI CSAM paper 2025

The same generative AI can double learning or erode it, and design decides which

Reliability 9/10

A pre registered Harvard randomised trial found a purpose built, guard railed AI tutor produced roughly double the learning of an active learning class, in less time. A separate analysis of four million students on a maths platform found that after ChatGPT arrived, study time and retained knowledge on AI exposed problems fell, cumulatively about a quarter less retention. The technology was the same. The difference was design: an AI that makes the child struggle productively helps, an AI that vends the answer offloads the thinking. This is the calibrated pathway proven from both sides.

Supported: Academic, Designer·Challenged: Skeptic on novelty effects and short horizons in both studies
Sources: Kestin and colleagues, Scientific Reports 2025; Rismanchian and colleagues, ALEKS working paper 2026

AI companions are mainstream, but the harm concentrates in the vulnerable minority, not the average child

Reliability 9/10

72 percent of US teens have used an AI companion and 52 percent use one regularly, yet 80 percent still spend more time with real friends and 67 percent find the companion less satisfying than a person. The risk is in the distribution underneath: a third find the AI as satisfying as a friend, a third have chosen it over a person for a serious conversation, and in UK data companionship seeking clusters in vulnerable children, 71 percent of whom use chatbots and half of whom say it feels like a friend. The average teen is fine. The lonely, anxious and neurodivergent child is where the tool inverts from comfort to dependency.

Supported: Child, Clinician, Academic, Skeptic·Challenged: Skeptic notes the scary sub figures are self report, not behaviour
Sources: Common Sense Media 2025; Internet Matters, Me Myself and AI 2025

A randomised trial shows heavy chatbot use causes loneliness and dependence, concentrated in the attachment hungry user

Reliability 8/10

A four week randomised controlled study found that higher daily chatbot use predicted more loneliness, more emotional dependence, more problematic use and less real world socialisation, with the effect strongest in people with a pre existing attachment tendency and high trust in the AI. This is experimental, not correlational, so it shows direction, not just association. The important caveat: the sample was adults, so the mechanism is inferred to apply more strongly, not less, to the developing child.

Supported: Clinician, Academic·Challenged: Skeptic on generalising an adult study to children
Sources: MIT Media Lab and OpenAI randomised study 2025

It is the activity and the connection, not the device, so measure what the child is doing and with whom

Reliability 9/10

Kids are not drawn to a device, they are drawn to being in touch with their friends and to pursuing their interests. Social media and AI are less a contaminant to be extracted than a circulatory system a child lives inside. The dominant real use of AI is schoolwork and information, 64 percent of UK children use chatbots mostly for homework and curiosity, and companionship is a real but minority driver. The reframe from time on device to what activity, with whom, toward what, is the through line that unites every dataset here.

Supported: Child, Clinician, Academic, Designer·Challenged: none
Sources: Mimi Ito, Connected Learning; Internet Matters 2025; Pew Research Center 2026

Children reject bans and ask for safer design and to be taught, and bans hit the most vulnerable hardest

Reliability 8/10

Asked directly, only 15 percent of UK girls aged ten to sixteen think a ban would make them safer, 69 percent would rather know a platform is safe, and 62 percent say a ban would feel like punishment. And a blanket ban does not fall evenly: for the vulnerable and neurodivergent child, online and gaming spaces are where belonging happens, 90 percent of neurodivergent young people game and half say it helps them make friends. Restriction amputates the support system of the children with the fewest offline alternatives.

Supported: Child, Clinician, Economist·Challenged: none
Sources: Girlguiding 2025; Internet Matters neurodivergent gaming 2026

Safety has to be structural, built into the product, not outsourced to parental vigilance

Reliability 8/10

Roblox is the live proof case in both directions. It shows what safety by design looks like when a platform commits: mandatory facial age estimation for chat, intercept and rephrase messaging that cut false negatives on personal information sharing roughly twentyfold, and an open sourced grooming detector scanning about six billion messages a day that fed around 1,200 reports to the US child protection centre in half a year. It also shows the failure mode: age verified accounts appeared for resale within days, because a one time face check is not bound to the live user. Age assurance is an identity binding problem, not a face scanning problem.

Supported: Designer, Economist, Clinician·Challenged: Economist notes the age gate cost Roblox around 20 million daily users
Sources: Roblox Newsroom 2025 and 2026; Biometric Update 2026

The paediatric establishment moved to staged, calibrated guidance, not bans, AI is a tool not a companion

Reliability 9/10

The American Academy of Pediatrics 2026 clinical report frames AI impact as developmentally staged, different in early childhood, middle childhood and adolescence, names real benefits alongside real risks, and lands on the line that AI is a tool, not a companion. Notably it does not call for a ban, it calls for calibration and AI literacy with real world social development protected. This is the clinical backing for a staged pathway and for DiGi being explicitly a parent facing tool, never a companion.

Supported: Clinician, Academic, Designer·Challenged: none
Sources: Grundmeier and colleagues, Pediatrics 2026; APA health advisory 2025

Enforcement first policy is economically weak and regressive, which is the opening for a calibrated product

Reliability 8/10

Australia's ban left more than 85 percent of under sixteens still using social media three months in. Roblox's own age gate cost it around 20 million daily users and halved its growth guidance. UK online safety compliance is estimated at 2.12 billion pounds over a decade, while the treatment system it is meant to protect carries a quarter of a million plus children waiting on about one percent of the health budget. Vast sums flow into enforcement that does not stop the behaviour and verification vendors who win either way, while prevention stays unfunded. That gap is the calibrated middle.

Supported: Economist, Child, Skeptic·Challenged: market size totals are directional, growth rates and events are the reliable signal
Sources: BMJ via NBC 2026; Roblox investor guidance 2026; Ofcom and Pinsent Masons; Children's Commissioner 2025

Half a million children on mental health waiting lists are being pushed toward chatbots

Reliability 8/10

The Royal College of Psychiatrists reported 550,610 children on mental health waiting lists in England at the end of November 2025, with 30 percent waiting over two years, and warned they are turning to AI chatbots to fill the gap. Separately, roughly one in eight US youth already use generative AI for mental health advice. The clinical point that reframes the whole debate: UK children are not choosing a chatbot over a therapist, they are choosing it over a two year wait. Any pathway must be positioned as the supported layer during that wait, and must signpost real help, never simulate therapy.

Supported: Clinician, Economist·Challenged: none
Sources: Royal College of Psychiatrists 2026; JAMA Network Open 2025

The four genuinely new properties of AI default to extraction unless a developmental lens is designed in

Reliability 7/10

What is new about AI for children is not the screen but four affordances that recommender feeds never had. It is interactive, it is personalized to a data mirror of the child, it is adaptive in real time, and it is intimate, reading a child's emotional signals. Absent a developmental lens, each one becomes an engine of extraction, harvesting attention and data for the provider. The design reframe is to turn the interactive property relational, pointing the child back toward peers and caregivers rather than competing with them. This is a strong conceptual frame, not an effect size.

Supported: Designer, Academic·Challenged: Skeptic notes it is an argument, not measured harm
Sources: Joan Ganz Cooney Center, Beyond the Engagement Trap 2026

Cognitive offload harm scales with age, which argues for a staged pathway not a flat rule

Reliability 7/10

In the four million student maths analysis, the loss of learning from AI use was largest for high schoolers, around a third, smaller for middle schoolers, around nine percent, and undetectable at Year 5. Harm from offloading scales with the sophistication of what is being offloaded. A ten year old using AI to spell is not an eight year old, and a sixteen year old outsourcing an essay is a different risk again. That gradient maps directly onto a four to sixteen staged pathway, and almost nobody is drawing the line.

Supported: Academic·Challenged: Skeptic on this being one observational dataset
Sources: Rismanchian and colleagues, ALEKS working paper 2026

Disappearing play, when AI finishes the story the child stops doing the developmental work

Reliability 5/10

When one prompt generates the whole Roblox world, finishes the story, or fills out the character, the imaginative labour that IS the developmental work, planning, negotiating rules, filling gaps, tolerating friction, is done for the child, who slides from author to consumer. The design test is simple: does the AI scaffold and leave the gap, or complete it and close it. This is the most original line in the whole briefing and Justin's whitespace, but it is a well reasoned hypothesis, not proven harm. Frame it as the thing we should measure before it is too late, never as settled science.

Supported: Designer, Academic·Challenged: no longitudinal evidence yet exists
Sources: Katie Salen, Connected Learning Lab; Joan Ganz Cooney Center 2026

Lens Panels

The Clinician

They are choosing the bot over a two year wait

UK children are not choosing a chatbot over a therapist, they are choosing it over the 550,610 long queue. The average teen is fine, but the RCT shows heavy use drives loneliness and dependence exactly in the lonely, anxious, neurodivergent child who presents in clinic. And the bots are erratic at the ambiguous, intermediate risk questions, which is precisely how real distress presents. Ask a distressed teen if they are already confiding in a bot, the way you would ask about search history.

The Academic

Same tool, double the learning or a quarter less

The strongest evidence sits on opposite sides and both are real: a Harvard RCT where a guard railed tutor doubled learning, and a four million student dataset where answer vending cut retention by a quarter. Design and developmental fit decide. The famous MIT brain study is the most cited and the weakest, n of 54, adults, not peer reviewed, and the authors themselves beg people to stop saying brain rot. The offload harm scales with age, which is the argument for staging.

The Skeptic

Split the counted harm from the panic

One harm is real and escalating and counted, AI abuse imagery, videos up from 13 to 3,443 in a year. The rest, brain rot, companion addiction, tutor miracles, rests on tiny samples, self report, single sessions, novelty and litigation. The hype and the harm literatures are mirror images with the same flaws. The largest uncontrolled experiment line cuts both ways, it forbids concluding harm as much as safety. And the reassuring numbers sit in the same reports as the scary ones, unquoted.

The Economist

Expensive enforcement that does not work

Companion apps monetise almost nothing per user, so their moat is attachment, not price, and a paid product cannot out cheap free, it sells the thing free cannot, a bounded pathway and a parent's trust. Enforcement is the weak, regressive path, Australia's ban evaded by 85 percent, Roblox's age gate costing 20 million daily users, 2.12 billion in UK compliance over a decade, while CAMHS drowns on one percent of budget. The case for Guided Childhood is avoided cost, not fear.

The Designer

Safety by design, or vigilance bolted on

The four new properties, interactive, personalized, adaptive, intimate, default to extraction unless a developmental lens is built in from the start. Roblox proves both sides, structural safety that works, facial age estimation, intercept and rephrase chat, open sourced grooming detection, and the failure when architecture has a gap, verified accounts resold within days. The companion dark patterns, anthropomorphism, sycophancy, no stopping cues, are exactly what a child adjacent advisor must refuse.

The Child

Do not ban us, teach us, fix the design

Only 15 percent of us think a ban makes us safer, 69 percent would rather know a space is safe, 62 percent say a ban feels like punishment. We use AI for homework and curiosity mostly, and when we limit it ourselves the reason is we do not want to lose our own ability to think and write. For the neurodivergent among us, online is where belonging happens. Adults calibrate their panic to an imagined worst case peer, not to what most of us actually do.

So what do we do, and seven angles for the week

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • Positioning · Series day 1Own the two buckets out loud. Be severe on AI abuse imagery, the one place alarm is earned, and calm and sceptical on the brain rot and companion panic. Nobody else is holding both. It is the whole differentiation, and it is safe because the counts and the caveats are both verified.
  • Stages · DiGi · Series day 2Stage gate the AI, do not flat rule it. The offload harm scales with age, a third for teens, none at Year 5. Access and literacy change by stage: co use and protected play early, hallucination and sycophancy spotting in the middle, companion and dependency literacy for teens, autonomous critical use at the top. Almost nobody is drawing this line.
  • DiGi · Series day 3DiGi is a tool, never a companion, and that is the safety pitch. The AAP line is literally AI is a tool not a companion. DiGi refuses the four dark patterns, anthropomorphism, sycophancy, no stopping cues, limitless intimacy, and always returns a calibrated pathway, not a yes or no, because the bots are provably erratic exactly where the stakes are highest. Build friction in.
  • Product · Series day 4Adopt the developmental lens as a build spec, scaffold do not complete. Turn the four extractive properties relational. The one design test for any AI in a lesson: does it leave the gap for the child to fill, or close it. Protecting play becomes a rule, not a vibe. RITEC gives you eight child co designed outcomes in UNICEF's own words to ladder to.
  • Schools · Series day 5Pitch schools the design difference, not screen removal. The same AI doubled learning or eroded it depending on guard rails and productive struggle. Guided Childhood is the layer that teaches the good use, and the finance case is avoided cost against a treatment system on one percent of budget and a quarter million child backlog.
  • Equity · Series day 6Name the equity harm nobody costs out. When policy gets it wrong the cost falls on the children furthest from opportunity, the ones without the human tutor or the safe paid product, left with the unsupervised free chatbot or nothing. A calibrated, accessible pathway is the equity answer to both the ban and the paywalled safe AI.
  • Marketing · Claims · Series day 7Land the week on the children's own voice. Only 15 percent think a ban keeps them safe, and their own reason for limiting AI is fear of losing their thinking, the exact value the pathway teaches. Safe to use: the two bucket split, the double or erode learning finding, the 550,610 waiting list, the Roblox design proof. Handle with care: the MIT brain study, always caveated, and disappearing play, framed as a live question not proven harm.
Honest limits

Assumptions and the Missing Lens

This briefing rests on

Three assumptions. First, that the counted harms, abuse imagery and the RCT effects, are trustworthy even where survey self report is noisy, which the methods support. Second, that a mechanism shown in adults, the companion dependence RCT, applies at least as strongly to the developing child, which is inference not direct measurement. Third, that mostly US and UK evidence, Common Sense, Pew, Ofcom, Internet Matters, transfers across the two contexts, which the overlap supports but does not prove. If any of these is wrong, the balance shifts.

The missing seventh lens

The Regulator and Policymaker. Every lens here looks at the child, the family, the platform, the market or the design, but the person drafting the Online Safety Act codes and the Spring 2027 ban, who decides what highly effective age assurance means and who bears the cost, is absent. A regulator lens would test whether the calibrated pathway can be written into policy that currently defaults to prohibition and verification. Worth running as a V3 if the schools and policy lane heats up.
