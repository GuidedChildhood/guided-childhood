The gift here is not SMRI's post. It is that you can now teach five things parents are drowning in half truths about, each from the person who actually originated it, calibrated by age. That is the difference between an evidence institute and a LinkedIn account trademarking common phrases.

Key Findings

Do not cite SMRI. Build on the real originators instead.

Reliability 9/10

SMRI (smri.world) is a for profit personal brand, not a research institute: no peer reviewed output, no university affiliation, no disclosed board or funding, and a founder whose background is PR and marketing. The five concepts are self coined, several trademarked. The country and date list is unverifiable and the concept dates predate the institute's own 2020 founding. Critically, do not confuse it with the legitimate Texas Social Media Research Institute (Tarleton State University), which publishes a real journal. Mention SMRI only descriptively if at all, never as scientific backing.

Sources: D1, D2, D3

Harm reduction is real, and applied to screens it means reduce, not remove.

Reliability 9/10

Harm reduction is an established public health philosophy, defined by peak bodies, born in 1980s needle exchange (Amsterdam 1984, the Liverpool Mersey Model) and sitting inside the WHO Ottawa Charter (1986): meet people where they are, reduce harm not necessarily use, dignity over moralising. Applied to young people it is not a slogan but a trial result: capping social media to one hour a day for three weeks measurably improved body image in distressed teens (Thai 2023, RCT, n=220). The RCPCH already refuses a universal screen time limit and prescribes negotiated limits per child, which is harm reduction by another name. This is the public health backbone of your pathway.

Supported: Harm reduction lens
Sources: H1, H2, H3, H4, H5

"Digital first aid" is teachable as the exact first moves, honestly framed.

Reliability 8/10

Mental Health First Aid (Kitchener and Jorm, 2000, the ALGEE plan) and Psychological First Aid (WHO, Look Listen Link) are genuine programmes, and teen MHFA has strong school trials. The honest limit, from the Cochrane review, is that these courses lift knowledge and confidence more than downstream outcomes, so never promise safety, teach the moves. And the moves that hold up are operational UK fact, not contested trials: do not panic, do not delete (screenshot and preserve), do not pay, block, report, tell a trusted adult. Real tools back this: Report Remove (IWF and Childline) can take a child's nude image down in hours, Report Harmful Content and CEOP exist, and the NCA has a specific first hour script for sextortion.

Supported: First aid lens·Challenged: Cochrane rates MHFA outcome evidence very low; frame as skill, not cure
Sources: F1, F2, F3, F4, F5

There is no "Social Media Dependency Disorder". The accurate story is stronger.

Reliability 9/10

The only recognised behavioural technology diagnosis is Gaming Disorder (ICD-11, 2019). Social media addiction is in no manual; DSM-5 lists only Internet Gaming Disorder, and only for further study. The accepted term is problematic use, defined by loss of control and real life harm, not hours, and about 1 in 9 teens show it (WHO HBSC 2024, 11% up from 7%, girls 13% boys 9%). Leading scholars warn against overpathologising ordinary use (Billieux 2015). So the teachable line is more accurate than the fear content parents are drowning in: it is not a disease, it is a designed reward schedule (variable reward, Skinner and Schultz), and you can change the schedule.

Supported: Problematic use lens
Sources: P1, P2, P3, P4

The curated online self has a gold standard lineage. Teach that, not the coined term.

Reliability 8/10

"Triple Conscious Theory" is self coined and clashes with a real, separate Black feminist concept. Skip it. The genuine intuition (we run a public self, a private self, and a curated online self) is covered rigorously by four canonical sources, and they make a beautiful lesson arc. Du Bois (1903): the strain of seeing yourself through others' eyes. Goffman (1959): the profile is a front stage performance, everyone has a backstage the feed never shows. Suler (2004): the online disinhibition effect explains cruelty and oversharing. Festinger (1954) plus recent reviews: upward comparison against edited images predicts worse body image. Feel it, see the stagecraft, understand the shift, catch the comparison, act.

Supported: Online self lens
Sources: O1, O2, O3, O4

"Awareness" is media and algorithm literacy, and it has a design rule.

Reliability 8/10

Media literacy education measurably works: d = 0.37 across 51 interventions (Jeong 2012), with two moderators that should shape your curriculum directly, more sessions help and more components hurt. So teach short, repeated, focused lessons, never one big workshop. Inoculation (prebunking) is the strongest modern layer: short videos teaching manipulation techniques improved detection in a real YouTube field trial (Roozenbeek 2022). And the UK gives you ready scaffolding schools trust: Ofcom's statutory media literacy remit and the Education for a Connected World framework's eight strands.

Supported: Online self lens
Sources: O5, O6, O7

The honest guardrail: harm reduction stops at the alcohol line.

Reliability 8/10

This is the finding that keeps your pathway credible rather than permissive. Harm reduction assumes a behaviour is happening and hard to stop. Where a behaviour is still optional and deferrable, delay beats harm reduction. The proof is the alcohol counter case: parents who supplied alcohol early, the classic "let them do it safely at home", made things worse, more binge drinking and more harm, not less (Mattick, Lancet Public Health 2018, six year cohort). So the pathway is prevention and delay early, negotiated reduction and skills later. Never "hand the young child the phone so they learn to cope". Different tools for different ages.

Sources: H6, H7

So what do we do

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • PositionDo not cite or associate with SMRI. Take the five ideas, teach each from its real originator. That is exactly the standard that separates your evidence institute from a LinkedIn brand, and it is a quiet competitive moat: anyone can retweet a trademark, almost nobody sources it properly.
  • CurriculumMap the four real frameworks onto the five stages. Stage 1 (4 to 7): delay and co-use, name feelings, one first aid rule ("tell me, you won't be in trouble"). Stage 2 (7 to 10): the 4Cs, negotiated limits begin. Stage 3 (10 to 13): problematic use self check, the online self arc (Du Bois to Festinger), full first aid moves. Stage 4 (13 to 16): harm reduction proper, a personal safety plan, media and algorithm literacy. Each stage teaches the same ideas at rising sophistication.
  • ScriptsBuild a Digital First Aid card and a Reduce Not Remove card. The first aid one is a memorable action plan (screenshot, don't pay, block, report, tell) in the DiGi Squad style, genuinely useful the day a family needs it. The reduction one gives the one hour target and the negotiation, not confiscation, framing. Both are database scripts, both stage calibrated.
  • DiGiBake the accuracy in. DiGi never says "you're addicted" (a label) or "it's fine" (an allow), it names the reward schedule and runs the self check. It never repeats "dependency disorder". It teaches the first hour moves and the real tools (Report Remove, CEOP). This is non negotiable rule one, in evidence form.
  • PostsThree posts almost write themselves, all myth busting generously. One: there is no such thing as Social Media Dependency Disorder, here is what is real. Two: the first hour that matters, the professional response to a shared image. Three: the trial no parent has heard of, one hour a day beat unrestricted. Each corrects a half truth without sneering, which is your whole brand.

This briefing rests on
