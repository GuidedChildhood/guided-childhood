Should you turn your best LinkedIn posts into animated videos?

You asked whether animating each of your high performing LinkedIn posts is a good idea. Six lenses researched it independently. The short answer: not as animations, and not as replacements. Here is the evidence.

60 Second Summary

Animating your top posts would trade your strongest asset for the weakest video format. Every independent 2025 and 2026 dataset agrees that posts showing a real face earn far more reach and engagement on LinkedIn, while faceless produced video is the one style that consistently underperforms. LinkedIn itself declared war on AI looking content in late 2025, and 83% of consumers now say they can spot AI video, with a third saying it lowers their trust in the brand. For a founder selling trust about other people's children, that is a bad trade.

The deeper problem is that video is not even LinkedIn's best format. Document and carousel posts beat video on engagement and, crucially, on the clicks that feed your starter pack page. The platform's video boom of 2024 has already corrected: independent trackers measured video views down 36% year on year, and your audience watches with the sound off for roughly 15 seconds. Peer reviewed studies add that animation shows no communication advantage over a person talking, and video adds surprisingly little persuasion over text.

The move the evidence supports: keep your winning posts as text, repurpose the best ones into PDF carousels, and add a parallel track of short captioned videos of your own face, filmed on your phone. Save the animation budget for DiGi and the product, where characters belong.

Key Findings

Your face is the asset. Animation deletes it.

Reliability 9/10

Posts showing real people earn up to 50% more engagement, and photos with the author in them gain up to 60% more reach. A study of 1.1 million images found faces attract 38% more likes and 32% more comments. Parents researching child related services literally use the visible face as their trust test, and paid benchmarks show ads fronted by a real person cost roughly 77% less per click than polished creative. Animating your posts removes the one variable with the strongest documented effect.

Supported: all six lenses
Sources: S2, S6, S14, S15, S20

Video is not LinkedIn's top format. Documents and carousels are.

Reliability 9/10

Across the largest independent datasets, document and multi image posts beat video on engagement (6.1 to 6.6% vs 5.6% by impressions; Buffer's 52 million post study puts carousels at roughly 3x video's median engagement). Video earns impressions but few clicks, and clicks are what move people to /starter-pack. If you want a visual upgrade of your best text posts, the durable path is a PDF carousel of 8 to 10 slides, not a video.

Supported: Skeptic, Historian, Academic, Economist, Practitioner
Sources: S1, S2, S3

The LinkedIn video boom already corrected. You would be arriving late.

Reliability 8/10

LinkedIn pushed video hard through 2024 and early 2025, then quietly cut it: independent trackers measured video views down 36% year on year across every page size, named creators fell from tens of thousands of views to a few thousand, and the dedicated video feed was pulled in several regions. Every LinkedIn format wave since 2017 has followed this arc, and the promotional subsidy for video has already been withdrawn. Starting an animation pipeline in mid 2026 is a late adopter bet on a corrected format.

Supported: Historian, Skeptic, Practitioner, Audience · Challenged: LinkedIn's own published figures
Sources: S1, S9, S10, S11

Your reader watches 15 silent seconds on a phone.

Reliability 8/10

Roughly 80% of LinkedIn video plays with the sound off, and creators report average watch times around 15 seconds. Completion falls below 20% for videos over a minute. A 700 word post that worked because people read the whole argument will not survive conversion into a 90 second animation that most people abandon at the hook. A LinkedIn video view is also counted at just 2 seconds on autoplay, so view counts on any remake would flatter the experiment.

Supported: Audience, Skeptic
Sources: S10, S16, S21

Peer reviewed evidence gives animation no edge over a person talking.

Reliability 8/10

The only recent head to head experiment found animated and talking head videos performed the same on knowledge transfer and evaluation, and a pair of large randomized experiments found video's persuasive advantage over text is minimal for attitudes and behaviour. Animation genuinely beats static content for explaining complex processes, which matters for your product, but it does not beat your face on a feed. One important nuance: voice carries the humanizing signal, so anything animated should at least be narrated by you.

Supported: Academic, Skeptic, Audience · Challenged: patient education meta analysis shows animation beats static, d = 0.35
Sources: S4, S5, S7, S8

In 2026, polished animation reads as AI slop.

Reliability 7/10

LinkedIn announced suppression of generic AI generated content and attention bait video in late 2025. Survey data says 83% of consumers believe they can spot AI video and 36% say it lowers their trust in the brand. The meaning of animation changed underneath the format: what read as premium explainer content in 2018 now pattern matches to machine made filler. On a children's wellbeing topic, one video that reads as AI made can tax the credibility of everything around it.

Supported: Practitioner, Historian, Audience · Challenged: Economist notes warm character animation is category congruent for a kids brand
Sources: S12, S13

The cash cost collapsed. Your hours did not.

Reliability 7/10

UK agency animation runs £2,000 to £5,000 per 60 second video on 6 to 8 week timelines, which cannot clear break even at your £7.99 to £12.99 price point. AI tools collapse that to roughly £20 a month, where one retained subscriber covers a video. But the real cost is founder hours: roughly 3 to 8 hours per video against 30 minutes for the text post it copies, and the ban news cycle rewards a fast plain reaction post over anything with a production pipeline.

Supported: Economist · Challenged: Audience flags the trust externality no cost model captures
Sources: S17, S18

Remaking old posts may or may not dodge the repeat penalty.

Reliability 4/10

One practitioner source claims a format change resets LinkedIn's duplicate penalty and cites a reworked post going from 243 to 36,000 impressions. Another claims a remake cannibalizes up to half the original's views. Both are weakly sourced blog claims and they point in opposite directions. Treat this as unknown, and remember your best posts are selected outliers: remakes face regression to the mean regardless.

Supported: Practitioner · Challenged: Skeptic
Sources: S19, S22

Six perspectives

Lens Panels

The Practitioner

Keep the text, add your face

Every dataset I trust converges: founder face video outperforms, faceless animation underperforms, and format changed repurposing is cheap. Run face video as a parallel track. Do not replace winning text.

The Academic

The animation advantage is a myth with one exception

Controlled experiments show video barely out persuades text and animation does not beat a talking head. The exception: animation beats static content for explaining complex processes. And voice, not face, carries the humanizing signal, so narrate anything yourself.

The Skeptic

You would be optimizing an inflated metric

A view is 2 autoplay seconds. Video has the lowest click through of any format. Only about 3% of LinkedIn engagement comes from people who could actually buy. Your best posts are outliers that will regress. The certain cost buys an unmeasured benefit.

The Economist

The decision turns on your hours, not cash

Agency animation can never pay back at your price point. AI animation breaks even at one subscriber, so the cash argument is dead. What is scarce is your time: 3 to 8 hours per video, and video is weakest at the link clicks your funnel needs.

The Historian

Every format wave pays early adopters and taxes late ones

Native video 2017, carousels 2022, the video feed 2024: the subsidy lasts 12 to 24 months, then the correction. Video corrected in 2025. The quiet survivor of every wave is the PDF document post, and owned distribution outlives every feed format.

The Audience

I stop for a person, silently, for 15 seconds

I watch sound off on a train. I trust a named face talking plainly about my kid's world. In 2026 slick animation makes me wonder if a machine made it, and I am already drowning in content I do not trust. Show me the person who did the homework.

So what do we do

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • Marketing Do not animate your top posts. Keep them as text and repurpose the best into PDF carousels. Documents are the top format for engagement and clicks, and an 8 to 10 slide carousel of a winning post (scripts, checklists, stage guides) is a two hour job with the strongest measured upside.
  • Marketing Add a face track: short captioned phone videos of you, under 60 seconds. Face content carries the trust premium with parents and heads, costs nothing, and is the one video style the platform still rewards. Caption everything, because 80% of viewers are on mute.
  • DiGi and brand Spend the animation energy on the product, not the feed. Teo, Olga, Alma and DiGi Junior belong in lessons and onboarding where animation demonstrably beats static explanation. On LinkedIn, use characters only as brief inserts inside a video fronted and voiced by you.
  • Ban readiness Protect your speed through the ban news cycle. Regulations land end of 2026 and the ban goes live Spring 2027. Each milestone is a same day text post opportunity. A production pipeline between you and publishing is a strategic cost, not just a financial one.
  • Founder time If you test animation anyway, time box it. AI tools at roughly £20 a month, two posts, narrated in your own voice, measured on profile visits and starter pack clicks rather than views. Kill it if it does not beat your text baseline in four weeks.
Honest limits

Assumptions and the Missing Lens

This briefing rests on

Most reach and engagement numbers come from large industry datasets (Socialinsider, van der Blom, Buffer, AuthoredUp) that are directionally consistent but not peer reviewed, and several key benchmarks are B2B figures extrapolated to a parent audience. It also assumes your goal is conversions to /starter-pack and school pilots rather than raw reach, and that your posting engine is your personal profile, not a company page.

The missing seventh lens

Your own analytics. Nobody in this briefing looked at your actual LinkedIn data: who follows you, which of your high performers earned clicks rather than likes, and how many engagers are parents or school leaders. Before any format experiment, that audit is the highest value hour available. Say the word and a V3 can include it.
