Guided Childhood Research Briefing

"Not fully developed": what the neuroscience of childhood actually says

A widely shared post asked how developmental language can support children's rights in one breath and erase them in the next. Five lenses researched the real science, the law, and what children themselves say. The answer is the strongest case yet for the pathway.

60 Second Summary

The phrase "not fully developed until 25" is not a scientific finding. It is an artefact of where old brain scanning studies stopped recruiting. There is no cliff at 25; a 2025 Cambridge study of over 4,000 scans puts the whole developmental era at roughly 9 to 32, and different circuits mature at different ages by different measures. The one load bearing, replicated fact is subtler and far more useful: calm, deliberative reasoning reaches adult levels around 16, while judgment under peer pressure and strong emotion keeps maturing into the twenties. Cold cognition matures early, hot cognition matures late.

That single distinction resolves everything the shared post was wrestling with, because it prescribes exactly one policy: supervised practice in low stakes conditions, rising with age. Not waiting for a birthday, and not throwing children in unsupervised. The law already runs on this logic. Article 5 of the UN convention grades rights by evolving capacities, and UK law has graded capacity by understanding rather than age since Gillick in 1985. Every serious domain already runs a capability ladder: graduated driving licences cut crashes about 22 percent in the riskiest age group, swimming lessons cut drowning, road and first aid skills are taught in staged steps. Digital life is the one domain where we still argue birthday versus free for all.

And the children are ahead of us. Two thirds of UK young people back restricting some platforms, Australian teens opposed the blanket ban and then circumvented it at 61 percent within months, and the young people adults call too undeveloped to consult hold a more calibrated position than most adults: fix the design, teach the skills, give us a say. The developmental science, the law and the children's own voices converge on the same architecture. It is a pathway.

Key Findings

"Not developed until 25" was never a finding. It was the edge of the data.

Reliability 9/10

No study ever identified 25 as an endpoint. The number crystallised because early MRI cohorts in the 1990s and 2000s recruited few people past their early twenties, so 25 became the visible edge of the chart and got reified as a cliff. Somerville (Neuron, 2016) argues there is no single signature of maturity to find: circuits mature at different ages by different measures. A 2025 Cambridge study of more than 4,000 scans mapped the developmental era at roughly 9 to 32, with the biggest structural shift near 32, not 25. Development is gradual, continuous and individually variable. Anyone who says "the brain is not finished until 25" is quoting an artefact.

Supported: Neuroscientist, Skeptic
Sources: N1, N2, N3

The one load bearing fact: cold cognition matures early, hot cognition matures late.

Reliability 9/10

The maturity gap study (Icenogle and Steinberg, 2019, 5,227 people across 11 countries) is the anchor: cognitive capacity, calm deliberative reasoning, reaches adult levels around 16 and plateaus, while psychosocial maturity, self restraint under emotional and social arousal, keeps developing past 18. A 16 year old can reason like an adult in a calm room and still act impulsively under peer pressure. This resolves the exact tension the shared post named, and it is the same distinction that reconciled the APA's notorious inconsistency (telling the US Supreme Court that 16 year olds were mature enough for one decision and too immature for another). It does not support a single age threshold in either direction.

Supported: Neuroscientist, Skeptic
Sources: N4, N5, N6

Adolescence is an opportunity window, not a deficit.

Reliability 8/10

Crone and Dahl (Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 2012) reframe adolescence from around age 10 as a sensitive period of heightened plasticity for social, emotional and motivational learning, a drive to matter and contribute. Blakemore's work casts the adolescent brain as adapted, not broken: the changes serve identity, social learning and independence. Plasticity is learning capacity. The developmental fact is not that teenagers are faulty adults to be waited out; it is that they are primed to learn precisely the social and digital skills the world is about to demand of them.

Supported: Neuroscientist
Sources: N7, N8

Capacities grow through supervised practice, not by waiting for a date.

Reliability 8/10

Greenough's experience dependent plasticity (1987, National Academies endorsed) is the mechanism: individual skills develop only through actual encounters with the specific environment. The brain builds capability for what the child does. A child kept out of an environment until 16 arrives without the neural learning that supervised doing would have laid down. Pair this with the maturity gap and the prescription is unambiguous: graduated practice in low stakes conditions, scaffolded and rising with age. This is the scientific core of the pathway, and it comes with an honest limit: scaffolding must match genuine readiness, so "give them everything now" is as wrong as "wait until 25".

Supported: Practitioner, Neuroscientist
Sources: N9, P1, P12

The law already grades capability by understanding, not birthday.

Reliability 9/10

The shared post was right that the UN convention holds both ideas at once, and the mechanism is precise. Article 5 requires adults to respect the child's "evolving capacities"; Article 12 gives weight to the child's views in accordance with age and maturity, grading by developing capacity rather than a fixed line. Lansdown's foundational work names three functions: developmental, participative and protective, with protection receding as competence grows. And UK law has run on this since Gillick in 1985: a child under 16 can consent when they have "sufficient understanding and intelligence", assessed decision by decision. The UN's own 2021 digital rights guidance, co produced with 709 children in 27 countries, applies evolving capacities directly to online life.

Supported: Rights Scholar
Sources: R1, R2, R3, R5

The system's own incoherence proves the point.

Reliability 8/10

England and Wales hold a child criminally responsible at 10, able to be arrested, tried and sentenced, years before the same system lets them open a bank account, and on developmental grounds the UN Committee calls a minimum age below 12 internationally unacceptable. Meanwhile developmental science was used to end the juvenile death penalty in the United States (Roper, 2005), where immaturity protected children. The same "developing brain" argument limits rights in one room and defends them in the next. That is exactly the erasure the shared post described, and it is why a single birthday switch, in any direction, cannot be the answer.

Supported: Rights Scholar·Challenged: protection is also a right, so a limit is not automatically an erasure
Sources: R6, R7

Every other risky domain already runs a capability ladder.

Reliability 8/10

Graduated driver licensing (supervised, then restricted, then full) cuts first year crashes for 16 year olds by roughly 22 percent in pooled evidence, the best evidenced child safety model anywhere, and it works best where risk is highest. Swimming lessons are associated with far lower drowning risk, framed always as layers of protection, never lessons instead of fences. Road skills (Kerbcraft) and first aid are taught in staged, real world steps. Enabling parental mediation, active support and gradual autonomy, builds digital skills where restrictive mediation shrinks them. Societies already run explicit age ladders in every serious domain except digital life.

Supported: Practitioner·Challenged: alcohol is the counter case; graduated exposure increased harm, so this works for skills, not consumption
Sources: P2, P3, P4, P9

Children already demonstrate the capability the phrase denies.

Reliability 7/10

Around 120,000 young carers aged 5 to 17 in England carry adult grade responsibility largely unseen. Children rate themselves ready for serious medical decisions around age 14, and their parents independently agree. Under the right conditions even young children give reliable testimony. And online, the competence is visible: teenagers build detailed folk theories of recommendation algorithms and organise to alter them, and the youth movements adults celebrate run on the very platforms adults want to remove. The blanket phrase "not fully developed" erases all of this present tense capability.

Supported: Practitioner, Child
Sources: P6, P8, C11

The children are more calibrated than the adults arguing about them.

Reliability 8/10

Two thirds of UK young people backed restricting under 16s from at least some platforms in the 2026 consultation. In Australia, most surveyed teens opposed the blanket ban, and then 61 percent of those who had accounts kept access within months of it, which is itself the digital competence the deficit framing denies. The consistent youth ask across consultations, including the 709 children who shaped the UN guidance, is not absolute access or absolute protection. It is fix the design, teach us the skills, and give us a say. In the Lundy model's terms, they get space and voice but rarely audience and influence.

Five perspectives

Lens Panels

The Neuroscientist

There is no cliff at 25

The number is an artefact of old samples. Calm reasoning matures around 16, hot judgment into the twenties, and the brain builds capability for what the child actually does. Waiting is not neutral; it withholds the practice that develops the skill.

The Rights Scholar

The law already solved this in 1985

Evolving capacities and Gillick grade rights by understanding, not birthday. The UN's digital guidance, written with 709 children, says the same. A blanket line is the crude instrument the law abandoned decades ago.

The Skeptic

Do not overclaim in either direction

Never say "not developed until 25". Never say "teens are just as capable as adults". Both are false. The honest, unglamorous sentence is the whole game: adult reasoning when calm, still maturing when aroused, so scaffold the practice.

The Practitioner

We already do this everywhere else

Driving licences, swimming lessons, first aid, medical consent, all staged ladders with hard safety numbers. Digital life is the exception. And name the limit: this works because using devices is a skill, like roads, not a toxin, like alcohol.

The Child

We are more measured than you are

We back restricting some platforms and we resent being banned without a say. We route around blunt bans in weeks, which rather proves the point. Fix the design, teach us the skills, and let us in the room. That is not too much to ask.

So what do we do

What This Means for Guided Childhood

  • The thesisThe science, the law and the children all point at one architecture: the pathway. This is the strongest evidence base you have. Cold cognition matures early, hot cognition late, capability grows through practice, the law grades by understanding, and every other risky domain runs a ladder. The pathway is not a product idea dressed as science. It is what the science actually prescribes.
  • The postLead with the myth, land on the ladder. Cold open on "not developed until 25" being an artefact, walk the cold and hot distinction, then the driving licence parallel, then the incoherence (criminal at 10, no bank account), close on the children being more calibrated than the adults. Series fit: this is its own strand, the capability case, sitting alongside The Wrong Villain.
  • CurriculumBuild the stages on the maturity gap explicitly. Low stakes supervised practice young, rising autonomy as hot cognition matures, with the highest support around the documented sensitivity windows (girls 11 to 13, boys 14 to 15). The five stages already exist; this gives them a peer reviewed spine you can show a school or a journalist.
  • DiGi and voiceRetire "not fully developed" from all copy. It is scientifically wrong and it erases the child. Replace with the evolving capacities frame: capable now, growing still. DiGi should model this, never dismissing a child on developmental grounds, always calibrating to demonstrated understanding, which is also non negotiable rule one.
  • Schools and rightsOwn the children's rights framing, carefully. The UN's 2026 guidance (safety by design, consult children, evolving capacities) is your position almost verbatim. But hold the honest line the briefing insists on: protection is also a right, so this is not an anti safeguarding argument. It is capability and protection together, which is exactly what the convention requires.
Honest limits

Assumptions and the Missing Lens

This briefing rests on

The maturity gap and dual systems models are the leading frameworks but have genuine critics, so present them as strong, not settled. Youth opinion figures come from self selected consultations and small polls where question wording drives the answer, so no single youth quote stands for a distribution. The graduated ladder analogy holds for skill domains and breaks for consumption domains, which the alcohol counter case proves. And the empowerment reading of developmental science rests on the same evidence restriction advocates cite, so the double edge cuts every position, including this one.

The missing seventh lens

The special educational needs and neurodivergent child. Evolving capacities and the maturity gap were mostly built on typical development, yet the children most affected by both blunt bans and blunt developmental labels are often those whose capabilities are most uneven. A pathway that grades by demonstrated understanding rather than age is potentially far fairer to them, but nobody in this briefing sat in that seat. It is the V2 worth running before the curriculum claims to serve every child.
