# The Starter Story playbook — the "Early" growth model applied to Guided Childhood

**Date:** 2026-07-11
**Source:** the Starter Story episode with Jake, founder of Early (the push up alarm app that went 0 to $50k a month in 4 months, 200k downloads in a year), plus the Sam Parr "how I work" segment Justin uploaded.
**Target it serves:** the same £10k MRR maths from plans/mrr-10k-review.md. 800 paying families needs roughly 2,500 free signups a month at 4 percent conversion. This plan is a third acquisition engine that sits next to the Glam Up flywheel (plans/guided-digital-pathway-growth-plan.md) and the LinkedIn and SEO and schools engines in the MRR review.
**Lane:** marketing strategy. No product code, no migration number. Draft PR claims the lane so no other session writes the same plan.

---

## 1. The one lesson, said plainly

Jake built the app in nineteen days and then spent every day after that on one thing: getting views. His words, twice over, were that building barely matters now because anyone can build, and the only skill that decides whether an app lives or dies is marketing, specifically video that performs. "Focus on top of funnel only." Sam Parr said the same thing in a different accent: quantity over perfection, ceaseless action, one or two time bound tasks a day, and a system or nothing changes.

We are the opposite shape right now. We have built a very deep product across ten weeks. The honest read from plans/mrr-10k-review.md is that distribution is one channel deep and none of it is worth anything until parents arrive. This plan is the counterweight. It treats getting views as a job with a system, not a hope.

Read this next to the Glam Up plan, not instead of it. Glam Up taught us the in product flywheel (the stage card artifact, the pay or invite fork, the trained creator matrix). Early teaches us something Glam Up did not: how to buy views with almost no risk, how to place the product inside content people already want to watch, and how to pick a hook that travels on its own. Those are the parts to steal.

## 2. The seven Early principles, and what each becomes for us

Same honest structure as the Glam Up plan. Transfers, mutates, or gets rejected.

**1. Solve a painful problem in a simple way. TRANSFERS.**
Jake's problem was "I cannot wake up early." Ours is "what age for what phone, is my child behind, and the ban is coming and I have no plan." It is painful (a parent thinks it more than once a day), and when you describe it, everyone says either I need this or my sister needs this. We already have the simple line in plans/marketing-message.md: ten minutes a day turns the cliff edge at 16 into a gentle ramp. Keep leading with that. Do not add features to say in content. Say the one thing.

**2. If it is not inherently viral, add a novel hook that is. MUTATES.**
"Push ups to turn off your alarm" is the whole reason Early travelled. It is a five word idea you can show in three seconds. We need our own. We do not have to invent a gimmick, because we already own one image no competitor owns: the cliff versus the ramp. The novel, showable hook is **the stage reveal**. A parent answers three questions and gets told, on a card, "your child is Stage 2, the Builder, 4 of 9 foundations in place." That is our push up moment: personal, arguable, done in fifteen seconds on camera. See section 4.

**3. Ride a timing window. TRANSFERS, and this is the big one.**
Jake got lucky that iOS 26 shipped Alarm Kit, which made a reliable alarm app possible for the first time, and a wave of alarm apps rode it. Our Alarm Kit is the UK under 16 social media ban, confirmed 15 June 2026, live Spring 2027 (plans/decisions.md). Every parent in the country is about to be told their child cannot have Instagram, and almost none of them have a plan for the years before or the day it lifts. That is a once in a decade wave of parent anxiety and search intent, with a deadline attached. Early moved fast because the window was short. Ours is open now and closing at Spring 2027. Everything in this plan assumes we are racing that clock.

**4. Native placement inside content people already watch. TRANSFERS, with a hard brand rule.**
Jake's best video was a day in the life of a college student where the alarm and the push ups just appeared, no ad energy, and the top comment was "what's the app name." He picked creators already making that content, so the product fit their existing videos and they got paid to keep making what they make. Win, win, win: creator keeps their page, we get downloads, viewer gets a solution not an ad.

Our version: parenting creators (the "day in the life of a mum" and dad accounts on Instagram and TikTok) whose content already includes the phone argument, the bedtime handover, the "when do I give her a phone" question. The product fits their real day. Our hard rule, non negotiable and different from Early: **the camera stays on the parent and the parent's phone, never on the child's face.** We are the brand that protects children online. We cannot grow by putting other people's kids on camera. The native beat is the parent opening Guided Childhood, doing the ten minute path, getting the stage card, asking DiGi the eleven at night question. That is showable and honest and on brand.

**5. View guarantees, low risk, power law. TRANSFERS directly.**
Jake's deal structure is the most useful concrete thing in the episode and we should copy it almost exactly. Two to four videos a month per creator, priced against a **guaranteed view count**, roughly £2 to £3 per thousand views. You cap what you pay, so the downside is fixed and known before anything goes live. Most videos do fine, and the money is made on the occasional one that hits a million plus. Treat it as a black box: put in £X, watch what comes out, keep the ones that return. See section 5 for the numbers.

**6. Attribution by spikes, not by perfect tracking. TRANSFERS.**
Jake stopped trying to attribute perfectly. He tracks views and conversions over time and when a video causes a spike in both, he credits that video. You lose money on some creators and never know exactly which, and that is fine. We do the same: a simple sheet, every video logged with date and guaranteed views and cost, and our daily signup and quiz completion numbers next to it. A video lands, we watch the next 48 hours, we keep or cut. Do not build attribution infrastructure. Build the sheet.

**7. Ship fast, validate the channel before scaling. TRANSFERS.**
Jake's first influencer video was live seven days after he decided to try it, and he knew the idea was validated the moment he saw purchases from it. We do not need a big creator programme to start. We need one creator, one video, one week, to learn whether a parenting audience converts on the stage card hook at all. Everything scales after that signal, nothing before it.

## 3. What we reject (write these on the wall)

- **No children's faces in paid content.** Ever. Non negotiable and above growth. See principle 4.
- **No fear first.** Early sold a fun push up. We sell readiness and calm, not panic (plans/marketing-message.md). A creator video that scares parents about their kids is off brand even if it converts.
- **No fake anything.** No staged reviews, no invented parent quotes, no pretend results. The stage assignment is real. Real parents, with permission, only. Same rule as the Glam Up plan.
- **No "rate to unlock" gate.** Apple rejects it and it is a dark pattern.
- **No dashes in any copy we hand a creator.** Justin's voice, warm, plain, direct. This is a house rule and it applies to the caption briefs too.

## 4. Our push up: the hook we put in every video

One hook, tested to death before we add a second. The candidate, and the reason it is the strongest:

**"What stage is your child? Answer three questions."**

Why it is our Early hook:
- It is showable in fifteen seconds. Parent taps, answers three, card flips to "Stage 2, the Builder."
- It is personal and arguable, which is what makes a comment section move. Parents will argue their own result, tag their partner, tag their sister.
- It routes to something we already built and that already works with no login (the starter pack quiz, non negotiable 9, every CTA on /join goes to /starter-pack).
- The result is a shareable artifact (the stage card from the Glam Up plan), so the video's viewers become the video's next distributors.

The two runner up hooks, held in reserve to test against it:
- **"The cliff edge at 16."** The image of nothing, nothing, nothing then everything at once. Strong but more abstract, harder to show in three seconds.
- **"The ten minute a day path."** True and calm, but a routine is less inherently curious than a personal result. Better as the payoff than the hook.

Decision: lead with the stage reveal, hold the other two as variants for the testing matrix (section 6). Do not run all three at launch. Pick one, prove it, then vary it.

## 5. The creator engine, with the actual numbers

**Who.** UK and Ireland parenting creators, 10k to 250k followers, whose existing content already includes real family life and the phone question. Micro over mega. A 30k follower mum account whose audience trusts her converts better than a celebrity. Same instinct Jake had: fit the content they already make.

**The pitch to them (win, win, win).** You keep making your day in the life content. In one of this month's videos, you open Guided Childhood, do the three question stage check, show your result, and ask DiGi one real question you actually have. You get paid whether or not it goes big. Your audience gets a genuinely useful tool for a problem they all have. We get the downloads. You never put your child's face on our behalf, the camera is on you and your phone.

**The deal.** Copy Early. Two to four videos a month, priced on a guaranteed view floor, around £2 to £3 per thousand guaranteed views. Cap the spend per creator per month so the downside is fixed.

**The maths, worked for our target.** At £2.50 per thousand views, a 100k guaranteed view video costs about £250. Parenting content link through to a no login quiz runs conservatively at 0.5 to 1 percent, so 100k views is 500 to 1,000 quiz starts. That is our black box input. If even a third finish and enter the funnel, one modest video feeds a meaningful slice of the 2,500 signups a month the £10k target needs, and the occasional power law video (a million plus views for the same fixed cost) does it on its own. Start with a test budget of £750 to £1,000 across two or three creators, one video each, and read the spikes before spending a pound more.

**The comment mechanic.** Jake's conversion happened in the comments: someone asks "what's the app name," the creator pins "Early, on the app store." Ours is the same and we script it. The creator's pinned first comment reads, in Justin's voice, no dashes: **"It's Guided Childhood. Three questions, no sign up, tells you your child's stage. Link in my bio."** Every brief ships with that pinned comment written for them.

**Tooling.** Jake used Koroi for creator tracking and payments and Sideshift for UGC sourcing. We do not need to buy those on day one. A Google Sheet and manual outreach is the day one system (Sam Parr: a system or nothing changes). Add tooling only once we are running more creators than a sheet can hold.

## 6. The system: how we actually run this (Sam Parr's half of the tape)

Sam Parr's segment is not filler. It is the operating manual for the person who has to do section 5 every day.

- **One or two time bound tasks a day, written down, put on the calendar.** For the next 30 days the daily task is "move the creator engine forward by one step": send five outreach DMs, or write one brief, or log yesterday's spike. Small, specific, time bound. Nothing else on the list is allowed to eat the day.
- **Quantity over perfection.** The pottery study he cites is the whole point. Ship more briefs and more creator tests than feels comfortable and let the winners emerge, rather than perfecting one video. Early's returns were power law, which only works if you take enough swings.
- **One lane, one session.** Per CLAUDE.md multi session rules, this marketing lane runs in one session at a time. This plan is that lane's claim. Do not also run "continue the build" in parallel against the same acquisition work.
- **The black box mindset.** Put money in, watch views and signups, keep what returns, cut what does not, and accept you will never know exactly which creator lost you money. Do not build dashboards to avoid that discomfort. Build the sheet and move.

## 7. The 30 and 90 day plan

**Days 1 to 7 (validate the channel).**
- Cut three vertical demo clips of the stage reveal on a real phone, camera on the parent, fifteen to twenty seconds each, one per hook variant from section 4.
- Build the one page creator sheet: creator, followers, guaranteed views, cost, video date, then daily signups and quiz starts alongside.
- DM twenty parenting creators with the win win win pitch. Book two or three for one video each inside a £750 to £1,000 test cap.
- Write each brief with the pinned comment and the no children on camera rule baked in.

**Days 8 to 30 (find the signal).**
- First creator videos go live. Watch the 48 hour spike after each. Log everything.
- Keep the hook that spikes, kill the two that do not. Do not add a fourth creator until one video has clearly moved the signup line.
- If nothing spikes, the problem is the hook or the offer, not the budget. Change the hook before adding spend. This is exactly Early's "took a whole day to walk and think about the direction" moment.

**Days 31 to 90 (scale what returned).**
- Move winning creators to a two to four video a month retainer on view guarantees.
- Break the winning video into the variable testing matrix (hook text, first frame, caption, the exact three questions shown, posting time), the same way the Glam Up plan describes. Optimise one variable at a time.
- Layer the creator engine on top of the ban timing window: every video written so it lands harder as Spring 2027 approaches and parents feel the deadline.
- Feed the whole thing back into the flywheel: every creator viewer who takes the quiz gets a shareable stage card with their referral link (Glam Up plan section 3), so bought views manufacture organic ones.
