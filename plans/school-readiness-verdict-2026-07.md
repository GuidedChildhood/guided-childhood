# Is the schools product good enough to teach from? Deep research verdict
**7 Jul 2026 · 104 verified research agents, every claim adversarially checked. Full trail in the session transcript.**

## Verdict

Verdict: the product as described is unusually well aimed at the formal UK adoption bar — its statutory mapping, sourced statistics, data minimisation, paper fallback and DSL notes line up almost item-for-item with the DfE's published resource-selection criteria and the renewed (Nov 2025) Ofsted framework's expectations — but no real school could teach it tomorrow, because adoption of RSHE-scope content runs through a mandatory process layer the product must feed: parental transparency (parents must be able to view all materials, and licence terms restricting that are void), policy consultation with sample resources, formal vetting of materials for legal accuracy and inclusivity, and data-protection documentation (an age-differentiated DPIA for a 4-18 service). The hard blockers are therefore: (1) a parent-view/sample-materials mechanism plus licence terms that explicitly permit parental sharing; (2) demonstrable mapping to the July 2025 RSHE guidance (compulsory 1 Sept 2026) including its newly named topics — pornography harms, incel/misogynistic cultures, deepfakes, online gambling, illegal online behaviours; (3) vendor-side DPIA and data-protection pack a school DPO can sign off. The main adoption-friction risks are script rigidity (evidence shows two-thirds of teachers adapt rather than adopt wholesale, and adapting can cost nearly as much as writing from scratch), SEND/differentiation evidence for the new Ofsted inclusion lens, and teacher CPD for the safeguarding-flagged modules. Shortest credible path to a first pilot: ship the compliance pack (DPIA, policy-ready RSE text, parent sample pack, RSHE-2025 mapping matrix, editable scripts), recruit one school in the summer 2026 term to consult parents ahead of the 1 September 2026 statutory switchover, and use that pilot both as the sales proof point and as the documented parent/child consultation evidence the ICO expects in the DPIA.

## Findings

### 1. (HIGH confidence)

The product's core design choices (statutory mapping, honest sourced statistics, age-staged modules) directly match the DfE's published selection criteria that schools apply to any external RSHE resource before use: alignment to statutory guidance, age-appropriateness, and being evidence-based with robust facts and statistics. But schools formally vet, not just pick up, vendor materials — auditing them for legal accuracy, balance and inclusivity against both the 2019 and 2025 guidance — so the product must arrive with citable documentation for that vetting, not just good content.

**Evidence:** DfE guidance: schools 'should consider if it: aligns to the teaching requirements set out in the statutory guidance ... is age-appropriate ... is evidence-based and contains robust facts and statistics.' Sector guidance on the 2025 transition: schools should 'audit teaching materials to ensure they are legally accurate, balanced and inclusive' and review curriculum against both 2019 and 2025 guidance. Note the DfE criteria are 'should consider' guidance rather than statutory 'must', and the full list includes further criteria (sensitivity to pupils' experiences, no distressing content) beyond the three headline ones.

**Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/plan-your-relationships-sex-and-health-curriculum · https://ssslearning.co.uk/safeguarding-articles/rshe-guidance-update

### 2. (HIGH confidence)

HARD BLOCKER — parental transparency: parents must be able to view all curriculum materials on request, schools cannot accept contractual or copyright restrictions from a provider that prevent this (the 2025 guidance renders such clauses void and unenforceable), the school's RSE policy must be published online free of charge, and schools must consult parents on the RSE policy — showing sample resources — before finalising it. A paywalled, login-gated lesson player therefore needs a built-in parent-view or shareable sample-materials mechanism, licence terms that explicitly permit parental viewing, and ideally policy-ready text a school can drop into its published RSE policy. This process layer also means no school starts teaching the sensitive modules 'tomorrow': a consultation step sits between purchase and classroom use.

**Evidence:** DfE: 'parents should be able to view all curriculum materials on request, and schools cannot agree to any contractual restrictions with external providers that would prevent parents from seeing content'; 'Schools must consult with parents and carers on the policy before finalising it ... provide examples of the resources that they plan to use'; 'A school's policies for these subjects must be published online, and must be available to any individual free of charge.' Verifiers confirmed the July 2025 statutory guidance strengthens this (restrictive clauses 'void and unenforceable'). Qualification: the statutory consultation duty attaches to the policy, not each resource purchase — a school with an up-to-date consulted-on policy covering these topics could adopt faster; the product's printables (parent note home) may partly satisfy the sharing route.

**Sources:** https://www.gov.uk/guidance/plan-your-relationships-sex-and-health-curriculum

### 3. (HIGH confidence)

HARD BLOCKER — RSHE 2026 mapping: the revised RSHE statutory guidance (published 15 July 2025) replaces the 2019 guidance and becomes compulsory 1 September 2026, so any scheme sold now must map to the 2025 framework to survive the 2026-27 academic year. It names specific new required content a digital literacy scheme claiming RSHE coverage must address: harms of pornography, misogynistic online cultures and incel groups, deepfakes, online gambling risks, and illegal online behaviours such as drug and knife supply. The product's existing module list (sextortion, radicalisation and misogyny, consent and image sharing) covers much of this, but the mapping must be explicit against the 2025 document, not the 2019 one or only Education for a Connected World.

**Evidence:** Verifiers confirmed via gov.uk, PSHE Association, NGA, Sex Education Forum and SWGfL: 'Published in July 2025, this new framework replaces the 2019 guidance and becomes compulsory from 1 September 2026'; the guidance 'includes teaching about the harms linked to pornography and misogynistic online cultures; and the influence of incel groups ... deepfakes, increased focus on the risks associated with online gambling, and new sections cover the risks of illegal behaviours online, such as drug and knife supply.' Timing note: the pilot window (summer/autumn 2026) coincides exactly with the compulsory switchover, which is a selling moment.

**Sources:** https://ssslearning.co.uk/safeguarding-articles/rshe-guidance-update · https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/relationships-education-relationships-and-sex-education-rse-and-health-education · https://pshe-association.org.uk/news/rshe-guidance-2025-what-you-need-to-know

### 4. (HIGH confidence)

HARD BLOCKER — data protection documentation: for a service spanning EYFS to KS5 (ages ~4-18) processing pupil names for quiz cards and booklets, the ICO expects a DPIA that accounts for differing ages, capacities and development needs — a generic one-population DPIA is insufficient. The ICO also expects providers to seek and document the views of children and parents as part of the DPIA (or record and justify why not), which means the first pilot school doubles as citable DPIA consultation evidence, not just a sales milestone. Note: the formal DPIA duty sits with the data controller (often the school), and Children's Code scope depends on the vendor's controller role — but the vendor operating its own platform with class codes and pupil first names makes controller status for some processing plausible, and school DPOs will in practice ask the vendor for this pack.

**Evidence:** ICO Children's Code Standard 2: 'Take into account differing ages, capacities and development needs and ensure that your DPIA builds in compliance with this code'; providers can 'seek and document the views of children and parents (or their representatives), and take them into account in the design of your online service', and if no consultation is done 'you should record that decision in your DPIA, and be prepared to justify it.' Verifiers noted the ICO's 2024-25 edtech audits found over 40% of providers had done no DPIA — an active enforcement concern. A stronger companion claim (blanket Standard 2 applicability to this product) was REFUTED 0-3, so the confidence here is about what DPOs will expect and what the ICO expects of controllers, not a proven statutory duty on this specific vendor.

**Sources:** https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/childrens-information/childrens-code-guidance-and-resources/age-appropriate-design-a-code-of-practice-for-online-services/2-data-protection-impact-assessments/

### 5. (HIGH confidence)

The renewed Ofsted framework (in effect 10 November 2025) raises the stakes and the documentation bar in the product's favour: PSHE/RSHE including online safety feeds directly into the graded 'Personal Development and well-being' report-card area, RSHE is now inspected under 'Curriculum and Teaching' like any other subject (requiring coherent sequencing, not a bolt-on), the PSHE Association advises schools to explicitly link their safeguarding policy to the RSHE curriculum (which the product's DSL notes and KCSIE mapping directly serve), and inspectors check whether disadvantaged and SEND pupils can effectively access the curriculum — so schools will ask the vendor for sequencing rationale, safeguarding-to-curriculum mapping, and SEND differentiation evidence.

**Evidence:** PSHE Association on the renewed framework: 'Comprehensive PSHE education (including RSHE) is central to success in Personal Development and well-being'; 'RSHE will also be considered under Curriculum and Teaching'; schools should make 'sure their safeguarding policy explicitly links to the RSHE/PSHE curriculum'; inspectors look at 'whether all pupils, including those who are disadvantaged or have SEND, can effectively access the PSHE education curriculum they need.' Ofsted's own toolkit corroborates: pupils must 'develop secure and detailed knowledge across the PSHE/RSE programme' for the Strong standard, and inclusion is a standalone graded area. Deep dives were scrapped; scrutiny now runs through whole-school curriculum evaluation.

**Sources:** https://pshe-association.org.uk/news/the-new-ofsted-framework-five-key-considerations-for-pshe/rshe-leads-and-school-leaders · https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/education-inspection-framework

### 6. (MEDIUM confidence)

Scope positioning: statutory RSHE is expected to constitute 80-90% of a school's PSHE curriculum under the 2025 guidance, with economic wellbeing and careers making up the rest — so a digital literacy/online safety scheme, however complete, is a component within a school's PSHE provision, not a whole-PSHE replacement. The product should be marketed as slotting into an existing PSHE scheme (or alongside Jigsaw/SCARF etc.), with mapping that shows exactly which strands it covers and which it deliberately does not.

**Evidence:** PSHE Association: 'statutory RSHE will constitute 80-90% of the PSHE education curriculum' with schools expected to also cover the remaining 10-20% (economic wellbeing, careers). Verified as the Association's editorial estimate, not a DfE figure — hence medium confidence on the number, high on the positioning conclusion. Incumbent comparison evidence (per-lesson table stakes from Jigsaw/Twinkl-class providers) did not survive verification, so the competitive-parity picture rests on the Oak data below rather than direct incumbent audits.

**Sources:** https://pshe-association.org.uk/news/the-new-ofsted-framework-five-key-considerations-for-pshe/rshe-leads-and-school-leaders

### 7. (MEDIUM confidence)

ADOPTION FRICTION — script rigidity vs adaptation reality: prep-time saving is a credible core adoption driver (73% of Oak users reported positive workload impact; median 4 hours/week saved among those reporting a decrease), but teachers overwhelmingly adapt rather than adopt wholesale — about two-thirds of Oak users used it to modify their existing curriculum and only 6-9% used it as their main curriculum — and teachers report that adapting off-the-shelf lessons can consume nearly as much time as writing from scratch. A word-for-word scripted lesson player must therefore either be genuinely teachable verbatim (which its projector-led, no-prep design plausibly is) or offer trivial editability (editable scripts, swappable slides, reorderable modules), or it will be cherry-picked rather than adopted as a full scheme.

**Evidence:** Oak/ImpactEd: '73% of Oak users said that using Oak had had a positive impact on their workload, with 45% saying it had decreased their workload'; 'Two-thirds of Oak users said they had used Oak to change their curriculum, with 39% adding or swapping topics, 22% changing the sequence, and 6% using Oak as their main curriculum' (24/25 update: 13% sequence, 9% main curriculum). Schools Week: one teacher said adapting resources 'was almost as time consuming as creating them from scratch.' Counterweight claims (54% no workload impact; 6% increased workload) were REFUTED/split in verification, and 40% of users did save time without editability — so treat verbatim-or-editable as a strong design heuristic, not a proven necessity. Two of the three merged claims passed 2-1.

**Sources:** https://www.thenational.academy/blog/impact-report-2023-2024 · https://schoolsweek.co.uk/most-teachers-say-oak-lessons-didnt-cut-workload/

### 8. (MEDIUM confidence)

Market opening — teacher confidence gap: the Youth Select Committee's 2025/26 inquiry (report published 25 March 2026) found PSHE quality and frequency vary as a 'postcode lottery' across UK secondary schools, that teachers often lack training or confidence to deliver specialist content, and that only 30% of surveyed students said PSHE lessons helped prepare them for adult life. This documents exactly the gap the word-for-word scripts and DSL notes address, and confirms teacher CPD for sensitive-topic delivery is a live expectation — meaning a short CPD/staff-briefing layer for the safeguarding-flagged modules (sextortion, pornography, radicalisation) would materially strengthen adoption, and pilot schools will want impact evidence (pupil outcomes), not just coverage evidence.

**Evidence:** Parliament.uk: 'The inquiry identified variation in the quality and frequency of PSHE, described as a postcode lottery, and found that teachers often lacked training or confidence to deliver specialist content'; 'In survey responses, only 30% of students said PSHE lessons helped prepare them for adult life.' Caveats from verification: the Youth Select Committee is a youth engagement programme, not a formal select committee; the survey is likely self-selecting; and the report does not itself endorse scripted resources — the fit to this product is inference. Medium confidence on the generalisation, high on the quoted findings themselves.

**Sources:** https://www.parliament.uk/business/news/2026/march-2026/youth-select-committee-publishes-report-on-pshe-education/

### 9. (MEDIUM confidence)

Shortest credible path to a first pilot school (synthesis of all findings): (1) build the compliance pack — vendor DPIA covering ages 4-18 by phase, policy-ready RSE policy text, a shareable parent sample pack per sensitive module, and licence terms that explicitly permit parental viewing; (2) publish an explicit mapping matrix to the July 2025 RSHE guidance (including pornography, incel culture, deepfakes, gambling, illegal online behaviours), KCSIE 2025 and Education for a Connected World, plus a safeguarding-to-curriculum crosswalk the DSL can paste into the school's safeguarding policy; (3) add editable-script/swappable-slide capability and SEND access notes per lesson; (4) add a short staff CPD briefing per safeguarding-flagged module; (5) recruit one school in the summer 2026 term so the parental consultation runs before the 1 September 2026 statutory switchover — the pilot then serves triple duty as sales proof, ICO-citable DPIA consultation evidence, and the impact-evidence baseline Ofsted's graded Personal Development area now rewards.

**Evidence:** This is the synthesized recommendation derived from the verified findings above, not a single-source claim. Every component traces to a confirmed requirement: parent access (DfE), 2025 mapping (statutory from 1 Sept 2026), DPIA with consultation (ICO Standard 2), CPD (Youth Select Committee training gap plus school staff-training expectations in the 2025 transition guidance), editability (Oak adaptation evidence), safeguarding crosswalk (PSHE Association Ofsted advice). Confidence is medium because the sequencing and sufficiency judgment is analytic rather than directly sourced.
