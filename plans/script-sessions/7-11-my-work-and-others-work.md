# Script session: My work and other people's work (7 to 11)

**Band:** 7 to 11 (parent first, child version via Star Lesson)
**Module:** My work and other people's work
**Cast:** Zara leads (truth finder, squad register with capes), DiGi opens and closes, DiGi Junior pause
**Single action outcome:** I put my name on what I make, and I ask first and say who made it when I use someone else's work.

---

## Track P — the parent session (60 to 90 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 12s)**
Your nine year old holds up a drawing they spent the whole afternoon on. Look what I made. That pride, right there, is the entire lesson, and most of us walk straight past it.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 25s)**
We usually teach this topic backwards, as a list of rules about not stealing other people's stuff, and children switch off because rules about strangers' pictures feel like nothing. Flip it. Start with THEIR ownership. The moment your child makes something, it belongs to them: the drawing, the story, the world they built in a game, the joke they invented at break. A child who has felt the pride of my name on my work understands in their bones why taking someone else's without asking stings. Teach the ownership and the respect arrives free. You never have to say copyright once.

**P-BEAT 3 — TRY THIS TONIGHT (mission beat, 20s)**
Tonight, take one thing your child has made and treat it like it matters. Ask them to sign it. Then ask their permission before you do anything with it: this is yours, may I show Grandma? Watch what being asked teaches them. That one moment of being the owner does more than any lecture about other people's work. When you are ready, send the kids version. Zara teaches it as a detective case and your child earns stars for cracking it.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: Questions about your child's creations or a school project? Ask me any time.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *Everything you make is yours*
Heading: The moment you make it, you own it.
Body: Your drawing. Your story. Your video. The world you built in a game. The joke you invented at break. The moment you make something, it belongs to you, and grown ups take this so seriously they wrote laws about it: makers have rights over what they make. So here is the first detective habit: put your name on your work. Signing it says I made this, and I am proud, and it sends your name travelling with your work wherever it goes.

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The perfect picture*
You are making a school project and you find the perfect picture online. Right size, right colours, exactly what you need. Freeze. Someone made that picture. Somewhere there is a kid, or a grown up, who spent their Saturday on it, just like you spent yours on your drawing. What would THEY want you to do next?

**SLIDE (keywords)** — *Detective words*
creator: the person who made it · credit: saying who made it · permission: asking before you use it · copying: using someone's work without asking, the thing that stings

```
            it. Name it, ask first, say who."
```

### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *Your friend's song*
Your friend made a brilliant song on the school tablet, and it would be perfect for your video. What is the detective move?
· Just use it. They are your friend, they will not mind (tap to reveal: they probably will not mind, and that is exactly why asking is so easy. Asking turns taking into sharing.)
· Ask first, then put music by Priya on the video (tap to reveal: case cracked. Your friend just became the proudest kid in school, and your video got better. ⭐)
· Use it but change it a tiny bit so it counts as yours (tap to reveal: drawing a moustache on someone else's painting does not make it your painting.)

**SLIDE (interactive: spread-race)** — *The name race*
Two copies of the same brilliant artwork line up at a start line. One has the creator's name signed in the corner. One has the name cropped off. Tap SHARE to send them racing across the screen from page to page. At the finish line the reveal: the signed one made its maker famous, kids are asking her to draw more. The nameless one got a thousand shares and its maker got nothing, nobody even knows who she is. Reaction line: your name on your work travels WITH the work. That is why creators sign.

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out*
Has anyone ever taken something of yours, a joke, an idea, a game you invented at break, and got the credit for it? How did that feel? Talk about it. (Steer: that feeling is the whole lesson. Every picture, song and video online has a someone behind it who can feel exactly that.)

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The volcano project*
You need three pictures for your volcano project, and grabbing the first three from a search takes ten seconds. But a detective has better options: use the search tools that show pictures which are free for anyone to use, draw your own and sign it, or ask the maker. Same project, and every picture in it is one you are allowed to be proud of. Which option would you pick, and why?

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
A picture is on the internet, so...
· It belongs to everyone now (reaction: this is the one almost everybody gets wrong. Being easy to copy does not make it yours. The internet is a gallery, not a lost property bin.)
· Someone made it, and it still belongs to them (reaction: case cracked. Behind every picture is a maker, just like you. ⭐)
· It belongs to the computer (reaction: computers do not own things. People do, and one of those people made this.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
You made an amazing comic and you want to post it. The detective move is...
· Post it with your name on it, proud and loud (reaction: name it! That is your work travelling the world with you attached. ⭐)
· Post it with no name so nobody can be mean about it (reaction: your work deserves your name. And if anyone is ever unkind about it, that is a different case, and it is never your fault.)
· Do not post it, someone might copy it (reaction: copies can happen, that is true. Detectives post proudly anyway, name in the corner, because MADE BY ME is the one part nobody can copy.)
