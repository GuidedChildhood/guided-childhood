# Script session: Scams, fraud and money online (11 to 14)

**Band:** 11 to 14 (parent primer short, child version leads)
**Module:** Scams, fraud and money online (module 13, KS3)
**Cast:** Vix the fox leads (street smart register, still 8365a8ff, first motion beats are a build task), DiGi opens and closes, DiGi Junior pause
**Single action outcome:** I can spot a scam's three tells before it costs me anything.
**The three tells (this module's tool, one breath):** it promises too much, it rushes you, and it asks for something.

---

## Track P — the parent primer (about 60 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 10s)**
Your 12 year old asks to borrow your card for a skin bundle that is 90 percent off for the next 41 minutes. There is a countdown timer on it and everything. They are not being played, they insist. It is a limited drop.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 20s)**
Teenagers are not scammed because they are naive. They are scammed because they are the most targeted, in the places they trust most: game stores, giveaways, creator promos, marketplace chats. And half the tricks are not even scams by name. Countdown timers, mystery boxes and loot mechanics use the same pressure machinery, legally, inside games they love. The move is not to police every purchase. It is to give them the tells, because a kid who can spot pressure spends better for life.

**P-BEAT 3 — TRY THIS TONIGHT (mission beat, 20s)**
Tonight, ask them to show you the best offer they have seen this week, and look at it together with one question: what is it doing to make you decide fast? That is the whole conversation. Then send the lesson. At this age the child version leads, send it and they earn stars, and Vix teaches all three tells with the respect a street smart kid deserves.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: Unsure about an offer, a seller or a charge? Ask me any time.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The three tells*
Heading: Every scam leaks the same three signals.
Body: Tell one: it promises too much. Free consoles, doubled money, rare skins for nothing. Real deals are boring. Tell two: it rushes you. Timers, last chance, first 100 people only. Pressure exists to stop you thinking, because thinking kills scams. Tell three: it asks for something. Money, a login, a code, card details, just pay postage. The ask is the whole point, everything before it was stage dressing. One tell means look closer. Two or three means walk away.

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The giveaway*
A post fills the frame: a big creator's face, WE ARE GIVING AWAY 50 CONSOLES!! Comments full of it worked!! and got mine!! A link, a form asking for your name, address and a small delivery fee by card. The account has the creator's face but the handle has an extra underscore. Question under the post: run the tells. How many light up?

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The casino inside the game*
Heading: Some pressure is built into games you already play.
Body: Mystery boxes, spin wheels, limited crates: you pay before you know what you get, the near miss animation plays almost every time, and the rare item glows on screen just often enough to keep you buying. That machinery is borrowed from gambling, and it runs on tell two: excitement now, thinking later. It is legal, it lives inside real games, and it reads exactly like a scam on the tells test. Same fox rules apply: if the rush is doing the deciding, it is not really you deciding.

### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *The marketplace DM*
You list old trainers online. Instant reply: buyer offers MORE than your asking price, needs them shipped today, sends a link to a payment page to claim your money, just enter your card details. What is the fox move?
· Take it, more money is more money (tap to reveal: tell one just paid you a compliment. Nobody pays extra for used trainers. That overpayment is the bait.)
· Run the tells: too much, too fast, and an ask for card details to RECEIVE money (tap to reveal: three for three. You never enter card details to receive money, anywhere, ever. Walk away with your trainers and your card. ⭐)
· Ask them to pay through the platform's own payment system (tap to reveal: solid instinct, and watch what happens. Real buyers say fine. Scammers vanish, because the platform was the thing they needed you to leave.)

**SLIDE (interactive: signal-meter)** — *The scam meter*
One offer builds on screen, detail by detail, and the class calls the meter after each one: a game currency top up site. 20 percent cheaper than the official store (meter rises: too much?). 5,000 five star reviews posted in the same week (rises: staged). Offer ends in 10 minutes (rises: too fast). Log in with your game account to receive the coins (slams to the top: the ask was your login all along). Reaction line: the cheap coins never existed. Your account was the product.

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *When it already happened*
Real talk: someone in every year group has already been caught by one of these. Lost a skin, lost an account, paid postage for nothing. Here is what matters: scams are built by teams of adults doing this full time, so getting caught is not stupidity, it is maths. The move afterwards is speed, not shame: tell an adult, contact the bank or the platform fast, change the password. Fast beats embarrassed every single time, and the person who tells someone always comes out better than the person who hides it.

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out*
Why do scam messages sometimes have obvious spelling mistakes and cheap graphics, when good fakes are easy to make? Talk about it. (Steer: some are just lazy. But some are filtering on purpose: anyone sharp enough to notice was never going to pay, so the sloppiness screens them out early. Which means never lean on it looks professional as your test. The tells work on polished scams too. That is the point of them.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
You would spot a scam easily, because scams look dodgy. What is wrong with that?
· Nothing, dodgy is dodgy (reaction: this is the belief every victim had the day before. The best scams look better than the real thing, perfect logos, real sounding names, glowing reviews. Looks are not a tell. Pressure is.)
· Scams look dodgy but only sometimes (reaction: closer, but you are still judging by looks. Judge by behaviour: too much, too fast, the ask.)
· Looks prove nothing, the tells are in what it promises, how it rushes you and what it asks for (reaction: that is a fox answer. Polished or sloppy, the three tells always leak. ⭐)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
A game offers a mystery crate: small chance of an ultra rare skin, timer on a bonus deal, buy before you see what is inside. Which tells are firing?
· None, it is inside a real game so it cannot be a scam (reaction: legal is not the same as fair. The machinery is borrowed from gambling and it runs on the exact same tells.)
· Too much and too fast: a glowing maybe plus a countdown, and you pay before you know what you get (reaction: spotted like a fox. It may be legal, but the rush is doing your deciding. Name the tells, then choose with your own head. ⭐)
· Only the ask, because it costs money (reaction: the ask is there, but look again at the glow and the timer. The rush is the engine.)
