# Script session: AI mastery and data rights (14 to 16)

**Band:** 14 to 16 (parent briefing, near adult child version)
**Module:** AI mastery and data rights (module 20)
**Cast:** Vix the fox leads (street smart register, still 8365a8ff, first motion beats are a build task), DiGi opens and closes and carries the inside knowledge slide, DiGi pause
**Single action outcome:** I can use AI as a ladder not a lift, run the three checks on its answers, and I know my data rights.

---

## Track P — the parent briefing (about 70 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 10s)**
The homework comes back suspiciously polished, and you honestly cannot tell whether your teenager wrote it. Neither can their teacher. And the question everyone keeps asking, did they use AI, is quietly the wrong question.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 25s)**
They will use AI. At school, at work, for the rest of their lives. The question that matters is whether it is a ladder or a lift. A ladder is asking it to explain, to quiz you, to pull apart your draft: you climb, and the skill compounds. A lift is asking it to do the whole thing: you arrive at the top having climbed nothing, and exams, interviews and real life all test legs. There are two more pieces most households never discuss. What they type into these tools goes to a company's servers, so private information about your family does not belong in a prompt box. And your teenager has real data rights: UK GDPR lets anyone ask a platform what it holds on them and ask for deletion. Most adults do not know that. Your teenager is about to.

**P-BEAT 3 — TRY THIS TONIGHT (mission beat, 25s)**
Flip the dynamic. Ask them to get an AI to explain one thing to you, something you genuinely do not understand, and then ask them the killer follow up: how do we know it is right? That one question is the whole skill. Then send the session. At this age it speaks to them straight, and it has an unfair advantage: DiGi is an AI, and DiGi tells them how it all works from the inside.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: Questions about AI, homework, or what a platform knows about your family? Ask me any time.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *Ladder or lift*
Heading: The tool is neutral. The habit is everything.
Body: Ladder use: explain this to me, quiz me on it, find the weak point in my argument, show me a worked example then give me one to do myself. You do the climbing, the AI holds the ladder, and the skill stays with you when the wifi goes. Lift use: write my essay, do my coursework, think my thoughts. You arrive at the top having climbed nothing, and everything that matters later, exams, interviews, the job itself, tests your legs. Nobody has to ban the lift. You just have to notice which button you are pressing, because one of them is quietly deciding what you will be capable of at twenty.

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The three checks work on AI too*
Heading: AI output is content. Content gets checked.
Body: Check one: who made this and how do they know? A model predicts likely words. It does not know things the way you know things, and it can be confidently wrong, complete with invented sources that look completely real. Check two: what do other places say? One search against a real source catches most of it. Check three: how is it trying to make me feel? With AI the manipulation is subtle: fluency. Polished, confident sentences read as true, and that is exactly the feeling to distrust. Smooth is not the same as right. The checks you already own were built for this moment too.

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The prompt box*
Someone types into an AI tool: my mum has just been diagnosed with something, here are her symptoms and her full name, what should we do? A kind question, a real worry, and a mistake. Everything typed into that box travels to a company's servers, where it may be stored, reviewed by humans, or used to improve the model, depending on settings nobody read. Question underneath: the medical question was fine to ask. What should have been left out, and what is the general rule hiding in this story?

### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *Essay night*
It is 9pm, the essay is due tomorrow, and the AI could write the whole thing in forty seconds. What is the fox move?
· Let it write the essay, change a few words, submit (tap to reveal: the lift. It works right up until the mock exam, where the version of you that never climbed sits alone at a desk. Also, teachers can usually tell, and the conversation that follows is worse than a late essay.)
· Make it quiz you on the topic, argue your plan against it, write the essay yourself, then ask it to attack your draft (tap to reveal: the ladder, used like a professional. You hand in your own work, sharpened by the best sparring partner ever built. That habit compounds for life. ⭐)
· Refuse to use AI at all, ever, on principle (tap to reveal: nobody here deals in bans. The tool is in your future whatever you feel about it. The skill is using it so you get stronger, not so the tool does.)

**SLIDE (interactive: verdict-sort)** — *The postcard wall*
Five things about to be typed into an AI tool slide in one at a time. Sort each: FINE TO SEND, STRIP IT FIRST, or NEVER. Your rough essay draft for feedback (fine to send). A question about a mate's mental health that includes their full name (strip it first, ask the question without the name). Your home address for a complaint letter template (strip it first, add the address after, outside the tool). Your password to check if it is strong (never, no tool ever needs your real password). A photo of a friend to make a joke edit, without asking them (never, their face is their data, not yours). Reaction line: the questions were mostly fine. The details were the problem. Postcard, not diary.

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (concept)** — *Your data rights, plainly*
Heading: The data is about you, so the law gives you a say.
Body: In the UK, data protection law, UK GDPR, gives you real rights over your own information, and they are simpler than they sound. You can ask any platform what data it holds about you, and it has to answer. You can ask for your data to be corrected if it is wrong, and deleted if you want it gone. Asking is free, and no lawyer is involved: platforms have request forms and privacy contacts for exactly this. Most adults never use these rights because most adults do not know they exist. You now know they exist, which puts you ahead of most of the internet.

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out*
If using the lift is so tempting and so easy, why do employers, universities and even AI companies keep saying they want people who can think without it? Talk about it. (Steer: because the tool is the same for everyone, so the tool is worth nothing in an interview. What is scarce is the person who can judge the output, catch the confident nonsense, and do the thinking the tool cannot. Ladder people become that person. Lift people become the thing the tool replaces.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
The AI's answer was detailed, fluent and totally confident, so it is true. What is the catch?
· No catch, confidence and detail mean it checked its facts (reaction: this is the misconception that catches almost everyone. A model predicts convincing sentences, not verified facts. It can be confidently wrong with invented sources that look completely real. Fluency is not accuracy.)
· Fluent and wrong happen together all the time, so the three checks run on AI answers like everything else (reaction: exactly. Who made this and how do they know, what do other places say, and notice the smoothness working on your trust. ⭐)
· AI is wrong so often that nothing it says is ever useful (reaction: too far the other way. The tool is genuinely useful, it is just not an authority. Use it, check it, both at once.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
A platform has years of your data and you want to know what it holds, and delete some of it. What is true?
· Nothing you can do, you agreed to the terms (reaction: the terms do not cancel the law. UK GDPR sits above them, and it is on your side here.)
· You can ask what they hold, ask for corrections, and ask for deletion, free, and they must respond (reaction: those are your rights, plainly. Knowing them is the first step to a digital life you actually control. ⭐)
· You can do it, but only through a lawyer and it costs money (reaction: no lawyer, no fee. Platforms have request forms and privacy contacts for exactly this.)
