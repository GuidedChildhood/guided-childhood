# Script session: Privacy, passwords and digital reputation (7 to 11)

**Band:** 7 to 11 (parent first, child version via Star Lesson)
**Module:** Privacy and digital reputation
**Cast:** Zara leads (truth finder, squad register with capes), DiGi opens and closes, DiGi Junior pause
**Single action outcome:** I keep my password a secret key, and I pause before I post because what I post can stay and can be copied.

---

## Track P — the parent session (60 to 90 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 12s)**
Your ten year old mentions, completely casually, that their best friend knows their password. Of course they do. Best friends share everything. That is the whole point of best friends.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 25s)**
Your child is not being careless. At this age, sharing secrets IS friendship, so the lecture about never sharing passwords is fighting their entire social world, and the social world wins. The move that works is ownership. A password is the key to everything they have online: their messages, their photos, their game worlds, their stars. Frame it as their key to their house and the question changes from why will you not share to who should hold a copy of MY key. Children guard what is theirs. The same goes for posting: what goes up can stay and can be copied, not always, but possibly, and possibly is enough reason for a pause.

**P-BEAT 3 — TRY THIS TONIGHT (mission beat, 20s)**
Tonight, borrow this question: if your password is the key to everything in your world, who should hold a copy? Let them land on the answer: you two, nobody else, not even the best friend. Then build a stronger one together, three random words beats a pet's name, because anyone who knows them can guess the pet. When you are ready, send the kids version. Zara teaches it as a detective case and your child earns stars for guarding the key.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: Questions about passwords or something your child posted? Ask me any time.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *Your password is a secret key*
Heading: One little word opens everything.
Body: Your password opens your messages, your photos, your game worlds and your stars. Whoever holds it can BE you online: send messages as you, spend as you, delete as you. That is why it is a secret key, and secret means exactly two people: you and your grown up. Not your best friend. Not even your best friend, because keys get passed on, and friendships change, and the key keeps working either way.

**SLIDE (concept)** — *What makes a strong key*
Heading: Long and random beats short and clever.
Body: The best key is three random words squashed together, like BananaRocketWinter. Easy for you to remember, almost impossible for anyone to guess. The worst keys are the ones made of facts about you: your pet, your team, your birthday. Anyone who knows you can guess those in minutes, and plenty of people online know more about you than you think.

**SLIDE (keywords)** — *Detective words*
password: your secret key · personal information: facts that point to the real you, like your full name, school, address or phone number · post: anything you put online, words, photos or videos · copy: what can happen to a post once it is up, saved by someone else even after you delete yours


### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *The best friend test*
Your best friend says: give me your password so I can play your account while you are on holiday. Best friends tell each other everything! What is the detective move?
· Share it. They are your best friend and they promised (tap to reveal: the friendship is real, but keys travel. When they let their cousin have a go, your key travels too, and it still opens everything.)
· Say: my grown up and me keep this one secret, but let us play together the second I am back (tap to reveal: key guarded, friend kept. That is detective work. ⭐)
· Share it but change it when you get home (tap to reveal: better than nothing, but for a whole week someone else could be you. The key stays home, even on holiday.)

**SLIDE (interactive: verdict-sort)** — *The share wall*
Five cards slide in one at a time. Sort each onto the wall: FINE TO SHARE, ASK A GROWN UP FIRST, or KEEP SECRET.
1. Your favourite football team. (fine to share)
2. A photo of you in school uniform with the school badge showing. (ask a grown up first)
3. Your password. (keep secret, always, no exceptions)
4. Your home address, because a prize form is asking for it. (ask a grown up first)
5. What you had for breakfast. (fine to share)
Reaction lines: correct sorts get Zara's "case cracked!", wrong sorts get a gentle "look again, could this open a door to the real you?"

**SLIDE (diagram)** — *The pause path*
One arrow path: YOU MADE SOMETHING TO POST → PAUSE → three quick questions: could this stay? could this be copied? does it give away the real me? → then choose: post it proudly, change it a bit, or keep it for yourself. The pause takes ten seconds. The post can last years.

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The old post*
Someone in Year 6 finds a silly video a boy posted two whole years ago and sends it round the class. Here is the twist: he deleted that video ages ago. But a friend had saved a copy back then, and the copy never got deleted. How do you think he feels today, and what does this tell a detective about posts?

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out*
Does the old post story mean you should never post anything, ever? Talk about it. (Steer: no. Detectives post their good stuff proudly. The pause is not there to stop you. It is there so the you of two years from now still smiles at what the you of today put up.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
If you delete a post, it is gone forever, everywhere. True or not?
· True. Delete means gone (reaction: this is the one that catches nearly everyone. Deleting your copy helps, but other copies CAN already exist. That is why the pause comes BEFORE the post.)
· Not quite. A post can stay and can be copied, so detectives pause first (reaction: case cracked. Ten second pause, years of glad you did. ⭐)
· Posts vanish by themselves after a week anyway (reaction: they do not. Some things stay up a very long time.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
Who should know your password?
· You and your grown up, nobody else (reaction: the key stays home. That is the whole case. ⭐)
· You, your grown up and your very best friend (reaction: best friends are brilliant, and the key still stays home. You can share everything else.)
· Anyone who pinky promises not to tell (reaction: a promise is not a lock, detective. Keys need locks.)
