# Script session: Sextortion (14 to 16)

**Band:** 14 to 16 (parent briefing, near adult child version)
**Module:** Sextortion (module 17, KS4, DSL flagged)
**SENSITIVE: DiGi only, calm register.** Maximum calm. No squad characters, minimal staging, nothing graphic, no jokes inside the hard content, warmth at the close. The spine throughout: recognise it, refuse it, report it, and you are not in trouble. Central message, said plainly and more than once: this happens to a lot of people, it is never the young person's fault, panic is the scammer's only weapon, and help works.
**Cast:** DiGi only, every beat
**Single action outcome:** I know exactly what to do and who to tell, and I know it is not my fault.

---

## Track P — the parent briefing (about 70 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 10s)**
Your teenager comes downstairs pale, snappy and glued to their phone in a way that looks nothing like scrolling. Something has happened online and they are deciding, right now, whether you are safe to tell. This briefing is about winning that decision before it ever gets made.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 30s)**
Sextortion is a scam with a script. An account that seems like a peer arrives with fast flattery, moves the conversation quickly, obtains an image or claims to have one, then flips to threats and demands, usually for money, on a countdown. The whole machine runs on panic and shame, and it targets ordinary, sensible teenagers precisely because they have reputations they care about. So the words matter enormously. Never say: how could you be so stupid, and never threaten the phone, one more thing like this and it is gone. Confiscation threats are the number one reason teenagers face this alone, and alone is exactly where the scam wants them. Say instead: you are not in trouble, I am so glad you told me, we will sort this together and your phone stays yours. Those words are the rescue.

**P-BEAT 3 — THE HANDOFF (mission beat, 25s)**
Know the plan yourself first: stop all contact, never pay, send nothing more, screenshot and save the evidence, and report it to CEOP, the police's child protection reporting service. Payment never buys silence, it only confirms that payment happens. Then share the session with them. DiGi walks it through, no lecture. Your teenager finishes it knowing the plan cold and knowing that telling you is the strongest move on the board.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: If anything like this is happening in your house right now, ask me any time, tonight included.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *How it looks from the inside*
Heading: It starts warm and it moves fast.
Body: A new follower or a message request arrives from an account that seems like a peer. Attractive, friendly, quickly very interested. The conversation moves faster than real ones do: compliments early, private chat quickly, then a push to trade images. The speed is the tell. It is not chemistry, it is a script, often run by an adult working through many targets at once. The moment an image exists, or they claim one exists, the tone flips in a single message: pay, or everyone you know sees it. A countdown appears. That flip, warm to threatening in an instant, is the signature of the whole scam.

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The flip, at one remove*
Someone in Year 10 gets a message from an account that seems like a student at a nearby school. The profile checks out at a glance: mutual style, believable photos, normal posts. Within an hour it is the best conversation they have had all week. Within a day comes the request to trade images. And the moment anything is sent, the account changes voice completely: a demand for money, a deadline, a list of their followers pasted into the chat. Question underneath: the person targeted here made one decision under pressure. The scammer made hundreds, on purpose, following a script. Where does the fault actually live?

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The plan, before you ever need it*
Heading: Six moves. Learn them once.
Body: Stop contact: no replies, no negotiating, the conversation is over. Do not pay: payment never makes it stop, it marks you as someone who pays. Send nothing more: no matter what is threatened. Screenshot and save: the messages, the account name, everything, because evidence helps the people who can act. Tell a trusted adult: tonight, not after a week of carrying it alone. Report to CEOP: the police's child protection reporting service, built for exactly this. And for the image itself, Report Remove, run by Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation, can work to take it down. Help is real, and it works.

### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *The countdown hour*
The account says: pay within one hour or everything goes to your followers. In that hour, what is actually true?
· The threat is real and paying is the only way to control it (tap to reveal: the threat is designed to feel that way, and the hour is designed to stop you thinking. Paying controls nothing. It confirms you pay, and the demands continue.)
· The scammer is running a script on many people at once, and panic is the only power they have (tap to reveal: exactly. Scripts have no answer for a person who stops, saves, tells and reports. People who do this come out the other side. ⭐)
· Deleting your whole account makes it all disappear (tap to reveal: it can destroy the evidence that helps the people who can act. Screenshot first, then stop contact. Save beats delete.)

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out, no names ever*
Why does this scam work best on careful people with good reputations? Talk about it in general terms only. (Steer: because they have the most to protect, so the shame hits hardest and the silence lasts longest. Shame is the engine of the whole scam, which is why saying it out loud to one adult is the move that breaks the engine. The scammer needs the secret more than they need the money.)

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
The demand is small. Paying it quietly would make the whole thing go away. True or not?
· True, pay once and it ends (reaction: this is the single most dangerous belief in this whole topic. Paying never makes it stop. It tells the scammer you are someone who pays, and the demands continue and often grow. Silence is not for sale here.)
· False. Paying marks you as a payer. The plan is stop, save, tell, report, and it is the plan because it works (reaction: exactly right. The scam has no move against a person who refuses to pay and refuses to carry it alone. ⭐)
· True, but only if you pay fast enough (reaction: speed is their trick, not your friend. The countdown exists to stop you thinking. Step outside it: stop, save, tell, report.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
A friend tells you this is happening to them right now. It is 11pm and they are panicking. What is the first move?
· Help them find the money quietly (reaction: that feeds the scam and leaves them alone inside it. Paying never ends it.)
· Tell them it is not their fault, help them screenshot and stop contact, and get a trusted adult tonight, then report to CEOP (reaction: this is exactly it, and the order matters: not your fault first, because shame is what keeps people silent. You might be the reason they come through this well. ⭐)
· Promise to keep it secret forever (reaction: the secret is the scam's oxygen. Real loyalty here is helping them tell an adult, not helping them hide.)
