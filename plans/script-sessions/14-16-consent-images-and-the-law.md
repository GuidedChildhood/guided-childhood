# Script session: Consent, images and the law (14 to 16)

**Band:** 14 to 16 (parent briefing, near adult child version)
**Module:** Consent, images and the law (module 16, KS4, DSL flagged)
**SENSITIVE: DiGi only, calm register.** No squad characters, minimal staging, nothing graphic, no jokes inside the hard content, warmth at the close. The spine throughout: recognise it, refuse it, report it, and you are not in trouble.
**Cast:** DiGi only, every beat
**Single action outcome:** I know the law and my options before anything is shared, and I know the way out exists at every step.

---

## Track P — the parent briefing (about 70 seconds)

**P-BEAT 1 — THE MOMENT (dialogue beat, 10s)**
A letter comes home from school: an image has been circulating in the year group. Your first feeling is relief that it is not your child. Here is the uncomfortable truth: every parent in that year group felt exactly the same relief, including the ones it was about.

**P-BEAT 2 — THE REFRAME (concept beat, 30s)**
The law surprises most adults. In the UK, an intimate image of anyone under 18 is illegal to make, keep or share. That includes an image of yourself. It includes images both people agreed to, inside a real relationship. And when these cases involve young people, the system's aim is safeguarding, not punishing, which is exactly the message your teenager needs from you too. So here is the sentence never to say: if I ever find out you have sent something like that, that phone is gone. Confiscation threats are the number one reason teenagers tell nobody when they are in trouble. Say this instead: whatever happens, you will not lose your phone and you will not be in trouble with me. We sort it together. Say it before anything happens. It is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

**P-BEAT 3 — THE HANDOFF (mission beat, 25s)**
Two names to know tonight. Report Remove, run by Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation, lets a young person confidentially report an intimate image and have the IWF work to take copies down. It exists, it is real, and knowing it exists changes how survivable a bad moment feels. Then share the session with them. DiGi walks it through, no lecture. It covers the law, the pressure tactics and the way out at every step, in a voice they will actually hear.

**P-BEAT 4 — DIGI TAG (5s, from the DiGi library)**
DiGi star wave: If this topic ever touches your family, ask me any time. No judgement lives here.

### SLIDES after Beat 1

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The law, plainly*
Heading: Under 18, the law draws one clear line.
Body: An intimate image of anyone under 18 is illegal to take, keep or share. That includes an image of yourself. It includes images that both people agreed to, inside a real relationship. Agreement does not move this line, because the law was written to protect young people, not to catch them out. And when schools and police deal with cases between young people, the aim is safeguarding first, not criminalising. One more piece most people miss: receiving an image and passing it on is sharing it. The law counts the forward, and so does the harm.

**SLIDE (scenario)** — *The couple*
Two people in Year 11 have been together for months. It is a real relationship, there is real trust, and one asks the other for a photo. Nobody is being cruel. Nobody thinks of themselves as breaking a law. And yet everything on the previous slide still applies: making it, keeping it, sharing it. Question underneath: the person asking may truly mean no harm. What can neither of them control once an image exists?

**SLIDE (concept)** — *Pressure never signs its name*
Heading: A real yes never needs a campaign.
Body: Pressure to send rarely looks like a demand. It looks like asking again after a no. It looks like you would if you trusted me. It looks like a trade, I sent one so you owe one, or a sulk that lasts until you give in. Here is the clean test: a genuine yes needs asking once. Anyone running a campaign has already answered the real question, which is whether they can be trusted with an image. They cannot. Refusing is not cold. Refusing is reading the situation correctly.

### SLIDES after Beat 2

**SLIDE (choice)** — *The forward*
An image of someone in your year lands in a group chat. People are reacting. In that moment, what is actually true?
· It is already out there, so looking and forwarding changes nothing (tap to reveal: every forward is a new share and a new harm, and in law it is a new offence. The chain stops at whoever refuses to pass it on. That person can be you.)
· Forwarding is part of the harm. Delete it, do not share it, and tell an adult so the person in it can get help (tap to reveal: exactly right. And notice: receiving it did not put you in trouble. What you do next is the only part you own. ⭐)
· Screenshot it first so you can show people how serious it is (tap to reveal: keeping a copy is keeping a copy. Tell an adult instead. They can act without the image travelling one screen further.)

**SLIDE (discussion)** — *Talk it out, no names ever*
Why do you think someone being pressured to send an image so often tells nobody at all? Talk about it in general terms only. (Steer: shame, and the fear of being blamed or losing the phone. Which is exactly why the law treats the young person as someone to protect, and why the only right answer to someone telling you is never blame. The silence is the most dangerous part, and it is also the most fixable.)

**SLIDE (concept)** — *The way out has a name*
Heading: Report Remove exists, and it works quietly.
Body: If an intimate image of someone under 18 is out there, or they fear it is about to be, there is a real tool built for exactly this. It is called Report Remove, run by Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation. A young person can report the image confidentially, and the IWF works to find copies and take them down. You do not need to have done everything right to use it. It was built precisely for the moment when things have already gone wrong. That, plus one trusted adult, is the way out. It stays open at every step, no matter how far things have gone.

### SLIDES after Beat 3

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 1*
Two 16 year olds in a relationship agree to share images with each other. Both said yes, so it is legal. What is true?
· Both agreed, so it is legal (reaction: this is the one almost everyone gets wrong, including adults. Under 18 the law applies even with agreement, even in a real relationship, even to an image of yourself. Agreement does not move the line.)
· It is still against the law, and the strongest position is that images like this never get made or sent (reaction: correct. And if it has already happened, nobody's aim is to punish you. The way out is a trusted adult and Report Remove, and it stays open. ⭐)
· It becomes legal as long as they delete it afterwards (reaction: deleting does not undo making or sending, and copies are outside your control the moment an image travels. The line sits before the image exists.)

**SLIDE (choice)** — *QUIZ, question 2*
Someone tells you an image of them has been shared and they are terrified. What do they most need to hear first?
· How could you be so careless (reaction: this is the sentence that makes people disappear into silence. Whatever you feel, this is never the move.)
· This is not your fault, you are not in trouble, and there is a real way to get it taken down (reaction: exactly this, in this order. Then help them reach a trusted adult and Report Remove, run by Childline and the IWF. That is how the way out actually gets used. ⭐)
· Say nothing and let it blow over (reaction: this needs action, not silence, and the action works. Takedown tools and trusted adults exist for exactly this hour.)
