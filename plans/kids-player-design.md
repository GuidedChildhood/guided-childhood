# The Kids Player Design Brief — what makes lessons kids beg for
**Created 6 Jul 2026 · Research distilled from teardowns of Duolingo, Kahoot, Blooket, Gimkit, Khan Kids, Prodigy, BBC Bitesize, Brilliant, Minecraft Education, TTRS and Sumdog, plus the child development and gamification evidence. This is the v2 design brief for the lesson player, answering JP's verdict: too corporate.**

## The verdict on the current player

The chassis is right (one question per screen, segmented progress, chunky gold buttons: closest to Duolingo and Brilliant, the two best in class). What is missing: (a) the verdict sheet feedback moment, (b) between question suspense beats for projected whole class play, (c) a KS3 skin that cools the palette and demotes the character before Year 8 calls it babyish, (d) projector mode contrast: cream washes out under classroom lighting and needs a higher contrast variant of the same tokens.

## The 12 design moves (ranked by impact, each with its evidence)

1. **The verdict sheet.** On answer lock in, a colour coded panel slides up within 100ms: green celebration for correct, kind red for wrong with the right answer shown, and the missed question silently requeued to the end so every pupil finishes on a win. KS2: character celebrates on the sheet. KS3: tick plus streak count, character as a 48px corner emote. (Duolingo core loop; immediate feedback is the most robust win in the research.)
2. **Projector owns the question, devices own only the answers.** Whole class mode: pupil screens show just the big colour AND shape coded answer buttons (shape too, for colour blind pupils), the question lives on the shared screen. This one split creates the heads up classroom. (Kahoot's whole social design.)
3. **Suspense beats between questions.** Countdown, lock, pause, answer distribution bars, top 5 leaderboard, end of lesson podium revealed 3rd 2nd 1st. The reveal is the reward. (Kahoot; highest positive emotions of any compared method.)
4. **One skeleton, two skins (the Bitesize move).** Same mechanics and grid; KS2 skin: saturated accents, characterful, mascot narrates; KS3 skin: darker cooler surfaces, sharper type, mascot demoted to a reactive emote, themes borrowed from current game grammar (heist, impostor, tycoon), zero kid language, no meme copy ever. (BBC Bitesize age banded art direction; NN/g teen research: babyish is the kiss of death at 13.)
5. **In lesson combo meter, not cross day streaks.** "3 in a row... 5 in a row!" flares on the progress bar; daily streaks belong to homework mode only.
6. **Luck compresses the leaderboard.** Periodic chest picks with variable payouts so the bottom half stays in contention to the last question. Doubles as the anxiety mitigation and the healthy variable reward (unexpected rewards dodge the overjustification effect; expected wages for learning kill intrinsic motivation, Lepper 1973, Deci 1999). (Blooket Gold Quest.)
7. **Answers as fuel, not just points.** Correct answers earn a spendable resource with 2 or 3 strategic choices. Choice is what adolescents rate above rewards. (Gimkit.)
8. **Projection readability floor.** Question text 40pt equivalent minimum, nothing under 24pt, farthest pupil within 6x image height, contrast boosted beyond phone levels: classroom mode gets a higher contrast variant of the tokens. Decoration lives between questions, never behind text (Mayer coherence).
9. **No shame errors, descriptive praise.** Wrong answers never flash a child's name in red; praise names the act ("You spotted the missing source") never the child ("Clever!"). Requeue instead of punish. No hearts or lives in classroom mode. (Khan Kids; leaderboard embarrassment findings.)
10. **A slow collectible identity economy.** Small coin drip into cosmetic only avatar items; avatars double as the anonymity layer for inter class play. Cosmetic ONLY: Prodigy's pay to win resentment is the proof of what to avoid. (TTRS model, about a quarter of UK schools.)
11. **Sound as a bonus layer with visual twins.** Ship mute first (teachers mute); every audio cue has an equal information visual twin. Invest in lobby and podium music for rooms with speakers, that is where Kahoot's magic audibly lives.
12. **Personal best and team framing at KS3.** Default to teams or beat your last score deltas, leaderboard capped at top 5, optional confidence mode. Competition energises many and reliably harms a minority; teams keep the energy, dump the anxiety.

## Register cheat sheet

- **KS2 (7 to 11):** high chroma accents, rounded characterful, the squad narrates and celebrates, collect and customise.
- **KS3 (11 to 14):** restraint IS the aesthetic; energy from mechanics and suspense, not decoration; cooler surfaces, sharper type; character as a small reactive emote; game grammar themes; the word kid never appears.
- **Both:** one idea per screen, immediate feedback, end on success, effort praised over ability, meaning never by colour alone.

## What this means against the existing rules

No conflicts: GSAP only covers every move here; tokens stay (classroom mode adds a high contrast variant, not new colours); characters stay canonical (the star, the squad); rule 3 (no generic patterns) is strengthened, the moves above are specific mechanics, not decoration. The character speech bubble convention from the print system is the paper twin of move 9.

Full teardown detail and citations live in the session research report; per product one liners: Duolingo = the verdict sheet and requeue; Kahoot = projector split and podium; Blooket = skins over one question engine and luck; Gimkit = answers as fuel; Khan Kids = descriptive praise; Prodigy = the cautionary tale (unrelated game plus toll gate questions plus pay to win); Bitesize = age banded skins; Brilliant = wrong answers open something to fiddle with; Minecraft = ownership beats polish; TTRS = coins, status and avatar anonymity; Sumdog = invisible per pupil difficulty.
