# Sample 5 minute explainer script — with illustration prompts

**Written 9 Jul 2026 · A worked example of the explainer video format · Feeds Higgsfield illustration generation directly · No dashes**

This is one finished script in the format we will use for every lesson explainer. It is built so the narration and the on screen picture always carry the same idea at the same time (Mayer's multimedia and modality principles), one idea per beat, no text read aloud word for word. Each beat gives you an illustration prompt you can paste into Higgsfield to generate the matching picture, in whatever house illustration style is chosen.

- **Lesson**: How the feed decides what you see
- **Stage**: Explorer, ages 11 to 13
- **Host character**: Olga, Online Smarts, curious and sharp
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: warm, plain, a little conspiratorial, never a lecture

---

### Beat 1 · The hook (0:00 to 0:30)

**Narration.** Have you ever picked up your phone to check one thing, and then looked up and twenty minutes were gone? That is not an accident. Someone built it that way. Today we find out how the feed decides exactly what to show you, and how to take the wheel back.

**On screen.** A young person glances at a phone. A clock spins fast behind them. The feed scrolls on and on by itself. Olga leans into the corner of the frame, eyebrow raised.

**Illustration prompt.** A curious eleven year old sitting on a sofa looking at a phone, a clock behind them spinning quickly to show time passing, an endless scroll of little posts flowing down the screen, one friendly cartoon guide character called Olga peeking in from the side. Warm, calm, a touch playful.

---

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)

**Narration.** Every app has an algorithm. That is just a recipe, a set of steps a computer follows. And this recipe has one job. Keep you watching. Not to make you happy. Not to tell you the truth. Just, keep you here.

**On screen.** A big friendly recipe card titled Keep Watching, with simple steps. A little computer reads it and nods.

**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly recipe card headed Keep Watching with three simple numbered steps, a small cheerful computer character reading the recipe like a chef. Clean, bright, one idea, plenty of white space.

---

### Beat 3 · What it watches (1:10 to 2:00)

**Narration.** So how does it choose what to show you? It watches you. Every video you finish, every one you rewatch, every thing you pause on for a second too long. It does not care what you say you like. It cares what you actually do.

**On screen.** A finger hovers and lingers on one post. Soft sensor lines notice the pause. A bar labelled watch time quietly fills.

**Illustration prompt.** A close up of a thumb pausing on one post in a feed, gentle glowing sensor lines noticing the pause, a small bar filling up labelled watch time. The mood is observed, not scary.

---

### Beat 4 · The loop (2:00 to 2:50)

**Narration.** Then it gives you more of that. And more. Tighter and tighter, until your feed becomes a mirror of your weakest moment, not your best self.

**On screen.** A loop drawn live: watch, signal, more of the same, watch more, circling faster and faster until it closes into a bubble around the child.

**Illustration prompt.** A circular loop diagram drawn in a hand made style, arrows going watch then more of the same then watch more, the circle tightening into a bubble around a small figure. Simple, two or three colours.

---

### Beat 5 · The twist (2:50 to 3:40)

**Narration.** Here is the part they do not advertise. The feed learns fastest from what upsets you. Anger and worry keep you scrolling more than calm does. So the loudest, most extreme stuff gets pushed to the front.

**On screen.** Two posts race across a track. A calm one and an angry one. The angry one pulls ahead and gets boosted to the top.

**Illustration prompt.** Two social media posts drawn as little race cars on a track, one calm and smiley, one angry and shouty, the angry one pulling ahead with a boost arrow lifting it to the top of a feed. Playful, makes a serious point gently.

---

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:40 to 4:20)

**Narration.** But you are not powerless. The algorithm only knows what you feed it. Skip fast. Do not linger on the stuff that makes you feel small. Follow the things that make you think, laugh, or make something. Train it on your best self.

**On screen.** A hand swipes past the loud stuff on purpose, chooses the good stuff. The feed visibly changes, brighter and calmer.

**Illustration prompt.** A hand deliberately swiping past a shouty post and choosing a bright creative one, the feed behind it changing from dark and noisy to calm and colourful. Empowering, in control.

---

### Beat 7 · The close and the mission (4:20 to 5:00)

**Narration.** The feed is not your enemy, and it is not your friend. It is a machine following a recipe. And now, you can read the recipe. This week, catch one moment the feed pulls you in, and name it out loud. Seeing the trick is how you beat it.

**On screen.** Olga gives a knowing nod. A young person closes the phone and looks up, calm and clear eyed.

**Illustration prompt.** The guide character Olga giving a warm knowing nod, next to a young person calmly putting their phone down and looking up with a small confident smile. Resolved, hopeful, calm.

---

## How to use this

1. Read it once as a whole. The narration is the voiceover script, ready for a voice tool or a real read.
2. Generate each beat's illustration from its prompt, in the house style, at 16 by 9.
3. Assemble in Higgsfield: one picture per beat, narration over the top, a gentle move on each still (a slow push in, a small parallax), Olga popping in only where the script marks her.
4. Keep the cuts on the beat changes, so a new idea always gets a new picture. That segmenting is what makes it land.

The full pedagogy, the age by age structure from 4 to 16, the reusable script template for every lesson, and the character rules are in the lesson structure plan, which the research feeds. This is the worked example of the format.
