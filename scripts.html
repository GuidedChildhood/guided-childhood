Stage 1 · Ages 4 to 7

The First Device Conversation

Foundation · KS1 · Years 1 to 3

Say this:

1
Say this

"We have a family rule about screens. When the timer goes off, screens go down. Every time, no exceptions. And then we do something together."

2
Not this

"Just five more minutes." Once you move the line, it moves every time.

3
Why it works

Children at this stage cannot regulate device use themselves. The rule is not a punishment, it is the structure that makes screen time safe. Consistent limits now build the self-regulation they will need at 13.

4
Tonight

Set up a charging station in the kitchen or hallway tonight. Devices do not sleep in bedrooms in this house.

Stage 2 · Ages 8 to 10

The Bedroom Rule

Builder · KS2 · Years 4 to 6

Say this:

1
Say this

"Where does your phone sleep at night? In our house, phones sleep in the kitchen. The bedroom is for sleep. That is our rule and it will not change."

2
Not this

"You need to earn the right to have it in your room." That makes it a prize, not a family norm.

3
Why it works

Sleep is the non-negotiable. Blue light, notification sounds, and the temptation to check at 2am are all removed by one consistent rule. Set it now, before they have a personal device, and it is just how things work.

4
Tonight

Have the bedroom rule conversation tonight. Not as a rule you are imposing but as a family decision. Where should our devices sleep?

Stage 3 · Ages 11 to 13 Critical window

The Algorithm Conversation

Explorer · KS3 · Years 7 to 9

Say this:

1
Say this

"Did you know your phone is learning what you like? Every video you watch, every post you pause on, it notes it. Let me show you something. Open your feed and we will look at it together."

2
Not this

"That app is rotting your brain." That shuts the conversation down before it starts.

3
Why it works

Children at 11 to 13 are in the highest-risk window identified by Cambridge MRC research. They are forming identity through comparison, and the algorithm amplifies whatever vulnerability it finds. The parent who can talk about this without alarm is the greatest protective factor.

4
Tonight

Open their feed together tonight. No judgment, just curiosity. Why do you think it showed you that? Stay for 10 minutes.

Stage 4 · Ages 13 to 15

Keeping the Door Open

Shaper · KS4 · Years 10 to 11

Say this:

1
Say this

"If anything ever goes wrong online and things do go wrong, I am the first call you make, not the last. I will not overreact. I will help."

2
Not this

"If I catch you doing that, I am taking your phone." That closes the door before anything happens.

3
Why it works

Teenagers at this stage need to know the door is open before anything goes wrong. A child who fears losing their phone is a child who hides problems. This script keeps the door open.

4
Tonight

Set up a no-agenda weekly 10 minutes. Not about screens, not about homework. Just checking in. Same day, same time.

Stage 5 · Ages 16 and above

Handing Over the Wheel

Independent · KS5 · Years 12 and 13

Say this:

1
Say this

"You are at the age where you get to decide what kind of digital life you build. What does your online presence say about who you are? Not to me. To the world."

2
Not this

"Get off your phone." That conversation is over. A different one has started.

3
Why it works

At 16, the goal is not compliance but genuine digital literacy. A young person who can articulate their own relationship with technology, what it gives them and what it costs them, is genuinely prepared for what comes next.

4
Tonight

Suggest a joint digital audit: go through their online presence together as peers, not parent and child. What does it say? What would they change?
