In 2012, a peer-reviewed journal published this sentence:

"It is likely that social networking will take the place of video games as the next folk devil."

That paper was published 14 years before the UK Social Media Ban.

Here's the pattern.

In the 1990s, politicians called video games "murder simulators" and "digital poison." The American Psychological Association issued policy statements warning of impending harm. Nine US states passed laws banning their sale to minors.

During the same period, youth violence fell to a 30-year low.

By 2011, the US Supreme Court had reviewed the evidence. The majority opinion, 7-2, stated the studies "do not prove that such exposure causes minors to act aggressively." The court added: these studies "have been rejected by every court to consider them, and with good reason."

Every law collapsed. Every restriction failed. And children were no safer.

The behaviour didn't stop. It relocated.

Australia banned social media for under-16s on December 10, 2025. Platforms removed 4.7 million accounts. Within weeks, two-thirds remained on banned platforms through workarounds — on Discord, Telegram, and unmoderated spaces with none of Instagram's safeguarding infrastructure.

The researchers who wrote that 2012 paper also published a moral panic indicator checklist. It maps almost exactly onto how this debate is currently running.

None of this means social media is harmless. Some of the evidence is specific and credible — particularly on sleep disruption and passive scrolling. Those mechanisms are real and targetable.

But there is a difference between targeting the mechanism of harm and banning the medium entirely.

The behavioral research on restriction is sixty years old and consistent. Access bans without skill-building increase risk exposure rather than reduce it. When two-thirds bypass the ban — as Australia measured — they do so in secret, on unmoderated platforms, without parental visibility. The ban doesn't pause digital life. It relocates it to where parents can't see it.

The child who complies hits 16 with no graduated experience and unrestricted access simultaneously. The child who bypasses is already in unregulated spaces without a framework.

Neither outcome is preparation.

The legislation is a signal. The signal is correct — platform design is causing harm and needs to change.

But signals are not solutions.
