THE CLIFF EDGE PROBLEM — 01

Three governments announced social media bans for children this week. None of them have an enforcement body. None of them have agreed what "safe" looks like.

This is not protection. It is the performance of protection.

Canada introduced Bill C-34 on June 10, 2026 — a ban on social media for under-16s that contains 50 key decisions deferred to future regulation. Which platforms are covered. What age verification looks like. What design features make a platform safe enough for exemption. The body that will make those decisions — the Digital Safety Commission — does not exist yet. It will take 18 months to establish after the bill passes. The government plans to fast-track the ban before the Commission exists. Michael Geist, Canada's leading internet law professor, describes it precisely: the government has enacted the promise and deferred the law.

No standards. No privacy review. No enforcement body. An exemption pathway with no criteria.

Geist calls it the toothpaste problem. Verifying children are under 16 requires verifying every adult is over 16 — the entire population submitting identity documents to foreign third-party services. Canada's own government acknowledged this creates "serious privacy risks" for tens of millions not targeted by the policy. That infrastructure, once built, cannot be dismantled. The privacy review that would assess those risks is the one they are fast-tracking past.

The UK followed. Keir Starmer announced "game-changer" restrictions for under-16s. Implementation: late 2026 at the earliest, 2027 more likely. Parliament rejected a blanket ban in February — 307 to 173. What is coming is feature restrictions: infinite scroll, autoplay, stranger-contact algorithms. The right targets. Secondary legislation still needs to be written, consulted on, and passed.

Australia went first. The ban came into force December 10, 2025. Platforms removed 4.7 million accounts. Australia's own eSafety Commissioner then reported two-thirds of the target group remained on banned platforms via workarounds.

Three countries. Three announcements. Three cliff edges. No bridge to the other side.

The exemption illusion is the belief that announcing a ban protects a child tonight. It does not. A deferred regulation does not replace a skill. An enforcement body that does not yet exist does not protect a 14-year-old this evening. Age verification mandated for tens of millions of adults creates privacy risks the government itself has documented — and has not yet solved.

The window before each of these bans arrives is not a waiting period. It is the only preparation time that exists.
