Starmer is announcing restrictions on social media for under-16s this week.

The debate is louder than ever. And almost everyone has the wrong villain.

Here's what the research actually says. And what parents should do right now.

Parliament rejected a blanket ban in February — 307 votes to 173. What's coming is an "Australian-plus" model: age-based access restrictions combined with targeting specific features. Infinite scroll. Autoplay. Stranger-contact algorithms. Overnight curfews.

That second category matters. Because the research has been saying something uncomfortable for years.

Orben and Przybylski (Oxford, n=355,358) found the effect size of social media on adolescent wellbeing: r=0.05. The same as wearing glasses. Swedish longitudinal research — n=4,810, three independent studies — found the real mediators of harm are sleep disruption, social comparison, and passive consumption.

The algorithm is the villain. Not the connection.

When Australia's ban came into force in December 2025, platforms removed 4.7 million accounts. Children drew facial hair to fool age estimation. VPN downloads surged. Australia's eSafety Commissioner reported two-thirds of the target group remained on banned platforms.

You cannot ban a behaviour out of someone who was never taught an alternative.

116,211 people responded to the UK consultation. 9 in 10 parents wanted restrictions. NSPCC's own youth voice — 14 to 17-year-olds — said they do NOT want a complete ban. 42 child-protection charities including NSPCC warned that blanket bans "push teens into darker, unregulated corners of the internet."

Those corners have names: Discord. Telegram. Anonymous forums. Gaming chat rooms. Spaces with zero safeguarding infrastructure.

The UN's Special Rapporteur said it plainly in May 2026: "Simply limiting access to platforms that remain unsafe cannot stand as the endpoint."

So what IS the endpoint?

Lave and Wenger (1991) gave us the answer: people don't learn to navigate risk by being excluded from it. They learn through guided participation, graduated exposure, and deliberate skill-building.

We teach children to cross roads. We don't ban roads.

Feature restrictions are the right direction. They target the design that hijacks developing brains — not the human need to connect. But regulation alone is not education.

Implementation is late 2026 at the earliest. That is a window.

The children most at risk aren't the ones whose parents know this. They're the ones who hit 16 with no digital literacy, no supervised experience, and no framework for navigating what's coming.

That's what Guided Childhood is building.
