In 1993, politicians called video games "murder simulators" and "digital poison." Nine US states passed laws banning their sale to minors.

The US Supreme Court struck them all down in 2011. The majority opinion stated the evidence "does not prove that such exposure causes minors to act aggressively."

During the same period, youth violence in the US fell to a 30-year low.

The fear was real. The mechanism was wrong. The legislation failed.

I'm watching the same structure now.

The fear around social media is real. Some of the evidence is real — the research on sleep disruption, passive scrolling, and upward social comparison is specific and compelling. But the policy response — blanket access bans — has the same shape as the gaming response.

Australia tried it. Platforms removed 4.7 million under-16 accounts. Within weeks, two-thirds of the target group remained on banned platforms through workarounds. Children displaced to Discord, Telegram, and unmoderated spaces — platforms with none of the safeguarding infrastructure of Instagram or TikTok.

Banning children from dangers does not teach them to navigate dangers.

We don't ban teenagers from roads. We teach them to drive.

The one thing the gaming panic got right was a ratings framework — something that gave parents better information, opened the conversation, helped families make decisions. Not a wall. A starting point.

That's the model that works. Preparation, not prohibition.

Children will encounter risk online regardless of what legislation passes. The children who will be safest are not the ones who were restricted longest. They are the ones who were prepared best — who had the vocabulary, the skills, and a parent who knew what questions to ask before the moment arrived.
