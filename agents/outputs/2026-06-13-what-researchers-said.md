There are zero experimental studies testing the effect of a social media ban on the mental health of healthy under-18s.

Parliament received this evidence. They debated a ban anyway.

The Digital Mental Health Group submitted written evidence to the UK Parliament. Their finding is precise: no scientifically published experimental study has tested how complete social media restriction impacts the mental health of children under 18. Not in Australia. Not in France. Not in Norway. Nowhere. The policy being proposed in three countries simultaneously has no experimental evidence base.

Dr. Monika Neff Lind and colleagues published in Frontiers in Developmental Psychology in May 2026: "We cannot ban our way out of a youth mental health crisis." The average benefit from reducing social media use, across the studies that exist, "barely registered." For teenagers specifically, the evidence is even harder to justify.

Dr. Emily Setty researches the harms that come to teenagers on social media. She does not support a ban. Her reason is important: the harms young people experience online are not separate from the harms they face offline. Bullying, body image pressure, racism, exclusion — these predate social media. Digital platforms amplify them. They did not create them. A ban that removes the platform does not remove the dynamic.

Young people, when asked what they want instead of a ban, consistently say the same things: better education, honest conversations, help understanding how algorithmic manipulation works, adults who actually understand their digital lives.

Dr. Harry Dyer at the University of East Anglia calls a social media ban "the wrong tool for the job." It does not hold platforms accountable. It pushes teenagers into riskier, unregulated corners of the internet. It fails to protect the children who need protection most — isolated young people, LGBTQ+ teenagers, those in rural communities for whom online connection is their only connection.

Here is what is happening in September 2026. The UK's RSHE curriculum is being updated — statutory changes that add deepfakes, AI-generated imagery, algorithmic manipulation, digital footprints, and online grooming prevention to what schools must teach.

The right vehicle exists. It is being built. It arrives in classrooms this autumn.

The ban debate happened before the evidence arrived. The curriculum update is the evidence-based response.
