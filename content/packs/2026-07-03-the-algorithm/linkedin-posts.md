## Post 1 · The scientific research post (centrepiece)
Format: text post, line broken for feed reading. Optional: attach the research briefing as a document in a first comment.
Best moment: Tuesday, 8:00am UK.

TikTok's own researchers worked out how long it takes a new user to form a lasting habit.

Roughly 260 videos. Under 35 minutes.

That number did not appear by accident. The science behind it is older than the internet.

1957. Ferster and Skinner show that rewards arriving after an unpredictable number of actions produce the most compulsive, most extinction resistant behaviour ever measured in a lab.

1997. Schultz, Dayan and Montague show in Science that dopamine neurons fire for surprise, not pleasure. Unpredicted rewards light the system up. Expected ones do not. Unpredictability itself is the active ingredient. (The honest term is prediction error. "Dopamine hits" is folk neuroscience.)

2009. BJ Fogg at Stanford publishes his model: behaviour happens when motivation, ability and a well timed trigger converge. A notification is a well timed trigger. Instagram cofounder Mike Krieger was one of his students.

2016. Sherman's fMRI work shows teenagers viewing their own photos with lots of likes light up the brain's reward hub, and they like things simply because they are already popular. Machinery tuned on adults landed on adolescents.

The build history is just as dated. 1994, the first automated collaborative filtering. 2006, Facebook's ranked News Feed. 2009, the Like button. 2012, YouTube switches its target from clicks to watch time. 2018, TikTok's For You page. A feed built on your behaviour, not your friends.

Then the builders started talking. Sean Parker: "exploiting a vulnerability in human psychology... God only knows what it's doing to our children's brains." Aza Raskin called his own infinite scroll "behavioural cocaine."

Now the part most posts leave out.

The population level evidence that this machinery caused the teen mental health crisis is weak. Technology use explains at most 0.4 percent of the variance in adolescent wellbeing. Switching people to chronological feeds cut their time on platform and did not improve their mood.

What is strong is narrower and worse. Audits found the feeds of teens showing distress fill with harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate. And in 2022 a UK coroner ruled that algorithmically recommended content contributed to the death of 14 year old Molly Russell.

So the honest claim is this. The design exploits attention by intent. The average child is probably coping. The struggling child's feed makes struggling worse.

Tonight, the single move with the best evidence behind it: the phone charges outside the bedroom. Sleep is the most measurable and most reversible pathway, and half of teen girls say social media costs them sleep.

Not panic. Not a shrug. A pathway.

## Post 2 · The $270 email
Format: short text post, no image.
Best moment: Thursday, 8:00am UK, same week as Post 1.

Somewhere inside Meta, someone put a value on a 13 year old.

Roughly $270 of lifetime revenue. The figure comes from an internal email, quoted in the 2023 lawsuit brought by 42 US attorneys general.

It is not an outlier. A peer reviewed model puts US ad revenue from under 18s at about $11 billion in 2022. For Snapchat that was 41 percent of US ad revenue. For TikTok, 35 percent.

Herbert Simon saw this coming in 1971: a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention. Attention became the product. Children hold a lot of it.

Which explains the feed. In a landmark Science experiment, users switched to a simple chronological feed spent substantially less time on platform. Time is revenue. So no platform volunteers a chronological feed. The engagement ranked feed is not a technical choice. It is the business model.

I am not asking you to be outraged. I am asking you to stop expecting the platform to fix it.

You cannot therapy your way out of an incentive. You can understand the incentive, and plan around it, at your own kitchen table.

Claims: $270 A13 · $11bn A14 · Simon A11 · feed experiment A15

## Post 4 · Sadtok: the parent script
Format: 60 second face video, single take, captioned; post the script text in the caption for sound off viewers.
Best moment: Sunday, 7:00pm UK.

Script (about 155 words spoken):

(0 to 8s) Kids have a name for it. Sadtok. One low evening, one sad video watched to the end, and the whole feed turns grey for weeks.

(8 to 20s) That is not your child's imagination. Researchers found a single stray search can dominate a feed for weeks, whatever buttons you press. And audits found feeds of teens showing distress fill with harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate.

(20 to 40s) So here is the script. Ask your child: "What does your feed think of you at the moment?" Not what do you watch. What does it think of you. Then do a feed reset together. Sit side by side, mark not interested, search the things they actually love, and let the machine relearn them.

(40 to 55s) Two more moves. The phone sleeps outside the bedroom. And aim for less, not zero. A randomised trial found cutting back to an hour a day helped distressed young people feel better about their appearance. Reduction works. Abstinence is not required.

(55 to 60s) You are not fighting your child. You are both up against a machine. Say that out loud. It changes the conversation.

## Post 5 · What schools should teach instead
Format: text post.
Best moment: Monday, 8:00am UK.

Regulation has quietly changed its question. Schools should too.

For a decade the question was "how much screen time". The regulators have stopped asking it. The American Academy of Pediatrics now says the digital ecosystem "can no longer be thought of as screen time for families to manage". The question has become: what does the feed do?

The evidence behind the shift is broad. WHO data across 44 countries shows 11 percent of adolescents now meet criteria for problematic use, up from 7 percent in 2018.

And the law has moved. Since July 2025, Ofcom's children's codes legally require recommender systems to filter suicide, self harm and eating disorder content out of children's feeds. The EU has preliminarily found TikTok's addictive design in breach of the DSA. Preliminary, so hold it lightly. But the EU already forced TikTok Lite's rewards scheme off the market, which is the first proof that regulating design, not content, actually moves a platform.

Meanwhile, most PSHE lessons still teach children that the feed is curated.

They know. 63 percent of UK 8 to 17s know platforms use algorithms to direct content at them. Among 16 to 17s it is 81 percent. A lesson announcing what four in five of the room already know is dead on arrival.

What the evidence says to teach instead:

The business model. Attention is revenue, and their attention is the product.
The mechanics. Why one stray search can steer a feed for weeks, and why the buttons feel dead.
The exits. Feed resets, turning off autoplay, and the sleep boundary.

The frame that lands with adolescents is autonomy, not protection. The platforms are engineered to hold you. Seeing the machinery is power.

One more sign of where this is heading. NICE guidance now instructs clinicians to ask about the feed as part of self harm assessment. Health has moved from time to design. The classroom is next.
