Last week one of mine was in the passenger seat, scrolling, and said, mostly to the window: "Why does it keep showing me this stuff? I don't even like it."

I started to say "just tell it you're not interested" and stopped, because I realised I did not actually know if that worked. So I went and did the homework. This issue is what I found: where the feed came from, the science underneath it, and the part almost nobody writes down because it is less dramatic than the headlines.

## Where the feed came from

The recommendation algorithm was not discovered. It was assembled, step by dated step.

1994. Researchers build one of the first automated collaborative filtering systems, sorting Usenet posts by what similar people liked. Amazon industrialises the idea and publishes the method in 2003.

5 September 2006. Facebook launches the algorithmic News Feed. Hundreds of thousands of users protest within days. Then they stay, because engagement climbs. The lesson every platform learned that week: people protest the ranked feed and then use it more.

9 February 2009. The Like button ships. By 2013, the simple human readable ranking formula is gone, replaced by machine learning weighing roughly 100,000 factors.

October 2012. YouTube switches its target from clicks to watch time. Not what you choose. How long you stay.

2018. TikTok's For You page completes the arc. A feed built on your behaviour, not your friends. You do not even have to follow anyone. You just have to hesitate.

Then the builders started confessing. Sean Parker, Facebook's first president, in 2017: "exploiting a vulnerability in human psychology... we did it anyway... God only knows what it's doing to our children's brains." Justin Rosenstein, who built the Like button, called likes "bright dings of pseudo pleasure". Aza Raskin, who invented infinite scroll, called it "behavioural cocaine".

And the money confirms the aim. An internal Meta email, quoted in the 2023 lawsuit brought by 42 US attorneys general, values a 13 year old at roughly $270 of lifetime revenue. A peer reviewed model puts US ad revenue from under 18s at about $11 billion a year. When researchers switched people to simple chronological feeds in a landmark Science experiment, time on platform fell substantially. Chronological feeds lose money. That is why you have never been offered one by default.

## The science underneath it

None of this needed new psychology. It needed old psychology, shipped.

In 1957, Ferster and Skinner showed that rewards arriving after an unpredictable number of actions produce the most compulsive, most extinction resistant behaviour ever measured in a lab. Pull to refresh is that finding with a glass screen on it.

In 1997, Schultz, Dayan and Montague showed that dopamine neurons fire for surprise, not pleasure. Predicted rewards do nothing. Unpredicted ones light the system up. The honest term is prediction error. Every time you hear "dopamine hit", someone is selling you folk neuroscience, but the underlying mechanism is real and foundational.

In 2009, BJ Fogg at Stanford published the model that turned this into a design manual: behaviour happens when motivation, ability and a well timed trigger converge. A notification is a well timed trigger. Instagram cofounder Mike Krieger came through his lab.

And in 2016, Sherman's brain imaging work showed why this lands hardest on teenagers: viewing their own photos with lots of likes lit up the brain's reward hub, and they liked things simply because those things were already popular. Peer approval sensitivity peaks in adolescence while the brain's control systems are still maturing. There may be sensitivity windows around 11 to 13 for girls and 14 to 15 for boys. All of this machinery was tuned on adult data. Children inherited it uncalibrated.

## The honest part

Now the section that most newsletters in this space skip.

The population level evidence that algorithms caused the teen mental health crisis is weak. Across three huge datasets, technology use explains at most 0.4 percent of the variance in adolescent wellbeing. Paying people to deactivate Facebook or Instagram for six weeks improved mood by amounts so small you would never notice them in one person. The chronological feed experiment cut screen time and improved nobody's wellbeing. Even the famous "1 in 3 teen girls" statistic from the Facebook Files is a misread: the internal slide said Instagram made body image worse for 1 in 3 of the girls who already felt bad about their bodies, a subgroup, and within that same subgroup 22 percent said it made them feel better.

So why write any of this? Because the strong evidence is narrower and darker. In 2022 a UK coroner ruled that algorithmically recommended content contributed to the death of 14 year old Molly Russell. It remains the only finding in this field with legal weight. Independent audits keep finding the same pattern: the feed of a teenager showing distress fills with harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate, within hours. And 60 percent of young people with depressive symptoms say feed content makes them feel worse about their looks, against 25 percent of those without.

Hold both truths at once. The design exploits attention, on purpose, by its builders' own admission. The average child is probably coping. And the child who is already struggling has a feed that makes struggling worse. A depressed 14 year old's engagement is a symptom, not a preference.

## Tonight

One conversation and one boundary.

The conversation: ask your child, "What does your feed think of you at the moment?" It is a better question than "what do you watch", because it puts you both on the same side, looking at the machine. If the answer worries either of you, reset the feed together, side by side.

The boundary: the phone charges outside the bedroom. Sleep is the most measurable and most reversible pathway in all of this research, and half of teen girls say social media costs them sleep. And aim for less, not zero. A randomised trial found that cutting back to an hour a day helped distressed young people feel better about their appearance. Reduction works. Abstinence is not required.

Your child already knows the feed is engineered. 63 percent of 8 to 17s know, and by 16 it is 81 percent. What they do not have is control. The gap between knowing and steering is exactly where a parent fits.
