Last week one of mine was in the passenger seat, scrolling, and said, mostly to the window: "Why does it keep showing me this stuff? I don't even like it."

I started to say "just tell it you're not interested" and stopped, because I did not actually know if that worked. So I did the homework. This issue is what I found: where the feed came from, what the best researchers actually say about it, and the part that surprised me most, which is how much of this a family can steer.

## Where the feed came from

The recommendation algorithm was not discovered. It was assembled, step by dated step.

1994, researchers build one of the first automated collaborative filtering systems. 5 September 2006, Facebook launches the ranked News Feed. 9 February 2009, the Like button ships. October 2012, YouTube switches its target from clicks to watch time. 2018, TikTok's For You page completes the arc: a feed built on your behaviour, not your friends.

Then the builders started confessing. Sean Parker, Facebook's first president: "exploiting a vulnerability in human psychology... God only knows what it's doing to our children's brains." Aza Raskin called his own infinite scroll "behavioural cocaine".

And the money confirms the aim. An internal Meta email, quoted in the 2023 lawsuit brought by 42 US attorneys general, values a 13 year old at roughly $270 of lifetime revenue. When researchers switched people to simple chronological feeds in a landmark Science experiment, time on platform fell substantially. Chronological feeds lose money. That is why you have never been offered one by default.

None of it needed new psychology. Skinner showed in 1957 that unpredictable rewards produce the most persistent behaviour ever measured in a lab. Schultz showed in 1997 that dopamine neurons fire for surprise, not pleasure. Sherman showed in 2016 that a teenager's reward system lights up for like counts on their own photos. Old science, shipped.

If I stopped there, you would be frightened. Most newsletters stop there. Here is the rest.

## What the careful researchers actually find

There is a group of scientists who have spent a decade testing the scary story against very large datasets: Candice Odgers, Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski, Matti Vuorre, Chris Ferguson. Their work is the spine of how we think about all of this.

Orben and Przybylski, across 355,000 adolescents: technology use explains at most 0.4 percent of the difference in young people's wellbeing. Vuorre and Przybylski tracked 946,798 people across 72 countries as Facebook arrived and spread: no evidence wellbeing fell, and the tilt, if anything, was slightly positive. That study skews adult and is correlational, so hold it loosely, but it is the largest test we have. Przybylski and Weinstein's study of 120,115 English teenagers found moderate use sat alongside the highest wellbeing, with declines only past roughly two hours on a school day. Ferguson's meta analyses put pooled effects below the level researchers call practically meaningful. His numbers are contested by the other camp, so treat him as one credentialed voice among several, but the direction matches. And Odgers, reviewing the famous "rewiring childhood" claim in Nature, called it not supported by science, and warned that blaming social media distracts from what actually drives the crisis in the hardest hit children: economic hardship, discrimination, and support that never arrives.

So why not shrug? Because the average is small for a reason, and the reason matters.

Harm concentrates. Adolescence has sensitivity windows, around 11 to 13 for girls and 14 to 15 for boys. And a feed cannot tell distress from appetite. A struggling child lingers on dark content, the machine reads lingering as interest, and audits keep finding the same result: the feeds of teens showing distress fill with harmful content at roughly ten times the normal rate. In 2022 a UK coroner ruled that algorithmically recommended content contributed to the death of 14 year old Molly Russell. Hold both truths at once. Most children are probably coping. The child already struggling has a feed that makes struggling worse. That is a design problem, and design problems have design fixes.

There is a genuine upside too, and honesty requires it. In a 2024 US survey of 14 to 22 year olds, 74 percent of LGBTQ+ young people said social media matters for feeling less alone, against 52 percent of their peers. Young people with depressive symptoms said the same, 66 against 48 percent. The same report shows those groups also meet more harm, which is the concentration problem again. The connection is real. So is the exposure. Remove the platform and you remove both.

## Living with it

Here is the bit that changed how I parent this. The algorithm is not one thing. Your child's phone runs four different machines.

TikTok learns from behaviour, and its own engineering paper shows the model retrains on you within minutes. That cuts both ways. It is why one sad evening can turn a feed grey. It is also why TikTok is the easiest feed to retrain: there is a full reset button in settings, and after a reset, changed watching visibly reshapes the feed within a session or two. Do it side by side.

Instagram ranks on relationships as well as behaviour. Stories shows the people your child interacts with most. So the useful conversation is who, not what.

YouTube optimises watch time, and the structural lever is autoplay. In a supervised account a parent can switch autoplay off so it stays off.

Snapchat barely has a content algorithm for friends at all. It ranks on how often you talk. The mechanic to watch is streaks, which run on obligation. A streak is a tap, not a friendship, and saying that out loud helps.

Then the two moves with the best evidence anywhere in this field. The phone charges outside the bedroom, because sleep is the most measurable and most reversible pathway, and half of teen girls say social media costs them sleep. And aim for less, not zero: a randomised trial found capping social media at an hour a day helped distressed young people feel better about their appearance. Reduction works. Abstinence is not required.

And one question. Ask your child: "What does your feed think of you at the moment?" Not what do you watch. What does it think of you. It puts you both on the same side of the table, looking at the machine together.

## The long view

Amy Orben has traced the same panic cycle through novels, radio, comics, television and video games. Every generation, the fear arrives before the evidence, and the panic eats the energy that should have gone into teaching. Our children will spend their whole lives inside recommendation systems. Much of that world is worth having. What we can give them is the machinery made visible: the business model, the mechanics, the exits, platform by platform.

That is the pathway we build, age by age. If you want the scripts for it, the starter pack is at guidedchildhood.com.
