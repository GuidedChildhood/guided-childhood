## MUMSNET POST
Board: Parenting. Post as a regular parent account. No links, no brand mention, ever.

**Title: DD's TikTok feed had turned really bleak. Sharing what actually helped in case useful**

Noticed a few weeks ago that whenever DD (13) showed me something funny on TikTok, everything around it was sad stuff. Lonely girl videos, "nobody would notice if I disappeared" type things. She said the feed had been like that for ages and she hated it but could not get rid of it. Pressing not interested did nothing, apparently.

I did some reading rather than confiscating, mostly because confiscating has never once worked in this house.

Things I did not know that other people might not either. TikTok's system updates on your behaviour really quickly, within minutes apparently, so one bad evening of watching sad videos to the end can tip the whole feed. Which sounds awful but the flip side is it can be retrained quickly too. There is an actual reset button buried in settings (search "refresh your For You feed"). We did it together on the sofa, then she spent ten minutes deliberately watching and liking the stuff she actually loves (horses and baking, since you ask) and skipping fast past anything grim. Feed was noticeably different within a couple of days.

Also learned the other apps work completely differently. Snapchat is basically ranked by who you message, not what you watch, so the sad feed problem is mostly a TikTok and Instagram thing. And on YouTube it is autoplay doing the heavy lifting, so we turned that off.

Not claiming any of this is a miracle cure. She still rolls her eyes at me. But doing the reset together went down so much better than any rule I have ever imposed, because it was us against the app rather than me against her. And she now says "my feed thinks I'm sad again" as an actual sentence, which feels like progress of some sort.

Has anyone else done the feed reset thing? And what do people do about Snapchat streaks, because that is the next battle and I can see it is about not letting friends down rather than the app itself.

## REDDIT POST
Subreddit: r/ScienceBasedParenting. Flair: Question/Discussion or Sharing research, per current sub rules. Zero promotion.

**Title: What the evidence actually says about recommendation algorithms and teen wellbeing: small average effects, concentrated risks, and real mechanistic differences between platforms**

Disclosure: UK parent. I work in parenting education. Nothing in this post links to or names anything I am involved with. Posting because I want this picture stress tested.

I have spent several months in the primary literature on feeds and adolescent mental health. My reading is that the population story and the individual story are different, and that the platforms differ enough mechanistically that "the algorithm" is not a useful unit of analysis. Every claim below carries its source. Please attack anything that looks wrong.

**1. Population level associations are small.**

Orben and Przybylski, 2019, Nature Human Behaviour (specification curve analysis, n = 355,358 across three datasets): digital technology use explains at most 0.4 percent of variance in adolescent wellbeing. Vuorre and Przybylski, 2023, Royal Society Open Science (n = 946,798, 72 countries, 2008 to 2019): no evidence that Facebook's spread was associated with reduced wellbeing; point estimates slightly positive. Caveats: adult skewed sample, correlational. Przybylski and Weinstein, 2017, Psychological Science (n = 120,115 English 15 year olds): quadratic relationship, moderate use associated with the highest wellbeing, declines past inflection points (about 1h57m weekday smartphone use). Descriptive, not a prescriptive threshold. Ferguson, 2021 meta analysis (33 studies): pooled effects on youth depression and anxiety below r = .10, his stated threshold for practical significance. Important caveat: Ferguson's methods are actively contested by Haidt and colleagues, so weight him as one credentialed voice, not a settled verdict. For the earlier moral panic parallel: Ferguson, 2015 (101 studies, video games and aggression, r = .06) and Orben, 2020, on the recurring cycle of technology panics.

**2. Risks appear concentrated, not uniform.**

Orben et al., 2022, Nature Communications (n = 17,409): developmental windows of sensitivity, roughly ages 11 to 13 for girls and 14 to 15 for boys. Amnesty International, 2023, and Center for Countering Digital Hate, 2022: simulated accounts signalling distress received harmful content at roughly ten times control rates, sometimes within minutes. Caveat: NGO audits, simulated accounts, not peer reviewed, though findings replicate across organisations and years. Molly Russell inquest, North London Coroner's Court, 2022: coroner concluded algorithmically recommended content contributed to her death. The only finding in this literature with legal weight. Odgers, 2024, Nature review: the "rewiring" framing is not supported; population trends track economic hardship, the opioid crisis and discrimination in the most affected groups. The same concentration cuts the other way: Common Sense Media and Hopelab, 2024 (NORC, n = 1,274, ages 14 to 22): 74 percent of LGBTQ+ youth versus 52 percent of straight and cisgender peers say social media is important for feeling less alone; 66 percent of youth with depressive symptoms versus 48 percent without say the same. The same report finds those groups also report more harms. Both findings are in the same population.

**3. Platforms differ mechanistically, which matters for any intervention.**

TikTok: leaked internal document (New York Times, 2021, authenticity confirmed) states optimisation for retention and time spent. ByteDance's Monolith paper (arXiv, 2022) describes online retraining on user behaviour within minutes. Wall Street Journal bot experiment, 2021: interests inferred within two hours, some in about 40 minutes, from dwell time alone. Instagram: per Mosseri's official explainers, separate ranking systems per surface; Stories ranks on relationship signals, Reels on watch probability, likes per reach and sends per reach. Multiplier figures circulating online are secondary reporting; I am using the ranked signal lists only. YouTube: Covington et al., 2016 (RecSys) describes expected watch time optimisation; Goodrow, 2021, describes the shift to "valued watch time" via satisfaction surveys. Structurally, autoplay is the default session extender; supervised accounts allow a parent side lock. Snapchat: Snap's WSDM 2022 paper describes friend Story ranking on communication frequency and recency, a friendship graph rather than a content graph; only Spotlight is interest ranked. Snapstreaks show peer reviewed associations with problematic use and fear of missing out, though Snap publishes contrary wellbeing research, so I hold that as association only.

**4. Interventions tested so far.**

Thai et al., 2023, Psychology of Popular Media (RCT, n = 220, ages 17 to 25): capping use at one hour daily for three weeks improved appearance and weight esteem versus controls. Reduction, not abstinence. SMART Schools study, Lancet Regional Health Europe, 2025 (1,227 pupils, 30 English schools): no wellbeing difference between restrictive and permissive school phone policies, though total phone time correlated with worse outcomes. I read this as "school bans alone are insufficient" rather than "phones are harmless". Roozenbeek et al., 2022, Science Advances (inoculation RCTs, n = 6,464 lab plus 22,632 field): short videos teaching manipulation techniques improved detection by about 5 percentage points at roughly $0.05 per view. Caveats: adult samples, and the outcome is recognition, not wellbeing. Promising, not proven. Regulatory context: Ofcom, 2025: harmful content reached secondary age UK children within four weeks at platform specific rates (TikTok 53 percent, YouTube 36 percent, Instagram 34 percent, Facebook 31 percent); the children's codes require recommender filtering from 25 July 2025. Instagram Teen Accounts: independent testing by Fairplay and the Molly Rose Foundation (47 features) rated 64 percent ineffective and 17 percent working as promised; advocacy organisations, so weight accordingly.

**My synthesis, which is what I want challenged:** average effects are small, risks concentrate in identifiable vulnerable groups partly because engagement systems read distress signals as interest, benefits also concentrate in some of the same groups, and the platforms are mechanistically different enough that both parenting advice and policy should be platform specific. The policy implications I draw are design regulation plus algorithm literacy rather than blanket restriction.

Where is this wrong? I am particularly interested in pushback on how much weight Ferguson deserves given the methodological disputes, and on whether the NGO amplification audits survive scrutiny.

## FACEBOOK PARENT GROUP POST
Post from Justin's personal profile into local and school parent groups. No link, no page tag.

A question that changed things in our house.

Not "what are you watching". This instead: "what does your feed think of you at the moment?"

My kids found it funny at first. Then genuinely interesting. Because that is what a feed is. A guess about who you are. Sometimes the guess is wrong. Sometimes it is unkind.

Here is the bit most parents do not know. On TikTok there is a full feed reset button in the settings. And the app relearns so fast that if you reset it and then watch different things, the feed changes within a day or two.

We did ours side by side on the sofa. No lecture. No confiscation. Just the two of us teaching a machine who she actually is.

Have you ever looked at your child's feed together? What did it think of them?
