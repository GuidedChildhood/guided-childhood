## 1. NEWSLETTER SECTION (long read)

**Subject line options**
1. Someone I trust sent me a reason to distrust the science
2. Follow the money, on everyone
3. What do we do with experts, once we know who funds them?

**Preview line:** A thoughtful message arrived this week, making the case that the scientists I cite are compromised by tech money. I checked all of it. The honest answer is more useful than either side wants it to be.

**Body:**

A message arrived this week from someone in my network, someone who spends their working life with children, and it was not a troll. It was sincere, warm, and it made a serious case: the researchers I lean on, the ones who argue the population level harm from social media is smaller than the headlines claim, are funded by or connected to the technology industry, and so their work should not be trusted.

It is a fair challenge, and it deserves a real answer rather than a defensive one. So I did what I always try to do. I checked every claim against the original sources.

Some of it held up. There are real connections, and they are worth naming. One foundation that funds one of these researchers does invest in education technology. One of the Oxford researchers has advised panels at Google and OpenAI, and previously advised a programme funded by a large corporation. None of this is hidden. Every one of them discloses it in the ordinary places, in their papers and on their university pages. The person who wrote to me was right that the connections exist, and right that transparency matters.

Some of it did not hold up. A researcher described as a tech institute's research director turned out to hold that title at her public university, not the institute, where her role is an unpaid advisory seat. A claimed tech funding link ran to a completely separate programme at a shared institution, not to her or her research. An app held up as proof of a commercial interest in screens turned out to be a clinical trial of a therapy for depressed teenagers. When you ask for accuracy, you have to lead with it, so I say that plainly.

But here is the thing that matters more than any single item, and it is the reason I am writing this to you rather than just replying privately.

The follow the money test cuts every way at once.

The most famous voice arguing that phones are rewiring childhood has sold more than two million copies of the book making that case, and commands speaking fees in the tens to low hundreds of thousands. The company that sells schools the pouches to lock phones away took millions in public contracts last year. I am not accusing any of them of bad faith. That is exactly the point. Money and interest sit on all sides of this argument. Mine included. I sell a pathway. If a financial interest disqualified a person's view, the bestseller and the pouch industry would be disqualified before any academic.

Which leaves the real question, the one my correspondent was circling and the one I think is genuinely useful for all of us. If everyone has an interest, how do you decide who to believe?

Not by the funder. By the method. Here is the test I actually use, and I would offer it to you for any expert, on any side, including me.

First, is it disclosed. A hidden tie is a warning. A declared one is just information.

Second, can a hostile stranger check the work. The findings I rely on run on public datasets anyone can download, with the analysis plan registered before the results were seen, and the code published. Their fiercest critics pull the same data and argue the opposite in public. That is not a weakness. That is the strongest defence there is, because a result an enemy can reanalyse line by line is the hardest kind to quietly buy.

Third, does the direction even make sense. An education technology funder is not served by the conclusion that screens barely move the average child. One of these researchers has spent years publicly fighting the platforms to force them to release their data. That is not what capture looks like.

Fourth, and most important, integrate rather than pick a tribe. Because the person who wrote to me is seeing something true. They witness harm in children, and I believe them. Nothing in the research contradicts it. The finding is not that no child is harmed. It is that across a whole population the average effect is small, because the average blends a large majority who are fine with a smaller group who are genuinely hurt. A caseload of struggling children is not the average. It is the tail, the concentrated harm the researchers themselves say is real and say we should target. The clinician and the statistician are describing the same reality from opposite ends of the same distribution.

So what do we do with experts. We ask all of them to show their funding, and we treat that as the beginning of the conversation, not the end of it. We judge them by whether their work is open, registered and replicable, not by whose logo is on the grant. We notice when someone bites the hand that could feed them. And we stop forcing a choice between the parent who sees harm and the scientist who measures its shape, because on this the two are not enemies. They are witnesses to the same thing.

That is the whole of what I try to build here. Not a side. A way of reading the evidence that survives being checked, including when someone kind and serious checks me.

Talk soon,
Justin

### Author first comment
To be clear, I am grateful for messages like this, not annoyed by them. The person who wrote to me is doing exactly what we should all do, asking who funds the science. My only push back is that the test has to be applied to everyone, or it stops being a principle and becomes a weapon you point at the side you already dislike. Applied evenly, funding does not tell you who is right. Method does. That is a harder habit, and a much more useful one.
