Hi Denise,

Thank you for this, genuinely. Twenty five years supporting social skill development is a body of experience I take seriously, and I would rather have people like you pushing on my sources than nodding along with me. So I did the thing I try to do with any claim, including my own: I checked all of it against the primary sources. Here is what I found, honestly, including where you are right.

Start with the parts that hold up, because they do. The funding ties are real and worth naming out loud. The Jacobs Foundation does run an edtech investment programme, it does put money into edtech venture funds, and it does fund Amy Orben's group. Andrew Przybylski did advise Ithra's Sync programme, and in 2025 he advised expert councils at Google and at OpenAI. You are right that these connections exist. You are also right that transparency matters, and I am not going to pretend any of it away.

A few of the specifics did not survive the sources, though, and I think you would want to know rather than repeat them.

Candice Odgers is not the research director of the Child Mind Institute. That is her University of California title. Her actual link to Child Mind is a seat on an external advisory council, unpaid. When I looked at who Child Mind's corporate partners actually are, I found a department store and a jewellery brand, not a single major tech company. If you have a source showing otherwise I would genuinely like to see it. CIFAR is real and she co directs one of its programmes, but the Meta money you are thinking of went to a different CIFAR programme, its machine learning one, not the child development programme she works in, and not to her research. And Kandoo is a clinical trial of a depression therapy app for struggling teenagers, not a product built to get children using more tech.

I am not pointing that out to score a point. I mention it because if we are the ones asking for accuracy, we have to be accurate first, or we hand the other side an easy way to wave us off.

Here is the harder thing, and I say it as someone standing on your side of caring about this. The follow the money argument cuts both ways, completely. The most famous voice arguing that social media is destroying childhood has sold more than two million books making exactly that case, and speaks for fees in the tens to low hundreds of thousands. The pouch company that schools buy their phone bans from took millions in public contracts last year. I am not accusing any of them of bad faith. That is the point. Money and interest sit on every side of this, mine included. Which is exactly why we cannot settle who is right by looking at who funds them. If we could, the bestseller and the pouch industry would be first out the door.

So how do you actually tell? Not by the funder. By the method. And this is the part that genuinely reassures me about these three. Their headline findings do not run on secret company data. They run on public datasets anyone can download, with the analysis plan locked in before they see the results, and the code published for anyone to check. Their sharpest critics, Jean Twenge among them, pull the very same data and argue the opposite in public. A finding a hostile expert can re run line by line is the hardest kind to quietly buy. And the direction does not even make sense as capture: an edtech funder is not served by the conclusion that screens barely move the average child, and Przybylski has spent years fighting the platforms in public to force them to release their data. That is not what a bought researcher does.

And now the part that matters most, because I think you are seeing something true.

You witness harm every day, and I believe you. Nothing here contradicts you. The research finding is not that no child is harmed. It is that across the whole population the average effect is small, on the order of half a percent of the variation in wellbeing. That average is small precisely because it blends a large majority of children who are basically fine with a smaller group who are genuinely hurt. Your caseload is not the average. By its very nature it is the tail: the children referred to you because they are struggling. You are looking straight at the concentrated harm the researchers themselves say is real and say we should be targeting. Odgers has written that offline vulnerabilities mirror and amplify online ones. Orben has mapped the exact developmental windows where the risk spikes. They are not denying your children. They are describing the whole distribution, while you spend your days with the part of it that needs you most.

So we are not really on opposite sides. You are right that the harm is real. They are right that it is not universal. And the useful conclusion lives inside both of those at once: find and protect the vulnerable children precisely, instead of either waving away what you see or telling every parent their child's brain is being rewired.

Please do send the rest of what you have. I mean it. I will check it the same way I checked this, and if something in it overturns what I found, I will say so in public and change my position. That is the whole of what I am trying to build.
