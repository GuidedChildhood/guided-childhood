## THE FULL ARTICLE (Substack / research page)

### Subject line options
1. The kids are alright, and we owe them the truth about it
2. They are capable and struggling, and both are true
3. What we get wrong when we tell a generation it is broken

**Preview line:** On almost every measure that matters, young people are doing better than we did. Here is the honest version, including the one exception that is real.

### The piece

I want to tell you the most under reported story about young people, and then be honest about the part that is genuinely going wrong, because the second part is where we keep aiming badly.

Start with the good news, because there is a mountain of it and almost nobody says it out loud.

In England, the under 18 conception rate has fallen by about 66 percent since 2007, to the lowest level since records began (Office for National Statistics). The number of children entering the justice system for the first time has collapsed by roughly 93 percent since 2007, from over a hundred thousand a year to around eight thousand (Youth Justice Board). Teenage smoking has almost disappeared, just 11 percent of pupils have ever tried a cigarette, down from 22 percent who were regular smokers in the mid 1990s (NHS England). Underage drinking and drug use are down too, and more young people stay in education and go further than any cohort before them.

It is not just here. In the United States, violent victimisation of 12 to 17 year olds has fallen 85 percent since 1993 (Bureau of Justice Statistics). The teen birth rate is down about 79 percent from its 1991 peak (CDC). In 2024, a record two thirds of American high school seniors reported using no alcohol, cannabis or nicotine in the past month (Monitoring the Future). This is the least violent, least pregnant, most sober, most educated generation of young people in the recorded history of both countries.

We do not say this, because it does not fit the story we have chosen. And the story matters, so let me be careful with it.

### Two things people get wrong, in the hopeful direction

If you are going to argue the kids are alright, you have to be honest about the bits that do not hold, or you deserve to be ignored.

The first is the claim that each generation tests a little smarter. For most of the twentieth century that was true, the Flynn effect, roughly three IQ points a decade. But it stalled and then reversed across much of the developed world from the mid 1990s, in Norway, the UK, Denmark, Finland and France. The strongest study found the decline even within families, younger brothers scoring lower than older ones, which rules out the easy explanations (Bratsberg and Rogeberg, 2018). So we should drop that line. It is no longer true.

The second is that young people are more empathetic. This is a genuine landmine, because the famous finding is the opposite, empathy among US students fell by about 40 percent between 1979 and 2009 (Konrath and colleagues, 2011). But there is a twist that is actually the better story. The same researchers found that the decline reversed from around 2009, and empathy has been climbing back in exactly the cohort we now call young. So the honest version is not a smooth rise, it is a fall and a recovery.

Being precise about this is not pedantry. It is the whole game. The moment you overclaim the good news, the people who want to believe in a broken generation get to dismiss all of it.

### The exception that is real

Now the part that is genuinely going wrong, and it is serious.

Since around 2008, reported anxiety and depression among young people have risen sharply. In England, the share of children with a probable mental health disorder went from about one in nine in 2017 to roughly one in five by 2023, and among 17 to 19 year olds it roughly doubled to about one in four (NHS England). In the United States, the share of teenage girls reporting persistent sadness or hopelessness reached 57 percent in 2021, and the suicide rate for 10 to 24 year olds rose 62 percent between 2007 and 2021 (CDC).

I will add the honest nuance an expert would demand, because it is how you earn trust. In the UK the suicide rate for young people has not shown the same clear sustained rise as the US, and part of the recent fall in self harm hospital admissions is a change in how they are recorded rather than a real improvement. The distress and the diagnoses are clearly up. The bleakest end of the picture is more mixed here than the headlines suggest.

So the honest line is not they are fine, and it is not they are broken. It is that they are capable and struggling at the same time, and both are true. Holding both is the entire job.

### The part that should change what we do

Here is a finding that ought to reshape the whole conversation. There is a serious hypothesis, with growing evidence behind it, that the way we talk about young people's mental health can itself make things worse for some of them. It is called prevalence inflation (Foulkes and Andrews, 2023). Awareness campaigns do two things at once. They help people recognise real problems, which is good. And they can nudge some young people to interpret ordinary distress as disorder, and to see themselves as fragile, which can become self fulfilling.

I want to be careful, because this can be misused to dismiss real suffering, and I am not doing that. But the direction is clear enough to act on. Set high expectations for this generation and they tend to meet them. Tell them, constantly, that they are anxious and broken, and we should not be surprised when more of them come to believe it.

### So what do we actually do next

If the good news is real and the exception is real, the response almost writes itself, and it is not a ban and it is not a panic.

**Aim at the real drivers, not the visible one.** The strongest predictors of a child's mental health are not screen time. They are the things in the background, the mental health of the adults around them, poverty, and adverse experiences at home. When a parent's depression is treated and lifts, their child's symptoms measurably improve (STAR*D-Child). Fund that. A better adult mental health and family support system is a children's mental health policy.

**Protect the one relationship that does the most.** One steady, trusted adult more than halves the risk for a child who has had a hard start, and it does not have to be the parent (Public Health Wales). That is a thing schools, coaches and grandparents can act on today.

**Change the story we tell them.** Stop describing a whole generation as broken. Name their competence out loud, the falling violence, the record sobriety, the recovering empathy, and give them problems to solve rather than a label to live down to.

**Build capability, not walls.** Teach children to read the machine, stage by stage, so that by sixteen they meet the full digital world clear eyed instead of falling into it, and rebuild the real world richness that a screen has been quietly replacing.

**Invest where belonging is built.** In the international evidence, raising a child's sense of belonging at school improved their life satisfaction several times more than reducing their social media use. Belonging is the lever, and it is buildable.

They are not a lost generation. By most measures they are outperforming the people worrying about them. The kindest and most effective thing we can do is tell them the truth about that, and then aim our help where the evidence actually points.
