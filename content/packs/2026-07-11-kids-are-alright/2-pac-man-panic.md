## THE FEED POST (expanded, verified)

In 1981 a British MP stood up in the House of Commons to warn that Space Invaders machines were leading children into truancy, theft and vice. He introduced a bill to control them. It lost by just 114 votes to 94.

A year later the US Surgeon General called video games possibly hazardous to health and said children were into them body and soul, with nothing constructive in them. Within days he admitted it was a personal opinion with no science behind it.

It sounds absurd now. It did not sound absurd then.

Go back further and the pattern is everywhere. In the 1700s, moralists warned that novels would corrupt impressionable young women. Victorian doctors coined railway madness for the damage they were certain train travel would do to the mind. In 1954 a psychiatrist convinced a US Senate committee that comic books were breeding delinquents, a claim later shown to rest on fabricated evidence, and the industry censored itself for decades. Radio. Television. Rock and roll. Every generation finds a new technology to blame for what is happening to its children.

Now the honest pivot, because this is not a free pass.

Saying moral panic does not prove the current worry is wrong. Some of these fears carried a grain of truth, and a few new things really are harmful. The pattern does not tell you a fear is baseless. It tells you to check the proportion before you act, because we have stood here many times, and we are almost always wrong in the same direction.

So here is the problem, and the way out of it.

The problem is not that we worry. It is that panic makes us grab the biggest, bluntest lever, aimed at the most visible thing, while the real drivers of children's distress sit in the background untouched. Panic feels like action. It rarely is.

The way out is duller, and it works. Ask how big is this really, and compared to what. Teach children to read the machine rather than simply fear it. And put our energy where the evidence actually points, into the homes and the support around a child, not only the screen in their hand.

As the developmental psychologist Candice Odgers puts it, every generation has its Pac-Man panic. The grown up move is not to laugh at the fear, and not to surrender to it. It is to keep our heads while we help.

**Prepared first comment:**
One caveat I want to make myself, because it is the fair one. Moral panic is not a magic word that wins the argument. Cohen coined it in 1972, Goode and Ben Yehuda gave it real tests in 1994, and the core one is proportionality, is the reaction the right size for the risk. Leaded petrol was genuinely dangerous even though most scares were not. So the lineage is a reason to check the proportion, never a reason to wave away a real harm.
