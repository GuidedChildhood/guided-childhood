## EPISODE 1 of 7 — Is a ban a plan?

On the tenth of December 2025, Australia became the first country in the world to lock children under sixteen out of social media. In the first two weeks the platforms restricted access to about 4.7 million under 16 accounts. The EU is building its own version. On the fifteenth of June, the UK confirmed ours.

So the argument is over. The line is drawn. And almost everyone is asking the wrong question about it.

The loud question is, is the ban right or wrong. I am not going to touch that one. Smarter people than me will fight it for years, and honestly, it is a fight that changes nothing on a Tuesday afternoon in a real house with a real ten year old.

The quiet question is the one that actually decides how our children turn out. Is a ban a plan?

Because a line on a calendar tells you when. It does not tell you how. It does not tell you what a child is ready for the moment they cross it. It does not tell you what happens to the child who climbs around it the night before. A date is not a plan. A date is a deadline.

And a deadline only means something if somebody is building toward it.

I have spent two years building toward this one. Not arguing about the line. Building the thing that has to exist behind it, so that when our kids reach it they arrive with judgement instead of a countdown timer.

Over the next six posts I am going to walk through exactly what a line can and cannot do, using the hard early evidence from the country that went first. The morning after the ban. The child it was actually meant to protect. The lock we are putting on the front door and what it costs. Who is really responsible. And the one thing no line on earth can teach a child.

By the end you will have your own answer to the only question that matters. Is a ban a plan, or is it the starting gun for one.

## EPISODE 2 of 7 — The morning after

Yesterday I asked whether a ban is a plan. Today, the morning after.

When Australia switched on the world's first under sixteen ban, the platforms did their part. Access restricted to roughly 4.7 million under 16 accounts in the first fortnight, on the eSafety Commissioner's own preliminary count. On paper, a wall went up overnight.

Then look at what the water did.

A study in the BMJ at the end of June, tracking 408 Australian adolescents before and three months after the ban, found more than 85% of under 16s were still getting onto at least one restricted platform. Not because they are bad kids. Because a determined child and a smartphone will find the gap in any wall you build.

A University of Chicago working paper, surveying hundreds of teenagers, found three quarters of them said getting around the ban was easy or very easy. The methods were exactly what any parent would guess. Lie about your age. Put in a false birthday. Borrow a parent's or a sibling's account. Use a VPN so the app thinks you are somewhere else. In the days after the ban, VPN demand in Australia jumped to its highest in years, and downloads of fringe apps nobody was checking climbed steeply.

Here is the part I want to be careful about, because this is where people misread me. None of that means the ban failed. It means a line, on its own, does not reach the child. The wall went up and most of the children were already on the other side of it, or over it by breakfast.

So the honest question is not, was the line drawn. It was, clearly. The honest question is, what actually reaches the child once the line is behind them.

That is the whole game. Not the wall. What is on the far side of it waiting for them, and whether we built anything there at all.

A deadline that nobody prepares for does not stop the thing. It just moves it somewhere darker, where nobody is checking. The plan is what you build so the child arrives ready, not the line you draw hoping they never arrive at all.

## EPISODE 3 of 7 — The child the line was meant to save

This is the one I most need to get right. We are 3 of 7. If you read only one, read this.

Every ban is sold with a face in mind. The vulnerable child. The one being hurt by what is on the screen. And that child is real. I am not going to pretend otherwise, not for a second.

But here is what two years in the evidence did to me. It moved my eyes off the screen and onto the room the screen is in.

Ask what the research actually says is driving the worst outcomes for children, and the same three things come up again and again. Poverty. Adverse experiences at home. And the mental health of the adults doing the raising. These are the heavy weights on a childhood. The screen is real, and it is not nothing, but next to those three it is a smaller piece than the noise around it suggests.

Now hold that next to how a ban works. A line at sixteen lands on every child the same. The comfortable child with a calm home and two present parents. And the child carrying all three of those weights at once. The line treats them as the same child. They are not.

Roughly one in ten children is carrying the real load. And for that child, the phone was very often the least dangerous thing in the day. Take the app and you have not touched the poverty, the hard home, or the exhausted parent. You have removed the one place some of them found a friend, a helpline, a door out of a bad room. Take it clumsily and you can make the ten percent worse while the other ninety are barely affected either way.

That is not an argument against the line. It is an argument for the plan. Because a line cannot tell the comfortable child from the carrying one. Only a human system can. A teacher who notices. A lesson that builds judgement. A pathway that meets a child where they actually are instead of where the calendar says they should be.

This is the quiet centre of everything I am building. The loudest fight is about the screen. The real work is in the home and the classroom around it. Aim only at the phone and you will feel like you acted while the thing actually hurting that one child in ten sits in the background, untouched.

A ban aims at the visible thing. A plan aims at the child.

## EPISODE 4 of 7 — The lock

We are 4 of 7. Now the uncomfortable middle of the story. The lock itself.

To keep a child off a platform, you first have to prove who is a child. That means age checking everyone. And age checking everyone means, in practice, every adult handing over more of themselves to more companies to prove they are old enough to be there.

In February this year, 438 experts and researchers from 32 countries signed a joint statement raising the alarm about exactly this. Not about protecting children, which they want. About the way mandatory age verification can quietly build a machine that checks the identity of an entire population to keep out a few, and about what happens to all that data once it exists.

This is the honest tension at the heart of every ban, and I am not going to pretend it away to make my point cleaner. You cannot build a wall around children without building a gate that everyone else has to be checked at. The safer that gate is designed, the more it costs and the harder it is to build. The cheaper it is, the more of everyone's private life it leaks.

So the lock is not free. It never was. And here is where it connects back to the plan.

If the whole strategy is the lock, then the whole strategy depends on a technology that the people who understand it best are warning us about. That is a thin plan. A wall whose only defence is a gate that leaks.

The thicker plan does not lean its whole weight on the lock. It uses the line as one layer, and puts most of its effort into the child arriving able to handle what is behind it. A locked door with an unprepared child on the far side is not safety. It is a delay, bought at the price of everybody's privacy, that runs out the moment the child turns sixteen or finds the VPN.

Verification has a place. It is a bolt on the door. But a bolt is not a plan, and a family that mistakes the bolt for the plan stops doing the thing that actually protects the child. Teaching them to handle the room they are about to walk into.

## EPISODE 5 of 7 — Who is actually responsible

We are 5 of 7. Time to name who carries this, because so far the whole weight has been landing in the wrong place.

Watch how a ban quietly reassigns blame. The law says children may not be on the platform. So when a child gets on anyway, and something goes wrong, the finger swings to the child who lied about their age, or the parent who did not police the phone hard enough. The design of the thing they were pulled into goes strangely unmentioned.

That is the sleight of hand I want to stop. A line drawn at the child moves responsibility onto the child and the family. It takes the pressure off the machine that was engineered, deliberately and expensively, to be as hard as possible for anyone of any age to put down.

Let me be fair to the wider picture, because this is not only about one country. Europe's Digital Services Act pushes in the other direction. It puts duties on the largest platforms themselves, on how they assess and reduce the risks their own design creates for minors. That is the more honest shape of the problem. Responsibility sitting with the people who built the thing, not only with the child who found their way in.

Because here is the truth a ban can accidentally bury. A twelve year old did not design the infinite scroll. They did not build the notifications timed to pull them back, or the metrics tuned to keep them there. Handing that child a locked door and then blaming them when they climb it is not a plan. It is a way of looking busy while the design carries on exactly as before.

So responsibility splits three ways, and a real plan names all three. The platforms owe safer design, and the law is right to demand it. The rest of us owe the child something a law cannot deliver. The ability to walk into that room and keep their feet.

That last part is not the platform's job and it is not the government's. It is ours. And it is the one part no line and no fine can do for us.

## EPISODE 6 of 7 — The thing a line cannot teach

We are 6 of 7. Everything has been building to this.

A line can keep a child out of a room until a certain date. It cannot teach them a single thing about the room. And on the day the date passes, the line stays behind and the child walks in with exactly whatever they were carrying the day before. Which, if all we built was the line, is nothing.

Think about how much of childhood is already inside that room, ban or no ban. A large four country study across the EU found that back in 2022, on a normal weekday, 96% of fifteen year olds were already using social media, more than a third of them for over three hours a day. That is the world on the other side of the wall. It does not get smaller because we drew a line in front of it. It waits.

So the only thing that actually protects a child in there is not the age on the door. It is what is in their head when they walk through it. Can they spot the thing designed to hook them and choose to put it down. Can they tell a real friend from a stranger wearing one. Can they feel the tug of the scroll and name it, instead of being pulled under by it. Can they meet something cruel and not fold.

None of that arrives by itself at sixteen. It is built, slowly, from far younger, the same way we build every other kind of judgement in a child. You do not teach a child to cross the road by keeping them off every road until they are sixteen and then pushing them into traffic. You walk with them. You start small. You hand over a little more as they show you they can hold it.

That is the whole idea behind what I have built. Not a wall at sixteen. A pathway from four. Social media arrives when the line says it arrives. Ready is built one small stage at a time, for years before, with a guide who never simply says no and always shows the next safe step. Arriving with habits beats arriving with rules. A child who has been taught to read the machine is safer at sixteen than a child who was only ever kept away from it.

That is the difference between a deadline and a plan. A deadline says not yet. A plan spends all the years before the deadline making sure that when the not yet finally becomes now, the child is ready for it.

## EPISODE 7 of 7 — So, is a ban a plan?

We started with a question. Is a ban a plan. Seven posts later, here is my answer, and my ask.

A ban is not a plan. A ban is a deadline. And a deadline is a real, useful thing. It concentrates the mind. It says the day is coming, get ready. But it has never once, on its own, made anybody ready. That part was always going to be down to us.

We saw what the deadline does alone. In Australia, the country that went first, 4.7 million under 16 accounts had their access pulled, and three months on more than 85% of under 16s were still finding their way onto a platform, most of them saying it was easy. We saw who the line misses, the one child in ten carrying poverty and a hard home and a stretched parent, for whom the phone was rarely the worst thing in the day. We saw the price of the lock, and the 438 experts warning about it. We saw responsibility slide onto the child when it belongs, in large part, with the design. And we saw the one thing no line can ever teach. Judgement. The thing that actually walks into the room with them.

So the deadline is set. Ours lands the same as everyone else's, ready or not. The only question left is the one I have been building an answer to for two years. What did we put behind the line.

I built a pathway. It starts at age four, not sixteen. A friendly guide who never just says no and always gives the next small step. A curriculum that teaches a child to read the machine instead of only fearing it. Built to run in schools, because the child this is really for is reached in a classroom, not by a parent who happened to buy an app. And built so that when the deadline arrives, the child arrives ready.
