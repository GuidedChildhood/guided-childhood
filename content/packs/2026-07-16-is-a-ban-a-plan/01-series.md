## EPISODE 1 of 7 — Is a ban a plan?

On the tenth of December 2025, Australia became the first country in the world to lock children under sixteen out of social media. In the first two weeks the platforms restricted access to about 4.7 million under 16 accounts. The EU is building its own version. On the fifteenth of June, the UK confirmed ours is coming.

So the argument is over. The line is drawn. And almost everyone is still asking the wrong question about it.

The loud question is, is the ban right or wrong. I am not going to touch that one. Smarter people than me will fight it for years, and honestly, it is a fight that changes nothing on a Tuesday afternoon in a real house with a real ten year old.

The quiet question is the one that actually decides how our children turn out. Is a ban a plan?

Because a line on a calendar tells you when. It does not tell you how. It does not tell you what a child is ready for the moment they cross it. It does not tell you what happens to the child who climbs around it the night before. A date is not a plan. A date is a deadline.

And a deadline only means something if somebody is building toward it.

I have spent two years building toward this one. Not arguing about the line. Building the thing that has to exist behind it, so that when our kids reach it they arrive with judgement instead of a countdown timer. And I did not start after the ban was announced. I started before it, on a hunch that the line was coming and that the line, on its own, would never be enough.

Over the next six posts I am going to walk through exactly what a line can and cannot do, using the hard early evidence from Australia, the country that went first and is now the closest thing the world has to a test. The morning after the ban, and what the children actually did. The one child in ten the line was really meant to protect, and why the line so often misses them. The lock we are all about to fit to the front door, and the price of it nobody mentions. Who is truly responsible when it goes wrong. And the one thing no line on earth can teach a child.

By the end you will have your own answer to the only question that matters. Is a ban a plan, or is it the starting gun for one.

## EPISODE 2 of 7 — The morning after

Yesterday I asked whether a ban is a plan. Today, the morning after, and the numbers nobody put on a poster.

When Australia switched on the world's first under sixteen ban, the platforms did their part. Access restricted to roughly 4.7 million under 16 accounts in the first fortnight, on the eSafety Commissioner's own preliminary count. On paper, a wall went up overnight.

Then look at what the water did.

A study in the BMJ at the end of June, tracking 408 Australian adolescents before and three months after the ban, found more than 85% of under 16s were still getting onto at least one restricted platform. Not because they are bad kids. Because a determined child and a smartphone will find the gap in any wall you build.

A University of Chicago working paper, surveying hundreds of teenagers, put numbers on exactly how. Three quarters of them said getting around the ban was easy or very easy. The methods were the ones any parent would guess if they sat and thought about it for a minute. Lie about your age. Enter a false birthday. Borrow a parent's or an older sibling's account. Use a VPN so the app believes you are in another country. In the days after the ban, VPN demand in Australia spiked to its highest in years, and downloads of smaller apps nobody was checking climbed steeply.

Here is the part I want to be careful about, because this is exactly where people misread me. None of that means the ban failed. It means a line, on its own, does not reach the child. The wall went up and most of the children were already on the far side of it, or over it by breakfast.

So the honest question is not, was the line drawn. It plainly was. The honest question is, what actually reaches the child once the line is behind them.

That is the whole game. Not the wall. What is on the far side of it, waiting, and whether we built anything there at all.

And notice where the effort goes. A workaround teaches a child one thing only, how to get around an adult. It does not teach them to spot the thing built to hook them, or to tell a real friend from a stranger, or to close the app when it starts to pull. A deadline that nobody prepares for does not stop the thing. It moves it somewhere darker, where nobody is watching and nobody is teaching. The plan is what you build so the child arrives ready, not the line you draw hoping they never arrive at all.

## EPISODE 3 of 7 — The child the line was meant to save

This is the one I most need to get right. We are 3 of 7. If you read only one in this series, read this one.

Every ban is sold with a face in mind. The vulnerable child. The one being hurt by what is on the screen. And that child is real. I am not going to pretend otherwise, not for a single second.

But here is what two years in the evidence did to me. It moved my eyes off the screen and onto the room the screen is sitting in.

Ask what the research actually says is driving the worst outcomes for children, and the same three things come up again and again. Poverty. Adverse experiences at home. And the mental health of the adults doing the raising. These are the heavy weights on a childhood. The screen is real, and it is not nothing, but next to those three it is a smaller piece than the noise around it would ever let you believe.

Now hold that up against how a ban works. A line at sixteen lands on every child the same. The comfortable child with a calm home and two present parents. And the child carrying all three of those weights at once, on the same morning, in the same body. The line treats them as the same child. They are not, and everyone who has ever worked with children knows they are not.

Roughly one in ten children is carrying the real load. And for that child, the phone was very often the least dangerous thing in the day. Take the app and you have not touched the poverty, you have not touched the hard home, you have not touched the exhausted parent. What you may have removed is the one place they found a friend, a helpline in their pocket, a door out of a bad room. Take it clumsily and you can make life worse for that ten percent while the other ninety are barely moved either way.

That is not an argument against the line. It is the argument for the plan. Because a line cannot tell the comfortable child from the carrying one. It has no eyes. Only a human system has eyes. A teacher who notices the change. A lesson that quietly builds judgement week by week. A pathway that meets a child where they actually are, instead of where the calendar insists they should be.

This is the quiet centre of everything I am building, and I will say it plainly here because it deserves to be said once without hedging. The loudest fight is about the screen. The real work is in the home and the classroom around it. Aim only at the phone and you will feel like you acted, while the thing genuinely hurting that one child in ten sits in the background, untouched, unfunded, unseen.

A ban aims at the visible thing. A plan aims at the child.

## EPISODE 4 of 7 — The lock

We are 4 of 7. Now the uncomfortable middle of the story, the part the campaigns skip. The lock itself.

To keep a child off a platform, you first have to prove who is a child. That means age checking everyone. And age checking everyone means, in practice, every adult handing over more of themselves to more companies to prove they are old enough to be there.

In February this year, 438 experts and researchers from 32 countries signed a joint statement raising the alarm about exactly this. Not about protecting children, which they plainly want. About the way mandatory age verification can quietly build a machine that checks the identity of an entire population in order to keep out a few, and about what happens to all of that data once it exists and cannot be un created.

This is the honest tension at the heart of every ban, and I am not going to pretend it away to make my own point land more cleanly. You cannot build a wall around children without building a gate that everyone else has to be checked at. The safer that gate is designed, the more it costs and the harder it is to build well. The cheaper and faster it is thrown up, the more of everyone's private life it leaks. There is no version of the lock that is free.

And here is where it connects straight back to the plan.

If the whole strategy is the lock, then the whole strategy is resting its full weight on a single technology that the people who understand it best are standing up in their hundreds to warn us about. That is a thin plan. A wall whose only real defence is a gate that leaks.

The thicker plan does not lean everything on the lock. It uses the line as one layer, useful but partial, and then puts most of its effort somewhere the lock can never reach. Into the child arriving genuinely able to handle what is behind it. A locked door with an unprepared child on the far side of it is not safety. It is a delay, bought at the price of everybody's privacy, and it runs out completely the moment the child turns sixteen, or simply finds the VPN that three in ten of them already found.

Verification has a place. Think of it as a bolt on the door, worth having. But a bolt is not a plan, and a family, or a country, that mistakes the bolt for the plan quietly stops doing the thing that actually protects the child. Teaching them, patiently and early, to handle the room they are about to walk into.

## EPISODE 5 of 7 — Who is actually responsible

We are 5 of 7. Time to name who carries this, because so far the whole weight has been quietly landing in the wrong place.

Watch how a ban reassigns blame without ever announcing that it is doing so. The law says children may not be on the platform. So when a child gets on anyway, and something goes wrong, the finger swings to the child who lied about their age, or to the parent who did not police the phone hard enough. And the design of the thing they were pulled into goes strangely, conveniently unmentioned.

That is the sleight of hand I want to stop, because it is so smooth most people never see it happen. A line drawn at the child moves responsibility onto the child and onto the family. It lifts the pressure clean off the machine that was engineered, deliberately and expensively, by some of the most talented people alive, to be as difficult as possible for anyone of any age to put down.

Let me be fair to the wider picture, because this is not only about one country. Europe's Digital Services Act pushes in the opposite direction. It puts duties on the largest platforms themselves, on how they assess and reduce the risks their own design creates for minors, with real weight behind it. That is the more honest shape of the problem. Responsibility sitting with the people who built the thing, not only with the child who found their way in.

Because here is the truth a ban can accidentally bury. A twelve year old did not design the infinite scroll. They did not build the notifications timed to pull them back at the exact moment their attention drifts, or the metrics tuned through millions of experiments to keep them there a little longer. Handing that child a locked door, and then blaming that child when they climb it, is not a plan. It is a way of looking busy while the design carries on doing precisely what it was always built to do.

So responsibility splits three ways, and a real plan is honest enough to name all three. The platforms owe safer design, and the law is right to demand it and to keep demanding it. The state owes a line that is proportionate and a lock that does not leak. And the rest of us, parents and teachers, owe the child the one thing that no platform and no government can hand over on our behalf. The ability to walk into that room and keep their feet under them.

That last part is not the platform's job and it is not the government's. It is ours. And it is the single part of this that no line, no fine, and no ministerial press release can ever do for us.

## EPISODE 6 of 7 — The thing a line cannot teach

We are 6 of 7. Everything in this series has been building to this one idea.

A line can keep a child out of a room until a certain date. It cannot teach them a single thing about the room. And on the day the date finally passes, the line stays behind on the calendar and the child walks in carrying exactly whatever they were carrying the day before. Which, if all we ever built was the line, is nothing at all.

Think for a moment about how much of childhood is already inside that room, ban or no ban. A large four country study across the EU found that back in 2022, on an ordinary weekday, 96% of fifteen year olds were already using social media, and more than a third of them were on it for over three hours a day. That is the world sitting on the other side of the wall. It does not shrink because we drew a line in front of it. It simply waits, exactly as big as it ever was.

So the only thing that genuinely protects a child in there is not the age printed on the door. It is what is inside their head when they walk through it. Can they spot the thing designed to hook them and choose, in the moment, to put it down. Can they tell a real friend from a stranger wearing the face of one. Can they feel the tug of the endless scroll and actually name it, instead of being quietly pulled under by it. Can they meet something cruel, aimed straight at them, and not fold.

None of that arrives by itself at sixteen, gift wrapped. It is built, slowly, from far younger, the same unglamorous way we build every other kind of judgement in a child. You do not teach a child to cross a road by keeping them off every road until they are sixteen and then, on their birthday, pushing them into traffic and hoping. You walk with them. You start on the quiet street. You hold their hand at the kerb, then you hold your breath, then you let go a finger at a time as they show you they can hold it.

That is the whole idea behind what I have built. Not a wall at sixteen. A pathway from four. Social media still arrives exactly when the line says it arrives. But ready is built one small stage at a time, for years before, with a guide who never simply says no and always shows the next safe step. Arriving with habits beats arriving with rules every time. A child who has been taught, patiently, to read the machine is safer at sixteen than a child who was only ever kept away from it and handed it cold.

That is the difference between a deadline and a plan, in one line. A deadline says not yet. A plan spends every one of the years before that deadline making sure that when the not yet finally turns into now, the child is genuinely ready for it.

## EPISODE 7 of 7 — So, is a ban a plan?

We started, seven posts ago, with a single question. Is a ban a plan. Here, finally, is my answer, and my ask.

A ban is not a plan. A ban is a deadline. And a deadline is a real, useful thing. It concentrates the mind like almost nothing else. It says, plainly and publicly, the day is coming, get ready. But it has never once, on its own, made a single child ready. That part was always going to be down to us.

We have seen across this series exactly what the deadline does when it stands alone. In Australia, the country that went first, 4.7 million under 16 accounts had their access pulled, and three months later more than 85% of under 16s were still finding their way onto a platform, most of them saying it was easy. We have seen who the line quietly misses, the one child in ten carrying poverty and a hard home and a stretched parent, for whom the phone was rarely the worst thing in their day. We have seen the real price of the lock, and the 438 experts from 32 countries standing up to warn about it. We have seen responsibility slide silently onto the child, when in truth it belongs, in large part, with the design and with the law. And we have seen the one thing no line can ever teach. Judgement. The only thing that actually walks through the door alongside them.

So the deadline is set. Ours lands the same as everyone else's, ready or not, and it does not care which. The only real question left is the one I have quietly spent two years building an answer to. What did we put behind the line.

I built a pathway. It starts at age four, not sixteen. A friendly guide called DiGi who never simply says no and always gives the next small step. A curriculum that teaches a child to read the machine instead of only fearing it. Built to run in schools, because the child this is really for is reached in a classroom, not by a parent who happened to buy an app. And built, from day one, with a single switch in the code that lets the whole thing respond to a ban without a rewrite, which is the only reason that when the UK confirmed ours in June, I did not have to start again. I only had to run.
