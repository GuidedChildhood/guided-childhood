# Is a Ban a Plan?

**The newsletter. One long read that holds the whole series.** Author: Justin Phillips. Every figure sourced (see 03-guide-and-sources). Ban neutral throughout: this is about what we build behind the line, never about whether the line should exist.

**Standfirst:** The world just drew the biggest line it has ever drawn around childhood and the internet. Australia went first. The EU and the UK are close behind. Almost everyone is arguing about whether the line is right. I want to ask a quieter question, the one that actually decides how our children turn out. Is a ban a plan, or is it just a deadline. And if it is only a deadline, what on earth are we building behind it.

---

On the tenth of December 2025, Australia switched on the first under sixteen social media ban in the world. Within the first fortnight the platforms restricted access to about 4.7 million under 16 accounts, on the eSafety Commissioner's own preliminary figures. The EU is assembling its own approach. On the fifteenth of June, the UK confirmed ours is coming too.

So the line exists now. This is not a piece about whether it should. I have watched that argument swallow whole comment sections and change nothing in a single real house, and I am not going to add to it. Reasonable people want the line. Reasonable people fear it. Both can be true. What I care about is the thing almost nobody is building, and it is the only thing that will actually be standing between our children and the room when the day comes.

Here is the distinction the whole argument keeps missing. A ban is a deadline. It is not a plan.

A deadline is a date. It tells you when. It does not tell you how, it does not tell you what a child is ready for the moment they cross it, and it does not tell you what happens to the child who climbs around it the night before. A deadline concentrates the mind. It has never once, by itself, made anybody ready. That part was always going to be down to us.

I want to walk you through what the deadline does on its own, using the hard early evidence from the country that went first, and then show you what I think we have to build behind it. This will not be short. It is the most important thing I have written, and I would rather you had all of it than a tidy version.

## The morning after

The platforms did their part. Access pulled from roughly 4.7 million under 16 accounts in a fortnight. On paper, a wall went up overnight. Then watch what the water did.

A study published in the BMJ at the end of June, following 408 Australian adolescents before and three months after the ban, found more than 85% of under 16s were still getting onto at least one restricted platform. A working paper from the University of Chicago, surveying hundreds of teenagers, found three quarters of them said getting around the ban was easy or very easy. The methods were exactly what any parent would guess. Lie about your age. Enter a false birthday. Borrow a parent's or a sibling's account. Use a VPN so the app thinks you are somewhere else. In the days after the ban, VPN demand in Australia spiked to a multi year high, and downloads of smaller apps nobody was checking climbed steeply.

I need to be careful here, because this is exactly where people misread me. None of that means the ban failed. It means a line, on its own, does not reach the child. The wall went up and most of the children were already on the far side of it, or over it by breakfast. The honest question is not whether the line was drawn. It plainly was. The honest question is what actually reaches the child once the line is behind them. That is the whole game. Not the wall. What is waiting on the other side of it, and whether we built anything there at all.

## The child the line was meant to save

Every ban is sold with a face in mind. The vulnerable child, the one being hurt by what is on the screen. That child is real. I will not pretend otherwise for a second. But two years in the evidence did something to me. It moved my eyes off the screen and onto the room the screen is in.

Ask what actually drives the worst outcomes for children and the same three things surface again and again. Poverty. Adverse experiences at home. And the mental health of the adults doing the raising. Those are the heavy weights on a childhood. The screen is real, and it is not nothing, but set beside those three it is a smaller piece than the volume around it suggests.

Now hold that against how a ban works. A line at sixteen lands on every child the same. The comfortable child, calm home, two present parents. And the child carrying all three of those weights at once. The line cannot tell them apart. Roughly one child in ten is carrying the real load, and for that child the phone was very often the least dangerous thing in the day. Take the app and you have not touched the poverty, the hard home, or the exhausted parent. You may have removed the one place they found a friend, a helpline, a door out of a bad room. Done clumsily, a blunt line can make the ten percent worse while the other ninety are barely moved either way.

This is not an argument against the line. It is the argument for the plan. A line cannot tell the comfortable child from the carrying one. Only a human system can. A teacher who notices. A lesson that builds judgement. A pathway that meets a child where they actually are, not where the calendar says they should be. The loudest fight is about the screen. The real work is in the home and the classroom around it.

## The lock, and what it costs

To keep a child off a platform, you first have to prove who is a child. That means age checking everyone, which in practice means every adult handing more of themselves to more companies to prove they are old enough to be there.

In February this year, 438 experts and researchers from 32 countries signed a joint statement raising the alarm about precisely this. Not against protecting children, which they want. Against the way mandatory age verification can quietly build a machine that checks the identity of a whole population to keep out a few, and against what happens to all that data once it exists.

This is the honest tension at the centre of every ban, and I am not going to smooth it away to make my own point cleaner. You cannot build a wall around children without building a gate everyone else is checked at. Design the gate to be safe and it is expensive and hard to build. Make it cheap and it leaks more of everyone's private life. The lock is not free. It never was. Which matters, because if the whole strategy is the lock, then the whole strategy leans its weight on a technology that the people who understand it best are warning us about. That is a thin plan. Verification has a place. It is a bolt on the door. But a bolt is not a plan, and a family that mistakes the bolt for the plan stops doing the thing that actually protects the child.

## Who is actually responsible

Watch how a ban quietly reassigns blame. The law says children may not be on the platform. So when a child gets on anyway and something goes wrong, the finger swings to the child who lied about their age, or the parent who did not police the phone hard enough. The design of the thing they were pulled into goes strangely unmentioned.

That is the move I want to stop. A twelve year old did not build the infinite scroll, or the notifications timed to pull them back, or the metrics tuned to keep them there. Europe's Digital Services Act, to its credit, pushes the other way, putting duties on the largest platforms themselves for the risks their own design creates for minors. That is the more honest shape of the problem. Responsibility sitting with the people who built the thing, not only with the child who found a way in. So it splits three ways, and a real plan names all three. The platforms owe safer design, and the law is right to demand it. The state owes a line that is proportionate and a lock that does not leak. And the rest of us owe the child the one thing no law can deliver. The ability to walk into that room and keep their feet.

## The thing a line cannot teach

A line can keep a child out of a room until a date. It cannot teach them one thing about the room. On the day the date passes, the line stays behind and the child walks in carrying whatever they had the day before, which, if all we built was the line, is nothing.

And the room is not shrinking. A large four country study across the EU found that in 2022, on a normal weekday, 96% of fifteen year olds were already using social media, more than a third of them for over three hours a day. That is the world on the far side of the wall. It does not get smaller because we drew a line in front of it. It waits. So the only thing that truly protects a child in there is not the age on the door. It is what is in their head when they walk through it. Whether they can spot the thing built to hook them and choose to put it down. Whether they can tell a real friend from a stranger wearing one. Whether they can feel the pull of the scroll and name it instead of drowning in it. Whether they can meet something cruel and not fold.

None of that arrives by itself at sixteen. It is built, slowly, from far younger, the same way we build every other kind of judgement in a child. You do not teach a child to cross the road by keeping them off every road until they are sixteen and then pushing them into traffic. You walk with them. You start small. You hand over a little more as they show you they can hold it.

## So, is a ban a plan

No. A ban is a deadline. And a deadline is a genuinely useful thing. It says the day is coming, get ready. But it has never made anybody ready, and it never will. That was always our job.

Here is what I built to do it. A pathway, not a wall. It starts at age four, not sixteen. A friendly guide called DiGi who never simply says no and always gives the next small step. A curriculum that teaches a child to read the machine instead of only fearing it. Built to run in schools, because the child this is really for is reached in a classroom, not by a parent who happened to buy an app. And built so that when the deadline arrives, the child arrives ready for it. There is a switch in the code, put there on the first day, that lets the whole thing respond to a ban without a rewrite, which is why when the UK confirmed ours in June I did not have to start again. I only had to run.

The apps arrive at sixteen whether we are ready or not. I would rather our children arrived with judgement than a countdown timer. A ban is a deadline. This is the plan.
