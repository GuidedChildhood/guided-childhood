## MUMSNET
**Board:** Teenagers

**Title:** My teenager told me she thinks she has anxiety, and reading the actual research changed how I handled it

She said it a few weeks ago, quite flatly over dinner. I think I have anxiety. My first instinct was the two things I now think are both wrong. Part of me wanted to say no love you are just a bit stressed, and part of me wanted to say right, we will get you seen. I did not know there was a road between those two, so I went and read some of the research instead of the headlines, and it steadied me.

There is a study out this month, sixteen UK mental health clinicians interviewed about exactly this, teenagers turning up with a label they gave themselves. What surprised me is that the researchers expected the clinicians to be worried about it, and mostly they were not. In most cases they said the label itself did not really matter. Sometimes it was simply correct. Often it was just a way in, the child trying to hand them their distress in a word small enough to say out loud.

One clinician said the thing that stuck with me. The reason a child reaches for a label is that the alternative is more frightening, feeling like you are uniquely mad or miserable. A borrowed word is better than that. Once I read that I stopped hearing my daughter's line as her copying TikTok and started hearing it as her asking me to understand her.

The bit that actually changed what I do. They found that when a young person genuinely feels heard, the self-diagnosis often just fades on its own. Not because you argue them out of it. Because the need it was standing in for finally got met by a person. But, and this matters, feeling heard is not the same as nodding along and agreeing she has anxiety. It means staying curious. Taking it seriously enough to explore it with her without either rubber stamping it or snatching it away.

So that is what I have been doing. Not you are wrong, and not well done for diagnosing yourself. Just tell me more, when does it feel like that, what is it like. Some of it turns out to be ordinary teenage stress. Some of it is real and worth watching. I would not have found out which if I had jumped to either verdict.

The one thing I am not relaxed about, because the study is honest about it too, is that for a minority it does get stuck. The label hardens into who they are, and that can make things worse. So I am staying curious rather than complacent. But for the everyday version, which is most of it, being curious has done more than either panicking or dismissing ever would have.

Anyone else been handed a label by their teenager. What did you do with it.

## REDDIT
**Subreddit:** r/ScienceBasedParenting

**Title:** New qualitative study, 16 clinicians on adolescent self-diagnosis of mental disorders. The finding is more nuanced than the moral panic, and the authors are careful about the limits. Sources inside.

I have been reading a study published this month and wanted to put it here accurately, because the topic usually gets flattened in both directions. This is about adolescents self-diagnosing mental disorders, depression, anxiety, bipolar. It is explicitly not about autism or ADHD, which is a different literature.

The paper is Cunningham-Rowe and Foulkes, "Adolescents' self-diagnosis of mental disorders: a qualitative study of mental health clinicians' perspectives," Child and Adolescent Mental Health, 15 July 2026. DOI 10.1111/camh.70102 https://doi.org/10.1111/camh.70102

What it is, methodologically, so no one over reads it. Sixteen UK mental health clinicians, a mix of psychotherapists, clinical psychologists, counsellors and mental health nurses. Semi structured interviews, reflexive thematic analysis. It reports what clinicians perceive. It does not measure prevalence, outcomes, or whether the self-diagnoses were accurate against any standard.

The main findings, as themes.

1. The authors went in expecting clinicians to view self-diagnosis as problematic, and most did not. In most cases they reported the label was not the core issue, and was sometimes correct and often therapeutically useful. The authors explicitly suggest public understanding may be oversimplified and overly negative.

2. Theme, self-diagnosis stems from an inherent need to be understood, to make sense of distress and communicate it.

3. Theme, diagnostic language has proliferated, especially via social media, TikTok and algorithms, but also schools and awareness campaigns. They link the escalation to bigger labels to the prevalence inflation hypothesis (Foulkes and Andrews).

4. Theme, many self-diagnoses are accurate and function as a useful starting point.

5. Theme, when adolescents feel validated, self-diagnosis often fades over time. The authors are careful that validation means staying curious, not uncritically accepting the label.

6. Theme, for a minority, an inflexible self-diagnosis becomes a barrier to change. Described as prison-like, a self-fulfilling prophecy, reinforced by peer groups, and capable of worsening symptoms.

Limitations the authors themselves flag, which I think are important. Sixteen participants, all White, no psychiatrists and no GPs in the sample, recruited partly through the senior author's network. Qualitative, so no prevalence claim is possible, including how large that stuck minority is. The authors note there are currently no clinician guidelines for handling self-diagnosis. They mention follow up work with psychiatrists showing similar themes, but that is not part of this paper's results.

My read. This is a useful corrective to the catching-a-diagnosis-off-TikTok narrative, in that the clinicians in the room mostly did not see the label as the problem. But it is perceptions from a small, demographically narrow sample and cannot bear population level weight in either direction. The clinical takeaway that seems robust across themes is take it seriously and stay curious rather than either dismissing or rubber stamping. Interested in critiques, especially on the snowball sampling and the absence of psychiatrists.
