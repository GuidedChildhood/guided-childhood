# How to use this pack — Adolescent self-diagnosis (In Proportion series)

Built from a single primary source held in full: Cunningham-Rowe and Foulkes, "Adolescents' self-diagnosis of mental disorders: a qualitative study of mental health clinicians' perspectives," Child and Adolescent Mental Health, 15 July 2026, DOI 10.1111/camh.70102. Because it is one qualitative paper rather than a field of competing studies, this pack was not run through the six-lens verification pipeline. Instead every claim is represented exactly as the paper states it, with the qualitative hedging kept in, never rounded up into fact.

## The one line
When a young person hands you a self-diagnosis, do not rule on it. Take it seriously and stay curious. For most, the label is a door that fades once they feel heard. For a few it becomes a cell, and the same curiosity keeps it from setting. One posture does both jobs.

## The line that carries the pack
The researchers expected the clinicians to be worried. Most were not. In most cases the label was not the problem, and often it was useful. That reframe is the hook for the whole series.

## Sequencing note
The newsletter is the source of truth and lands the full thesis in week one. The five LinkedIn posts each carry one theme of the study, so spread them across the week in order rather than firing them together. Post 5 is the counterweight and must come last, because it only reads correctly after the four calmer posts have built the case for proportion. Keep the plain thesis landing to roughly one in ten across the wider calendar so it stays a slow reveal, not a headline.

## The one CTA rule
One direct ask only, to guidedchildhood.com/starter-pack, and it appears in exactly two places: LinkedIn Post 4 and the newsletter close. Nowhere else. Every other LinkedIn post uses the soft comment keyword only (LABEL, REACH, HEARD, PROPORTION).

## Guardrails baked in
- No dashes anywhere.
- Qualitative framing kept throughout. Always clinicians in this study, in most cases, a minority. Never a bare statistic, because the paper reports perceptions, not prevalence.
- Proportionality both ways. Calm and reassuring across Posts 1 to 4 and most of the newsletter, against the moral panic. Serious and unsoftened in Post 5 and the newsletter's fifth section, on the minority who get stuck. Never blend the two registers.
- Mental disorders only. Depression, anxiety, bipolar. Autism and ADHD are explicitly excluded everywhere, because the study excludes them and the evidence is different.
- Connection over control. Validate and stay curious. Never model dismissing the label, and never model just affirming it.
- Never claim developmental outcomes. The pack claims only a calmer conversation and a next step. DiGi is a calibrated pathway, never a treatment, never allow or deny.
- Author names are correct everywhere. Katie Cunningham-Rowe and Lucy Foulkes. Check any repost or graphic before it goes out.
- Haidt does not appear. This is not his topic and the pack does not need a foil.
- If challenged on the evidence, concede the limits first, they are the honest strength of the piece: 16 clinicians, all White, no psychiatrists or GPs, snowball sampling through the senior author's network, qualitative so no prevalence claim. The follow up psychiatrist work is flagged as outside this paper and must not be cited as a finding of it.
