# When your child hands you a diagnosis

**Source:** Cunningham-Rowe K and Foulkes L, "Adolescents' self-diagnosis of mental disorders: a qualitative study of mental health clinicians' perspectives," Child and Adolescent Mental Health, 15 July 2026, DOI 10.1111/camh.70102. **Author:** Justin Phillips. **Series:** In Proportion.

## The issue

A researcher sat down to study something she expected to be a problem. Teenagers diagnosing themselves with mental disorders, off TikTok, off awareness campaigns, off each other. Every headline had already decided it was a crisis. A generation pathologising ordinary life, catching depression like a trend.

Then she and her co author interviewed sixteen mental health clinicians who actually sit in the room with these young people. Psychotherapists, clinical psychologists, counsellors, mental health nurses. And most of them said something the headlines never do. Most of the time, the label the young person arrives with does not matter. And often it is useful.

That is the finding I want to walk you through, because if you have a teenager, or you will, one day one of them is going to hand you a word. I think I have anxiety. I think I am bipolar. I think there is something wrong with me. And what you do in the ten seconds after that word lands matters more than whether the word is correct.

Let me be precise before we go further, because the authors are. This study is about mental disorders. Depression, anxiety, bipolar. It is not about autism or ADHD. That is a different conversation with different evidence, and I am not going to blur the two, because blurring them is how bad advice gets made.

### What surprised the people who did the work

Katie Cunningham-Rowe and Lucy Foulkes designed this study expecting the clinicians to find adolescent self-diagnosis problematic. That was the honest prior. It is what media and anecdote had primed everyone to expect. Instead, when they read the transcripts, in most cases the clinicians said the label was not the issue. Sometimes it was simply correct. Often it was useful therapeutic material, a way into the real conversation. The authors conclude, in plain terms, that public understanding of this may be oversimplified, and overly negative.

Sit with the shape of that. The panic got the front page. The people who do the actual work reported something quieter and more human, and almost nobody heard it. We spent the alarm before we asked the room.

### Why a child reaches for a label

Here is the line from the study that stayed with me. One clinician described what a young person is running from when they reach for a label. The alternative, they said, is more frightening. It is feeling like you are uniquely mad or miserable. A name, even a borrowed one, is better than that.

The first theme the authors draw out is exactly this. Self-diagnosis stems from an inherent need to be understood. To make sense of distress. To communicate it to someone. To build an identity around something nameable rather than something formless and frightening. Once you see it that way, the blame drains out of it. A teenager who says I think I have anxiety is usually not performing. They are trying to hand you their experience in a word small enough to pass across a kitchen table.

The clinicians did not pretend the words come from nowhere, and neither will I. Diagnostic language has proliferated, especially on social media. They named TikTok. They named recommendation algorithms. They named schools and awareness campaigns too, which give young people the vocabulary. And as anxiety and depression get normalised, some young people escalate to bigger sounding labels to be taken seriously. That last point connects to Foulkes and Andrews' prevalence inflation hypothesis, the idea that raising awareness can tip over into over-identifying ordinary distress as disorder.

I want to give the worry its strongest form, because it is real and it is in the paper. Awareness campaigns and social media genuinely do put labels within reach that a child might not have found alone. And rising waiting lists push young people to name it themselves while they wait for anyone qualified to. Both true. But hold the contrast. One reading says the child caught a diagnosis like a trend. The other says the child was in enough distress to go looking for a word, and the world handed them one. Same behaviour. Only one of them is the truth, and it is the one that earns your compassion.

### The part that will change how you sit at the table

Now the finding that I think every parent should carry. The clinicians described what happens to a self-diagnosis once a young person genuinely feels heard. It does not have to be argued away. In their words, it melts away. It fades away naturally. The label they arrived clutching quietly loosens its grip, on its own, because the need it was doing a job for finally got met by a person instead of a word.

But here is the bit that is easy to get wrong, and the authors are firm on it. Validation does not mean accepting the label without question. It is not nodding along and saying yes, you have bipolar, well done for spotting it. It means staying curious. Taking the young person seriously enough to explore what they are feeling, together, without either rubber stamping the diagnosis or snatching it away.

That is a narrow path, and both ditches are tempting. Dismiss the label and you confirm the child's deepest fear, that no one will understand them, so they grip it tighter. Just affirm the label and you can harden it into a fixed thing before you have understood a single day of what it is actually made of. Curiosity is the road between the two. And it is not a polite way of disagreeing, because the study is clear that many self-diagnoses turn out to be accurate. Staying curious is how you find out which kind you are dealing with. Sometimes the young person is right, and the word they arrived with gets you straight to the crux.

### The one time proportion cuts the other way

I have spent this whole issue arguing for calm against the panic. Here is where I stop, because there is a group in this study I will not soften.

For a minority of young people, the clinicians said, the self-diagnosis stops being a doorway and becomes a cell. One word they reached for was prison-like. The label hardens into a fixed identity, and the young person cannot see a way out of it. The mechanism is grim. It becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy, so a young person expecting to be unwell behaves and interprets themselves into staying unwell. Peer groups can reinforce it, until a young person's whole social life is organised around being the ill one. The clinicians said this can worsen symptoms.

This minority is real and it is serious, and pretending the whole thing is harmless would be its own kind of dishonesty. Proportion is not the same as calm. It means severe where the harm is real, and calm where it is not.

And here is the honest limit that keeps me humble about all of it. This is sixteen clinicians, all White, no psychiatrists and no GPs in the sample, recruited partly through the senior author's own network. It is qualitative, which means it can tell you that this minority exists but it cannot tell you how big it is. There are currently no clinician guidelines at all for handling self-diagnosis, which tells you how new this ground is. The authors mention that follow up work with psychiatrists produced similar themes, but that is not in this paper's results, so I will not lean on it.

### What this actually asks of you

Read across the whole study and a practice falls out of it, and the beautiful thing is that it is the same move for both children. The one who will be fine, and the one at risk of getting stuck.

A young person hands you a label. You do not rule on it. You take it seriously, and you stay curious with them. Because they feel heard, the label either does its useful job and points you at something real, or it quietly fades. For the few at risk of the cell, that same curiosity is exactly what stops the identity setting, because you never lock the door and you never let the word become the whole of them. One posture does both jobs. It walks most of them through, and it keeps a few from getting stuck.

That is not a verdict. It is a pathway. And I will be careful about what I am claiming, because the study is careful too. This does not improve developmental outcomes. It does not fix anything. No product and no clever line does that, and I will not pretend otherwise. What a pathway gives you is narrower and more honest. A calmer conversation, and a next step, instead of a slammed door or a rubber stamp.
