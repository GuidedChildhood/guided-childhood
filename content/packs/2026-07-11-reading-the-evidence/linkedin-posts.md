## POST 1 — The honest ledger on Haidt (flagship)
**Format:** text. **When:** day 1. **Pillar:** evidence commentary.

Across roughly 355,000 adolescents, the most careful analysis we have found that digital technology use explains at most about 0.4 percent of the variation in their wellbeing. Roughly the same as eating potatoes (Orben and Przybylski, Nature Human Behaviour).

Hold that number, because I am about to defend Jonathan Haidt, and then show you where his case runs past its evidence. Both halves matter if you actually want the truth.

Where Haidt is right, and his critics rarely say it out loud.

The rise in teenage distress since about 2012 is real, international, and largest in adolescent girls. Sleep loss from phones is well evidenced. There are genuine windows in early adolescence when children are more sensitive, a finding that comes from his own critics. And the single strongest study in the field, a staggered rollout of Facebook across US colleges, found it truly worsened student mental health (Braghieri, Levy and Makarin, American Economic Review). That is serious work and it deserves respect.

Now the honest pivot, and it is a big one.

Haidt takes that real, specific, mostly small evidence and stretches it into a claim it cannot carry, that phones rewired a generation and caused the whole epidemic at a population level. The National Academies reviewed the field in 2024 and called it mixed and inconclusive. Candice Odgers, reviewing his book in Nature, said there is no evidence platforms are rewiring children's brains. Rewiring is a metaphor standing in for a mechanism nobody has shown in a scan.

And here is the move to watch for, the one that should bother you most. When the peer reviewed evidence is modest, the case leans more and more on in house surveys run by campaign groups, built only to find harm, then cited as if they were science.

More on exactly how one of those surveys is made in the next post. Once you see the machine, you cannot unsee it.

None of this means screens are harmless. It means the size of the response should match the size of the evidence. That is the whole game.

## POST 2 — How a scare is built (the Snapchat survey, media literacy)
**Format:** text, strong carousel candidate. **When:** day 3. **Pillar:** insight, capability.

A survey landed this year with a frightening headline about Snapchat. I want to show you how it was built, because once you can see it, you can read every one of these for yourself.

It asked around a thousand teenagers, through a panel their parents had signed up to, about seven bad things they might have seen on Snapchat in the past year. Bullying. Unwanted contact. Sexual content. And so on.

Here is the first thing to notice. It only asked about harm. There was no question about anything good, useful or ordinary. A survey that only looks for harm will only ever find harm. That is not a study of the teen experience, it is a search party with one instruction.

Second thing. Look at what its own numbers say. On the report's own figures, 69 percent reported no bullying, 75 percent no sexual content, 82 percent no hate speech across a whole year.

Now the honest pivot, because this cuts both ways and I will not let it only cut one way.

That does not make Snapchat safe. On the same numbers, 56 percent of those teens did report at least one unpleasant thing over the year, and unwanted contact from strangers was the most common. That is real and it matters. The survey does not prove harm is everywhere, and it does not prove children are fine. It was not built to measure either. It has no sense of how often, how serious, or compared to what.

The third thing is the tell. The report described children who saw something unpleasant and responded by closing the app or scrolling past as desensitised. Sit with that. A child using good judgement, choosing not to engage, recast as a symptom. When avoiding harm gets counted as harm, the scare can only grow.

So here is your toolkit for the next frightening headline about children and screens. Ask three questions. Did it only go looking for harm. How often and how serious, not just whether it happened once in a year. And compared to what.

That last one is the big one, and it is the whole of the next post.

## POST 3 — Compared to what (proportionality, real drivers, the plain post)
**Format:** text. **When:** day 5, standalone. **Pillar:** the plain real drivers post.

Compared to what. It is the question that turns a scary number into an honest one, and almost nobody asks it about children and screens.

So let us ask it, with the real figures.

When American researchers asked high school teenagers about their actual lives, not their screens, about three in four reported at least one serious adverse experience. Roughly one in five reported four or more. Emotional abuse at home, around 61 percent. Physical abuse, around 32 percent. Living with an adult in serious mental distress, around 28 percent (US CDC, Youth Risk Behavior Survey, 2023). The UK numbers on adverse childhood experiences tell the same story, about half of adults carrying at least one.

These are not things teenagers saw on a screen. They are things that happened to them, in homes, neighbourhoods and schools.

Now the honest pivot, because I am not doing the mirror image trick.

This is not screens are fine, look over there instead. Some children are genuinely hurt online, and the design of these platforms deserves hard scrutiny. Holding both at once is the entire point.

But look at where the alarm, the laws and the money actually go. Toward the most visible thing, the phone in the hand. Meanwhile the things doing the most measurable damage to children, abuse, poverty, addiction and untreated mental illness in the adults around them, are old, unphotogenic, and chronically underfunded.

A moral panic is not the claim that something is bad. It is a response that is out of proportion to the evidence, aimed at the easiest target instead of the biggest one.

So the most useful question we can ask about any harm to children is not is it bad. It is how big, compared to what, and are we spending our attention where the damage really is.

## POST 4 — This time it is different (the moral panic lineage)
**Format:** text, or 60 second face to camera. **When:** day 8. **Pillar:** insight.

Jazz. The telephone. Comic books. Television. The Walkman. Video games. Pac-Man. Every one was going to ruin the young. Every one, at the time, was urgently different.

There is a pattern in how a society reacts to a new thing in children's hands, and it is old enough to have a name and a set of tests.

The sociologist Stanley Cohen described it in 1972. Goode and Ben Yehuda sharpened it in 1994 into criteria you can actually apply. Is the concern out of proportion to the real risk. Does it flare fast and fade. And has a complex problem been pinned onto a single, symbolic villain.

Now the honest pivot, because the pattern is a warning, not a defence.

Saying moral panic is not the same as saying nothing is wrong. Sometimes the new thing really does carry a risk. Leaded petrol was genuinely dangerous even though most scares were not. The lineage does not tell you a fear is baseless. It tells you to check the proportion before you legislate, because we have a long record of getting the size spectacularly wrong and doing real harm in the rush.

The tell is always the same sentence. This time it is different. It was said, in deadly earnest, about every item on that list, often with medical bodies and confiscations and bans behind it.

The grown up move is not to laugh at the fear, and not to surrender to it. It is to ask, calmly, how big is this really, and is our response the right size. That is the difference between protecting children and performing that we are protecting them.

## POST 5 — The teachable skill (capability, pathway, the one CTA)
**Format:** text, or face to camera. **When:** day 10, closes the arc. **Pillar:** pathway, soft ask.

If reading a scary study is a skill, then here is the good news. It is a teachable one, and teaching it is the whole job.

We spend enormous energy arguing about whether to let children near the machine. We spend almost none teaching them, or ourselves, how to see it clearly. That is backwards.

A child will live their entire life inside recommendation systems. So will we. The question was never whether they meet the machine. It is whether they meet it able to notice what it is doing, question what it feeds them, and step back when they choose to. That is not a ban. It is literacy, built in the right order, one age at a time.

And here is the honest pivot, the one this whole series has walked toward.

The same skill that lets a fourteen year old spot a manipulative feed is the skill that lets an adult spot a manipulative headline about feeds. Reading the evidence and reading the algorithm are the same muscle. How big. Compared to what. Who made this, and what did they want me to feel.

So we do two things at once. We rebuild the real world so a screen has less to fill, the point of the last few weeks. And we teach the digital one on purpose, stage by stage, so that by sixteen a young person steps into full access clear eyed instead of falling into it.

Not a wall. A pathway. Calm, sequenced, evidence led, built by adults who model the thing they are teaching.
