## MUMSNET
**Where:** parenting or education board. **Voice:** a parent sharing a useful trick.

A little toolkit for reading scary phone headlines, because I was driving myself mad

I kept seeing terrifying stats about kids and social media and feeling helpless. Then I read something that gave me three questions to ask, and honestly it has calmed me right down.

One, did the study only look for harm. A lot of these surveys only ask about bad experiences, never good or neutral ones. If you only go looking for harm you will only find harm.

Two, how often and how bad, not just did it ever happen. A stat like a third of teens saw something nasty in a year sounds huge until you realise almost anything happens at least once in a year.

Three, and this is the big one, compared to what. When you look at what teenagers actually deal with offline, at home and school, the screen starts to look like a smaller piece of a much bigger picture.

To be fair the harms online are real and some kids do get hurt. It is not nothing. But these three questions stopped me panicking at every headline and helped me think about where the real risks in my own kid's life actually are. Anyone else use something like this to stay sane

## REDDIT
**Where:** r/ScienceBasedParenting or r/Parenting. **Voice:** evidence first, neutral, fair.

**Title:** A fair-minded toolkit for evaluating "social media harms kids" studies, with the Haidt debate as the worked example

Sharing a way of reading the kids-and-screens literature that tries to be fair to all sides.

Steelman first: the post-2012 rise in adolescent distress is real and internationally patterned, largest in girls; sleep effects are decent; developmental sensitivity windows are real (Orben, Przybylski, Blakemore 2022); and the Braghieri et al. 2022 Facebook-rollout natural experiment is genuinely strong causal evidence. Haidt is not making things up.

Where the caution comes in:
1. Effect sizes at population level are small (Orben & Przybylski 2019: ~0.4% of variance, n≈355k). NASEM 2023/24 called the evidence "mixed and inconclusive." Odgers (Nature 2024) disputes the "rewiring" causal claim.
2. "Rewiring the brain" has no direct structural neuroimaging support; it is metaphor.
3. Reduction/deprivation experiments are confounded by demand characteristics.
4. Advocacy surveys (harm-only items, opt-in panels, no denominator) increasingly get cited as if peer-reviewed. Example: the Dec 2025 "Hidden Dangers of Snapchat" poll, whose own numbers show 44% reported no unsafe content in a year and which labels teens who close the app as "desensitised."

Three questions I now apply to any such study: (a) did it only look for harm; (b) frequency and severity, not one-off occurrence; (c) compared to what (e.g. CDC YRBS 2023 shows ~76% of US teens report at least one adverse childhood experience offline).

Not arguing screens are harmless, arguing for proportionality and method literacy. Interested in where r/SBP thinks this framework is too generous or too harsh, especially on the deprivation-experiment critique.

## FACEBOOK
**Where:** parent groups. **Voice:** warm, short, ends on a question.

A quick trick that stopped me panicking at every scary phone headline.

Three questions. Did the study only look for bad stuff. How often and how serious, not just did it happen once. And the big one, compared to what.

Because when you look at what kids actually face in the real world, at home and at school, the screen is one piece of a much bigger picture, not the whole story.

The online harms are real, I am not waving them away. But asking those three questions helps me put my energy where the real risks in my own child's life are, instead of jumping at every headline.
