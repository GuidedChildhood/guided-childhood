## Editorial judgement — what I kept from the source, and what I cut (READ THIS)

The source was a polemic. I kept its one strong idea, proportionality, and cut everything that would damage your credibility. This is deliberate.

**Kept (the defensible spine):**
- The proportionality argument (online harm scale vs offline adversity scale).
- The critique that harm-only advocacy surveys manufacture scares.
- The specific, fair audit of where Haidt outruns his evidence, steelmanned first.
- The moral panic lineage, used precisely (Cohen 1972; Goode and Ben-Yehuda 1994).

**Cut entirely (do not reintroduce):**
- "Young people are justified in defying the ban." You never relitigate the ban or encourage law-breaking. Out.
- The partisan and conspiratorial framing (Musk, Epstein, "far-Right authoritarians", biometric-harvesting plot). Not your voice, not evidence.
- The reverse spin that social media is "astonishingly safe" or should be "prescribed as a refuge." That flips into denial of harm and breaks the Livingstone standard. 56% of teens in that survey did report at least one unpleasant thing.
- The entire Australia section. The "did the ban work" angle is ban relitigation, which your non-negotiables forbid, and the source inflated Australia's child-protection numbers anyway ("1,200/day" is really ~330; "45,000 removed" is the out-of-home-care stock, which includes neglect and family breakdown, not "abuse removals").

## Verification notes — the numbers, corrected

**Use these (verified):**
- 0.4% of variance, n~355,000 (Orben and Przybylski 2019, Nature Human Behaviour).
- NASEM 2023/24 "mixed and inconclusive"; Odgers 2024 Nature review ("no evidence of rewiring").
- Braghieri, Levy and Makarin 2022 (AER) Facebook rollout, causal worsening. Concede this to Haidt.
- Developmental sensitivity windows (Orben, Przybylski, Blakemore 2022, Nature Communications). Concede.
- Snapchat survey (Heat Initiative / ParentsTogether Action / Design It For Us, Dec 2025, ~1,016 teens, weighted opt-in panel, harm-only, not peer-reviewed). Confirmed figures: bullying 31%, unwanted contact 36%, sexual content 25%, 44% reported no unsafe content (so 56% at least one). "Desensitised" framing confirmed.
- US CDC YRBS 2023 (offline adversity): ~76% at least one ACE, ~18.5% four or more, emotional abuse 61.5%, physical abuse 31.8%, household poor mental health 28.4%. (CDC MMWR 2024, Fowler et al.)
- UK ACEs ~47% at least one (Bellis 2014; Public Health Wales).
- Perinatal cost, ~72% falls on the child (Bauer/LSE).

**Corrected away from the source's inflated versions (do NOT use the polemic's numbers):**
- "44% suffered three or more" ACEs → does not exist in CDC data. Use 18.5% with four or more.
- "33%" household mental illness → real figure 28.4%.
- "29%" household substance use → real figure 25.1%.
- "18%" household incarceration → real figure 14.5%.
- "99% got alcohol and vapes offline" → rhetorical. Say "the large majority" (NYTS/YRBS directionally support it).
- Keep source labels distinct: the ACE items, community violence (~24%), and racism-at-school (~31.5%) are from separate CDC reports. Do not stack them into one "at home" total.

**Caveats worth knowing:**
- Teen YRBS ACE figures (76% / 18.5%) run higher than adult ACE benchmarks (~61-64% / ~1 in 6). Different instrument and sample. Do not present teen numbers as overturning the adult ACE literature.
- Four Snapchat sub-figures (hate speech ~18%, drugs ~17%, violence ~8%, self-harm ~7%) were the "less frequent" categories but were not read from the primary PDF (it blocks automated access). If you publish those exact four, open the report and confirm first.
- The source is US-centric. Your audience is UK. Lead the proportionality point on UK ACE data where you can, and cite the US CDC numbers as corroboration.
