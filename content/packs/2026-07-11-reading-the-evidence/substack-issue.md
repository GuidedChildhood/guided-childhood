**Preview line:** A toolkit for the next frightening headline about children and screens, built from the evidence and fair to everyone.

## The issue

I want to give you something more useful than another opinion about phones. I want to give you a toolkit, so that the next time a frightening headline about children and screens crosses your feed, you can take it apart yourself in about thirty seconds.

We will build it on a real example, and I am going to start by defending the man most associated with the alarm, because being fair to him is what makes the rest of this trustworthy.

### First, credit where it is due

Jonathan Haidt has done something valuable. He noticed that teenage distress started climbing around 2012, across several countries, and hardest among girls. That trend is real. He is right that phones cost children sleep. He is right that there are windows in early adolescence when children are more vulnerable, a point that comes, awkwardly for his critics, from his critics' own research. And the strongest single study in the whole field, a staggered rollout of Facebook across American colleges, found it genuinely worsened students' mental health. That is a serious case and I will not pretend otherwise.

Now here is where it comes loose.

The best large analysis we have, covering around 355,000 young people, found that digital technology use explains at most about 0.4 percent of the variation in their wellbeing. About the same as eating potatoes. The National Academies of Sciences reviewed the whole field in 2024 and called the evidence mixed and inconclusive. And when Candice Odgers reviewed Haidt's book in Nature, she wrote that there is no evidence platforms are rewiring children's brains. Rewiring is a vivid word doing the job of a mechanism that nobody has actually found in a scan.

So we have a real but modest body of evidence, and a claim, that phones rewired a generation and caused the entire crisis, that is much bigger than the evidence underneath it. Keep that gap in mind, because it is where the toolkit comes from.

### The three questions

**One. Did it only go looking for harm?**

A survey landed this year with a scary headline about Snapchat. It asked around a thousand teenagers, through a panel their parents had signed up to, about seven bad things they might have seen in the past year. Bullying. Unwanted contact. Sexual content. And nothing else. No question about anything good, ordinary or useful.

A study that only asks about harm can only ever find harm. That is not a picture of children's lives online. It is a search party with one instruction. So the first question is always, what did this study allow itself to discover.

**Two. How often, and how serious, not just whether it happened once?**

Read that same survey's own numbers and something interesting appears. On its figures, 69 percent of teens reported no bullying, 75 percent no sexual content, 82 percent no hate speech, across a whole year.

I am not going to spin that into Snapchat is safe, because that would be the same trick in reverse. On the same numbers, 56 percent did report at least one unpleasant thing over the year, and unwanted contact from strangers was the most common, at 36 percent. That is real and worth acting on. The honest reading is that the survey proves neither pervasive harm nor safety, because it never measured how often, how severe, or compared to what. It measured whether a bad thing happened at least once in twelve months, which almost anything does.

There was a tell in that report worth naming. It described children who saw something unpleasant and responded by closing the app or scrolling past as desensitised. Think about what that means. A child using good judgement, choosing not to engage, counted as a symptom. When avoiding harm is scored as harm, a scare can only grow.

**Three. Compared to what?**

This is the big one, and it is where being honest gets uncomfortable for everyone.

When researchers asked American teenagers about their actual lives rather than their screens, about three in four reported at least one serious adverse experience. Roughly one in five reported four or more. Emotional abuse at home, around 61 percent. Physical abuse, around 32 percent. Living with an adult in serious mental distress, around 28 percent. UK figures on adverse childhood experiences tell the same story, about half of adults carrying at least one.

These are not things children saw on a screen. They are things that happened to them, in homes and neighbourhoods and schools.

I am not saying look over here instead, screens are fine. Some children are genuinely harmed online and platform design deserves hard scrutiny. I am saying look at where the alarm and the laws and the money go, toward the most visible thing, the phone in the hand, while the things doing the most measurable damage to children sit in the background, old and unphotogenic and underfunded.

That is what a moral panic actually is. Not the claim that something is bad. A response that has come loose from the size of the thing, aimed at the easiest target rather than the biggest one. The sociologist Stanley Cohen named it in 1972, and disproportionality has been its core test ever since. It has never meant the harm is imagined. It means check the proportion before you act.

### Why this matters more than winning an argument

We have done this before. Jazz, comic books, television, the Walkman, video games, all urgently different at the time, all met with genuine alarm, sometimes bans and confiscations. The tell is always the same three words. This time it is different.

Sometimes, of course, the new thing does carry a real risk. Leaded petrol was dangerous even though most scares were not. So the lineage is not a reason to dismiss every worry. It is a reason to ask, calmly, how big is this really and is our response the right size, before we reach for the biggest lever we have.

And here is the part that turns all of this from cynicism into something you can use. The skill that lets you take apart a scary study is the same skill your child needs to take apart a manipulative feed. How big. Compared to what. Who made this, and what did they want me to feel. Reading the evidence and reading the algorithm are the same muscle.

So we do two things at once. We rebuild the real world so a screen has less to fill. And we teach the digital one on purpose, stage by stage, so that by sixteen a young person meets the full online world clear eyed instead of falling into it. Not a wall. A pathway, built by adults who model the very thing they are teaching.

The next scary headline is already being written. Now you have the three questions to meet it with.
