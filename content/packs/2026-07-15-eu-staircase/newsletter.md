# The line is only the deadline
### What the EU actually recommended this week, and the question sitting underneath every version of it

**LinkedIn newsletter / Substack issue. Author: Justin Phillips.**
Subject line options: (1) The line is only the deadline. (2) A staircase, not a wall. (3) On the day a child is finally allowed in, who prepared them?
Preview line: On Monday the EU drew a line at thirteen. The number is not the story. The staircase underneath it is.

---

On Monday, two researchers handed Ursula von der Leyen a document that most of the coverage got slightly wrong.

The headline was the age line. An EU wide restriction on social media for children under thirteen, with member states free to set the bar higher. That is the part that travels. It fits the story we already tell ourselves about children and phones, which is a story about keeping them out.

But read what the Commission President said when she took the report. It is not about whether children can access social media, she said. It is about whether and when social media can access our children. And then the phrase that matters more than the number: phased and gradual access for different age ranges.

That is not a wall. It is a staircase.

The report, co chaired by the child psychiatrist Jörg Fegert and the epidemiologist Maria Melchior, is built on a developmental spine. No screens at all in the earliest years. Supervised, time limited, age appropriate use through childhood. Evolving autonomy from thirteen, on services that have proven they are safe. Full autonomy at eighteen. Every stage assumes the one before it did some work. The line at thirteen is a floor for that structure, not the structure itself.

It did not come from nowhere. The panel met three times through the spring, on the fifth of March, the sixteenth of April and the sixteenth of June, and pulled in academics, clinicians, and youth and parent representatives from across Europe. It sits alongside a wave of the same instinct. France is proposing fifteen. Greece fifteen. Denmark under sixteen. Sweden the year a child turns fifteen. In June the G7 agreed common principles on the same problem for the first time. And a recent Eurobarometer found ninety two percent of Europeans think protecting children online should be an EU priority. Britain has drawn its own line, at sixteen, for spring 2027. Whatever else is true, the political will has arrived.

## The part worth sitting with

This is a precautionary report. It backs an age restriction. It does not pretend the risks are imagined, and neither do I. And even so, its own evidence keeps landing on the same uncomfortable finding. The line is necessary, and the line is not enough.

It notes that where blanket bans have been tried, most children route around them. That is not a guess. The early data from Australia, three months after its under sixteen restriction began, found more than eight in ten young people still reaching at least one restricted platform every week.

It points to prospective work suggesting a first smartphone at twelve rather than thirteen may be associated with higher rates of depression a year later, and then is careful to treat the age as a marker, not a magic switch. The report's own footnote points to a study whose title says the quiet part plainly. Age limits alone will not fix smartphone risks.

And it puts the burden of proof where it belongs. Not on parents, not on children, but on the platforms, to show their products are safe before they reach a child at all. That single move is the most important sentence in the whole document, and the one least likely to make a headline.

If you have read anything I have written this year, you will recognise where this goes.

## Where the danger actually sits

The report locates the real danger almost exactly where the evidence has been pointing for years. Not in the raw hours, but in who carries what into the feed, and what the design does when it finds them.

Its equality principle names the groups without flinching. Children with pre existing mental health conditions. LGBTIQ plus young people. Those with special educational needs. Children growing up with less. The same principle names the mechanism: addictive features, recommender systems, the rabbit holes that deepen whatever keeps a child watching. It marks ten to twelve as the point of most acute convergence, where developmental sensitivity and algorithmic amplification meet.

That is the pathway of harm. The platform does not invent the vulnerability. It finds it, and then it feeds it. A restriction can slow the finding. It cannot undo what the child brought to the door, and it cannot teach a thirteen year old, on the morning the door finally opens, how to hold their own online.

This is also why I keep asking people to hold two ideas at once. The best population level studies we have still find the average effect of screen time on wellbeing to be small. In the largest single analysis, across more than three hundred and fifty thousand young people, digital technology use explained under half a percent of the variation in wellbeing. Candice Odgers, one of the most careful researchers in the field, has said plainly there is no good evidence that platforms are rewiring children's brains. None of that means no harm. It means the harm is concentrated, not universal, and it lands hardest on exactly the children the EU report names. Small on average, and very real in the tail. Both are true, and any honest policy has to carry both.

## For parents

If you are reading this as a parent, here is the version that matters at your kitchen table.

The line at thirteen, or sixteen, is a date on a calendar. It is not a plan. Your child will still, one day, be allowed in, and the question is what they carry when they walk through. The single most protective thing in the whole evidence base is not a setting or a ban. It is a steady, trusted adult who stays in the conversation as the child climbs. In UK data, one always available trusted adult more than halved the later mental health risk for children who had a hard start. That is you, and it does not require you to understand every app.

So the practical move is small and it is not a confiscation. Do not start by taking the screen. Start by staying close to it. Watch their favourite thing with them without criticising it. Keep the door open so that when something frightens or confuses them online, coming to you is the obvious first move, not the last. And rebuild a little of the real world the screen has quietly been filling, because a bored child with nothing else will always choose the feed, and should.

## For schools

If you are reading this as a head, a designated safeguarding lead or a PSHE lead, the report is quietly pointing at you.

On almost every page it calls for the thing a law cannot deliver: media and AI literacy embedded in schools, structured opportunities to build digital wellbeing, guidelines for parents, and particular attention to the most vulnerable children. It asks schools to be where the preparation happens, because school is the one place that reaches every child, including the ones no parent is going to reach by buying an app.

There is a piece of evidence heads should hold onto here. In the international data behind this year's World Happiness Report, raising a child's sense of belonging at school improved their life satisfaction several times more than moving their social media use from high to low. Belonging is not a soft extra. It is one of the strongest levers you have, and it is buildable. A warm classroom is protective in a sense you could almost measure.

## For researchers and anyone who reads the evidence for a living

The report is careful in a way that deserves credit. It treats age as a marker not a mechanism. It insists any age check be proportionate and privacy preserving, explicitly ruling out harvesting identity documents or biometrics and pointing to zero knowledge approaches, so that protecting children does not quietly build the surveillance of them. And it does the most honest thing a policy document can do: it calls for the research that would tell us whether it worked, long term European longitudinal studies and randomised trials, because it knows most of what we have is still correlational.

That restraint is the standard I try to hold myself to, the one Sonia Livingstone models better than anyone: let the evidence do the work, never lean on the label. Moral panic, used properly, is not a way to wave away harm. It is a test of proportion. This report, to its credit, keeps passing that test even as it backs a restriction, because it never lets the visible thing crowd out the bigger, quieter drivers behind it.

## The question underneath every version of this

So the EU has done the hard political thing and drawn the line. What it has not done, what no age check can do, is build the preparation that makes the line mean something. The report gestures at this on almost every page. Guidelines for parents. Media literacy in schools. The infrastructure that helps a family move a child up the staircase one safe step at a time.

That is a category. It is the one I have spent this year building inside, a staged pathway from age four to sixteen that treats readiness as something you build, not a switch that flips on a birthday. I am not going to sell it to you in a newsletter. I will just say that the report and I are describing the same missing piece, and I would rather build it than argue about the number.

Britain has drawn its line at sixteen for spring 2027. The EU has drawn its at thirteen. Argue about the number if you like. The more useful question sits underneath every version of this policy, in Brussels and in Westminster both.

On the day a child is finally allowed in, who prepared them, and how?

That is the work. The line is only the deadline.
