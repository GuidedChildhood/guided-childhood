## The three rebuttals (ready as comment replies)

### 1. "Your platform is for children, so of course you think screens are fine, you have edtech in your back pocket."
Fair challenge, and I will not dodge it. Yes, I have a commercial interest, and no framing removes it, so judge the position against the independent researchers, not against me. But look at what my incentive actually rewards. A platform paid by engagement profits from more screen time and is biased toward "stay on longer." A preparation product like mine only has a market because unguided screen use is a real problem parents want handled. My incentive points toward "screens need structure", not "screens are fine." And notice the easier sale I am refusing: fear converts better than calm. The bestselling book in this space is an alarm. A cynical founder would tell you your child is being rewired and only I can save them. I do the commercially harder thing, the calibrated evidence, precisely because I will not sell you panic. Hold me to that. If I ever start, say so in the comments.

### 2. "The researchers you cite are all funded by big tech."
Funding can corrupt science, so this is a legitimate instinct, and the answer is transparency, not indignation. Here is why these findings survive it. They run on open, public datasets with the analysis plan registered before the results and the code published. The single strongest proof they are not captured: their fiercest critic, Jean Twenge, downloaded their own open data and published a reanalysis arguing the effect is bigger (Nature Human Behaviour, 2020). Industry captured science does not hand its raw data to its loudest opponent. Their funding is mainly public and charitable (Orben at the MRC unit in Cambridge, Przybylski at Oxford), and where a platform supplied data it was firewalled, not commissioned or shown the results before publication. Apply the test evenly and it stops being a weapon: judge by whether the data is open and has survived a hostile reanalysis, not by whose logo is on the grant. On that test this work scores unusually well. (One honesty note I hold to: I do not claim none of them ever touched industry money; I claim their method makes funding hard to hide inside.)

### 3. "You just hate big tech."
The opposite, and here is the tell: my position is internally consistent, which a reflex is not. I want three things at once. Regulate the design, because the target is specific engineered features, infinite scroll, streaks, manipulative defaults, which is exactly what the EU's 2025 minor protection rules and the UK Online Safety Act now go after. Meet the developmental need, because adolescents are wired for peer connection and you cannot subtract it. And guide by age, a pathway not a gate. A tech hater would just want it all gone. And here is the part worth sitting with: removing access does not remove the need, it relocates it. When Australia's under 16 ban began, most kids kept access anyway and the rest moved to less moderated corners; child safety groups warned it pushes children toward the least governed rooms of the very industry the critics distrust. So the honest question to anyone demanding a ban is not "do you love big tech", it is "where do the children go, and is that room safer?" Regulate the machine, teach the child to read it, and keep the connection they actually need.

### Post 1, The graveyard
In 1954, a bestselling book told parents a new medium was rewiring their children into delinquents. It was about comic books.

The author, a psychiatrist, testified to the US Senate. The industry folded into a censorship code. Sixty years later, historians opened his archive and found he had, in a researcher's words, "manipulated, overstated, compromised, and fabricated" the evidence.

That is not a one off. It is a pattern with a shape you can learn to see.

Dungeons and Dragons was going to cause suicide and Satanism. The American Association of Suicidology, the CDC and Health Canada looked, and found no link. Video nasties were going to corrupt a generation; the survey that proved it collapsed when children reported watching films that did not exist. Video games were going to breed killers; youth violence then fell to a thirty year low as games boomed.

Each time: a new thing, a frightened headline, a folk devil, a law. Then the fear fades and the censorship is what is left.

Now the honest part, because this is where lazy versions of this argument go wrong.

"It is just a moral panic" is not a magic wand. Some alarms were real and dismissed as hysteria, tobacco and opioids among them. The lesson of the graveyard is not that worry is always wrong. It is that fear is a bad guide to which worry is real.

So the question for children and phones is not "panic or relax." It is "which parts are the comic book scare, and which parts are the real harm hiding underneath it." That is the whole of this series.

### Post 2, The five tests
A sociologist named Stanley Cohen gave us a checklist in 1972, and it is the most useful thing you can hold up against any scare about children.

He said a moral panic shows five things. Concern, a sudden spike in worry. Hostility, a group turned into a folk devil. Consensus, broad agreement it is a threat. Disproportionality, a reaction bigger than the measured danger. And volatility, it erupts fast and fades, usually leaving a law behind.

Run the phone panic through it honestly and you get a split decision, which is exactly why certainty in either direction is the tell.

Concern, hostility, consensus, volatility: all clearly present. The reaction has the full shape of a panic.

But the fourth test, disproportionality, is where honesty is required, and it is the one people skip. To call a reaction disproportionate you need to know the real size of the harm. And the real size is not zero. Across whole populations the average effect on wellbeing is small. But the harm is concentrated, real and sometimes severe for specific children in specific windows, and there are genuine design and criminal harms, sextortion and algorithmic eating disorder content, that are growing fast.

So this is not a clean moral panic. It is a real, concentrated harm wrapped inside a panic sized reaction.

That distinction is everything. Treat it as pure hysteria and you abandon the vulnerable children who are actually being hurt. Treat it as a universal catastrophe and you aim a blanket response at an average that does not warrant it, and miss the tail that does.

The evidence literate move is to separate the two with a scalpel, not a slogan.

### Post 3, The law that got struck down
In 2011, the highest court in the United States read the science on violent video games and children, and here is what it wrote.

The studies "do not prove that violent video games cause minors to act aggressively." The effects were "small and indistinguishable" from a child watching Bugs Bunny. "Nearly all of the research is based on correlation, not evidence of causation."

That was the Supreme Court, seven to two, striking down a law that banned selling violent games to minors. Every similar state law fell with it.

This matters now because we are watching the same film with a new monster.

The psychologists' own body, the APA, had backed a link in 2015. By 2020 it issued a correction: "there is insufficient scientific evidence to support a causal link between violent video games and violent behavior." It did that because politicians had used the earlier line to blame mass shootings. More than 230 scholars had already called the original claims "misleading and alarmist."

And the real world number that should have ended it years earlier: American youth violent crime peaked in 1994 and fell about 67 percent over the next quarter century, while games became one of the biggest entertainment industries on earth.

Now the honest pivot, because I am not running the lazy version.

This does not prove screens are harmless. The careful researchers always said the effect was small aggression, not murder, and small is not nothing. The point is narrower and it is damning: the loudest prediction, that the new medium would create a generation of violent children, was wrong, in court and in the crime data, and the panic left censorship behind while the real drivers of youth violence went underfunded.

We are about to repeat it, unless we aim at the real harms instead of the folk devil.

### Post 4, Where do the children go?
In December 2025, Australia did the thing a lot of parents are asking for. It banned under 16s from social media.

By March, a survey of children found 61 percent of those who had accounts still had them. Seven in ten said getting around the ban was easy. Most had simply never been removed. The rest did not need clever tricks; only about one in twenty used a VPN.

Here is the question the ban cannot answer, and it is the one that matters most.

Where do the children go?

Because the need does not disappear when the account does. Adolescence is the period the brain is most wired for peer connection; the psychologists' own advisory says turning to peers is "a normal part of adolescent development." And for some children that connection is a lifeline. For LGBTQ+ teens, for disabled and rural kids who cannot find their people locally, the research is consistent: online community protects them, at the same time as it exposes them. Australia's own regulator said it plainly, the aim was never to cut children off from their "digital lifelines."

So when you remove the safest, most moderated front door, the need walks to the next door along. Messaging apps. Private servers. The least governed rooms of the same industry.

Now the honest part. A delay for the youngest children is not wrong, and the busiest feeds are exactly where the vulnerable are most targeted, so this is a real trade off, not a slam dunk either way. But a rule that mostly moves children to darker rooms is not protection. It is the feeling of protection.

The alternative is not a free for all. It is teaching a child to read the room before they are standing in it.

### Post 5, Prepare, do not panic
We know how to raise children into risk. We do it everywhere except online.

We do not ban teenagers from cars until they are 18 and then hand them the keys. We run a graduated licence, supervised, then restricted, then full, and it cuts crashes for the youngest drivers by about a fifth. We do not cure a fear by avoiding it; the clinical evidence is the opposite, graded exposure heals and avoidance feeds the fear. We do not fight manipulation by hiding it; we inoculate, and a study of more than five million people showed short prebunking videos made them measurably better at spotting it.

Preparation beats avoidance, for skills. That is the rule, and it has one honest limit, which is the whole game.

It does not hold for addictive or toxic things. You do not teach safe smoking. So the real question about any part of a child's digital life is: is this more like learning to drive, or more like learning to drink? Most of it, seeing how a feed works, spotting a manipulation, steering an algorithm, is a skill. Some of it, for some vulnerable children, is closer to the bottle, and there the answer is delay and targeted protection, which we already have machinery for.

That is the calm, evidence literate position the panic crowds out. Not "relax, it is fine." Not "ban it all." A pathway, built by age, that prepares a child for the world they will actually live in, and points the serious protection at the children and the harms that genuinely need it.

The panics of the past left censorship behind and helped nobody. This time we can leave capability behind instead.
