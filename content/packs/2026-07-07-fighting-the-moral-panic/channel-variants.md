# Fighting the Moral Panic, channel variants

Substack issue, Mumsnet post and Facebook post, built from the verified research (see pack.md and the Drive briefing). No dashes. UK English. The guardrail holds throughout: panic is the wrong response because it aims at the wrong target, not because children are safe.

===============================================================
SUBSTACK ISSUE
===============================================================
Subject line options:
1. The graveyard of panics, and the one thing they all left behind
2. Comics, Dungeons and Dragons, video games, phones
3. Panic is the wrong response, but not for the reason you think

Preview line: Every generation gets told a new thing is destroying its children. The pattern is real, the history is clear, and the honest version is more useful than either side wants.

---

In 1954 a bestselling book warned parents that a new medium was turning their children into delinquents. It was about comic books. Its author, a psychiatrist, testified to the United States Senate, the industry folded into a censorship code, and titles closed. Decades later historians opened his archive and found, in one scholar's words, that he had "manipulated, overstated, compromised, and fabricated" his evidence.

I start there because it is not a curiosity. It is a template, and once you can see it you cannot unsee it.

Dungeons and Dragons was going to cause suicide and Satanism. The American Association of Suicidology, the United States Centers for Disease Control, and Health and Welfare Canada all looked, and all found no link. The British "video nasties" scare produced a censorship law in 1984, and its keystone survey collapsed when children reported having watched horror films that did not exist. Violent video games were going to breed a generation of killers, and then youth violence fell to a thirty year low while games grew into one of the largest industries on earth. In 2011 the United States Supreme Court read that science and wrote that the studies "do not prove that violent video games cause minors to act aggressively", and that the measured effects were "small and indistinguishable" from a child watching Bugs Bunny.

Each time the shape is identical. A new thing. A frightened headline. A folk devil to carry the blame. A law. Then the fear fades, and the censorship is what is left behind.

Sociologists have a name for this. Stanley Cohen called it a moral panic in 1972, and the standard test, from Goode and Ben-Yehuda, is a checklist of five: concern, hostility, consensus, disproportionality, and volatility. It erupts fast, it names an enemy, everyone agrees, the reaction outruns the measured danger, and then it burns out.

Now the part where the lazy version of this argument falls apart, and where I want to be careful, because getting this wrong is how you lose people who are right to worry.

"It is just a moral panic" is not a magic wand you can wave to make a worry disappear. Some alarms were real and were dismissed as hysteria. The tobacco industry spent decades reframing solid health science as panic. The early warnings on opioids were waved away as overblown while a genuine mass casualty crisis built. Those were not moral panics. They were the opposite: real harm, and an industry manufacturing calm about it. So the lesson of the graveyard is not that worry is always wrong. It is that fear is a terrible guide to which worry is real.

Which brings us to children and phones, and to the only honest thing anyone can say about it.

Run the phone worry through Cohen's five tests and you get a split decision. Concern, hostility, consensus, volatility, all clearly present. But the fourth test, disproportionality, is the one that requires honesty, because to call a reaction excessive you have to know the true size of the harm. And the true size is not zero. Across whole populations the average effect on wellbeing is small, which is why "phones are rewiring every child" overclaims. But the harm is concentrated, and for specific children in specific windows it is real and sometimes severe. And there are genuine design and criminal harms that are growing fast, sextortion and algorithmically amplified eating disorder content among them, that have nothing to do with averages.

So this is not a clean moral panic. It is a real, concentrated harm wrapped inside a panic sized reaction. And that is the hardest thing to hold, because it asks you to do two things at once that feel contradictory.

Treat it as pure hysteria, and you abandon the vulnerable children who are genuinely being hurt. Treat it as a universal catastrophe, and you aim a blanket response at an average that does not justify it, while the tail that does need help gets lost in the noise.

The evidence literate move is to separate the two with a scalpel, not a slogan.

And history tells you what the blanket response costs, because we have run this experiment. The video game panic did not make children safer; it produced a struck down law and left the real drivers of youth violence underfunded. The comic panic did not protect anyone; it closed an industry on fabricated evidence. Each panic spent its enormous energy on the folk devil and left the actual, specific, addressable harms exactly where it found them.

We are about to do it again, unless we choose differently.

The alternative is not "relax, it is fine." It is not "ban it all" either. It is the thing every previous panic refused to do: aim at the real target. Regulate the design that genuinely exploits children. Build the digital literacy that teaches a child to read a feed before they are standing in it. And point the serious protection, the safeguarding, the clinical help, the criminal enforcement, at the vulnerable children and the specific harms where the evidence says the danger actually sits.

The panics of the past all left censorship behind, and it helped nobody. This time we could leave capability behind instead. That is a better inheritance.

MUMSNET POST (genuine forum contribution, no links, no marketing)

Board: Parenting / Teenagers
Title: Went down a rabbit hole on "moral panics" and it has genuinely changed how I read the phone stuff

I keep seeing the same fear cycle and something made me actually look up the history of it, and honestly it has changed how I read the whole phones and kids conversation, so sharing in case it helps anyone else.

Turns out there is a whole pattern to this. In the 1950s it was comic books, an actual bestselling book and a Senate inquiry saying comics turned kids into delinquents. Then Dungeons and Dragons was going to cause suicides, and every health body that checked found nothing. Then video nasties, then video games were going to make a generation of killers, and youth crime actually fell while games got massive. Even the US Supreme Court eventually said the evidence that games make kids violent just was not there.

Same shape every time. Scary headline, blame one thing, pass a law, and years later it turns out the panic was mostly the story, not the thing.

BUT, and this is the bit that stopped me feeling smug about it, that does not mean every worry is nonsense. Some real dangers got called a panic and ignored, smoking being the obvious one. So it is not "relax it is all fine." It is more that being scared does not tell you which worry is the real one.

Where I have landed with my two is that the average effect of social media is apparently pretty small, but it is not zero, and it is much worse for a smaller number of kids who are already struggling, plus there are genuinely nasty specific things like sextortion that are real and growing. So the answer is not panic and not "here you go, unlimited phone". It is teaching them how the thing actually works, bit by bit, and keeping a proper eye on the ones who are vulnerable.

Anyway. Made me a lot calmer and weirdly a lot more careful at the same time. Anyone else find the fear cycle exhausting? What did you actually change at home versus just worry about?

Something that made me calmer this week, and a bit more careful at the same time.

Every generation gets told a new thing is destroying its children. In the 1950s it was comic books, and there was a Senate inquiry. Then it was Dungeons and Dragons causing suicides, and every health body that checked found no link. Then video games were going to make a generation of killers, and youth crime actually fell while games boomed. The US Supreme Court even said the evidence just was not there.

Same pattern every time. A scary headline, one thing to blame, a new rule, and years later the panic turns out to be mostly the story.

Here is the honest bit though, because it matters. That does not mean every worry is wrong. Real dangers have been waved away as "panic" before, like smoking. So being scared does not tell you which worry is the real one.

With phones, the average effect on kids looks small, but it is not zero, and it is worse for children who are already struggling, plus there are real, specific harms we should take seriously. So the answer is not panic, and it is not a free for all. It is teaching them how it works, and watching over the ones who need it most.

Fear points at the whole world. Wisdom points at the right part of it.
