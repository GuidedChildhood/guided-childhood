"Not fully developed until 25" is one of the most repeated sentences about children. It is not a scientific finding.

Judgment under peer pressure and strong emotion keeps maturing into the twenties. Cold cognition matures early. Hot cognition matures late. That finding has replicated.

Now the honest part.

It does not mean teenagers are adults. In a heated moment, a 16 year old is not making a 30 year old's call. And it does not mean the phrase is harmless when you flip it. A single age line misreads the science in either direction.

**Full body (ready to paste):**

There is a sentence you have heard a hundred times. The brain is not fully developed until 25. It gets said about teenagers who want a phone, about young drivers, about anyone under a certain age doing anything an adult would rather they did not. It sounds like settled neuroscience. It is not.

No study ever found an endpoint at 25. The figure is an accident of measurement. The brain scanning studies that built our picture of adolescence, back in the 1990s and 2000s, recruited very few people past their early twenties. Twenty five was simply where the data ran out. The edge of the chart got mistaken for a cliff, and a whole culture of low expectations was built on top of it.

When you look at the newer work, the cliff disappears. A 2025 study in Nature Communications mapped more than 4,000 brain scans and placed the developmental era at roughly 9 to 32, with the biggest structural shift nearer 32 than 25. And as Somerville argued back in 2016, there is no single signature of maturity to go looking for in the first place. Different circuits mature at different ages by different measures. Development is gradual, continuous and personal. It does not switch off on a birthday.

So if 25 is a myth, what is the real finding?

Here is the one that holds, and it is far more useful than the myth it replaces. Icenogle and Steinberg, in a 2019 study of 5,227 people across 11 countries, separated two things we usually lump together. Cold cognition is calm, deliberative reasoning. Hot cognition is judgment under peer pressure and strong emotion. Cold cognition reaches adult levels at about 16 and plateaus. Hot cognition keeps maturing into the twenties. This has replicated, and it resolves an old embarrassment. The American Psychological Association once told the US Supreme Court that 16 year olds were mature enough to make one kind of decision and too immature to make another. The cold and hot distinction is the only thing that makes both statements true at once.

Sit with what that means. A 16 year old can reason like an adult in a quiet room and still act rashly in a car full of friends. Neither half of that sentence is the whole truth. Say only the first and you hand a child adult risk with no scaffolding. Say only the second and you erase a capacity they plainly have.

This is why developmental language is so slippery. It is not a fact you can point one direction. It is a tool, and it cuts both ways. The same still developing argument ended the juvenile death penalty in the United States in 2005, where a child's immaturity was rightly used to protect them. That very same argument keeps the age of criminal responsibility at 10 in England and Wales, so a child can be arrested, tried and sentenced years before the system trusts them to open a bank account. One idea. Opposite uses. When people worry that talk of developing brains can quietly erase a child's voice, this is what they mean, and they are right to worry.

The good news is that the law already worked out the answer, and it did so decades ago. The UN Convention holds two ideas at once. Article 5 asks adults to respect a child's evolving capacities. Article 12 gives weight to a child's views in line with their age and maturity, both together, not one instead of the other. Lansdown's foundational work names three jobs developmental language should do at once. It should support development, enable participation, and offer protection, with protection stepping back as competence grows. And in England we have run on exactly this since Gillick in 1985, where a child under 16 can consent to treatment when they have sufficient understanding, assessed decision by decision rather than by their date of birth. The crude age line is the instrument the law abandoned a generation ago. We just never applied the same wisdom to screens.

Everywhere else, we already do. Think about how we teach genuinely risky things. We do not ban them until 18 and then wish children luck. We run a ladder. Graduated driving licences, supervised then restricted then full, cut first year crashes for 16 year olds by around 22 percent, one of the best evidenced child safety measures we have, and it works best where the risk is highest. Swimming lessons are taught as one layer of protection among several, never as a substitute for the fence. Medical consent is graded by understanding. In every serious domain, we stage the exposure and coach the skill. Digital life is the strange exception, the one place we still argue about a single birthday.

There is one honest limit, and it matters. The ladder works for skills, not for substances. Alcohol is the counter case. When parents supplied alcohol to teenagers in the belief that a gentle introduction would build sensible drinking, the research found it increased harm rather than reducing it. So the test is simple. Is this a skill, like crossing a road, where practice under guidance builds competence? Or is it a substance, where exposure mostly builds exposure? Using a device is a skill. That is why calibrated practice is the right model, and why hand it all over now is as wrong as wait until 25.

And here is the part the myth hides completely. The children we call not fully developed are, on this question, more measured than the adults arguing about them. Two thirds of UK young people back restricting under 16s from at least some platforms. In Australia, most surveyed teenagers opposed the blanket ban, and then a majority of those with accounts kept their access within months, which is itself the digital competence the deficit story says they lack. Across consultation after consultation, their ask is neither everything nor nothing. Fix the design. Teach us the skills. Give us a say. That is not the voice of an unfinished brain. It is the voice of someone thinking clearly about their own life.

So put it together. The science says capability grows through supervised practice, not by waiting. The law says grade by understanding, not by birthday. The children themselves are asking for exactly that. Three separate roads, one destination. Not a gate at 16. A pathway from 4, where a four year old and a fourteen year old meet the digital world at the level they are actually ready for, with the support to grow.

Capable now, growing still. Children are both at once, and any sentence that forgets either half will let them down.
