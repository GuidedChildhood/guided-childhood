## The issue

There is a photograph I keep thinking about from this week's Atlantic piece. Two men who spent years on the same side, at the same nonprofit, believing they were fighting the same fight for children. They have not spoken in two years. They fell out over a single question, and it is the question the whole country is arguing about without realising these two ever disagreed.

The men are Jonathan Haidt and Peter Gray.

You know Haidt, even if you do not know the name. The Anxious Generation. The book that spent more than two years on the bestseller list, got translated into dozens of languages, and turned "take the phones away" into something close to common sense. Smartphones and social media, the argument goes, rewired a generation and caused an epidemic of anxiety and depression.

Peter Gray is 82, an evolutionary psychologist, and until recently he was Haidt's colleague. They co founded a nonprofit together called Let Grow, built on Gray's life work: the idea that children need real, self directed, unsupervised play, and that we have spent seventy years taking it away from them. Haidt admired him. He once called Gray the star academic on play and said he wished every school in America could hear him speak.

Then Haidt sent Gray an early copy of The Anxious Generation. Gray read it in his study overlooking the river, and, in his words, it made him angry. He thought it was not just wrong but unethical. He stepped off the board. He wrote a rebuttal. And he began a whole book to argue the opposite, out this September, for which he was reportedly paid a five hundred thousand dollar advance.

Gray's case is that the crisis is real, but it has almost nothing to do with phones. He blames school. The narrowing of the curriculum, the testing, and above all the long, slow removal of children's freedom to do anything on their own.

So here we are. Two serious people. One crisis. Two villains that could not be further apart.

And I think the most useful thing I can tell you as a parent is that they are probably both half right, and both making the same mistake.

### The mistake

Watch how each man handles an inconvenient fact.

Teen suicide actually fell by around 40 percent between 1990 and 2010, right as home computers and video games arrived in children's lives. Gray, who thinks the digital world is good for children, reads that as evidence: kids finally had a place to connect, make their own rules, and feel competent. Haidt, who thinks the digital world is the problem, has a completely different explanation for the same fall. He suggests it might have been the phasing out of leaded petrol.

Candice Odgers, a psychologist at UC Irvine and one of the calmest voices in this field, points at exactly this move. No serious epidemiologist, she says, explains an improvement with one single cause and then a decline with a completely different single cause. Real trends, moving across seventy years and dozens of countries and wildly different technologies, are never the fingerprint of one thing.

They are the fingerprint of many things at once. And when you insist on one villain, your solution comes out simple and usually wrong.

### The number that fell apart

Here is a small story that taught me more than any statistic in the whole debate.

Gray built part of his book on a striking figure. In 2009, 43 percent of American teenagers said school stressed them out. By 2013, that had jumped to 83 percent. Almost double, right after a major schools reform. It looks like a smoking gun.

Then a journalist checked it. Two problems. The survey had changed how it asked the question between those years, from "pick your top two stressors" to "score this whole list", so the two numbers are not comparable. And the scores had roughly doubled for other stressors too, including family finances. To his real credit, when this was put to him, Gray asked his publisher to pull the figure.

I tell you this because I am on the side that thinks the phone panic is overblown, and that number helped my side, and it does not hold up, so it has to go. That is the only rule worth keeping in this argument. You report the finding that hurts your case as loudly as the one that flatters it. Everyone who does that is worth your trust. Everyone who does not, in either direction, is selling you something.

### The finding underneath all of it

If you take one sentence from this issue, take this one, also from Odgers, in the same piece.

Caregiver mental health is by far the strongest predictor of childhood mental health.

Not the phone. Not the school. The state of the adult raising the child.

I want to hold that gently, because it can be misheard as blame, and it is the opposite of blame. A parent under financial strain, or grief, or their own unsupported anxiety is not failing. They are carrying something heavy with almost no help, and children read the weather in a house long before they can name it.

And none of this means phones do nothing. For a child who is already struggling, a screen can be exactly where the distress pools. That is real, and I would never wave it away.

But look at where all the attention and all the legislation is going. Toward the most visible thing in a child's hand. Meanwhile the strongest predictor we have, along with poverty and adverse childhood experiences and support you cannot get an appointment for, receives a fraction of the airtime, because it is not new and it does not photograph well.

### What a parent can actually do tonight

Not a ban. Not a panic. One quiet shift.

Ask what the screen is standing in for. When a child reaches for a device by default, it is usually filling a space where something real used to be. A walk taken alone. An afternoon nobody organised. A bit of boredom, which is where children invent things.

So tonight, do not start by removing the screen. Start by rebuilding one small piece of the real world beside it. One freedom you had at their age that you have not yet handed over. One unstructured hour that belongs to them. The screen loosens its grip on its own once there is something else worth doing.

That is the whole idea behind what we build. Not a wall between children and technology. A pathway through it, brick by brick, real world richness on one side and guided digital skill on the other, both climbing together, so that by sixteen a young person steps into the full digital world ready, instead of falling off a cliff into it.

The phone was never the whole story. It moved into a room the childhood had already left. Our job is to make that room worth being in again.
