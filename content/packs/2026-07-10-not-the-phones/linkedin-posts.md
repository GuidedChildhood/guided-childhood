## POST 1 — Flagship, news commentary
**Format:** text. **Best moment:** the morning after you first see the article circulating. **Pillar:** ban world commentary.

The National Academies of Sciences reviewed the evidence on social media and teen mental health in 2024 and reached a one line conclusion most people never heard.

Mixed and inconclusive.

This week The Atlantic published a piece that sits right on that fault line. It is called "What if It's Not the Phones?"

Here is the story underneath it.

Two psychologists. One youth mental health crisis. Two completely different villains.

Jonathan Haidt says smartphones and social media rewired a generation. Peter Gray, who Haidt once called the star academic on play, and who co founded a nonprofit with him, says the crisis is real but has almost nothing to do with phones. Gray points at school. The narrowing of the curriculum. The testing. The slow removal of nearly every scrap of freedom children used to have.

They fell out over it. They have not spoken since.

Now the honest pivot, because this is where most takes stop and mine does not.

They are probably both half right, and both making the same mistake.

Candice Odgers, a psychologist at UC Irvine who has read both men closely, put it best. No serious epidemiologist explains an improvement in one decade with one single cause, then a decline fifteen years later with a completely different single cause. Real childhoods do not run on one villain.

School pressure is real. Pew found 68% of teenagers feel a great deal or fair amount of pressure over grades, more than pressure about their looks or fitting in. Phones are real too. So is something both men walk straight past.

More on that in the next post. It is the strongest predictor in the entire field and almost nobody names it out loud.

This is the question I have been asking about our approach for a year. Not which villain. What actually helps the child in front of you.

## POST 2 — The reasoning flaw (evidence brick)
**Format:** text. **Best moment:** 2 days after Post 1. **Pillar:** evidence story.

Here is a claim that sounds convincing and falls apart the moment a proper epidemiologist looks at it.

Peter Gray, arguing that the youth mental health crisis is about schools and not phones, makes a striking point. Teen suicide fell by around 40% between 1990 and 2010, exactly as home computers and video games arrived. His reading: the digital world gave children a place to connect, make their own rules, and feel competent.

Jonathan Haidt, who blames phones, has a different explanation for the very same improvement. He suggests it might be the phasing out of leaded petrol.

Notice what just happened.

Both men explain the good years with one cause, and the bad years with a completely different one. Candice Odgers at UC Irvine said it plainly. No serious epidemiologist reasons like that. You do not get to pick a fresh single villain for every bend in the line.

This is the honest pivot. It does not make either man foolish. Gray's work on play is genuinely important. Haidt named a real feeling that children recognised in themselves. But a trend that moves over seventy years, across dozens of countries, through wildly different technologies and school systems, is not the fingerprint of one thing.

It is the fingerprint of many things moving together. Money. Family stability. What we let children do on their own. What their parents are quietly carrying.

When a story has one villain, the solution feels simple and is usually wrong. When you accept several causes, you have to do the harder thing. You have to look at the actual child.

That is the whole idea behind a calibrated pathway. Not one rule for every family. The right next step for this child, at this age, in this house.

## POST 3 — The strongest predictor (the thesis reveal, the 1 in 10 post)
**Format:** text. **Best moment:** midweek, its own day, not stacked on another post. **Pillar:** the rare plain thesis post.

Caregiver mental health is by far the strongest predictor of childhood mental health.

That is Candice Odgers, one of the most careful researchers in this field, in The Atlantic this week. Read it twice, because it is the sentence almost nobody in the phone debate wants to sit with.

Not the phone. Not the school. The mental health of the adult raising the child is the strongest single signal we have for how that child will do.

I want to be careful here, because this can be misheard.

This is not a post blaming parents. It is the opposite. A parent under financial strain, or grief, or their own untreated anxiety, is not failing. They are carrying something heavy with very little support, and children feel the weather in a house long before they can name it.

And here is the honest pivot. None of this means phones do nothing. Screens can make a hard situation harder, and for a vulnerable child they can become the place the distress lands. That is real.

But look at where the attention goes. We run national debates and write laws about the most visible thing in a child's hand, while the strongest predictor in the evidence gets a fraction of the airtime and almost none of the funding.

Poverty. Adverse childhood experiences. Parents who cannot get an appointment. A waiting list for support measured in years.

These do the most damage, and they do not make a headline, because they are not new and they are hard to photograph.

I build tools that help parents guide their children through the digital world, so you might expect me to tell you the phone is the whole story. I will not, because the evidence does not.

Often the kindest and most effective thing we can do for a child's mental health is to support the adult beside them.

## POST 4 — How a killer statistic fell apart (evidence integrity)
**Format:** text, strong PDF carousel candidate (5 slides: the number / problem one / problem two / the retraction / the two questions). **Best moment:** end of week. **Pillar:** evidence story, depth.

A number went round this year that looked like a knockout blow. It has just quietly fallen apart, and the story of how is worth far more than the number ever was.

The claim. In 2009, 43% of American teenagers said school was a source of stress. By 2013, 83%. Almost double, right after a big schools reform. Case closed, it is the schools, not the phones.

Peter Gray built part of his new book on that figure. Then a journalist checked it properly.

Two problems.

First, the survey changed how it asked the question between 2009 and 2013. The earlier one asked teens to pick their top two stressors. The later one asked them to score a whole list. Different method, different numbers. You cannot compare them cleanly.

Second, the stress scores roughly doubled for other things too, including family finances. So even if the rise is real, it is not uniquely about school.

To his credit, when this was put to Gray, he asked his publisher to remove the figure. That is what integrity looks like in practice.

Here is why I am telling you this, and it is the honest pivot.

I am on the side that thinks the phone panic is overblown. This statistic helped my side. And it does not hold up, so it has to go.

That is the whole discipline. You do not get to keep the flattering number and bin the awkward one. The people worth trusting in this debate are the ones who report the finding that hurts their case as loudly as the one that helps it.

So when someone hands you a single dramatic statistic about children and screens, in either direction, ask two questions.

Compared to what. And measured how.

That habit will protect your child better than any headline will.

**Prepared first comment:**
This is exactly why I use the phrase moral panic carefully. It is not a way to wave away worry. It is a specific test, from Cohen in 1972 and Goode and Ben Yehuda in 1994: is the reaction proportional to the evidence. A number that doubles because the question changed is how a proportion gets lost.

## POST 5 — What we actually do (pathway lane, carries the CTA)
**Format:** text, or 60 second face to camera video. **Best moment:** the following week, to close the arc. **Pillar:** the pathway, soft ask.

The 82 year old at the centre of this week's Atlantic piece is right about one enormous thing, even if he pushes it too far.

Peter Gray has spent thirty years arguing that children need real, unsupervised, self directed play. Time that is not organised by an adult. Freedom to take a small risk, solve their own problem, fall out with a friend and repair it, get a little lost and find the way back.

We have spent seventy years quietly removing that. Fewer walks to school alone. Fewer afternoons no one is watching. Less boredom, which is where invention lives.

And here is the part the phone debate keeps missing. When you take away the real world a child used to explore, something has to fill the space. Very often it is a screen. The screen did not push the childhood out. The childhood left, and the screen moved into the empty room.

So the answer was never going to be a single rule that removes the screen and hopes the old childhood floods back on its own. It will not. That world has to be rebuilt on purpose, brick by brick, at the same time as we teach children to handle the digital one they will live in for the rest of their lives.

That is what a calibrated pathway means. Not a ban. Not surveillance. A sequence. The right amount of freedom and the right digital skill at each age, so that by sixteen a young person steps into full access ready, instead of falling off a cliff into it.

Real world richness on one side. Guided digital literacy on the other. Both climbing together.
