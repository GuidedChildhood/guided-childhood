## MUMSNET
**Where:** AIBU or a parenting or education board. **Voice:** a parent who read something and is thinking out loud.

Did anyone else read the Atlantic piece about phones this week

I went in expecting the usual take your kids phones away and everything gets better. It was not that. It was about two psychologists who used to be friends and now do not speak, because one blames phones for the state of kids mental health and the other blames schools and the loss of freedom to just be a child.

What stuck with me was a line from a researcher who has read both of them. She said neither is right to pick one villain, and that the strongest single predictor of a child's mental health is actually the mental health of the parent. Not the phone. Not the school. The parent.

I found that weirdly reassuring rather than guilt inducing. It is not really about how many hours of TikTok. It is about whether the adults in a house are ok and supported, and whether kids still get any real freedom at all. Mine barely go anywhere alone compared to how I grew up.

Not sure what the answer is. But it made me want to worry a bit less about the screen and a bit more about giving them an afternoon nobody is managing. Anyone else feel like we swapped roaming the streets for a tablet and are surprised it went the way it did

## REDDIT
**Where:** r/ScienceBasedParenting (every claim linked) or r/Parenting. **Voice:** evidence first, neutral, structured for discussion.

**Title:** The Atlantic on the phones debate: two researchers, opposite conclusions, and the finding they both underplay

The Atlantic published a piece this month ("What if It's Not the Phones?", Kaitlyn Tiffany) on the split between Jonathan Haidt and Peter Gray. Worth reading in full, but a few things stood out and I would like other people's read.

1. Haidt (The Anxious Generation) attributes the youth mental health decline mainly to smartphones and social media. Gray (Free to Learn, and a new counter book) argues it is schools and the loss of independent play, and that phones and social media are close to a moral panic.

2. Both explain the same trend line inconsistently. Teen suicide fell roughly 40% from 1990 to 2010. Gray credits the arrival of the digital world, Haidt suggests the phase out of leaded petrol. Candice Odgers (UC Irvine) points out that swapping in a different single cause for each segment of a trend is not how epidemiology works.

3. A widely shared stat, school stress rising from 43% (2009) to 83% (2013), turns out to be shaky. The survey changed its method between those years, and other stressors like family finances also roughly doubled. Gray asked his publisher to remove it.

4. The National Academies 2024 review concluded the literature on social media and health effects is "mixed and inconclusive."

5. Odgers' line that I have not stopped thinking about: "caregiver mental health is by far the strongest predictor of childhood mental health."

The takeaway I land on: the single villain framing (whichever villain) keeps losing to multi factor reality, and the strongest predictors (caregiver mental health, poverty, adverse experiences) get the least policy attention because they are the hardest to legislate. Curious whether people here read the evidence the same way, particularly on the reduction experiment critique Haidt pushes back on.

There was a big article this week called "What if It's Not the Phones?"

The bit that got me was simple. A top researcher said the strongest thing predicting how a child does is not the phone or the school. It is how the parent is doing.

That is not a guilt trip. It is the opposite. It means the most powerful thing for our kids is often just support for us. And it means the answer is probably not one big ban, it is giving children a bit more real world again alongside teaching them the digital one.

I grew up roaming outside for hours. Mine get a fraction of that. Maybe we took away the childhood and were surprised the screen filled the gap.
