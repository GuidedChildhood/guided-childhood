## DAY 2 — Stage gate the AI, do not flat rule it
**Angle:** The age gradient. Stage it, do not ban it.
**Format:** Text post, near character limit.
**Best posting day:** Tuesday.

**Post text:**

In one analysis of over 4 million students, an AI tutor cut how much children retained by about a quarter. For teenagers in high school the loss was closer to a third. For children in Year 5 it could not be detected at all.

This is In Proportion, continued.

Same tool. Same subject. Completely different effect depending on the age of the child using it. That single result, from a 2026 working paper tracking millions of real tutoring interactions on the ALEKS system, is the whole argument against a flat rule.

We keep asking one question. Should children use AI, yes or no. It is the wrong question, because there is no such thing as children. There is a four year old, and there is a sixteen year old, and treating them as one person is how you get a policy that fits neither.

The American Academy of Pediatrics reviewed this in 2026 and landed exactly here. AI is a tool, not a companion. It should be introduced in developmental stages. And it should not be banned. Three sentences that quietly reject both camps at once.

The reason to stage it rather than ban it is that children are already through the door. UNICEF finds children adopt AI roughly three times faster than adults. Internet Matters finds 64 percent of UK children already use chatbots, mostly for homework and curiosity. The gate is not a question of if. It is a question of when, and with whom beside them.

None of this means age gating is simple. It is genuinely hard. Roblox now runs mandatory facial age estimation, and even that is off by about 1.9 years on average, and age verified accounts turn up for resale on eBay within days. Knowing a child's age is not a solved problem, and anyone who tells you it is has not read the enforcement data.

But hard is not the same as pointless. A flat rule treats a six year old and a fifteen year old as the same child. They are not. One needs the tool kept well away. One needs to be taught to steer it before the world hands them the keys with no lesson at all.

Stage the gate. Do not bolt the door and call it parenting.

## DAY 3 — Tool, not a companion
**Angle:** Companion literacy and the design of DiGi.
**Format:** Text post, near character limit.
**Best posting day:** Wednesday.

**Post text:**

72 percent of American teenagers have used an AI companion. Just over half use one regularly. That is not a fringe behaviour any more. It is the water they are swimming in.

This is In Proportion, continued.

The word companion is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and it is where the panic gets loud. So look at what the same research actually found, because the teenagers are more sensible than the headlines about them.

Common Sense Media surveyed them in 2025. Yes, 72 percent have tried one. But 80 percent still spend more time with real friends than with the AI. And 67 percent find the companion less satisfying than talking to an actual person. Most of them have used the thing, weighed it, and quietly filed it below their friends. That is not addiction. That is judgement.

Then comes the part I will not smooth over. Among the teenagers who do use companions, about a third find the AI as satisfying as a human friend, and about a third have chosen to talk to it instead of a real person about something serious. That minority is the group I would watch, closely.

There is an adult study people reach for here, from MIT Media Lab and OpenAI, 981 people. Heavier voluntary chatbot use went with more loneliness and more dependence, and it clustered in the people who were already attachment hungry. But when the researchers assigned the conditions, they found no effect. So this is a strong association running from heavy use, not proof that the chatbot caused the loneliness. It may be that the loneliest reach for it most. The single tragic Character.AI case in the news is exactly that, a single tragedy, and it is not a population.

None of this means a companion is a safe default for a child. It is not.

The design fix is not a ban. It is teaching a child that this is a tool, not a friend, and building the tool so it says so. That is why DiGi is not built to be liked, missed, or waited for. It hands the child back to the room. A friend keeps you. A tool returns you.

## DAY 4 — Scaffold, do not complete
**Angle:** Protect play. The one design test.
**Format:** Text post.
**Best posting day:** Thursday.

**Post text:**

In November 2025 a GPT powered teddy bear was pulled from sale after it gave a child dangerous instructions. A soft toy, with a large language model inside it, talking to a four year old.

This is In Proportion, continued.

I do not lead with that to frighten you. I lead with it because it is the cleanest example of the one test that actually sorts the good AI from the bad, and it is not the test we usually apply.

We ask, is it safe. Better question. Does it scaffold, or does it complete.

A tool that scaffolds prompts the child to do the thinking, then gets out of the way. A tool that completes does the thinking for them and hands back the finished thing. Same technology. Opposite effect on a childhood. One hands the crayon back. The other draws the picture and calls it help.

The Joan Ganz Cooney Center, the people who have studied children and media since Sesame Street, put it plainly in 2026. AI has four new properties. It is interactive, personalised, adaptive and intimate. And absent a developmental lens, every one of those properties defaults to extraction. Built to hold the child, not to hand them back. The teddy bear was not a freak. It was those four properties with nobody minding the design.

This is why the child advocacy group Fairplay, backed by more than 150 experts, went after AI toys specifically. Not screens in general. The toys that sit closest to the youngest children and are built to be loved.

None of this means every AI toy is a hazard, or that a child should never touch these tools. That is the ban reflex, and it fails.

It means you get to ask one question of anything you put in front of them. When my child finishes with this, have they built a muscle, or borrowed a crutch. Protect the play. Protect the boredom the play grows out of. Choose the tools that give the work back.

Scaffold, do not complete. That is the whole design test.

## DAY 5 — The design difference [CARRIES THE CTA]
**Angle:** Schools. The same AI doubles or erodes learning.
**Format:** Text post. This is the pack's single direct CTA, routed to /starter-pack.

**Post text:**

A guard railed AI tutor built at Harvard more than doubled how much a class of students learned, compared with a well taught active learning lecture. A different AI system, studied across millions of students, cut how much they retained by about a quarter. Same underlying technology. The only thing that changed was the design.

This is In Proportion, continued.

Hold those two results next to each other, because together they end the is AI good or bad for learning argument for good.

The first is a randomised trial, Kestin and colleagues, published in Scientific Reports in 2025. 194 students. A purpose built tutor with real guard rails, designed to make the student do the work, produced more than double the learning of a strong in person class. That is not a small effect. That is enormous.

The second is a 2026 working paper analysing the ALEKS system across more than 4 million students. Retention fell by about a quarter, and the loss was worst for the oldest students. Same category of tool. Opposite result.

So AI does not have an effect on learning. A design has an effect on learning. One was built to hand the thinking back to the child. The other let the child hand the thinking away. Everything rides on which one a school actually buys.

You can watch the same truth in child safety. Roblox rebuilt its systems so that chat which leaks personal information is intercepted and rephrased, cutting the misses by around twentyfold, and its grooming detector now scans roughly 6 billion messages a day and fed about 1,200 reports to the child protection authorities in half a year. Design did that. Not a ban. A better machine.

None of this means schools will pick the good design by default. Most of the market is the second kind, the kind built to complete the work, not scaffold it. The pressure has to come from parents and heads who know to ask.

## DAY 6 — The equity harm nobody costs out
**Angle:** The hidden thread stated plainly. Poverty, adverse childhood experiences, parental mental health, CAMHS capacity and equity are the real drivers. AI gets the attention because it is visible and rich.
**Format:** PDF carousel. Caption text below, slide breakdown at the foot.
**Best posting day:** Wednesday of the following week.

**Post text:**

There are 550,610 children on a mental health waiting list in England right now. The service meant to help them, CAMHS, runs on roughly 1 percent of the health budget.

This is In Proportion, continued.

I want to say the quiet part plainly in this one, because it is the thread running under the whole series.

The things that most shape how a child fares are not new and do not photograph well. Poverty. The hard things that happen to a child at home, the ones researchers call adverse childhood experiences. The mental health of the adult raising them. And whether there is any help to be had when a family reaches for it. Those are the heavy levers. They are old, they are dull, and they are expensive to move.

AI is none of those things. It is new, it is visible, and it is rich. So it gets the attention, the headlines and the legislation, while the actual drivers sit in the dark where they always have.

Watch where the money goes and you can see the swap happening in real time. The government's own impact assessment puts the cost of online safety compliance at 2.12 billion pounds over a decade. Roblox alone cut its bookings growth guidance from 22 to 26 percent down to 8 to 12 percent after bringing in age checks. Enormous sums and effort, aimed at the visible thing. Meanwhile the Royal College of Psychiatrists reports those half a million children on waiting lists are being quietly pushed toward chatbots, because there is nothing else free at 2am.

And the children carrying the most are the ones already carrying the most. Internet Matters finds 71 percent of vulnerable children, those with special needs or a health condition, use chatbots. 90 percent of neurodivergent young people game, and half of them do it to make friends. The tool is not their problem. It is often their only bridge.

None of this means the money on online safety is wasted. Some of it is doing real good.

It means the proportion is wrong. The visible cause gets the billions. The strongest drivers get the scraps and a waiting list. If we spend the decade fighting the thing in the child's hand, the thing in the child's home will still be there, untouched, when we look up.

Cost out the equity harm. It is the biggest number nobody puts on a slide.

We have spent this whole series on what the researchers found. Let me end it on the group we keep talking over. The children themselves.

That is the whole of it. Listen to the count. Then listen to the children. They have been telling us the answer while we argued about the ban.
