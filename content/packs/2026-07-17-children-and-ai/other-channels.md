## MUMSNET
**Board:** Parenting

**Title:** I read the actual research on kids and AI this week and it is not the story I expected

I have a 12 year old who uses AI for homework and a younger one who mostly wants to chat to it, and I have been quietly worried about all of it. This week I stopped reading the headlines and read some of the studies instead, and I feel a bit steadier, so putting it here in case it helps.

Two things are true at once and I think that is the bit that gets lost.

The first is genuinely frightening and I do not want to soften it. The Internet Watch Foundation assessed just over 8,000 AI made child abuse images last year, and AI abuse videos went from 13 to more than 3,000 in a year. Girls are 97 percent of the victims. That is a real, counted harm and it is worth every bit of alarm. I have got much stricter about what the younger one can reach and who can reach her.

The second thing surprised me. The same tools that do that harm can also just be tools. A Harvard trial found children learned more than twice as much with a properly guard railed AI tutor. But another study found that when the AI just hands over answers, retention drops by about a quarter, and it was worst for teenagers and made no difference at all for the little ones. So it is not the machine that decides. It is what the child is actually doing with it. Being tutored, or being given the answers. Those are two different activities wearing the same app.

On the companion chatbots, which is what I was most scared of, the numbers calmed me down rather than the opposite. Most US teens have tried one, but 80 percent still prefer real friends and two thirds find the bot less satisfying than a person. There is a heavy use group that worries me, and there is a study linking heavy use to loneliness, but that was in adults and it was an association, not proof the bot caused it. So I am watching for the heavy, hiding it, cannot leave it alone pattern, rather than panicking that she opened one once.

The thing I have actually changed. I stopped trying to ban my way out of it, partly because when you ask children, only 15 percent think a ban makes them safer and most would rather just know a thing is safe. So we talk about it instead. What did you ask it, what did it say, did that seem right to you. More connection, less policing. That has done more in our house than any block ever did.

Not saying it is fine. The abuse imagery side is as bad as it sounds. Just that for the everyday stuff, watching what my child is doing with it beats fearing the device itself. Anyone else landed somewhere similar, or am I being naive.

## REDDIT
**Subreddit:** r/ScienceBasedParenting (every claim linked)

**Title:** On children and AI the evidence splits cleanly into one severe, well counted harm and a set of diffuse harms that are much weaker than the coverage suggests. Sharing the primary sources.

I have been reading the primary literature on children and AI and wanted to put the strongest recent findings in one place, because the public conversation collapses two very different categories into one panic. Keeping them separate is the whole point.

**Category one, the severe and well counted harm.**

1. AI generated child sexual abuse material is real, rising steeply and deserves the alarm. The Internet Watch Foundation assessed 8,029 AI child abuse images in 2025, and AI abuse videos rose from 13 to 3,443 in a year, with girls making up 97 percent of victims. https://www.iwf.org.uk/about-us/why-we-exist/our-research/how-ai-is-being-abused-to-create-child-sexual-abuse-imagery/

2. Safety by design measurably works but leaks. Roblox reports its facial age estimation plus intercept and rephrase chat systems cut personal information false negatives by roughly 20 times, and its Sentinel system scans about 6 billion messages a day, feeding around 1,200 NCMEC reports in half a year. The same reporting notes verified accounts being resold on eBay within days, so mitigation reduces harm rather than eliminating it. https://about.roblox.com/newsroom

**Category two, the diffuse harms, which are weaker than the framing implies.**

3. The same tool doubles or erodes learning depending on how it is used. A Harvard randomised controlled trial (n=194) found more than double the learning with a guard railed tutor. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-97652-6 A separate ALEKS analysis found retention down about a quarter when AI supplies answers, largest for high schoolers and null for Year 5 (2026 working paper). https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.21629 The variable is the activity, not the device.

4. On companions, teen uptake is high but so is scepticism. Common Sense Media (2025) found 72 percent of US teens have used an AI companion and 52 percent use one regularly, yet 80 percent still prefer human friends and 67 percent find companions less satisfying. Among companion users, about a third find the AI as satisfying as a friend, which is the subgroup worth watching. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/research/talk-trust-and-trade-offs-how-and-why-teens-use-ai-companions

5. The loneliness link is real but not causal and not about children. A randomised study of 981 adults (MIT and OpenAI, 2025) found heavy use tracked loneliness, but the association came from voluntary heavy use rather than the randomised arms, so it is an association, not proof, and the sample is adults. https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.17473

6. UK usage is broad and mostly mundane. Internet Matters found 64 percent of children use chatbots, mostly for homework and curiosity, rising to 71 percent among vulnerable children, and separately that 90 percent of neurodivergent young people game, half to make friends. https://www.internetmatters.org/hub/research/me-myself-and-ai-chatbot-research/

**Context that should shape policy.**

7. Children reject bans and prefer safety. Girlguiding (2025) found only 15 percent think a ban makes them safer, 69 percent would rather know a platform is safe, and 62 percent feel a ban is punishment. https://www.girlguiding.org.uk/about-us/press-releases/social-media-companies-need-to-do-more-to-protect-young-people/

8. Paediatric guidance is measured. The AAP (2026) frames AI as a tool, not a companion, and does not call for a ban. https://publications.aap.org/pediatrics/article/157/4/e2025074912/206753/

9. Displacement risk from a strained system. RCPsych (2026) warns that 550,610 children on mental health waiting lists in England risk turning to chatbots for support. https://www.rcpsych.ac.uk/news-and-features/latest-news/detail/2026/02/11/half-a-million-children-on-mental-health-waiting-lists-in-england-risk-turning-to-ai-chatbots-for-support--warns-rcpsych

**Two caveats I would flag before anyone cites the usual viral items.** The National Academies (2024) verdict on the wider question was mixed and inconclusive. The MIT "Your Brain on ChatGPT" study is an n=54 adult preprint and should be treated as a caveat only. The Character.AI teen death is a single tragic case, not a population level effect, and should not be used as one.

My read is that the evidence supports treating the abuse imagery harm as the genuine emergency it is, while handling the companion and cognition worries calmly and by targeting how children actually use these tools rather than banning the tools. Interested in where people think this is wrong.
