# Children and AI: the two fears, and which one is real

## The issue

In a single year, the number of AI generated child sexual abuse videos assessed by the Internet Watch Foundation went from 13 to 3,443. Not a rise of a few percent. From 13 to more than three thousand, in twelve months.

I want to start there because it is the truth almost nobody says out loud in the AI and children conversation. There are two completely different fears wearing the same word, and we keep mixing them up. One deserves cold, hard alarm. The other deserves a calm cup of tea and a closer look. If you only take one thing from this issue, take the habit of asking which bucket you are in before you feel anything.

### The bucket that earns the alarm

Let me give you the rest of it, because it does not get softer.

In 2025 the Internet Watch Foundation, the UK charity that assesses this material so the rest of us never have to, logged 8,029 AI generated child sexual abuse images. Alongside the video jump I just gave you, that is a category going from a rumour to an industry inside a year. Girls are 97 percent of the victims. And the part that should end any debate about whether this is a real threat or a moral panic: open image models can now be trained on roughly 20 photos of one specific, real child and produce abuse imagery of that child in about 15 minutes. The holiday album. The school play photos. The ones on a relative's public profile.

This is the fear that has earned every bit of its alarm. It is not about your child's screen time or their attention span. It is a safeguarding emergency, it is criminal, and the honest response is not nuance. It is reporting it, backing the people who fight it, and never, ever posting another person's child where a scraper can reach them. Hold that bucket tight. Now let me take the pressure right down on everything else.

### The bucket that mostly needs a calmer head

The other fear is the one filling your feed. Brain rot. Robot friends replacing real ones. A generation talking to chatbots instead of humans. It is loud, it is frightening, and it is nowhere near as settled as the headlines pretend.

Start with the friends. Yes, 72 percent of American teenagers have tried an AI companion and 52 percent use one regularly. Sounds like a takeover. But keep reading the same Common Sense Media study, because the teenagers do not agree. 80 percent still spend more time with real friends than with any bot. 67 percent find the companion less satisfying than talking to an actual person. Even among the teenagers who use these companions, only about a third find the AI as satisfying as a human friend. They are not confused about which one is real. Most of them are, frankly, less impressed than the adults panicking on their behalf.

### What the loneliness study actually found

There is one study everyone reaches for to prove chatbots make children lonely. It is worth handling with care, because it does not say what the headlines say.

Researchers at MIT Media Lab and OpenAI ran a randomised study of 981 adults. The finding people quote: heavier voluntary chatbot use went hand in hand with more loneliness and more emotional dependence, and that clustered in the people who were already attachment hungry going in. All real. But here is the part that gets left off. When the researchers assigned people to different conditions, the conditions themselves showed no effect. In plain terms, that is a strong association from heavy use, not proof that the bot caused the loneliness. It is just as likely that lonely people reach for the bot as that the bot makes them lonely. Anyone telling you this study proved AI causes loneliness has not read past the abstract.

While we are being careful: you will have seen the MIT "Your Brain on ChatGPT" study, the one with the scary brain scans. It is 54 adults, not children, and it is a preprint that has not been through full review. Interesting. Not a foundation to build your parenting on.

### The same tool, twice, opposite results

Here is the finding that reframed the whole thing for me, because it uses the exact same technology and lands in two opposite places.

In a Harvard trial published in Scientific Reports last year, a purpose built, guard railed AI tutor more than doubled how much students learned compared to a normal class. That is 194 students, a proper trial, and the result is genuinely large. Then look at a 2026 working paper that studied over 4 million students on a maths platform. There, AI help cut how much they retained by about a quarter. The loss was worst for the high schoolers, about a third, and it basically vanished for the youngest, undetectable at Year 5.

Same underlying tool. One version made children learn more. Another made them retain less. The difference was not the AI. It was the design. One was built to make a child think. The other was built to hand over the answer. That is the whole ballgame, and it is why "is AI good or bad for kids" is the wrong question. The question is always: built to do what?

### The bot is not winning a fair fight

Now the fact that quietly explains most of the companion panic, and it has nothing to do with technology.

There are 550,610 children on mental health waiting lists in England. That figure comes from the Royal College of Psychiatrists this year. Half a million children waiting, and while they wait, they are being pushed toward chatbots to fill the gap. So when a distressed child ends up pouring their heart out to an AI at two in the morning, understand what actually happened. The bot did not out compete a good service. It walked into a vacuum where the service should have been. That is not an AI story. That is a CAMHS capacity story wearing an AI costume.

I want to name the single tragic case you may be thinking of, the Character.AI teen death, only to say this. It is one family's unbearable loss, and it should change how these products are built. It is not evidence of what happens to a population of children, and it is wrong to use it that way.

### Ask the children, and copy Roblox

Two things point the way out, and neither is a ban.

First, ask them. Only 15 percent of UK girls aged 10 to 16 think a ban would actually make them safer. 69 percent would rather know a platform is safe. 62 percent say a ban feels like a punishment for something adults failed to build properly. That is Girlguiding data, straight from the girls, and it matches what we already know: children mostly use these tools for homework, curiosity and getting unstuck, according to Internet Matters. Not for a robot best friend.

Second, safety by design genuinely works. Roblox open sourced a grooming detector that scans around 6 billion messages a day and fed roughly 1,200 reports to the US child protection centre in half a year. Its system that intercepts and rephrases risky chat cut a specific failure, missed personal information, about twentyfold. That is what building for safety looks like, and it is a world away from a wall. Though keep your feet on the ground: a verified account was resold on eBay within days, so proving who a child really is remains an identity problem no clever filter fully solves.

This is roughly where the grown ups in the room have landed too. The American Academy of Pediatrics reviewed all of this in 2026 and did not call for a ban. Their line is the one I would put on the wall: AI is a tool, not a companion. Developmentally staged. Different rules at 6 than at 16. UNICEF notes children pick these tools up about three times faster than adults do, which is a reason to guide early, not to slam a door they will simply walk around.

### The thing underneath, said gently

Notice where the fear and the funding are pointing. At the visible thing. The chatbot in the child's hand photographs well, it is new, and it belongs to companies rich enough to make headlines.

Now notice what is doing the quiet damage and getting a fraction of the attention. Poverty. The things that happen to a child at home. A parent's own mental health, carried with almost no support. A mental health service half a million children deep in a queue. None of that is the fault of a struggling parent, and I would never let it be read that way. A parent under strain is not failing. They are holding something heavy with too little help. AI gets the spotlight because it is visible and rich. The real drivers sit in the dark because they are old, expensive and awkward to film.

As the developmental psychologist Mimi Ito puts it, it is the activity and the connection that matter, not the device. The screen is rarely the story. It is where the story shows up.

### What a parent can do tonight

One thing. Sit with your child while they use an AI for something real, a homework question, a curiosity, whatever they already do. Then ask them to explain the answer back to you in their own words.

That single move tells you which bucket you are in. If they can rebuild the reasoning, the tool made them think, and you have nothing to fear from it. If they cannot, the tool did the thinking for them, and now you both know it, calmly, without a fight. You are not policing minutes or banning an app. You are teaching the one habit that turns AI from a crutch into a coach: use it, then prove you learned. Do that a few times and your child starts doing it without you in the room. That is the whole job.

I will not promise you it makes children cleverer or happier. Nobody can honestly promise that yet. What it builds is a calmer house and a clear pathway through this, instead of a wall your child climbs the moment your back is turned.

The AI fears are not equal. One is an emergency that needs your fury. The rest need your attention, staged and guided, at exactly the age your child is now.
