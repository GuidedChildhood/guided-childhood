## POST 1 · The two rooms

**Label:** Evidence story, centrepiece, The Wrong Villain series continued
**Format note:** Text post, no image. The two quotes at the top do the visual work. Could become a PDF carousel later (one room per slide) but publish the text version first.

**Body:**

In 2024, challenged by NBC News, Jonathan Haidt put the correlation between social media and poor mental health in girls at "around 0.17" and accepted that the historical timing of the crisis "does not prove causation."

At Davos in January 2025, the World Economic Forum summarised his message as clear evidence that smartphones "caused a mental health pandemic."

Same researcher. Same evidence. Different room.

Before anything else, credit where it is earned, because plenty is.

Haidt raised an alarm most institutions were ignoring. He is right that sleep matters. He is right about free play, where Peter Gray's evidence base has gone essentially unattacked. And his sharpest insight is the collective trap: a 2025 paper in a top economics journal found that 64% of TikTok's young adult users derive negative welfare from the platform's existence and would pay for it to disappear. Children are not freely choosing these platforms. They are trapped by everyone else being on them. That insight survives everything that follows.

Now the split readers of this series already know.

Candice Odgers at UC Irvine, reviewing the book in Nature, wrote that its central claim "is not supported by science", and that hundreds of researchers looking for large effects have found a mix of no, small and mixed associations.

Haidt's counter evidence is real. Unblend the data and the correlation for girls sits at roughly 0.17. He has catalogued genuine experiments and quasi experiments.

And here is the part almost nobody says out loud: the strongest weapons on both sides have documented flaws. The biggest null meta analysis contains documented extraction errors that remain in dispute. His own citation base has been audited and challenged. This is an unresolved standoff, and anyone speaking with certainty in either direction is doing rhetoric, not science.

Which brings us back to the two rooms.

The certainty is the product. And the certainty aims the entire response at one lever: restriction. Delay, remove, ban.

Meanwhile the thing his own framework quietly requires never gets built. No smartphone before 14, no social media before 16. Fine. But if a child is not ready at 15 years and 364 days, what changes overnight? Nothing changes at 16 except the calendar. Readiness has to be built in the years before it, and almost nobody is building that.

And that asymmetry is how the 3% problem ends up with the airtime while the 32% problem stays invisible. Readers of the original Wrong Villain post know that ratio: social media accounts for roughly 3% of child mental health outcomes, child poverty an estimated 32%. Certainty about the 3% sells pouches, books and keynotes. The 32% sells nothing.

Notice what else sells nothing: preparation. Restriction has products, contracts and a revenue line. Teaching a child to actually understand and steer these systems before they arrive at full access has none. That is the gap this series exists to close.

## POST 3 · What changes at 16

**Label:** Education post, school lane crossover, The Wrong Villain series
**Format note:** Text post. Strong candidate for the PDF carousel remake later: the four layers map cleanly to four slides. Also flag as the pack's face to camera option, the four layers spoken to camera in 60 seconds.
**Posting moment:** Friday 10 July, 07:45, end of the school week when heads and PSHE leads are planning September policies.

**Body:**

After three years of phone free school time, psychological consultations among Norwegian girls fell by about 60%.

Bullying fell 46% for girls and 43% for boys. The biggest gains went to girls from the poorest households.

One honest caveat before anyone shares that: it is a working paper, not yet peer reviewed. But it is the strongest number anywhere on Jonathan Haidt's side of the ledger, and he deserves the credit for dragging it into the mainstream.

Because parts of his programme are simply right.

Sleep protected from the phone in the bedroom. Free play restored, where Peter Gray's evidence base has gone essentially unattacked. And the collective trap, his sharpest insight: a 2025 paper in a top economics journal found 64% of TikTok's young adult users derive negative welfare from the platform's existence. No single family can solve that. Coordinated norms can. He is right about all of it.

Then, this year, the first major test of the one policy both camps endorse came back.

The SMART Schools study, published in a Lancet regional journal, compared 1,227 pupils across 30 schools and found no difference in wellbeing between schools with restrictive phone policies and permissive ones. One study, and contested: a rebuttal notes most of the permissive schools still banned phones in lessons. But it points at the question the whole restriction agenda cannot answer.

Taking the phone away does not put anything in the child.

His own framework says no social media before 16. So walk it forward. A child arrives at 16 and gets full access. What do they actually need to be ready? Four layers, and no ban provides any of them.

The business model. These platforms sell attention. A child who knows they are the product reads their feed differently.

The mechanics, per platform, because they differ. TikTok ranks on your behaviour. Instagram ranks on relationships plus behaviour. YouTube ranks on watch time. Snapchat runs on streak reciprocity. Different machines, different lessons.

The exits. How to mute, block, reset a feed, and leave. Knowing the way out changes how you walk in.

Practice. Supervised use that increases with age, where the mistakes happen while an adult is still close enough to talk it through.

That is a pathway: incremental, age based capability, built across years, not granted at midnight on a birthday.

What changes at 16? Nothing, unless someone builds the years before it.

## SUBSTACK ISSUE · The certainty economy

**Subject line options:**
1. The certainty economy
2. What Jonathan Haidt says when nobody pushes back
3. Two million books, one unresolved standoff

**Preview line:** Under challenge he concedes 0.17. On friendly stages the hedges vanish. The gap between those two rooms explains the whole panic, and what it is crowding out.

**Body (approximately 1,150 words):**

In 2024, challenged by NBC News, Jonathan Haidt put the correlation between social media and poor mental health in girls at "around 0.17" and accepted that the historical timing of the crisis "does not prove causation."

At Davos in January 2025, the World Economic Forum summarised his message as clear evidence that smartphones "caused a mental health pandemic." Those are the WEF's words describing his message, and that distinction matters, but the direction of travel between the two rooms is the story of this issue.

I want to walk you through how a book that was disputed in Nature within three days of publication became the most influential parenting text of the decade, what it got right, where the science actually stands, and what all that certainty is quietly crowding out.

**How the book won**

The Anxious Generation was published on 26 March 2024, debuted at number one, has sold more than two million copies on the publisher's figures, and spent roughly 111 consecutive weeks on the New York Times bestseller list. Within about two years, 35 US states plus DC had legislated on school phones, and Australia passed the world's first national under 16 platform law in November 2024, effective December 2025.

None of that happened by accident, and very little of it happened because the science was settled.

The distribution was built years in advance. The Coddling of the American Mind in 2018. Open collaborative review documents from 2019, where he compiled the evidence in public. The After Babel Substack from February 2023, which had built a subscriber base of more than 190,000 before the book landed. Then a saturation launch: the Atlantic cover, the morning shows, endorsements running from Barack Obama to Sarah Huckabee Sanders. And the book's real genius: it converted a contested scientific literature into four norms a parent could execute that same evening, without waiting for the researchers to agree.

Causal humility is owed here too. The wave predates him. France banned phones in schools in 2018. UNESCO called for school phone limits in July 2023, eight months before publication. Wait Until 8th has organised parents since 2017, Smartphone Free Childhood since February 2024. The window was already open. He supplied the door handle, and supplied it better than anyone.

**What he got right**

Plenty, and it needs saying first. He raised an alarm about children's mental health that most institutions were ignoring. Sleep matters, and the phone in the bedroom takes it. Free play matters, and Peter Gray's evidence base behind that half of the book has gone essentially unattacked. Phone free lessons have the strongest independent support in the whole debate: a Norwegian working paper found about 60% fewer psychological consultations among girls after three years of school phone bans, with the biggest gains for the poorest girls, and a Dutch national evaluation found 75% of secondary schools reporting better concentration, though that one is school leader self report with no control group.

His sharpest insight is the collective trap. A 2025 paper in a top economics journal found 64% of TikTok's young adult users and 48% of Instagram's derive negative welfare from the platforms' existence and would pay for them to disappear. Children are not freely choosing these platforms. They are trapped by everyone else being there. That finding justifies coordinated norms all on its own, whatever the effect sizes turn out to be.

**Where the science actually stands**

The causal epidemic claim is another matter. Candice Odgers at UC Irvine, reviewing the book in Nature, wrote that the rewiring claim "is not supported by science" and that hundreds of researchers looking for large effects have found a mix of no, small and mixed associations. A study of 2.4 million people across 168 countries found only small, inconsistent wellbeing changes as the internet spread.

But "no evidence" is false, and he can flatly refute anyone who says it. Unblend the data and the correlation for girls is roughly 0.17. He has catalogued genuine experiments. And the null side's strongest weapon, a much cited meta analysis finding no significant effect, contains documented extraction errors that remain in dispute, just as his own citation base has been audited and challenged.

The honest verdict is an unresolved standoff in which each camp accuses the other of the same sins, usually correctly. Anyone speaking with certainty in either direction is doing rhetoric, not science.

**The venue pattern**

Which is what makes the two rooms so telling. Under challenge, he is careful: "around 0.17" and "does not prove causation" to NBC. Pressed by Tyler Cowen, he engaged his critics by name and put the combined correlation at 0.10 to 0.15. He debated Candice Odgers on the record in October 2024, so nobody should ever claim he hides from challenge.

But by our count, his friendly venues outnumber the adversarial ones roughly six to one. And on the friendly stages, the hedges vanish, all the way to that WEF summary of his Davos message. No mind reading is required to see the pattern. It is dated, sourced and quotable.

**The certainty economy**

Why does the pattern persist? Not, as far as anyone can document, because of greed. He pledged his Substack profits publicly and honoured the pledge beyond its terms, donating $30,000 against roughly $91,200 of first year revenue and publishing the receipts. His Let Grow charity predates the book by seven years.

The explanation is structural, and Amy Orben published it in 2020, four years before the book: technology panic runs a predictable cycle in which political pressure demands certainty and rewards whoever supplies it. The rewards are now measurable. Speaker bureaus list his keynotes at $100,000 to $200,000. Yondr, the phone pouch company, went from $174,000 in government contracts in 2021 to $5 million in three quarters of 2024. Screen consultants charge up to $250 an hour.

There is simply no bestseller market for "the evidence is mixed." The market pays for certainty, and certainty is what we got.

**The question he leaves open**

Here is the cost. The certainty aims everything at restriction: delay to 14, delay to 16, ban, remove. Yet the first major test of the policy both camps endorse, the SMART Schools study in a Lancet regional journal, found no wellbeing difference between restrictive and permissive schools. Restriction buys time. It does not build readiness.

His own framework ends at a birthday. No social media before 16. So what changes at 16? Nothing, unless someone spends the years before it building capability: how the business model works, how each platform's machinery differs, where the exits are, and supervised practice that grows with age. A pathway, not a gate.

Haidt owns restriction. Nobody owns preparation. That is the work.
