**Preview line:** In the last two months, three research teams answered the question parents keep asking about social media. The answers do not match the headlines, and together they change what we should actually do.

---

Two months ago, I would have told you the evidence on children and social media was a mess of contradictory studies you could bend to say anything.

Then three things landed, one after another, and for the first time in years they pointed in the same direction. A national study of school phone bans. An experiment inside the feeds of YouTube, Instagram and TikTok. And the UK regulator, in writing, naming the mechanism at the centre of all of it.

I have spent a week reading the primary sources rather than the coverage. Here is what they actually say, and what I think we do about it.

### One. They took the phones away, and the problem barely moved

Start with the study that should have ended the debate.

This May, six economists across Stanford, Duke, Michigan and Penn published the largest analysis yet of school phone bans, comparing more than forty thousand American schools. In the schools that locked phones in pouches, teachers reported classroom phone use collapsing from 61 percent to 13 percent over three years.

That is close to the largest reduction in a target behaviour you will ever see from a single policy.

And the effect on test scores was, in the authors' own words, close to zero. Online bullying showed no measurable improvement. Student wellbeing actually fell in the first year, and only rose above where it started by year three.

Let me be scrupulously fair, because this matters. It is a working paper, not yet peer reviewed. It is a natural experiment, not a randomised trial. Wellbeing did improve by year three, which is a real gain, and a calmer maths lesson is worth having for its own sake. I am not against a pouch.

But sit with the size of the gap. The single biggest drop in phone use anyone has measured did not carry the mental health outcomes with it. When the cause is removed and the effect stays, the thing you removed was not the whole cause.

So what was?

### Two. The feed reads your child in a single session

The second study answers it, and it is the one that genuinely unsettled me.

Researchers at the University of California, Davis, led by Martin Hilbert and Drew Cingel, built 396 social media accounts and had them behave like children. Every account was registered as an adult. Then the team watched what YouTube, Instagram and TikTok chose to recommend.

The accounts that behaved like eight year olds were served almost seven times more child directed content than the accounts behaving like sixteen year olds. Not after a fortnight of training. After one session.

The platform was told it was dealing with adults. It worked out who was really a child from behaviour alone, and re sorted the feed accordingly, within a single sitting. The researchers named their paper for the finding: these systems have actionable knowledge about child users and at risk teens. Their conclusion was that the platforms personalise, but, in their words, not for the child's protection.

I want to be precise, because precision is the whole point of doing this properly. That almost sevenfold figure is about child directed content, the age targeting, not about harmful content specifically. What it proves is detection and sorting: the system identifies a child's real age and state fast, and tailors to it. That is the load bearing fact. I am not going to stretch it into a harm number it was not measuring.

But hold it against the phone ban result and the picture resolves.

A locked pouch cannot touch any of this. The detection, the sorting, the tailoring, all of it lives inside a system that has already read the child before anyone reaches for a screen time setting. That is why removing the device keeps disappointing the people who stake everything on it. The outcome is being shaped by a mechanism the device is only a window onto.

And it explains the thing almost every headline gets wrong: why the harm is not spread evenly.

If a feed can spot an eight year old's behaviour in one session, it can spot a distressed teenager's behaviour too. A struggling child searches, lingers and reacts differently, and a system built to notice and match behaviour will find that child fastest and feed them most precisely. The average teenager is broadly fine. The already vulnerable one meets a machine that has learned exactly what holds them. Same app. Completely different exposure.

This is the single most important idea in the whole investigation. The harm concentrates. Not across a generation, but in the tail the algorithm can see and the rest of us cannot. It is also why the population level studies keep finding small average effects while individual families live through something far worse. The average is small because it flattens the fine majority and the harmed minority into one number.

### Three. The regulator moved the fight from access to design

The third piece is the UK regulator catching up to the mechanism, in public, this May.

Ofcom asked children about their month online. 73 percent of 11 to 17 year olds had encountered harmful content in the previous four weeks. And when Ofcom traced how it reached them, the biggest single route was not a search or a stranger. It was their own personalised feed. Just over a third, 35 percent, met harmful content simply by scrolling. Ofcom's phrase for it: content feeds are children's primary pathway to harm.

Then Ofcom asked the platforms to make the feeds themselves safer. Snapchat, Meta and Roblox agreed to changes, mostly around preventing strangers contacting children. TikTok and YouTube, whose feeds children live inside most, did not commit to changing how those feeds recommend. Ofcom's written response was that it is deeply concerned by the overall response from industry to its challenge to fix the problem.

Read that as regulator for not good enough.

Here is why I think this is a genuine turning point. For fifteen years the entire public argument has been about access. What age, how many hours, which app, phones in school or not. Ofcom has just moved the argument to design. Not whether a child can reach the feed, but how the feed behaves once they do. It is even exploring new powers to inspect the recommendation systems directly rather than trust the platforms' account of them, with strengthened rules due this autumn. Being fair, those powers are being explored, not yet used, and the platforms would say their feeds are already safe. Let that be tested. The point is the question has changed. Even the regulator now treats the feed, not access, as the primary pathway.

### What we actually do

Braid the three together and the conclusion is not comfortable for either camp.

It is not comfortable for the just ban it camp, because the biggest ban we ever measured left the outcomes almost untouched. And it is not comfortable for the it is all a moral panic camp, because the mechanism is real, the regulator has named it, and it finds the vulnerable child on purpose.

The honest middle is this. The phone is the delivery. The feed is the mechanism. And a mechanism that detects and tailors cannot be beaten by a taller fence alone. It is beaten by a child who can see it working and steer it.

That is the whole of what we build at Guided Childhood, and it is the opposite of panic. A pathway, by age. A four year old learns that screens finish and feelings have names. A ten year old learns that a feed is designed to keep them there, and how to notice when it is. A fifteen year old learns how their actual platforms rank content, where the exits are, and how to reset a feed that has learned the wrong thing about them. Each year the same idea, more sophisticated, until they can read any recommendation system from the outside, including the ones that do not exist yet.

Delay while they are young. Keep the pouch in the classroom if it makes the room calmer. Neither of those is wrong. But neither is the finish line. The goal was never a protected child. It was a capable one, and capability is the only protection that travels with them.

If you want the staged version of this, built for exactly this, that is what we do.

Read the machine. Do not just remove it.
