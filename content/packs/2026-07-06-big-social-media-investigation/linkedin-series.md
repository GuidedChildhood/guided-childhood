## Post 1 · The opener · Three findings, one investigation

**Format:** text post, launches the series. **Slot:** Tuesday morning.

Over the last two months, three separate research teams answered a question parents have been asking for years, and the answers do not say what the headlines say.

Start with the one that should have settled the debate.

A national study of school phone bans, released this May, followed more than forty thousand American schools. In the schools that locked phones away in pouches, teachers reported in class phone use falling from 61 percent to 13 percent in three years. A near total collapse in the behaviour everyone wanted to stop.

The effect on test scores was, in the authors' own words, close to zero. Online bullying did not measurably improve. Student wellbeing actually dipped in the first year before recovering.

Take the phone out of the room and the thing you were worried about barely moves.

That is not an argument for doing nothing. Wellbeing did rise by year three, and a calmer classroom is worth having. But it is a very loud clue. If removing the device does not shift the outcome, then the device was never the whole mechanism.

So what is?

This is the question this series answers, and the other two studies point straight at it. One shows what the feed is actually doing to a child in real time. The other is the UK regulator, this May, naming the mechanism out loud and telling two of the biggest platforms it is not good enough.

Put the three together and you get the clearest picture we have had in years of where the harm really sits, and it is not where most of the noise is aimed.

I am going to walk through all three over the next week, one post at a time, then pull them into one long read.

This is The Big Social Media Investigation, part one.

## Post 2 · The device was not the mechanism

**Format:** text post. **Slot:** Wednesday morning.

61 percent to 13 percent. That is how far in class phone use fell in the American schools that locked phones in pouches, on teachers' own reports, over three years.

If the phone were the cause of the crisis, that is the number that should have fixed it.

It did not. The same national study, six economists across Stanford, Duke, Michigan and Penn, found the effect on test scores was close to zero. No measurable improvement in online bullying. Wellbeing dropped in the first year and only climbed above where it started by year three.

Here is the honest part, because it matters.

This is not a case for letting phones run riot in classrooms. A first year dip that becomes a gain by year three is a real gain. Fewer phones in a maths lesson is worth having on its own terms. And a working paper built on a natural experiment is strong evidence, not the last word.

But notice what the ban could and could not reach.

It reached the room. It did not reach the feed.

A locked pouch changes where a child cannot scroll for six hours. It changes nothing about what the recommendation system learned about that child the evening before, or what it will serve them the moment the pouch opens at 3pm. The pouch is a fence around the delivery van. The engine is somewhere else entirely.

That is the whole reason removal keeps disappointing the people who pin everything on it. You can take away the device and leave the mechanism completely untouched, because the mechanism was never the plastic rectangle. It is what the plastic rectangle connects to.

Which raises the obvious next question. What exactly is the feed doing that a locked pouch cannot touch?

That is part three, and it is the one that genuinely surprised me.

This is The Big Social Media Investigation, part two. Follow Justin Phillips for the next one.

## Post 3 · The mechanism · The feed already knows

**Format:** text post, the centrepiece. **Slot:** Thursday morning.

Researchers at the University of California, Davis, built 396 fake social media accounts and taught them to behave like children.

Then they watched what YouTube, Instagram and TikTok decided to feed them.

The accounts that behaved like eight year olds were served almost seven times more child directed content than the accounts behaving like sixteen year olds. Not after weeks. After a single session.

Read that again. The platform was told every account was an adult. It worked out who was really a child from behaviour alone, in one sitting, and re sorted the feed around that. The researchers, led by Martin Hilbert and Drew Cingel, put it plainly in their title: these systems have actionable knowledge about child users and at risk teens.

Actionable knowledge. The platform knows. It could act. The study's finding is that it personalises, in their words, but not for the child's protection.

Now hold this next to the phone ban result and the whole picture snaps into focus.

A locked pouch cannot reach any of this. The detection, the sorting, the tailoring, all of it happens inside a system that has already read the child by the time anyone thinks about screen time limits. That is why removing the device keeps failing to move the outcome. The outcome is being shaped by a mechanism the device is merely a window onto.

And this is also why the harm is not spread evenly, which is the part almost everyone misses.

If a feed can detect an eight year old's behaviour in one session, it can detect a distressed teenager's behaviour too. The child who is already struggling behaves differently, searches differently, lingers differently. A system built to notice and match that behaviour will find the vulnerable child fastest and feed them most precisely. The average teenager is roughly fine. The already vulnerable one meets a machine that has learned exactly what holds their attention. Same app, completely different exposure.

That is where the real harm concentrates. Not across a generation. In the tail the algorithm can see and the rest of us cannot.

This is The Big Social Media Investigation, part three. Part four is the regulator, this May, naming this exact mechanism and telling two platforms it is not good enough.

### Author first comment
One honest boundary on this study, because precision is the point. The almost seven times figure is about child directed content, the age targeting, not about harmful content specifically. It proves the system detects and sorts by a child's real age and state within one session, which is the load bearing finding. It does not by itself measure harm. I am keeping those two separate on purpose, because the moment we exaggerate is the moment we lose the parents who are checking.

## Post 4 · The regulator caught up

**Format:** text post. **Slot:** Friday morning.

This May, the UK regulator said the quiet part in public.

Ofcom asked more than three thousand children about their month online. 73 percent of 11 to 17 year olds had seen harmful content in the previous four weeks. And when Ofcom looked at how it reached them, the single biggest route was not a search, not a stranger, not a dark corner of the internet.

It was their own feed. 35 percent, just over a third, met harmful content while scrolling the personalised feed built for them. Ofcom's own phrase: content feeds are children's primary pathway to harm.

That is the mechanism from part three, confirmed by the regulator, at national scale.

Then Ofcom did something more interesting than another warning. It went to the platforms and asked them to make the feeds themselves safer for children. Snapchat, Meta and Roblox agreed to changes, mostly around stopping strangers contacting children. Useful.

TikTok and YouTube, the two whose feeds children live inside most, did not commit to changing how those feeds recommend content. Ofcom's response, in writing: we are deeply concerned by the overall response from industry to our challenge to fix the problem.

Deeply concerned is regulator language for this is not good enough.

Here is why this is the turning point, and why I am spending a week on it.

For fifteen years the entire public conversation has been about access. How old, how many hours, which app, phones in schools or not. Access, access, access. Ofcom has just moved the fight to design. Not whether a child can reach the feed, but how the feed behaves once they do. It is even exploring new powers to look inside the recommendation systems directly, rather than take the platforms' word for it, with strengthened rules due this autumn.

The regulator has stopped arguing about the pouch and started asking about the engine. That is the right question. It is the one this series has been building to.

## Post 5 · The pathway · What you actually do

**Format:** text post, carries the CTA and the newsletter link. **Slot:** Monday morning.

Five posts, three studies, one conclusion. Here is the whole investigation in four lines.

Remove the device and the outcome barely moves (40,000 schools, close to zero on test scores). The feed reads your child in a single session and sorts itself around them (UC Davis, almost seven times more child directed content to younger behaving accounts). The regulator now agrees the feed, not access, is the primary pathway to harm (Ofcom, 73 percent, this May). And the harm concentrates in the child the algorithm can see and you cannot.

So what does a parent do with that, tonight, without panicking?

You stop treating the phone as the enemy and start treating the feed as a system your child can learn to see.

Because here is the thing the whole investigation quietly proves. If the mechanism is a recommendation system that detects and tailors, then the durable protection is not a taller fence around it. It is a child who can recognise what the feed is doing, name it, and steer it. A locked pouch protects a child for six hours. Understanding the machine protects them for life, including every new platform that does not exist yet.

That is not a lecture you deliver once. It is a pathway, built by age. A four year old learns that screens finish and feelings have names. A ten year old learns that a feed is built to keep them there and how to tell. A fifteen year old learns how their specific platforms rank content, where the exits are, and how to reset a feed that has learned the wrong thing about them. Same idea, rising each year, until they can read any algorithm from the outside.

Removal has its place, especially when they are young. But the goal was never a protected child. It was a capable one.

This is where Guided Childhood lives. Not fear, not a ban, a staged pathway that teaches children to live well with the machine instead of pretending we can take it away.
