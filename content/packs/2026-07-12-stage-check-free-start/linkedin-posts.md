## POST 3 — The number nobody puts in the headline (evidence brick)

**Pillar:** evidence story. **Format:** text, strong candidate for a PDF carousel later. **Post:** Wednesday.

Across more than 350,000 young people, screen use explained less than half of one percent of the difference in their wellbeing.

That is the finding from Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski, published in Nature Human Behaviour in 2019, at Cambridge and Oxford. As Candice Odgers at UC Irvine summarised the wider picture, roughly 99.7 percent of the variation in adolescent depression is down to other things.

That number is not zero, and I will not pretend it is. For some children the harm is real and serious. But look at where it lands. The evidence from Odgers, Orben, Przybylski and Chris Ferguson keeps pointing the same way: the damage is not spread evenly across all children, it concentrates in the ones already carrying more, the vulnerable tail.

Two children can spend the same three hours on the same phone and walk away completely differently, because they did not start from the same place.

Which is the whole reason one rule for every child at every age was never going to be enough. Your child is not the average in a study. They are at a specific stage, with specific ground already in place and specific gaps.

## POST 4 — What ten minutes actually looks like (script)

**Pillar:** script and practical. **Format:** text. **Post:** Thursday.

"Give me one more level." You say no. It escalates. Ten minutes later you are both upset and the tablet is still on.

Most evenings the screen fight is not really about the screen. It is about the handover having no shape. So here is the shape, and it takes about ten minutes, not an evening.

One. Name the finish before it starts, not during. "Two more goes, then the tablet sleeps in the kitchen." Decided at the start, the ending stops being a negotiation.

Two. Make the off ramp worth more than the level. In our house the jobs and the going outside earn the most, so screen time is the thing that already did its job, not the thing you fight to end.

Three. Trade the lecture for one question at bedtime. Not why were you on it so long. Try, what was the best thing you saw today. You learn more, and so do they.

None of this is about taking the phone away. It is about the years before 16 having a structure, so independence when it comes is a step up and not a fall.

The right version of these three is different for a 6 year old and a 13 year old. That is what the stages are for.
