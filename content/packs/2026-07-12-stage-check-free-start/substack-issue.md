Sixteen is the cliff edge.

Under the new law, a child in Britain has no access to the named social media platforms until they turn 16. Then, on one birthday, all of it opens at once. The feed, the infinite scroll, the whole machine, handed to a young person who has spent years being kept away from it and no time being taught to read it.

Nothing, nothing, nothing, then everything. I keep coming back to that shape, because it is the thing almost nobody is talking about. We have argued for two years about whether to build the wall. We have barely started on what happens the day it comes down.

I am a dad of three in Bath, and I run a small platform called Guided Childhood. I am not a researcher. I spent six months reading the primary literature on children and screens because I could not find a straight answer to a simple question, and I want to share where that left me, because it is calmer than the version your feed is selling you.

## What the evidence actually says

Start with the number that never makes the headline. Across three national datasets and more than 350,000 young people, Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski, at Cambridge and Oxford, found that digital technology use accounted for less than half of one percent of the difference in wellbeing between children. Candice Odgers at UC Irvine, reviewing the wider field, put it plainly: roughly 99.7 percent of the variation in adolescent depression is explained by other things.

That number is not zero, and I will never pretend it is. For some children the harm is real, and serious, and worth every ounce of a parent's worry. But here is the part that changes what you do about it. The harm does not spread evenly across all children. The work of Odgers, Orben, Przybylski and Chris Ferguson keeps pointing to the same place: it concentrates in the children who are already carrying more. Two children can spend the same three hours on the same phone and walk away completely differently, because they did not start from the same place.

That single fact is why one rule for every child, at every age, was never going to be enough on its own.

## Why the wall is not the plan

I am not against the law. A ban can buy time and shift a default, and both of those are worth something. But look six months up the road, to Australia, who did this before us.

After their under 16 ban, 4.7 million accounts were removed in a single month. That looks like success until you read the rest. Professor Sander van der Linden of Cambridge, who has advised our own government, said on camera that around 60 percent of children are getting around it, and the most influential children are still on the platforms, so the social norms are not shifting. Two thirds of affected young people in Australia had moved to Discord, Telegram and private group chats within weeks. Not safer places. Less watched ones.

A wall removes one visible thing from a child's environment and leaves every underlying pressure exactly where it was. Then it lifts on their 16th birthday, and the same machine is waiting, designed exactly as it was, for a young person who has had two years away from it and no preparation for it.

The preparation is the part only a parent can do. That is not a criticism of the law. It is just the honest division of labour.

## The ramp

So here is the reframe I have staked everything on. Turn the cliff into a ramp.

A little more freedom at each stage, earned, guided, with the device settings easing on purpose as the judgement grows. So that full independence at 16 is a step up, not a fall off. It is not a lockdown and it is not a free for all. It is a path, walked in small pieces.

In practice it is about ten minutes a day, and it does four quiet things. It protects, with settings set once across every screen and then relaxed deliberately as your child earns it. It teaches, with short lessons that grow real judgement, spotting the advert, the fake, the pull of the feed. It sends them outward, with the household rewards paying the most for going outside, making things, and real conversation. And it reminds you of the one thing worth handling this week, so nothing important slips.

None of it is about taking technology away. Your children will live their whole lives alongside recommendation systems. The job is not to hide the machine from them. It is to teach them to see how it works, steer it, and use it well, one stage at a time.

## One thing you can do tonight

You do not need my platform to start. You need to know where your child is standing on the path, because the right next step for a 6 year old and a 13 year old are not remotely the same.

So the smallest useful thing I have built is a free check. Three questions about your child, and it tells you what stage they are at and the one thing to do next. No sign up, no bill, nothing to install. I would genuinely rather you did that and felt a little calmer tonight than anything else.
