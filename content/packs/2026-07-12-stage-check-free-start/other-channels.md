## MUMSNET — a genuine forum contribution

**Where:** AIBU or Parenting, or a relevant screens or teens thread. **Rule:** first person, humble, no link, invite discussion. If a poster asks what you used, then and only then reply with the link.

**Suggested title:** Anyone else worried less about now and more about the cliff edge at 16

**Body:**

I have been going round in circles about phones and my three, and I think I have finally worked out what was actually bothering me, so wanted to see if it is just me.

It is not really the here and now. We manage the here and now, more or less. It is that under the new law they get nothing until 16 and then the whole lot at once. That is the bit that keeps me up. Two years of it being completely off limits, then on one birthday the full thing, and by then I have not taught them anything about how any of it works because there was nothing to practise on.

I went a bit down a rabbit hole reading the actual research rather than the scare pieces, and the thing that stuck with me is that the harm is not really evenly spread, it lands hardest on the kids already struggling for other reasons. Which weirdly made me feel less panicked and more like the job is just to prepare my specific child, at their specific age, rather than lock everything down or throw my hands up.

So now I am trying to think of it as a slope rather than a cliff. A bit more freedom at each stage, earned, with me actually teaching them to spot the nonsense as we go, so 16 is a step and not a shock.

Does anyone else think about it this way, or am I overthinking it. And if you have older ones who have come out the other side, what did you actually do in the years before.

## REDDIT — evidence first, for r/ScienceBasedParenting or r/Parenting

**Where:** r/ScienceBasedParenting (every claim needs a source, so links stay in), or r/Parenting (softer). **Rule:** neutral, professional, zero promotion, structured for discussion. No product mention unless asked in comments.

**Title:** The under 16 ban lifts access all at once at 16. What does the evidence say about preparing for that, versus the ban itself

**Body:**

With the under 16 social media ban confirmed and landing Spring 2027, most of the discussion I see is about whether the ban is right. I am more interested in a question I cannot find much on: what happens at 16, when access opens all at once, and what the years before that should look like.

A few things from the literature that shaped how I am thinking about it, sources below.

1. Population level effects are small. Orben and Przybylski (2019, Nature Human Behaviour), across three datasets and over 350,000 adolescents, found digital technology use explained about 0.4 percent of the variance in wellbeing. Odgers and Jensen (2020) summarise that roughly 99.7 percent of the variation in adolescent depression is attributable to other factors.

2. Effects are not zero, and they concentrate. The recurring finding across Odgers, Orben, Przybylski and Ferguson is that harm is concentrated in already vulnerable children rather than spread evenly. That points to targeted preparation over blanket rules.

3. Bans displace rather than remove. Australia's ban (Nov 2024) removed 4.7 million accounts by January 2026, but their regulator reported major gaps, an estimated 60 percent circumvention, and large migration to Discord, Telegram and private messaging, which have less moderation, not more.

Putting that together, the case for using the pre 16 years to build digital literacy incrementally seems stronger than either lockdown or hoping the ban does the work. But I would like to be pushed on this.

Questions for the group: is there good evidence on what actually builds durable digital judgement in children, and is there anything on outcomes when restricted access is suddenly removed at an older age. Genuinely after sources, not opinions.

**Sources:** Orben and Przybylski, Nature Human Behaviour 2019. Odgers and Jensen, 2020. Australia figures via eSafety Commissioner reporting and Prof. Sander van der Linden, Cambridge.
