## VIDEO 1 — The clock is not the enemy

The strongest thing I can tell you about screens this year is this. A study followed four thousand two hundred children for four years, and how long they spent on a phone told you almost nothing about their risk. How they used it told you almost everything.

It was published last year in JAMA, one of the most serious medical journals there is. And the researchers did something most screen time studies never bother with. They did not just measure the hours. They measured the shape of the use.

Here is what came back. Total screen time was not linked to suicidal behaviour. But a rising, compulsive, addictive pattern of use roughly doubled the risk.

Think about two children. Same three hours, same app. One is making things and messaging friends and is completely fine. The other is in a spiral and is in real danger. Same number on the report. Two different children.

So the thing we all obsess over, the clock, is the wrong measure. The clock is not the enemy. The loop is. That is the difference between a rule and a relationship. A rule watches the clock. A parent watches the child.

## VIDEO 2 — The fire was lit in 1971

Here is a pair of numbers that quietly dismantles the story everyone is telling.

In 1971, about eighty percent of seven and eight year olds in England walked to school on their own. By 1990, it was under ten percent. The iPhone did not exist until 2007.

So whatever we did to childhood, we started doing it long before a screen could be the cause. We took the walk to school, the unsupervised afternoon, the freedom to get a bit lost and sort it out, and we removed almost all of it.

That is the real point the psychologist Peter Gray has been making for years, and on that, the evidence is on his side.

Now, this does not prove phones do nothing. A generation that had already lost its freedom and its boredom then got handed a device built to fill every empty minute. The phone did not empty the streets. It moved into streets that were already empty.

So you do not win this by taking the phone away and leaving the empty room. You win it by making the room worth being in again. One freedom handed back. One hour that belongs to them. The screen loosens its grip on its own once there is something real beside it.

## VIDEO 3 — What happens the day after the ban

Australia removed four point seven million children's accounts under its under sixteen ban. Then researchers followed the children, and more than eighty five percent were still using the apps.

I am not here to argue about the ban. It is coming here in Spring 2027, that is settled. The useful question is the one nobody is asking. What happens the day after.

Australia is our preview, because they went first. Four point seven million accounts removed. It sounds like a wall. Then they tracked actual teenagers, and most were still on, some through accounts that simply say they are older. Removing the shopfront did not remove the behaviour.

And we have done this before. The comics code in the fifties. The game ratings in the nineties. Both calmed the adults and changed the shelf. Neither reached the child.

A ban is not nothing. Delaying the age a child gets pulled into an engagement machine is sensible. But a ban a child walks around in an afternoon is not a plan. It is a headline. The plan is the boring part. Teaching a child, from primary school up, how these systems actually work, so that by sixteen they step through the gate ready instead of falling off a cliff.

## VIDEO 4 — The man who deleted his own best statistic

A psychologist built part of his new book on a striking statistic. A journalist checked it. He asked his publisher to delete it. That is the most encouraging thing I read all week.

The psychologist is Peter Gray, and he is on the side I mostly agree with, the side that thinks the phone panic is overblown. His figure was that school stress in teenagers had leapt to eighty three percent by 2013. A smoking gun.

Then a journalist actually checked it. The eighty three percent was real, from a 2013 survey of thirteen to seventeen year olds. But the earlier number he set against it came from a different survey. It asked a different question, about worry rather than stress, of a wider age group that included eight year olds. Two surveys that changed what they asked and who they asked cannot be read as one clean jump.

And to his real credit, when this was put to him, Gray asked his publisher to pull the figure.

That is the rule worth keeping. You report the finding that hurts your case as loudly as the one that flatters it. None of this means the harm is imaginary. It means the confident numbers have to survive checking, in both directions.

## VIDEO 5 — The strongest predictor nobody legislates for

The strongest predictor of your child's mental health is not their phone. It is not their school. It is the mental health of the adult raising them. And almost nobody is legislating for that.

That comes from Candice Odgers, one of the calmest researchers in this field. And I want to hold it gently, because it sounds like blame and it is the opposite of blame. A parent under financial strain, or grief, or their own anxiety with a two year wait for help, is not failing their child. They are carrying something heavy with almost no support.

Now look at where the money goes. Six platforms made close to eleven billion dollars in one year from advertising to children. Your child is not the customer. Your child is the product on the shelf.

None of this means phones do nothing. But if we spend the whole decade fighting the thing in a child's hand, we will miss the things doing the real damage.

So look after the adult in the house too. Then build the real world back up, one brick at a time.

## VIDEO 6 — It is not the phones (the short manifesto)

Two famous psychologists, two bestselling books, one crisis, and they cannot agree on the villain. One says it is the phones. The other says it is school. After all of it, the honest scientific verdict is still mixed and inconclusive.

So here is where I have landed, as a parent who read the studies.

It is not the phones. And it is not only the phones. The crisis is a seventy year loss of real childhood, a genuine school pressure problem, and a family and money story underneath both. The phone is an accelerant on a fire that was already lit. It hits the children who are already struggling the hardest.

Which means neither a blanket ban nor a free for all fits what we actually know. A ban treats every child as the one in trouble. A free for all pretends the ones in trouble are not real.

The thing that fits the evidence is quieter. Watch the pattern, not the clock. Support the whole family, not just the device. Build a pathway through this, one brick at a time, so that by sixteen a young person walks into the digital world ready.
