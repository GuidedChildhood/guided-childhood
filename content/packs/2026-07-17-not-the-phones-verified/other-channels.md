## MUMSNET
**Board:** Parenting / Being a parent
**Tone:** genuine parent sharing what they found, no marketing scent, no link

**Title:** I went down a rabbit hole on screen time research and it changed how I do it

I have a 9 year old and I have been in the usual low level panic about phones and screens like everyone else. This week I actually read some of the studies instead of the headlines and I feel a bit calmer, so sharing in case it helps someone.

The thing that stuck with me. A big study this year followed thousands of children for four years and found that the number of hours on a screen barely predicted anything. What did predict harm was the pattern, whether it was tipping into a compulsive, cannot put it down, miserable without it kind of use. Same hours, very different children, depending on the shape of it.

So I have quietly stopped counting minutes and started noticing the pattern instead. Is she calm when she comes off it or agitated. Is it filling a gap where something else used to be. That has been far more useful than any time limit I ever set.

The other thing that surprised me. Independent play and freedom for kids collapsed decades before smartphones existed. Something like 80 percent of little ones walked to school alone in the 70s and it was under 10 percent by 1990. Made me think the screen is partly filling a hole we dug long before phones, so I have been trying to give her a bit more unstructured, unsupervised time rather than just taking things away.

Not saying phones are fine. For a child already struggling they can clearly make things worse. Just that watching the pattern and rebuilding a bit of real world freedom beside it has done more in our house than any ban. Anyone else found the same, or am I overthinking it.

## REDDIT
**Subreddit:** r/ScienceBasedParenting (every claim linked)
**Tone:** evidence first, neutral, structured for discussion

**Title:** The best recent evidence suggests screen time hours barely predict teen mental health, but the pattern of use does. Sharing the studies.

I have been reading the primary literature on adolescents and screens and wanted to put the strongest recent findings in one place for discussion, because the public conversation and the evidence base have drifted quite far apart.

1. Population level effect is small. Orben and Przybylski (Nature Human Behaviour, 2019) pooled 355,358 adolescents across three datasets and found digital technology use explained at most 0.4 percent of variance in wellbeing, comparable to trivial correlates. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-018-0506-1

2. Pattern beats hours. Xiao et al (JAMA, 2025), using the ABCD cohort of 4,285 youths followed four years, found total screen time was not associated with suicidal behaviour, but an increasing addictive use trajectory carried a risk ratio of 2.14. This is the key distinction most coverage misses. https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2835481

3. The best causal study is narrow. Braghieri et al (American Economic Review, 2022) used the staggered Facebook college rollout and found real increases in depression and anxiety, but the sample is college students on 2000s Facebook, so it does not straightforwardly transfer to under sixteens on algorithmic feeds. https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20211218

4. The consensus verdict. The National Academies of Sciences (2024) concluded the literature on social media and adolescent health is "mixed and inconclusive." https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/27396

5. Context that predates phones. Independent childhood mobility fell from about 80 percent to under 10 percent of young children between 1971 and 1990 (Hillman, One False Move, PSI 1990), and caregiver mental health is reported as the strongest single predictor of child mental health (Odgers, Nature 2024). https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-00902-2

Happy to be corrected on any of these. My read is that the evidence supports targeting compulsive use patterns and the wider family and social context, rather than blanket time limits or bans, but I am interested in where people think this is wrong.

I read the actual screen time studies this week so you do not have to, and one finding genuinely changed how I do it at home.

It is not the hours. A big four year study found the number of hours on a screen barely predicted how a child was doing. What mattered was the pattern. Calm use that they can walk away from is very different from the compulsive, cannot put it down kind, even when the hours are identical.

So I have stopped counting minutes and started watching the shape of it. Is he settled when he comes off it, or wound up. Is it filling a gap where boredom or play used to be.

Then instead of just taking the tablet away, I try to put one real thing back beside it. A bit of unstructured time that is his. It works better than any limit I have tried.

Not saying screens are harmless, they are not. Just that watching the pattern beats counting the clock.
