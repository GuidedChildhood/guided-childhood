## POST 1
**Pillar:** Evidence story
**Format:** Text post, near character limit. Also the pack's PDF carousel pick, slide breakdown at the foot of this file.
**Best posting moment:** Week 1, Monday, research reframe slot

A study followed 4,285 children for four years and found that how long they spend on a phone tells you almost nothing about their risk. How they use it tells you almost everything.

This is In Proportion, continued.

The study was published last year in JAMA, one of the most serious medical journals there is. It is part of ABCD, a huge American project that has tracked thousands of children from the age of ten. The researchers did something most screen time research never bothers with. They did not just measure the hours. They measured the shape of the use.

Was it climbing into a compulsive pattern. The kind where a child cannot put the phone down, and feels awful when it is gone.

Here is what came back.

Total screen time was not associated with suicidal behaviour. The number every parent counts, and every headline shouts about, did not predict the outcome that matters most.

But a rising, addictive pattern of use roughly doubled the risk. The exact figure was a risk ratio of 2.14. And nearly a third of the children were on that path by the age of eleven.

Two children. Same three hours on the same app. One is making things and messaging friends and is completely fine. The other is in a compulsive spiral and is in real danger. Same number on the screen time report. Two different children.

None of this means screens are harmless. The addictive pattern is real, and it is dangerous, and the design of these apps is built to create it. That is exactly the point.

But it means the thing we obsess over, the clock, is the wrong measure. Counting minutes tells you almost nothing. Watching the pattern tells you almost everything.

The clock is not the enemy. The loop is.

That is the difference between a rule and a relationship. A rule watches the clock. A parent watches the child.

I am building the thing that helps families watch the pattern instead of policing the clock. It grows with the child and it is built on the studies, not on fear.

## POST 2
**Pillar:** Evidence story
**Format:** Text post, near character limit
**Best posting moment:** Wednesday

In 1971, about 80 percent of seven and eight year olds in England walked to school on their own. By 1990, it was under 10 percent. The iPhone did not exist until 2007.

This is In Proportion, continued.

I keep coming back to that pair of numbers, because it quietly dismantles the story everyone is telling.

It comes from a British study called One False Move. Same survey, same places, one generation apart. In two decades we took the walk to school, the unsupervised afternoon, the freedom to get a bit lost and sort it out, and we removed almost all of it. Before smartphones. Before social media. Before the thing we now blame for everything.

So whatever happened to childhood, we started doing it long before a screen could be the cause.

This is the real contribution of Peter Gray, the 82 year old psychologist who has spent his career arguing that children need genuine, self directed, unsupervised play, and that we have spent seventy years taking it away. On that, the evidence is on his side. Independent childhood collapsed, and it collapsed early.

But that does not let the phone off the hook, and I will not sell you a single villain either.

This does not prove phones do nothing. A generation that already lost its freedom, its boredom, and its unsupervised play then got handed a device engineered to fill every empty minute. The phone did not empty the streets. But it did move into streets that were already empty, and it is very good at keeping children indoors.

The phone is not the fire. The fire was lit in 1971. The phone is what we handed children once the room had already emptied out.

Which changes what you do about it. You do not win this by taking the phone away and leaving the empty room. You win it by making the room worth being in again. One freedom handed back. One unstructured hour that belongs to them. The screen loosens its grip on its own once there is something real beside it.

## POST 3
**Pillar:** Ban commentary (settled background, preparation not debate). CARRIES THE CTA.
**Format:** Text post
**Best posting moment:** Thursday, pegged to Spring 2027 ban readiness

Australia removed 4.7 million children's accounts under its under sixteen ban. Then researchers followed the children. More than 85 percent were still using the apps.

This is In Proportion, continued.

I am not here to relitigate the ban. It is coming here in Spring 2027, that is settled, and arguing about it is a waste of the months we have. The useful question is the one nobody in the headlines is asking. What happens the day after.

Australia is our preview, because they went first. The eSafety Commissioner confirmed 4.7 million accounts were removed or restricted. It sounds like a wall. Then the University of Newcastle tracked actual twelve to seventeen year olds, and more than 85 percent were still on at least one restricted platform three months later. Some through their own accounts. Some through accounts that simply say they are older.

Removing the shopfront did not remove the behaviour. It almost never does.

We have banned scary media before. The 1950s comics code and the 1990s game ratings both calmed the adults and changed the shelf. Youth violent crime actually fell as game sales soared. Neither code reached the child. This will be the third time.

And a ban is not nothing. Delaying the age a child gets pulled into an engagement machine is a real, sensible aim, and I am glad the pressure on platforms exists.

But a ban a child walks around in an afternoon is not a plan. It is a headline. The plan is the boring part nobody legislates, teaching a child, brick by brick, from primary school up, how these systems work, how to see the machinery, and how to steer it. So that by sixteen, when the gate opens, they step through it ready instead of falling off a cliff.

## POST 4
**Pillar:** Evidence story, the honesty move
**Format:** Text post
**Best posting moment:** Week 2, Monday or Tuesday (held back so each week has only one plain thesis landing)

A psychologist built part of his new book on a statistic. A journalist checked it. He asked his publisher to delete it. That is the most encouraging thing I read all week.

This is In Proportion, continued.

The psychologist is Peter Gray, and he is on the side I mostly agree with, the side that thinks the phone panic is overblown. His striking figure was that school stress in American teenagers had leapt to 83 percent by 2013, right after a major schools reform. A smoking gun for the idea that school, not phones, is the problem.

Then Kaitlyn Tiffany, writing in The Atlantic, actually checked it.

The 83 percent was real, from a 2013 survey of thirteen to seventeen year olds. But the earlier, lower number he had set it against came from a different survey. It asked a different question, about worry rather than stress. It asked a wider age group that included eight year olds. Two surveys that changed both what they asked and who they asked cannot be read as one clean jump.

And to his genuine credit, when this was put to Gray, he asked his publisher to pull the figure.

I find that more persuasive than any statistic in the whole debate. Because this is the test I trust.

You report the finding that hurts your case as loudly as the one that flatters it.

That number helped my side. It did not hold up. So it has to go. Everyone who plays it that way, in either direction, has earned your trust. Everyone who quietly keeps the flattering number and buries the inconvenient one has not.

And by my own rule I have to say the rest. None of this means the harm is imaginary. It means the confident numbers have to survive checking, in both directions. Gray's wider point survives too. Ask children directly and school comes first. In the most recent Pew data, 68 percent named pressure over grades, well ahead of looks or fitting in. The stress is real. It was only the tidy statistic that broke.

Two famous men. Two bestselling books. And the most useful thing in the whole story is one of them deleting his own best line.

## POST 5
**Pillar:** Founder story, quiet thesis
**Format:** The pack's 60 second face to camera video pick. A talking head carries the misheard as blame nuance a carousel would flatten.
**Best posting moment:** Week 2, Sunday, founder slot. This is the LinkedIn thesis reveal, staggered a week clear of the newsletter so only one plain landing goes out per week.

The strongest predictor of a child's mental health is not their phone. It is not their school. It is the mental health of the adult raising them. Almost nobody is legislating for that.

This is In Proportion, continued.

That line comes from Candice Odgers, a psychologist at UC Irvine, one of the calmest voices in this field. Caregiver mental health, she says, is by far the strongest predictor of a child's mental health and life chances. Not the strongest we can sell a product for. The strongest, full stop.

I want to hold that carefully, because it can be misheard as blame, and it is the opposite of blame. A parent under financial strain, or grief, or their own anxiety with a two year wait for help, is not failing their child. They are carrying something heavy with almost no support. And children read the weather in a house long before they can name it.

Now put that next to where the money and the attention actually go.

One Harvard study found six platforms made close to 11 billion dollars in a single year from advertising to American under eighteens. A single teenager on Instagram is worth somewhere around 200 to 240 dollars a year in advertising. Your child is not the customer. Your child is the product on the shelf.

So follow the two things at once. On one side, a business model that earns billions from a child's attention and will not fix itself without being made to. On the other, the strongest driver we actually have, the wellbeing of parents, poverty, the things that happen to a child at home, and a mental health service you cannot get an appointment for. One of those is new and photographs well. The others are old and do not. Guess which one gets the headlines and the legislation.

None of this means phones do nothing. For a child already struggling, a screen is often exactly where the distress pools. That is real. But if we spend the whole decade fighting the most visible thing in a child's hand, we will lose it, and the actual drivers will still be sitting there untouched.

The most radical thing you can do as a parent is unglamorous. Look after the adult in the house too. Then build the real world back up beside the screen, one brick at a time.

That is the whole idea behind what we build. Not a wall. A pathway you walk with them.
