## The issue

Here is a number that should have ended a lot of arguments by now.

Take the three biggest datasets we have on teenagers and screens, put 355,358 young people together, and ask how much of the difference in their wellbeing is explained by how much they use digital technology. The answer is at most 0.4 percent. To put that in plain terms, the researchers who ran it, Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski at Oxford and Cambridge, pointed out that technology use is about as tied to a teenager's wellbeing as whether they regularly eat potatoes. Wearing glasses is slightly more strongly linked. Getting enough sleep matters far, far more.

I want to be careful here, because that number gets misread in both directions. It does not say phones are harmless. It says that across all children, on average, the total amount of screen time is a tiny part of the story. And if you build a national policy on a tiny part of the story, you should not be surprised when it does very little.

So if it is not the hours, what is it?

### The clock is not the enemy, the loop is

This is the finding that changed how I read every screen time headline.

Last year, a study came out in JAMA, one of the most serious medical journals in the world. Researchers followed about 4,285 American children from the age of ten for four years, part of a huge long running project called ABCD. They did not just measure how long children spent on their phones. They measured the shape of the use. Was it climbing into a compulsive, addictive pattern, the kind where a child cannot put it down and feels awful without it.

Two things came back. Total screen time, the number every parent counts and every headline shouts about, was not associated with suicidal behaviour. But an addictive, compulsive pattern of use roughly doubled the risk. The exact figure was a risk ratio of 2.14. And nearly a third of the children were on that rising addictive path from the age of eleven.

That one distinction changes how you parent this. The clock is not the enemy. The loop is. A child can spend two hours making things and messaging friends and be fine. Another child can spend the same two hours in a compulsive spiral and be in real danger. Same number on the screen time report. Completely different children.

Counting minutes tells you almost nothing. Watching the pattern tells you almost everything. That is the whole difference between a rule and a relationship.

### The fire was lit long before the phone

Now the part that should reframe the entire debate.

There is a famous piece of British research called One False Move. In 1971, about 80 percent of seven and eight year olds in England were allowed to walk to school on their own. By 1990, that had fallen to under 10 percent. Same survey, same places, one generation, and a childhood turned inside out.

The iPhone launched in 2007. So the collapse of independent childhood, the loss of the walk to school, the unsupervised afternoon, the freedom to get a bit lost and sort it out yourself, was already two decades deep before the smartphone existed. Whatever we did to childhood, we started doing it long before we could blame a screen.

This is the real contribution of Peter Gray, the 82 year old psychologist at the centre of that Atlantic piece. He has spent his life arguing that children need genuine, self directed, unsupervised play, and that we have spent seventy years quietly taking it away. On that, the evidence is firmly on his side. The phone did not empty the streets of children. It moved into streets that were already empty.

### Watch how each side handles an inconvenient fact

Gray and Jonathan Haidt, the author of The Anxious Generation, agree on almost everything except the villain. Haidt says it is the phones. Gray says it is school and lost freedom. They co founded a charity together, and they have not spoken in two years.

Here is how you spot the flaw in both of them at once. Teen suicide actually fell by around 40 percent between 1990 and about 2007, right as home computers and games arrived. Gray, who likes the digital world, reads that fall as proof games were good for children. Haidt, who dislikes it, explains the same fall a completely different way, suggesting it might have been the phasing out of leaded petrol.

Candice Odgers, a psychologist at UC Irvine and one of the calmest voices in this whole field, names the trick exactly. No serious epidemiologist, she says, explains an improvement with one single cause and then a decline with a completely different single cause. Trends that move across seventy years and dozens of countries and wildly different technologies are never the fingerprint of one thing. They are the fingerprint of many things at once. Insist on one villain and your solution comes out simple and usually wrong.

### The number that fell apart

Gray built part of his new book on a striking figure. School stress in American teenagers, he said, jumped to 83 percent by 2013, right after a major schools reform, and that looked like a smoking gun.

Then the journalist checked it. The 83 percent was real, from a 2013 survey of thirteen to seventeen year olds. But the earlier figure he set against it came from a different survey that had asked a different question, about worry rather than stress, of a wider age group that included eight year olds. Two surveys that changed both their wording and who they asked cannot be read as one clean jump. To his real credit, when this was put to him, Gray asked his publisher to pull the figure from the book.

I tell you this because I am on the side that thinks the phone panic is overblown, and that number helped my side, and it did not hold up, so it has to go. That is the only rule worth keeping in an argument like this. You report the finding that hurts your case as loudly as the one that flatters it. Everyone who does that has earned your trust. Everyone who does not, in either direction, is selling you something.

His wider point survives, by the way. Ask children directly what weighs on them and school comes first. In the most recent Pew data, 68 percent named pressure to get good grades, well ahead of pressure to look good or fit in. The stress is real. The tidy statistic was not.

### Ask the children

While two famous men argue about children, it is worth asking the children. When you do, they say something strange and consistent. Nearly half of teenagers, 48 percent, think social media is harming people their age. Only 14 percent think it is harming them. They can see the danger everywhere except in their own hand.

That gap is the reason a blanket rule fits so badly. It treats every child as the one in trouble, when most say they are fine and, on the numbers, most are. And it does nothing for the real minority who are genuinely drowning, because for them the problem was never the average child's afternoon.

### Follow the money, and follow Australia

Two facts the psychologists mostly skip.

First, the money. One study out of Harvard found that six major platforms made close to 11 billion dollars in a single year from advertising to American under eighteens. A single teenager on Instagram is worth somewhere around 200 to 240 dollars a year in advertising. Your child is not the customer. Your child is the product on the shelf. That is why polite requests for the platforms to behave never work, and it is the strongest argument for regulating how these systems are designed.

Second, Australia. They brought in an under sixteen ban ahead of us. Platforms removed 4.7 million accounts. And in the first study to follow it, more than 85 percent of the children carried on using the apps anyway. Removing the shopfront did not remove the behaviour. It rarely does.

### The finding underneath all of it

If you take one sentence from this issue, take this one, also from Odgers.

Caregiver mental health is by far the strongest predictor of a child's mental health.

Not the phone. Not the school. The state of the adult raising the child. And I want to hold that gently, because it can be misheard as blame, and it is the opposite of blame. A parent under financial strain, or grief, or their own unsupported anxiety is not failing. They are carrying something heavy with almost no help. Children read the weather in a house long before they can name it.

None of this means phones do nothing. For a child who is already struggling, a screen is often exactly where the distress pools. That is real and I would never wave it away. But look at where the attention and the legislation are going, toward the most visible thing in a child's hand, while the strongest predictors we have, a parent's mental health, poverty, the things that happen to a child at home, and a mental health service you cannot get an appointment for, receive a fraction of the airtime, because they are not new and they do not photograph well.

After two famous psychologists and two bestselling books, the honest scientific verdict on whether social media causes the crisis is still, in the words of the US National Academies of Sciences in 2024, mixed and inconclusive. That is not a dodge. That is the finding.

### What a parent can do tonight

Not a ban. Not a panic. One quiet shift.

Stop counting the minutes and start watching the pattern. Ask what the screen is standing in for. When a child reaches for a device by default, it is usually filling a space where something real used to be. So do not start by removing the screen. Start by rebuilding one small piece of the real world beside it. One freedom you had at their age that you have not yet handed over. One unstructured hour that belongs to them. The loop loosens its grip on its own once there is something else worth doing.

That is the whole idea behind what we build at Guided Childhood. Not a wall between children and technology. A pathway through it, brick by brick, real world richness on one side and guided digital skill on the other, both climbing together, so that by sixteen a young person steps into the full digital world ready, instead of falling off a cliff into it.

The phone was never the whole story. It moved into a room the childhood had already left. Our job is to make that room worth being in again.
