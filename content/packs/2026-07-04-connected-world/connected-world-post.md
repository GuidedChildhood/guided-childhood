**Body (2,784 characters, verified in range 2,400 to 2,950, zero dashes):**

In 2020 the UK government published a complete, age by age map of every digital skill a child needs from 4 to 18.

It contains 330 statements. Almost no parent I have ever spoken to has heard of it.

It is called Education for a Connected World, written by the UK Council for Internet Safety, the government's own advisory forum where the Department for Education, the Home Office, tech firms and child safety charities sit at one table.

Eight strands cover the whole of a child's online life: self image and identity, online relationships, online reputation, online bullying, managing online information, health, wellbeing and lifestyle, privacy and security, copyright and ownership.

Each strand runs through four age bands, from early years to 7, then 7 to 11, 11 to 14 and 14 to 18. Every statement is written in the child's voice, as an "I can".

At age 4 to 7: "If something happens that makes me feel sad, worried, uncomfortable or frightened I can give examples of when and how to speak to an adult I can trust."

By 14 to 18: "I can describe the laws governing online sexual content." And: "I can describe the difference between online misinformation and disinformation."

The same child. Fourteen years. One skill built on the last, the way we teach reading or swimming.

Credit where it is due. This is exactly the right idea: incremental, age based, capability first. The DfE's own guidance on teaching online safety points schools in England to it. And SWGfL built ProjectEVOLVE, which resources every single statement with free lessons and activities.

Here is the honest part. A framework is not delivery.

Ofcom asked children in 2025. 92% of 8 to 17s could recall at least one online safety lesson at school. Only 45% found those lessons very useful. And only around 3 in 10 could recall regular lessons rather than one offs. A one off assembly is not a journey.

And nobody ever handed the map to parents. It was written for schools. But of the 168 hours in a child's week, roughly 130 are spent outside the school gate. The gap between the government's map and the kitchen table is exactly where preparation gets built, age by age, one calibrated step at a time.

This is The Wrong Villain series continued. We pour our energy into arguing about bans while the government's own preparation map gathers dust on a shelf.

And if this is something you agree with, follow Justin Phillips for more. This conversation needs more people in it.

Had you ever heard of this document before today?

One objection worth getting ahead of, because it is the right one to raise. If this framework already exists, and every statement is resourced for free, why does anyone need Guided Childhood?
