# Reply to Anita B. Petersen (in the comment thread)

Context: Anita, a coach and mindfulness facilitator and a 1st degree connection, commented on "Stop Looking at the Phone. Look at the Child." She argued children mirror the mental health of the adults raising them, invoked dysregulated nervous systems and intergenerational trauma, and suggested the title should be "Don't look at the phone. Look at the trauma family legacy."

The reply honours her fully, adds the hopeful evidence, and gently upgrades one word (legacy to pattern) without correcting her in a way that stings. No pseudoscience adopted. No parent blame.

---

## The reply (paste in the thread)

Anita, this is exactly the layer I wanted the post to reach. Thank you for taking it there.

You are pointing at something the evidence backs strongly. The state of the adults around a child is one of the most powerful signals for how that child does. In the UK about half of us are carrying at least one adverse childhood experience of our own, so you are right that a great deal of parenting happens with an old wound still open.

Let me add the part that gives me hope, because it sits in the same research.

It is patterns, not single moments. A child's sense of themselves is built by the repeated way they are spoken to over years, not by one hard sentence on one hard day, which means no loving parent is ever one mistake away from harming their child.

And it is relationships, not genes. When a parent's own depression is treated and lifts, their children's symptoms measurably improve. What passes down a family is mostly learned, and anything learned can be interrupted.

The most freeing finding of all: a child does not need a perfect parent. They need repair after the ruptures, and one steady, caring adult. In the data, that single relationship more than halves the mental health risk for a child who had a hard start.

So I would keep your instinct and soften just one word. Not the trauma legacy, which can sound like a sentence handed down. The family pattern, which is the thing we actually get to change. That is the whole reason I build what I build.

Would genuinely love to keep talking about this.
