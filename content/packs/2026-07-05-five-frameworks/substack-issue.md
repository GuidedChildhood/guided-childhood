**Preview line:** A widely shared post listed five concepts for keeping children safe online. Four of them are real and useful. Here is where they actually come from, and what a family can do with each one tonight.

---

A post went round the parenting internet recently. It listed five concepts for understanding children and social media, each with a confident name, presented as a small body of discoveries.

I read it the way I read everything now, which is to ask one question before I share anything. Where does this come from, and can I check it.

The answer, when I looked, was that the five names traced back to one person's personal brand rather than to any research body, any university, or any peer reviewed work. One of the five was a coined phrase dressed up as a medical diagnosis. That is enough for me to not repeat it, because the entire value of being a calm voice in a loud space is that when I tell you something, you can trust that I checked.

But here is the interesting part, and the reason I am writing to you rather than just scrolling past.

The underlying ideas were mostly good. They were simply borrowed, renamed, and detached from the real, checkable, sometimes decades old work that gives them their weight. So I went and found the real sources. Here are four that will genuinely help you, each with something you can do this week.

### One. It is not an addiction. It is a schedule you can read.

There is no recognised diagnosis called social media dependency. The only screen related condition in the World Health Organisation's manual is Gaming Disorder, added in 2019. Social media addiction is in no manual at all.

That does not mean nothing is wrong. Compulsive use is real for a minority. The WHO's 2024 survey found around 11 percent of teenagers showing problematic use, roughly one in nine, and it is measured by loss of control and real harm, not by hours on a clock. Failed attempts to cut down. Conflict. Neglecting things that matter.

What you can do. Stop counting hours as the measure. Ask instead whether your child can stop when they mean to, and whether the important things are still happening. That is the real signal, and it is a far kinder conversation than accusing a child of an illness they do not have.

### Two. There is a first hour, and it has a script.

If an image of your child ever ends up somewhere it should not, the National Crime Agency's guidance is clear and worth knowing before you need it. Do not pay. Stop contact and block. Do not delete, because it is evidence. Screenshot. Tell a trusted adult. Report it. And the line that matters most, the child is the victim of a crime, not the cause of one.

There is a UK tool most parents have never heard of. Report Remove, run by the Internet Watch Foundation with Childline, lets an under 18 report a nude image of themselves so it can be fingerprinted and blocked across platforms.

What you can do. Tell your child, once and warmly, that if anything online ever goes wrong they can come to you and they will not be in trouble. That single sentence is the foundation every other step depends on.

### Three. Less, with agreement, beats all or nothing.

In a 2023 trial, 220 young people already under emotional strain were split into two groups. One kept unrestricted access. The other held to about an hour a day for three weeks. The limited group ended up feeling measurably better about how they looked. Not zero. Not unlimited. Reduced, by agreement.

The Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health takes the same view. It refuses to set one magic number for every child and asks families to negotiate a limit that fits, and to watch whether screens are crowding out sleep, exercise and friendship.

What you can do. Drop the fight over a total ban or a free for all. Agree a number together, with the reason attached. A limit your child helped set is a limit that survives the moment you leave the room.

### Four. Delay while you can. This is the honest guardrail.

Reduction is not the answer for everything, and I would not trust anyone who told you it was. The alcohol research is the warning. Parents who supplied alcohol to teenagers early, to teach safe drinking at home, did not reduce harm. A six year study of nearly two thousand adolescents found they increased it.

So the rule is not reduce everything from birth. It is delay while the behaviour is still deferrable, then reduce and teach the skills once it is genuinely part of their life. Not yet for the four year old. An agreed hour and the reasons for the fifteen year old.

What you can do. Ask one question about any new access your child wants. Is this still something we can reasonably delay, or is it already woven through their friendships. That answer tells you whether to hold the line or to move to teaching.

---

I put the fifth idea aside, the one presented as a diagnosis, because it is not one. The other four are the spine of how we build our pathway, and now you have them from their real sources, which means you can check every one of them yourself.

That is the whole idea here. Not another voice telling you to be afraid. A voice that shows you the working.

If this was useful, the staged version, delay early and reduce and skill later, mapped to your child's age, is what we build. There is a link in the pathway.
