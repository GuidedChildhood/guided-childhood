## Post 1 · label pillar · There is no diagnosis called Social Media Dependency Disorder

**Format:** text post. Strong candidate to also run as a PDF carousel (slide 1 the claim, slides 2 to 5 the accurate story).
**Best moment:** a weekday morning, the myth is circulating now.

There is a condition doing the rounds in parenting feeds called Social Media Dependency Disorder.

It appears in no diagnostic manual anywhere. Not the WHO's ICD-11. Not the DSM-5-TR. It was coined by a personal brand, not a research body, and repeating it as if it were clinical does parents a quiet disservice.

Here is what is actually true, because the accurate story is stronger than the invented one.

The only screen related behaviour that carries a recognised diagnosis is Gaming Disorder, added to the ICD-11 in 2019 (code 6C51). Social media addiction is in neither manual. The DSM lists Internet Gaming Disorder only as a condition for further study.

That is not me saying nothing is ever wrong.

Compulsive use is real for a minority of children, and it deserves to be taken seriously. The accepted research term is problematic use, and the WHO's 2024 survey of adolescents put it at around 11 percent, up from 7 percent in 2018, higher in girls at 13 percent than boys at 9 percent. Roughly one teenager in nine. Real, measurable, and worth acting on.

But look at how it is measured. The validated Social Media Disorder Scale (van den Eijnden and colleagues, 2016) has nine items and flags a child only when five of the nine are present. Every item is about loss of control and real life harm. Preoccupation. Failed attempts to cut down. Conflict. Deception. Neglecting other things that matter. Not one of them is hours on a clock.

That distinction is the whole point. Leading psychologists have warned for a decade that if you build a checklist looking for addiction, you can turn almost any enjoyable behaviour into a disorder (Billieux and colleagues, 2015). Call ordinary teenage use a dependency and you frighten eight families for every one who genuinely needs help.

So the honest line, the one worth teaching a child, is this. It is not a disease you have caught. It is a designed reward schedule you can learn to read and change. The pull is real because it is engineered to be. And a variable reward you understand is a variable reward you can steer.

That is more accurate than the fear content parents are drowning in. It is also more useful, because you can actually do something with it.

## Post 2 · script pillar · The first hour that matters

**Format:** text post. The six moves become a Digital First Aid PDF card (the strongest lead magnet in this pack).
**Best moment:** midweek, when school safeguarding stories are in the news cycle.

The National Crime Agency's guidance for a child being blackmailed over an intimate image has six moves. The first few take about ninety seconds. Most families instinctively do the opposite of every one of them.

The instinct is to pay, to delete everything, to panic, and to keep it secret. All four make it worse.

Here is what the guidance actually says. Do not pay, because payment marks the child as a target and the demands continue. Stop all contact and block. Do not delete anything, because the messages are evidence. Screenshot what you can. Tell a trusted adult. Report it, to the platform and to the police.

And the line that matters most to a frightened child. They are the victim. The National Crime Agency says it plainly. A child who has been manipulated into sharing an image has had a crime committed against them. They are not in trouble.

There is a UK tool almost no parent knows exists. Report Remove, run by the Internet Watch Foundation with Childline, lets anyone under 18 report a nude image of themselves. It is turned into a digital fingerprint so platforms can find and block it. Childline says UK hosted content is often gone within a few hours.

Now the honest part, because you deserve it.

Mental health first aid, as a field, has strong evidence that it builds knowledge, confidence and the willingness to step in. A large school trial taught it to 1,942 Year 10 students and their intentions to help a struggling friend improved measurably (Hart and colleagues, 2018). But the best independent review of the field, from Cochrane in 2023, is careful. The evidence that it improves the outcome for the person on the receiving end is still low certainty.

So we do not promise a cure. We teach the moves.

That is the difference between first aid and theatre. A parent who knows to screenshot before blocking, who knows the child is the victim, who knows Report Remove exists, is not calmer because we told them it would all be fine. They are calmer because they know what to do in the first hour, before fear makes the decisions.

Panic improvises. Training remembers.

This is The Accurate Story, part two. The point of the series is simple. Children are safer when the adults around them are equipped, not alarmed.

### Author first comment (post immediately)
One clarification, because it matters. Do not delete does not mean do not act. Block the person, absolutely. Preserve the messages, yes. The two are not in conflict. The mistake is wiping the whole thread in a panic and losing the only evidence, then paying because you feel you have no proof and no options. Screenshot first, then block, then report. Ninety seconds, in that order, changes how the next week goes.

## Post 3 · evidence story pillar · The trial no parent has heard of

**Format:** text post. Pairs with a Reduce Not Remove card and a short face to camera version.
**Best moment:** a Sunday evening, when the week's screen time battles are fresh.

In a 2023 randomised controlled trial, young people who capped their social media at one hour a day for three weeks felt measurably better about how they looked.

The sample was 220 young people who were already experiencing emotional distress. One group kept their usual unrestricted access. The other was asked to hold to roughly an hour a day. By the end, the limited group scored higher on appearance esteem and on how they felt about their weight (Thai and colleagues, Psychology of Popular Media, 2023).

Not zero hours. Not unlimited. Less, with agreement.

That is the finding parents almost never hear, because the debate is usually stuck between ban it and let it be. The Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health takes the same middle road. It deliberately refuses to set one universal screen time limit for every child and instead tells families to negotiate a limit that fits their child and to watch whether screens are pushing out sleep, exercise and time with people.

Reduce, do not just remove. It sounds soft. It is actually the harder, more honest position.

Now the part that keeps it honest, because reduction is not the answer for everything.

There is a warning sitting in the alcohol research. Parents who supplied their teenagers with alcohol early, the classic let them try it safely at home, did not reduce harm. They increased it. A six year study of 1,927 Australian adolescents found more binge drinking and more harm among the young people whose parents supplied, not less (Mattick and colleagues, Lancet Public Health, 2018).

So the rule is not reduce everything. It is delay while you still can, then reduce and skill when the behaviour is already part of their life. For a four year old that means not yet. For a fifteen year old it means an agreed hour and the reasons why, not a confiscation that teaches nothing.

Two children, same phone. One has it taken away in a row and learns only that you do not understand. The other agrees an hour, understands why, and keeps the skill when you are not in the room. Same device. Completely different outcome.

This is The Accurate Story, part three. Reduction with a reason beats removal without one, at the right age.

### Author first comment (post immediately)
To get ahead of the obvious question. If parental supply of alcohol backfired, why would an agreed hour of social media not backfire the same way? Because the two behaviours sit at different ages on the same line. Alcohol for a young teen is still deferrable, so delay wins. Social media for an older teen is already woven through their friendships, so removing it removes the friendships too, and reduction plus skill is the realistic tool. The judgement is which side of that line your child is on. That is the whole pathway in one sentence.
