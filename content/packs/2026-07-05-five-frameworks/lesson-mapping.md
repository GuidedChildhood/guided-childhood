# Lesson mapping: four real frameworks across the five stages

Source briefing: `briefings/2026-07-04-five-frameworks-real-sources-v2.html`
Editorial rule for this doc: we teach every idea from its real originator, never from a coined trademark. That sourcing discipline is the product.

This maps the four frameworks that actually stand up (harm reduction, digital first aid, problematic use, the online self) plus media and algorithm literacy onto the Guided Childhood pathway. Each stage teaches the same ideas at rising sophistication, so a child meets a concept as a feeling at four and as a framework at fifteen.

Ofcom media literacy remit and the Education for a Connected World eight strands are named against each stage so a school sees instant alignment.

---

## Stage 1 · ages 4 to 7 · Delay and co use

**The frame for parents:** at this age the honest science is delay, not manage. Optional behaviours are best deferred, and co use beats solo use. We are not handing a small child a feed to "learn to cope".

**What the child learns**
- Feelings have names. We can feel a pull to keep watching, and we can notice it. (Seeds the online self arc: the body signal comes first.)
- One first aid rule, said warmly and often: "If anything online ever makes you feel funny or scared, you tell me, and you will never be in trouble." (The disclosure rule that every later first aid move depends on.)
- Screens are something we do together and then finish. Finishing is normal, not punishment.

**Framework roots:** harm reduction (delay for the deferrable), digital first aid (the disclosure rule).
**Connected World strands:** Self image and identity; Online relationships.

---

## Stage 2 · ages 7 to 10 · The map and the first limits

**The frame for parents:** the child now goes places online. They need a map of what can go wrong that is calm and complete, and their first negotiated limits.

**What the child learns**
- The 4Cs as a simple map: Content you see, Contact from others, Conduct (yours and theirs), and Contract (the deal the app makes to keep you). Four kinds of thing to watch for, not one big scary blur.
- Limits are agreed, not imposed, and they exist to protect sleep and play. The child helps set them, which is why they hold.
- The first aid rule grows a second step: tell a trusted adult, and it is never the child's fault.

**Framework roots:** the 4Cs risk taxonomy (Livingstone and Stoilova), harm reduction (negotiated limits), digital first aid.
**Connected World strands:** Online relationships; Online bullying; Privacy and security.

---

## Stage 3 · ages 10 to 13 · The self check and the online self

**The frame for parents:** this is where a child's own use becomes theirs to steer, and where the performing self online becomes felt. We teach both the honest picture of "addiction" and the tools to see the performance.

**What the child learns**
- The self check, not the label. There is no diagnosis called dependency disorder. What is real is a small pattern of losing control, and it is spotted by loss of control signs, not by hours. The child runs a short honest self check and learns the reset moves.
- The online self arc, taught as a story, not a lecture: the strain of being watched, the front stage everyone performs, why we say things online we would not say to a face, and the trap of comparing ourselves upward to edited images.
- The full first aid moves for the things that actually happen at this age, including a shared image: do not panic, screenshot, do not pay, block, tell a trusted adult, and use the real UK tools.

**Framework roots:** problematic use (the self check, loss of control not hours), the online self lineage (Du Bois, Goffman, Suler, Festinger), digital first aid (full moves and real tools).
**Connected World strands:** Health, wellbeing and lifestyle; Self image and identity; Managing online information.

---

## Stage 4 · ages 13 to 16 · Harm reduction proper and the safety plan

**The frame for parents:** the teenager is living the full online life. Now harm reduction is the right tool: reduce and skill, because the behaviour is happening and cannot simply be switched off. This is where the pathway earns its keep.

**What the child learns**
- Reduce, do not just remove. A concrete target the teen owns, plus the negotiation that keeps it real. Reduction with agreement beats confiscation.
- A personal safety plan the teen writes: their warning signs, their reset moves, the two adults they would tell, the tools saved on their phone. First aid becomes self directed.
- Algorithm literacy that goes past knowing. Each platform ranks differently, so the teen learns the exits, the reset and the settings for the platforms they actually use, not a generic "be careful online".

**Framework roots:** harm reduction (reduce and skill), digital first aid (self directed safety plan), media and algorithm literacy.
**Connected World strands:** all eight, revisited at depth; Copyright and ownership and Managing online information come forward.

---

## Stage 5 · ages 16 plus · The literate adult

**The frame for parents:** the goal was never a protected child, it was a capable young adult who can see the machinery and steer it for life.

**What the young person learns**
- They can read any new platform's incentives from the outside, because they learned four platforms deeply, not one rule.
- They can support a younger sibling or friend with the first aid moves and the calm the pathway gave them.
- They hold the honest position: population level harm is small and concentrated in the already vulnerable, design should be regulated, and the individual skill is to steer the machine rather than fear it.

**Framework roots:** all four, held as one settled outlook.

## The two database scripts this produces

**Digital First Aid card** (DiGi Squad style, stage calibrated): a memorable action plan for the shared image and the sextortion attempt. Do not panic, screenshot, do not pay, block, tell a trusted adult, report. Genuinely useful the day a family needs it. Younger stages get the disclosure rule only; older stages get the full plan and the real UK tools.

**Reduce Not Remove card:** the reduction target and the negotiation, not confiscation, framing. Introduced at Stage 4, previewed as "agreed limits" from Stage 2.

## The DiGi rule this locks in

DiGi never says "you are addicted" (a label) and never says "it is fine" (an allow). It names the variable reward, runs the self check, teaches the first hour moves, and points to the real tools. It never repeats "dependency disorder". This is non negotiable rule one, in evidence form.
