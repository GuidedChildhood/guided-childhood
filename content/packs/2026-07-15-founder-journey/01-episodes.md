# THE LAST STRETCH — the ten episodes

Every documented fact below is real, pulled from the build record. Every **YOUR TRUTH HERE** beat is yours to tell, and I have not invented it. Cold open lines are written for you to say to camera; change any word so it sounds like you out loud.

---

## EPISODE 1 — The day I noticed my daughter had changed

**Cold open (first 2 seconds):** "I did not set out to build a company. I set out to understand what happened to my daughter."

**The story.** Open on the ordinary. A normal house, a normal week, and one small moment where something was different in your own child. This is the emotional origin. It is also, deliberately, the title of a reel you already had in your own roadmap and never filmed. So film it.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE.** Only you can tell this. What was the moment? Not a statistic, a specific afternoon. What did you notice, what did you first assume, what did you feel as a parent before you knew anything. Keep it small and real. The smaller it is, the more every parent watching sees their own kid.

**The struggle / why people watch.** Every parent has had a version of this moment and been told the same thing: it is the phones, take them away. You did the thing almost nobody does. You did not believe the easy answer, and you went to check.

**The cliffhanger:** "Everyone told me it was the screens. So I spent two years finding out if that was even true. What I found made me angrier than the screens ever did."

**Caption idea:** The day I noticed my daughter had changed. Part 1 of the last stretch before we launch.

## EPISODE 2 — Two years of research, and the thing nobody is saying

**Cold open:** "Back in 2012, a paper predicted that social media would become the next thing we blame our children's problems on. That was published fourteen years before the ban."

**The story.** You went looking for the evidence and found a gap between what the data says and what the country is arguing about. This is the episode where the hidden thread surfaces, gently, once. Poverty, adverse childhood experiences, and the mental health of the adults in the home are the biggest drivers of a child's mental health. The screen is real, but it is a smaller piece than the noise suggests. You are not dismissing harm. You are pointing at proportion.

**Real material to use.** The 2012 prediction that social media would be the next folk devil (you cite it as the origin of the thesis). Your own framing: the biggest share of children's mental health outcomes traces to poverty and adverse experiences and parents' own mental health, and those get the least attention because they are old and they do not photograph well. Name the researchers you actually stand on so it never sounds like opinion: Candice Odgers, Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski, Chris Ferguson. The standard you hold yourself to: Sonia Livingstone, let the evidence do the work.

**The struggle / why people watch.** This is the turn that makes you different from every other parenting account. You are the calm one in a room full of alarms, and you have the receipts. Say the quiet part: the thing hurting children most is the thing we fund least.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE (light).** What was the single finding that stopped you? The one that made you go, if this is true, then everyone is building the wrong thing.

**The cliffhanger:** "If it was never really about the hours on the screen, then everyone was building the wrong tool. So I decided to build the right one. With no money, no team, and a daughter watching."

**Caption idea:** It was never the hours.

## EPISODE 3 — So I decided to build the opposite of a panic

**Cold open:** "The ban takes the apps away at 16. Nobody is asking the obvious question. Ready for what, and built how?"

**The story.** The idea crystallises into a line and a shape. Not a wall, a pathway. Social media arrives at sixteen, and readiness is built from age four, one stage at a time. You introduce DiGi, the character, the friendly face of a calibrated pathway that never just says no. You are bootstrapping it, alone, at night.

**Real material to use.** The positioning lines that survived every rewrite: "Social media arrives at 16. Ready is built from age 4," and "arriving with habits beats arriving with rules." The philosophy: education not eradication, the intervention order (design regulation, algorithm accountability, sequenced digital literacy, rebuilt outdoor play). DiGi never allows or denies. It always returns a calibrated pathway. Founder rate capped at fifty, enforced in the code, not just the copy, because you refuse to fake scarcity.

**The struggle / why people watch.** Building a real platform, alone, bootstrapped, while holding down a life. The romance of the impossible to do list.

**The cliffhanger:** "I had a plan for a world that did not exist yet. Then on the fifteenth of June, the government did the one thing that could either make this whole idea, or break it."

**Caption idea:** Not a wall. A pathway.

## EPISODE 4 — Then the government did the one thing that could break it

**Cold open:** "On the fifteenth of June, the UK confirmed a full social media ban for under sixteens. And I realised I had been building for it before I knew it was coming."

**The story.** The ban lands. The world you were building for arrives overnight. Here is the twist that is genuinely true and genuinely cinematic: you had already built a configuration flag for exactly this, before the announcement. A switch in the code, built on day one, that let the whole product respond to a ban without a rewrite. You had architected for a future you could not have known was weeks away.

**Real material to use.** 15 June, full access ban confirmed for under sixteens on the named platforms, on the Australian model, regulations due end 2026, ban live Spring 2027. The `social_media_law` flag, built on the very first day of the project, flips from speculative to central. Suddenly you have weeks, not years.

**The struggle / why people watch.** The clock starts. Vindication and terror in the same breath. You were right, and now you have almost no time to prove it.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE (light).** Where were you when you heard? What did the first hour feel like, knowing you had built for this and now had to actually deliver it?

**The cliffhanger:** "I had built for the ban before it existed. But building in public, alone, with real users already on the site, is a special kind of chaos. And that week, I nearly lost all of it in a single day."

**Caption idea:** I built for a ban I did not know was coming.

## EPISODE 5 — I nearly lost a week and a database in one day

**Cold open:** "Two things went wrong on the same day, and either one could have ended it. This is the messy part nobody puts in the launch video."

**The story.** The warts and all engine room. This is the episode that earns trust because it shows the failure. Building at speed, with help running in parallel, two streams of work silently built the exact same thing twice, a whole week of effort duplicated. And on the same stretch, you discovered the live database, with real users on it, had quietly fallen ten steps behind where it should have been. Both were caught. Both were fixed. Calmly. But it was close.

**Real material to use.** The duplicate build (two sessions both told "continue the build," both built the same phase, one closed as superseded), which is why there is now a hard rule that the visible draft is the single lock everyone checks. The database discovery: the live project had only ever run a fraction of its migrations, and you ran the full catch up by hand, then verified every script and lesson was intact.

**The struggle / why people watch.** This is the honesty that makes people root for you. Founders who only show the wins are forgettable. The one who shows the near miss and the calm fix is the one you trust with your kid.

**The cliffhanger:** "I patched it, verified every record, and went to bed. The build survived. But the thing I was most proud of, the front door, still was not good enough. So I tore it down. Five times. In one day."

**Caption idea:** The part nobody puts in the launch video.

## EPISODE 6 — I rebuilt the front door five times, chasing something human

**Cold open:** "My own note on my own homepage was two words. Basic, and AI looking. So I deleted it and started again. Then again. Then again."

**The story.** The craft obsession episode. You would not ship a front door that felt like a template. Five rebuilds in a single day, chasing the warmth of the parenting voices you admire and the clarity of the apps you love. This is where taste becomes the product. It is also lighter, a breather after the near disaster, and it shows the standard you hold.

**Real material to use.** The hero rewritten five times in one day to your live briefs. The verdict that drove it: "basic and AI looking." The reference points, clarity like Duolingo, premium like Apple, warm like the best parenting brands. The font swap to Nunito for "Duolingo level clarity." No black backgrounds. DiGi closing every lesson with a golden star. The rule for the printed pupil booklets: a child should want to keep it. And the smallest, most telling rule of all, no dashes anywhere, ever, because the details are the difference.

**The struggle / why people watch.** The relatable madness of caring too much about something small. Every maker recognises the 2am "no, again."

**YOUR TRUTH HERE (light).** Why does the detail matter so much to you? What are you actually afraid of when something looks "AI looking"?

**The cliffhanger:** "It finally looked like something a real person made with love. It was beautiful. And then a real person tried to use it, and the entire thing went down."

**Caption idea:** Basic and AI looking. Start again.

## EPISODE 7 — The night the whole thing went down, during a fundraise

**Cold open:** "The site was live for the fundraise. Then it returned one error, on every single page, and I had no idea why."

**The story.** The disaster episode, and it is real, because it happened this week. The whole site went down. Every page. And it turned out the deploys had been quietly failing for a week, so a week of work all shipped at once and buried the very pages you needed live for the fundraise. The waitlist that investors were meant to see was gone. You spent the night pulling it apart, found the cause, rolled the whole site back by hand, and split the platform onto its own home so it could never take the front door down again. You got it back at 2am. Warts and all, on camera.

**Real material to use.** The whole site returning a server error on every route. Deploys silently failing for a week on a plan limit, so the frozen site suddenly caught up and overwrote the live pages. The waitlist and evidence pages gone during a fundraise. The calm triage: find the last good version, roll production back to it, lock it so it holds, then give the new platform its own address. Fixed live, at night, one step at a time.

**The struggle / why people watch.** This is the most human episode in the series. Everything on the line, at the worst possible moment, and a founder quietly refusing to panic. It also, quietly, is the mission in miniature: do not panic, find the proportion, fix the real cause.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE.** What went through your head at the moment you saw every page was down and the fundraise was live? Be honest about the fear. That is the episode.

**The cliffhanger:** "I got the site back at two in the morning. I thought that was the hardest thing that would happen that week. Then someone tried to discredit me publicly, and it had nothing to do with the code."

**Caption idea:** Every page. One error. Fundraise live.

## EPISODE 8 — Then someone tried to discredit me, and it made the mission clearer

**Cold open:** "The message said, follow the money. It implied I only believe what I believe because I am selling something. I sat with that for a day before I answered."

**The story.** The attack episode. Someone came at your credibility, the classic move: you sell to parents, so of course you say the panic is overblown. You did not fire back. You held the evidence based line, generously, and you refused to name or attack the person. This is where the Sonia Livingstone standard becomes a character trait, not a slogan. Being accused of a motive is what happens when your argument starts to land.

**Real material to use.** The "follow the money" conflict of interest attack (keep the person anonymous, that has always been the rule). Your response discipline: never name or attack the personal brand, correct generously, lead back to the pathway. The principle underneath it: your position does not depend on your product, the evidence stands on its own, and you can prove it. The moral panic point stated precisely, proportionality, never denial of harm.

**The struggle / why people watch.** Conflict is story. And the way you handle an attack, with grace and receipts, tells people exactly who you are.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE (light).** How did it actually feel to be accused of only believing this because it pays? What did you want to say, and what did you say instead?

**The cliffhanger:** "The attack did not shake the mission. It sharpened it. Because the people it is really for are not on social media arguing. They are in classrooms. And the classrooms were the biggest fight left."

**Caption idea:** They said follow the money. So I did.

## EPISODE 9 — Beat Twinkl, Oak and Jigsaw combined, or it does not matter

**Cold open:** "I gave myself one instruction for the schools build. Do not stop until this is better than everything a teacher already uses, combined."

**The story.** The mission scales. This is where the story widens from one family to every child, and especially the ones the hidden thread is really about, the vulnerable kids who are not going to be reached by a parent buying an app. Those kids are reached at school. So you built a full curriculum, a lesson engine, an educator dashboard, and the compliance backing a school actually needs, aiming to beat the tools teachers already trust. And you sent real lessons to real kids.

**Real material to use.** The instruction, beat Twinkl, Oak and Jigsaw combined. The full multi module curriculum built out, the lesson engine, the premium educator dashboard, the compliance hub built as live documents to clear the real blockers, star lessons delivered to children. Schools as a separate product because the mission does not stop at the parents who can pay.

**The struggle / why people watch.** The ambition is huge and slightly mad for one bootstrapped founder, and that is the point. It also quietly answers the "you only care about paying parents" charge from the last episode, the schools work is where you reach the children who need it most.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE (light).** Why schools, when it would be easier to just sell to parents? What is the child you are picturing when you build the schools side?

**The cliffhanger:** "The platform was ready. The curriculum was ready. Fifty founder places, capped in the code so I could never cheat it. There was one thing left to do. Open the doors."

**Caption idea:** Better than everything they already use. Combined.

## EPISODE 10 — Fifty spots. This is where you come in.

**Cold open:** "This is the last one before we launch. So I want to tell you the truth about who this is really for."

**The story.** The payoff. You bring the series home and, for the second and final time, you say the quiet part fully out loud: the thing hurting children most is not the screen, it is poverty and adversity and the weight the adults are carrying, and this platform is the calm, staged, evidence led answer to a panic that keeps aiming at the wrong thing. Then you make the ask. Fifty founder places, real, capped in the code, not a fake countdown. The people who watched from episode one are the first fifty.

**Real material to use.** The founder 50, enforced in code. The two doors (a no card trial and the founder subscription). Every call to action routes to the starter pack. The mission, stated plainly this once: education not eradication, ready built from age four, the real drivers named. The gratitude to the people who watched the build.

**YOUR TRUTH HERE.** Speak to your daughter, or to the parent you were in episode one. What do you want to have built by the time she needs it? End on that, not on the product.

**The cliffhanger (into launch, and beyond):** "The apps arrive at sixteen whether we are ready or not. I would rather my daughter, and yours, arrived with judgement than with a countdown timer. The doors are open. Fifty of you. Come build it with me."

**Caption idea:** Fifty spots. Warts and all. Come build it with me.

## Series mechanics (so it actually performs)

- **Post cadence:** two a week across the last five weeks, or daily in a final ten day countdown sprint. Number them on screen so the series feels like a series.
- **Every episode ends on its written cliffhanger line, spoken, then the same end card.** The cliffhanger is the retention. The end card is the brand.
- **Pin the previous episode in the comments** so new viewers can binge backwards.
- **The comment mechanic stays:** invite people to comment a word (KEEP the series, or PATHWAY) to get the evidence and the launch list. That is how watchers become the fifty.
- **The one link, once, in episode 10 and the bio:** guidedchildhood.com/starter-pack.
- **Warts and all is the rule, not the exception.** If something breaks between now and launch, that is episode 11. The realness is the whole point.
