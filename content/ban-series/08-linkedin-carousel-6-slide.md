# THE CAPTION

Social media is the cigarette of our time.

It is the most honest wrong thing we say about our children.

I have spent three years reading the primary research, and I understand why we reach for that line. Sit in a CAMHS waiting room. Talk to the head of year who watched a bright child go quiet over one autumn term. They will tell you, with total certainty, that it is the phones. That certainty is not panic. It is what the frontline sees, and it deserves a better answer than a statistic.

Here is the quieter, truer story.

Across the largest studies ever run, social media is one of the least influential factors in predicting teen mental health. The link is smaller than wearing glasses. For boys there is no link at all. For girls, the data shows depression leads to more use, not the other way around.

And the single most useful number in the whole field is one almost nobody quotes. Late night social media use and disrupted sleep correlate at about 0.36. Social media use and depression correlate at about 0.05. Sleep is roughly seven times the intervention. A device out of the bedroom at 9pm costs nothing and can start tonight.

We are about to ban the thing with the smallest signal, while the things with the biggest signal go unfunded. One school counsellor for every 500 children. A ban no study has ever shown improves a single child's mental health.

Carousel below. Sources on every slide. Read it before you share the next scary headline.

The full evidence review is free, in the comments.

## SLIDE 3 of 6 · THE SIZE OF THE EFFECT

EYEBROW (mono): THE STAT THAT SHOULD END THE DEBATE

HEADLINE: Social media is one of the least influential factors in predicting teen mental health.

FACT: Across 355,000 adolescents, digital use explained under 1 percent of the variation in wellbeing, a link smaller than wearing glasses. For boys, no meaningful link. For girls, depression leads to more use, not the reverse. And no study has ever tested whether switching it off actually helps.

SOURCE: Orben and Przybylski, Nature Human Behaviour, 2019. Odgers, 2025. National Academies of Sciences.
