# THE CAPTION (post body)

Social media is the cigarette of our time.

It is the most honest wrong thing we say about our children.

I have spent three years reading the primary research on kids and screens, and I understand exactly why we reach for that line. Sit in a CAMHS waiting room. Talk to the head of year who watched a bright child go quiet over one autumn term. They will tell you, with total certainty, that it is the phones. That certainty is not panic. It is what the frontline sees, and it deserves a better answer than a statistic.

But the developmental psychologist Candice Odgers has been tracking thousands of teenagers every single day since 2008. Their sleep, their steps, their school records, their words. And the story in that data is not the story we are sold.

Scary stories sell. The more often you hear one, the more you believe it. So here is the quieter, truer one.

Teen violence, drinking and pregnancy are at historic lows. This is the most educated generation in history. And across the largest studies ever run, social media is one of the least influential factors in predicting teen mental health.

The strongest predictor of how a teenager is doing is how the adults around them are doing. Between 2011 and 2021, overdose deaths among parents more than doubled. Adults were in distress. Children were watching.

We are about to ban the thing with the smallest signal, while the things with the biggest signal go unfunded. One school counsellor for every 500 children. A ban that no study has ever shown improves a single child's mental health.

The carousel below lays it out, slide by slide, with the sources. Read it before you share the next scary headline.

The full evidence review is free, in the comments.

## SLIDE 5 · THE STAT THAT SHOULD END THE DEBATE

**Eyebrow (mono):** THE SIZE OF THE EFFECT

**Headline:** Social media is one of the least influential factors in predicting teen mental health.

**Fact:** Across 355,000 adolescents, digital technology use explained under 1 percent of the variation in wellbeing. The size of the link sits near eating potatoes or wearing glasses. Real, but tiny, and far too small to carry a population wide ban.

**Source:** Orben and Przybylski, Nature Human Behaviour, 2019. National Academies of Sciences expert panel.

**Waterfall image prompt:**

Beside the tiny sliver, drawn at the same tiny scale for comparison, a small charcoal potato and a small pair of round green glasses. Visual punchline is how absurdly small the coral sliver is. Lots of calm empty space.

## SLIDE 6 · THE ARROW POINTS BACKWARDS

**Eyebrow (mono):** WHICH WAY DOES IT RUN

**Headline:** The cause and effect runs the other way.

**Fact:** For boys, there is no meaningful link at all. For girls, the data shows that girls who are already depressed go on to use social media more. Not the reverse. Social media does not meaningfully predict future mental health problems. Distress comes first.

**Source:** Odgers longitudinal studies, 2025.

A faint ghosted greyed out arrow shows the wrong direction everyone assumes. The correction is the point. Clean, diagrammatic.

## SLIDE 7 · THE SOLUTION NOBODY TESTED

**Eyebrow (mono):** THE MISSING EVIDENCE

**Headline:** Not one study has ever tested whether a ban improves a child's mental health.

**Fact:** Adults are converging on banning social media for under 16s as the single fix. There is not one study to date showing that switching it off helps. When the same off switch is tested on adults, the effect is close to indistinguishable from zero.

**Source:** Odgers, 2025. National Academies of Sciences.

The emptiness where the proof should be is the subject. Minimal, pointed.

## SLIDE 8 · THE REAL PREDICTOR

**Eyebrow (mono):** WHO IS ACTUALLY STRUGGLING

**Headline:** The strongest predictor of a teen's mental health is the mental health of the adults around them.

**Fact:** We are in the middle of an adult mental health crisis. Between 2011 and 2021, overdose deaths among parents more than doubled. People ask what changed besides social media. The answer is that adults were in distress and parents were dying.

**Source:** Odgers, 2025 (US data).

## SLIDE 9 · WHY THE CIGARETTE FRAME BREAKS

**Eyebrow (mono):** THE ANALOGY FAILS

**Headline:** A cigarette and a feed are not the same animal.

**Fact:** A cigarette harms everyone who uses it. A feed harms a vulnerable minority. A cigarette has no upside. A feed is, for some isolated kids, the only safe room they have. The harm in a cigarette is the substance. The harm in a feed is the design, and design can be changed without removing the child.

**Source:** Guided Childhood evidence review, 2026.

## SLIDE 10 · WE ARE PUNISHING THE VICTIMS

**Eyebrow (mono):** WHO PAYS FOR THE BAN

**Headline:** We are kicking kids out of the rooms they go to for help.

**Fact:** A ban pushes children into less safe, less regulated spaces, and removes the places they gather to find friends and, often, to escape harm offline. Meanwhile there is one school counsellor for every 500 children, and the money goes on pouches to lock up phones instead of on people.

**Source:** Odgers, 2025 (US ratio). Guided Childhood evidence review.

## SLIDE 11 · THE MIRROR

**Eyebrow (mono):** THE QUESTION UNDERNEATH

**Headline:** The algorithm did not invent any of this. It learned it from us.

**Fact:** It did not teach a teenager to compare herself to a stranger. It is trained on what we click, and we click the worst story every time. The feed is a mirror with a volume knob. It reflects how we already act and turns it up. Banning the mirror does not change the face.

**Source:** Guided Childhood evidence review, 2026.

## SLIDE 12 · WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS (CTA)
