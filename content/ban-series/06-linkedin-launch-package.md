## PART 2 — THE ARTICLE

**Title:**
What the evidence actually says about the under-16 social media ban. Three evidence bases. One conclusion.

**Subtitle:**
Two Cambridge professors who advised the government said on camera that the evidence base is not there. I want to show you why they are right and what should have happened instead.

---

I am Justin. I am a parent of three children. I am not an academic. I am the founder of Guided Childhood, a platform that helps families build digital literacy and resilience. I have spent two years reading the primary research on children and social media.

I want to be precise about what I am saying before you read this, because this topic attracts a lot of heat and very little light.

I am not saying children are fine. They are not.

I am not saying social media is harmless. It is not.

I am not saying the government should have done nothing. Something needed to happen.

What I am saying is this: the ban as implemented does not match the mechanism of the harm. And two Cambridge University professors, both of whom advised the government directly, said so on camera when the bill passed. One said the evidence base was not there. The other said she would not expect a meaningful improvement in mental health.

They said this. On camera. After advising the government. The government heard it and acted anyway.

Here is why that matters. And here is what should happen instead.

---

**We have been here before.**

Before I get to the research on social media, I want to name something that the current debate is ignoring.

We have done this before. In the 1990s and early 2000s, following a wave of school shootings in the United States, politicians in multiple states passed laws restricting the sale of violent video games to minors. The concern was real. Children were dying. Parents were frightened. Something had to be done.

Multiple US states passed legislation. In 2011, the US Supreme Court reviewed the evidence and ruled against California's law. The judgement was explicit: the evidence for causal harm from violent video games was insufficient to justify a restriction on constitutional grounds. The research that had been cited to support the legislation, primarily Craig Anderson's meta-analyses, was found to have significant methodological weaknesses. Subsequent independent meta-analyses by Christopher Ferguson, published between 2009 and 2017, found no robust causal link between violent game exposure and violent behaviour.

Those laws are now gone. The panic was real. The evidence base did not support the conclusion.

I am not saying the social media situation is identical. I am saying that moral panics about new technology are not new, that legislation based on insufficient evidence is not new, and that the pattern of acting before the science is settled is one we have seen before and should name clearly.

---

**What the evidence base actually shows.**

Six major systematic reviews and meta-analyses, published between 2014 and 2019, examined the association between digital technology use and adolescent mental health. Their findings are consistent across all six.

Huang (2017): meta-analysis across 67 samples, 19,652 participants. The mean correlation between social networking site use and psychological wellbeing: r = 0.07. That is not a typo. 0.07 across the full range of wellbeing measures combined.

Orben and Przybylski (2019), published in Nature Human Behaviour, ran 3.2 billion analytical approaches across three national datasets totalling over 350,000 adolescents. Digital technology use accounted for 0.4% of the variation in wellbeing. As a comparator: wearing glasses accounted for more variance in adolescent wellbeing than social media use in this dataset.

The most frequently cited paper used to justify this ban is Twenge et al. (2018), drawing on the Monitoring the Future Study. A re-analysis of the same data found that social media use accounted for less than 1% of variation in depressive symptoms. As Odgers and Jensen (2020), in the most comprehensive review of this literature, summarise: 99.666% of the variation in adolescent depressive symptoms was due to other factors.

The parliamentary Digital Mental Health Group submitted evidence to Parliament during the bill's passage (SMR0005, public record). They cited zero experimental studies.

---

**The expert who advised the government.**

Professor Amy Orben is a researcher at Cambridge University's MRC Cognition and Brain Sciences Unit. Her research on adolescent technology use is the most cited in this policy debate. Her Nature Human Behaviour paper is one of the foundational documents behind the bill.

When the bill passed, she said on camera: "I wouldn't expect a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term." She described it as "really disappointing" that the government concluded it could not make social media safe through platform design.

Professor Sander van der Linden is a Professor of Psychology at Cambridge and one of the government's own advisers. He said on camera: "I've told the government this over the last year. I know they've heard it from other influential academics and charities that the evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution, but it is not the nuanced one."

He told them. They heard him. They acted anyway.

I am also grateful to Catherine Nibbs [ADD INSTAGRAM HANDLE], who has spent 30 years working directly with children and families on these exact questions. Her work in this space is grounded in what actually happens to real children, not just what the data shows at population level. Both the research and the direct clinical experience point to the same place.

---

**What happened in Australia.**

Australia implemented a comparable ban in November 2024. They are six months ahead of the UK. We have real-world data.

4.7 million accounts were removed in January 2026. Within eight weeks, two thirds of affected children were active on Discord, Telegram and private messaging groups.

Read that again. Two thirds of children who were removed from Instagram and TikTok moved to Discord and Telegram within eight weeks.

Discord and Telegram are not Instagram. They have no content moderation for minors. No safeguarding infrastructure. No age verification. No algorithmic controls on what a child can see. Children who move from Instagram to a Discord server run by strangers have not moved to a safer environment. They have moved to a more dangerous one.

The eSafety Commissioner in Australia reported "major gaps in enforcement" in March 2026. No mental health outcome data has been published. No evidence that the ban improved how Australian children feel has emerged in six months of real-world operation.

Van der Linden on what happened: "60% of kids are getting around the ban, and those kids who are deemed most influential in terms of popularity are still on the platform, which leads other kids to still be on the platform. So the social norms aren't changing."

The ban is not working as intended. The children who most needed it are the ones most likely to have found their way around it. And those who complied have not found safety. They have found Discord.

---

**The alcohol and cigarettes argument.**

When you share this research, someone will say the following: "Even if children find ways around the ban, we do not remove laws just because some people break them. We do not scrap alcohol laws because teenagers drink."

This sounds persuasive. It is not a valid comparison. Here is why, precisely.

Alcohol laws work at the point of supply. A shop, a pub, a licensed vendor. There is a physical supply chain with adult gatekeepers at every node. Minimum purchase age laws reduce consumption because they make supply physically harder. The research supports this. These laws work.

The social media ban works at the point of a login page with an age checkbox. There is no supply chain. There is no adult gatekeeper. A child does not need to physically obtain something from an adult. They need a false birth year and an email address. The circumvention rate in Australia was 60% within eight weeks. This is not comparable to teenage alcohol consumption.

The second problem is displacement risk. When a minor circumvents alcohol laws, they obtain alcohol. The substance and its risk profile are the same. When a minor circumvents the social media ban, they move to Discord and Telegram. The displacement worsens the risk profile. That is the opposite of what happens with alcohol circumvention.

If we apply the alcohol analogy consistently, what it actually supports is: education about the risks, support for those most affected, and design standards on the product itself. In alcohol: PSHE education, addiction treatment, and regulations on labelling, strength and advertising. In social media: digital literacy curricula, support for vulnerable young people, and statutory design standards on how platforms are built for under-18s.

The alcohol argument, applied properly, supports the alternative approach. Not this ban.

---

**What platforms actually need to change. Right now.**

I want to be clear: I am not arguing that platforms are fine. They are not. The systems are not neutral. The design decisions that harm children are not accidents.

Infinite scroll with no endpoint was engineered to maximise time on platform. Variable reward notification systems, the same mechanism used in slot machines, were deliberately applied to adolescent users. Algorithmic amplification of appearance-based social comparison content to 11 to 13 year old girls was not an oversight. These are engineering decisions made by adults with predictable and documented effects on children.

Canada is approaching this differently. Their proposed legislation uses the ban as a lever: restrict access temporarily as an injunction, require platforms to remove the specific harmful features, then restore access when the platforms are safe. No infinite scroll. No autoplay. No messages from strangers. Notifications off between 10pm and 7am. Then the ban lifts.

Van der Linden describes it directly: "We're going to ban social media temporarily as a strategic lever to give social media companies the opportunity to actually make their products safe for kids. When you've done that, we're going to let kids back onto the platforms."

That is a mechanism. That addresses the design. The UK ban does not require platforms to change a single line of code. The machines stay the same. The children are just blocked until 16 and then returned to the same machines with no preparation.

We cannot wait for big tech to act voluntarily. They have had years and they have not. The statutory design standards that would make a real difference:

No infinite scroll on any account with a registered user under 18. No autoplay. No variable reward notifications. No algorithmic amplification of social comparison or appearance-based content to under-18 accounts. No notifications between 10pm and 7am. No like counts visible on posts made by under-18 users.

These are technically achievable. Platforms have the engineering capacity. They need to be required.

---

**Who this actually affects.**

The children most harmed by this ban are not the children most likely to be in the conversation about it.

LGBTQ+ young people in rural and isolated communities. For many of them, social media was the first place they encountered people who understood their experience. The ban does not give them something safer. It gives them nothing. The question of what replaces that function has not been answered.

Children with pre-existing anxiety and depression. Research from Rideout and Fox (2018) found adolescents with moderate to severe depressive symptoms are twice as likely as their peers to turn to social media for emotional support. CAMHS waiting lists are 18 months in many areas. The alternative is not available.

Children in poverty. For families who have watched youth clubs, leisure centres and after-school provision disappear over the past decade, smartphone-based social connection is often the only social infrastructure available. These children will have less to fall back on than children from well-resourced families.

Children of working parents. When two parents are working and a child comes home to an empty house after school, the group chat with their friends is not a luxury. It is connection. Moderate, age-appropriate digital connection, managed well, is not the problem. The design features that make it compulsive and harmful are the problem.

Parents who use digital tools as part of how they educate. YouTube is education for millions of children. The question is not whether children are online but whether they are equipped to navigate it. That distinction matters enormously and is absent from this conversation.

---

**The constant.**

Here is what does not change regardless of which platforms exist, what legislation passes, or what the next moral panic is about.

A child who understands how algorithms work, who can recognise social comparison for what it is, who knows what manipulation looks like, who can use technology rather than be used by it: that child is protected in a way that no ban creates.

The ban handles access until 16. Education handles the 80 years after.

Whatever platform a child is on at 11, or 14, or 17, the skills that protect them are the same. The platform changes. The mechanism does not. A child who has been helped to understand the mechanism is genuinely safer, regardless of what Ofsted reports, what government announces, or what big tech does or does not change.

That is what Guided Childhood is built for. Digital literacy, conversation tools, and a safeguarding advisor for families and schools. Not because the ban is wrong to be concerned, but because the ban alone is not the preparation. The preparation is the preparation.

**An open letter to the Prime Minister on the Children's Wellbeing and Schools Act**

Prime Minister,

The Children's Wellbeing and Schools Act received Royal Assent on 29 April 2026. I am writing as a parent of three children, the founder of a digital education platform, and someone who has spent two years reading the primary peer-reviewed literature on children and social media.

I want to raise three concerns about the evidence base behind Part 3 of the Act, not to relitigate the decision, but because the implementation period between now and Spring 2027 is a genuine opportunity to do the things this legislation did not do.

**The professors who advised you said the evidence base was not there.**

Professor Sander van der Linden, Cambridge University, who advised the government on this legislation, said on camera after Royal Assent: "I've told the government this over the last year. I know they've heard it from other influential academics and charities that the evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution, but it is not the nuanced one."

Professor Amy Orben, also Cambridge, whose research directly informed this bill: "I wouldn't expect a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term."

Both said this on camera. Both had advised your government directly. I believe you heard them. I am asking what was weighed against their counsel.

**The most rigorous evidence in this field points in a different direction.**

Orben and Przybylski (2019), published in Nature Human Behaviour and one of the most cited studies in this debate, ran 3.2 billion analytical approaches across 350,000 adolescents. Digital technology use accounted for 0.4% of the variation in wellbeing.

The Parliamentary Digital Mental Health Group submitted evidence to Parliament (SMR0005) citing zero experimental studies. This is on the public record.

I am not saying adolescent mental health is fine. It is not. But the evidence that social media access is the primary causal mechanism is weak. The evidence that a platform-access ban produces measurable mental health improvements is, as of June 2026, nonexistent. Australia's six months of data does not show improved outcomes. It shows 60% circumvention and documented migration to less regulated platforms.

**The implementation window is the opportunity.**

The ban takes effect in Spring 2027. Between now and then, the children who will be most affected by the ban are in school, with families, with access to the conversations and education that the legislation does not provide.

Three things that would make a material difference before Spring 2027:

First: statutory design standards for platforms. Require platforms to remove infinite scroll, autoplay, variable reward notifications and algorithmic amplification of social comparison content for under-18 accounts before the access ban takes effect. Canada is using this mechanism. Platforms can comply. This addresses the actual harm mechanism the research identifies.

Second: a statutory digital literacy curriculum from Year 3. Education for a Connected World already defines what this looks like. Most schools are not delivering it. Making it statutory, with resource and inspection, would mean children arrive at the ban with the skills to navigate the world they will enter when the ban lifts.

Third: a commitment to measure outcomes. Australia has no baseline. They cannot tell you whether the ban worked because they did not measure wellbeing before and after. The UK has the time to set a baseline before Spring 2027. Commission it now.

I recognise that this legislation reflected a genuine and sustained concern about children's wellbeing. That concern is justified. Children are struggling. The question is whether this mechanism addresses the right target.

With respect and as a parent,

Justin
Founder, Guided Childhood
