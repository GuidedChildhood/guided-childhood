# SERIES ARTICLE 3: The ban has happened. Here is what the data says comes next.

*Justin Farrow, Guided Childhood. June 2026.*

---

Spring 2027 is the date. The law is passed. The debate about whether it was the right decision is not over, but the decision is made.

So let us talk about what actually happens next. Not the press release version. The data version.

---

## What Australia found after six months

Australia is six months ahead of us. Here is what their data shows.

4.7 million accounts were removed in January 2026. That sounds like success. It is not the full picture.

Two thirds of affected users migrated to Discord, Telegram and private messaging groups within eight weeks. These platforms have no content moderation for minors. No safeguarding infrastructure. No statutory obligations. No age verification. The children did not stop being online. They moved somewhere with no one watching.

The eSafety Commissioner reported "major gaps in enforcement" in March 2026. Cambridge Professor Sander van der Linden, who has advised the UK government, confirmed on camera: "60% of kids are getting around the ban, and those kids who are deemed most influential are still on the platform, which leads other kids to still be on the platform. The social norms aren't changing."

No peer-reviewed mental health outcome data has been published from Australia. Six months in and we do not know if a single child's wellbeing improved as a result of the ban.

---

## What the forecasts say for the UK

The UK has 16 million children under 16. Based on the Australia pattern and the peer-reviewed literature on displacement, here is what the evidence suggests is likely.

**In the first six months (Spring to Autumn 2027):**
A reduction in cyberbullying reports on major platforms as activity moves to less monitored environments. This will look like progress. It is not. It is the problem becoming invisible.

An increase in unmoderated contact on Discord, Telegram and private group chats. The children most at risk from adult contact online are now in environments with no safeguarding.

**By month ten:**
Children who used social media to access peer mental health support have lost that access. Children with moderate to severe depressive symptoms are twice as likely as their peers to use social media for emotional support (Rideout and Fox, 2018). They now have nowhere to go.

LGBTQ+ young people in rural and isolated communities report increased isolation. For many of them, social media was the first place they encountered people who understood their experience. The ban does not give them something safer. It gives them nothing.

**At 12 months:**
No statistically significant change in adolescent depression or anxiety prevalence. The NHS CAMHS pipeline is 18 months. The ban does not create capacity.

**At 16:**
The ban lifts. A child who has spent two years excluded from the platforms and given no preparation encounters the same machines with the same algorithmic design. The infinite scroll is still there. The social comparison amplification is still there. The variable reward notification system is still there.

This is the question no one in government has answered: what happens at 16?

---

## What actually protects your child right now

The ban handles access. It does not handle the machines, the literacy or the conversations.

Here is what the research shows actually works.

**Device out of the bedroom at 9pm.**
The strongest effect size in the literature for adolescent mental health is r = 0.36, for late-night social media use and sleep disruption (Scott et al., 2019). Sleep disruption causes depression, anxiety, impaired cognition and physical health deterioration. This intervention costs nothing. It can start tonight.

**Active use over passive use.**
The research distinguishes between passive scrolling (negative associations with wellbeing) and active communication (neutral to positive associations). Teens who sent more text messages reported feeling less depressed than those who were texted less frequently (Odgers and Jensen, 2020). The type of use matters more than the amount.

**Conversations that start young.**
Children encounter screens at four. The conversation about how platforms work, why they are designed to keep us watching, what social comparison feels like and what to do about it, cannot wait until GCSE. It starts now. It runs through every difficult moment.

**Parent engagement as the primary protective factor.**
Odgers and Jensen are clear: children from more supportive and well-resourced homes are more likely to have positive experiences online. The protective variable is not the ban. It is the relationship. Promoting supportive parent-child relationships that encourage disclosure, rather than overly restrictive or coercive monitoring strategies, produces better outcomes in both offline and online contexts.
