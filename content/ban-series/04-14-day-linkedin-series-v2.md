# 14-DAY "WHAT HAPPENS NOW" LINKEDIN SERIES (v2)

*Reordered and rewritten to lead with insight and usefulness rather than critique. Platform accountability and resilience threads run throughout. The alcohol comeback is handled scientifically on Day 9.*

## Day 1 — The real question behind the ban

The government banned social media for under-16s today. Spring 2027 is the date.

The concern driving this decision is real. Children are struggling. The mental health data is genuine and alarming. Any parent looking at their child's relationship with their phone knows something is not right.

The question is not whether the concern is valid. It is.

The question is whether this mechanism addresses it.

Professor Sander van der Linden, who advised the government directly, said on camera today: "The evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution, but it is not the nuanced one."

Professor Amy Orben, whose research directly informed this policy: "I wouldn't expect a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term."

I have published a full research article below. Every claim cites a primary source. Challenge anything.

What I want parents to take from today: the ban handles access. It does not handle your child's preparation for the digital world. That part is still yours.

## Day 2 — The sleep finding

I want to start with the most useful thing in the entire research literature. Not the most controversial. The most useful.

Scott et al. (2019), published in the BMJ, found a correlation of r = 0.36 between late-night social media use and sleep disruption in adolescents.

For comparison: the correlation between social media use and depression, the finding most frequently cited to justify the ban, sits at r = 0.05.

Sleep is seven times the intervention.

Sleep disruption at adolescent developmental stages causes depression, anxiety, impaired prefrontal cortex development, reduced academic attainment and physical health deterioration.

The intervention: device out of the bedroom at 9pm.

This costs nothing. It can start tonight. It does not require a parliamentary act.

If you take one thing from this series, take that.

## Day 3 — What the machines actually do

The ban is real. But here is the thing nobody is talking about.

The infinite scroll is still there.

The variable reward notification system is still there.

The algorithmic amplification of social comparison content is still there.

These are the specific design features that the peer-reviewed literature identifies as the actual harm mechanism. Professor Orben named them today: "those things that make it really hard for us to leave social media, even if we want to."

We can all agree that these features should change. Nobody sensible defends infinite scroll as a children's design requirement. Nobody credible argues that autoplay and variable reward notifications serve children's wellbeing.

The question is whether the UK ban requires platforms to change them. It does not.

Canada's Social Media Safety Act uses the ban differently: temporary restriction until platforms remove these specific features. Infinite scroll gone. Autoplay gone. Messages from strangers gone. Then access restored.

The machines are unchanged. That matters whether you support the ban or not.

---

## Day 4 — The type of use question

Not all social media use is the same. The research makes this distinction clearly and consistently.

Passive scrolling: associated with negative wellbeing outcomes. You consume content. The algorithm feeds you more of what keeps you watching. Social comparison happens. This is where the harm concentrates.

Active communication: associated with neutral to positive outcomes. Teens who sent more text messages over a study period reported feeling less depressed than those who were texted less frequently (Odgers and Jensen, 2020). Peer connection, arrangement of offline meetups, emotional support between friends.

The ban removes both. It does not distinguish.

And here is the resilience question that follows from this: even if we accept that passive scrolling on Instagram is harmful, children will encounter passive consumption loops on YouTube, on gaming platforms, on TikTok alternatives, on whatever emerges next. The mechanism does not live on one platform. It lives in a design pattern.

The child who understands why passive scrolling affects their mood is better protected than the child who was simply blocked from it until 16.

## Day 5 — The conversation that prepares your child

The ban is Spring 2027. Your child is in front of you now.

Here is the age-by-age conversation that the research supports.

At 4 to 7: screens are a choice. We notice how we feel before, during and after. Some things on screens are real and some are made to make us watch more.

At 8 to 10: platforms are designed to keep us using them. Understanding the design does not mean we cannot use it. It means we are in charge of it.

At 11 to 13: social media shows us the best version of everyone else. You are comparing your inside to their outside. The images are edited. The life is curated. What does social comparison feel like in your body?

At 14 to 16: how does the algorithm decide what you see? What is engagement optimisation? How do you design your digital environment rather than be designed by it?

At 16: the ban lifts. The machines are the same machines. The literacy acquired in the years before is what makes the difference.

The ban does not build this. You do.

## Day 6 — Active vs passive use at home

Building on yesterday: there is something practical you can do this week based on the research distinction between active and passive use.

Passive: YouTube autoplay on. TikTok scroll. Instagram explore. The algorithm decides what comes next. This is where the mood effects concentrate.

Active: texting a friend. Planning something together. A group chat about a shared interest. Calling someone. Creating content rather than consuming it.

The practical question is not "how much screen time" but "what kind of screen time."

Two things that change the equation tonight: autoplay off on every platform and device in your household. And one conversation about what your child is actually doing when they are on their phone rather than how long they are on it.

Neither of those requires the ban to work.

## Day 7 — The Australia data, used properly

Six months ago, Australia did this. Here is what their data shows, and what it is useful for.

4.7 million accounts were removed in January 2026. Within eight weeks, two thirds of affected children were active on Discord, Telegram and private messaging groups. These platforms have no content moderation for minors. No safeguarding infrastructure. No age verification.

The eSafety Commissioner reported "major gaps in enforcement" in March 2026.

This is not an argument against the ban. It is a planning document for anyone who cares about what happens next.

If two thirds of children move to less regulated environments when blocked from regulated ones, the question is not whether to ban but what support structure exists in the spaces they move to.

And there is a harder question underneath it: what is the child who moves to Telegram getting that they were getting from Instagram? Often it is connection. Peer support. A sense of belonging. Removing the platform without addressing the underlying need does not address the need.

Education and resilience are the constant regardless of which platform a child is on. That is the point that no policy change takes away.

## Day 8 — What platforms need to change

I want to be clear about something.

I am not arguing that platforms are fine. They are not.

Infinite scroll with no endpoint was designed to maximise time on platform. Variable reward notifications were designed to create compulsive checking behaviour. Algorithmic amplification of appearance-based content targeting 11 to 13 year old girls was not an accident. These are engineering decisions made by adults that have predictable effects on children.

The statutory design standards that would actually protect children:

No infinite scroll on any account with a registered user under 18. No autoplay on any account with a registered user under 18. No variable reward notification systems for under-18 accounts. No algorithmic amplification of appearance or social comparison content to under-18 accounts. No notifications to under-18 accounts between 10pm and 7am.

These are technically achievable. Platforms can implement them. They would address the actual harm mechanism the research identifies.

The ban does not require any of them.

## Day 9 — The alcohol argument

Someone will say this to you: "Even if children get around the ban, we don't remove laws just because some people break them. We don't remove alcohol laws because teenagers drink."

It sounds persuasive. The science says it is not a valid comparison. Here is why.

Alcohol laws work at the point of supply. A shop, a pub, a licensed vendor. The gatekeeper is an adult who can check ID. Minimum purchase age laws demonstrably reduce consumption because they make supply harder.

Social media bans work at the point of platform interface. A login page with an age checkbox. There is no supply chain, no adult gatekeeper, no physical transaction. The circumvention rate in Australia was 60% within eight weeks. The enforcement mechanism is fundamentally different.

The second problem is displacement. When a minor circumvents alcohol laws, they obtain alcohol. The substance is the same. The risk profile is the same. When a minor circumvents the social media ban, they move to Discord, Telegram and private groups where there is no content moderation, no safeguarding and no age verification. The displacement worsens the risk profile. That is the opposite of what happens with alcohol circumvention.

If we take the alcohol analogy seriously, what it actually supports is: education about the risks (the equivalent of PSHE alcohol education), treatment for those most affected, and design standards (the equivalent of labelling, strength limits and advertising restrictions). All of which have direct parallels in digital education and platform regulation.

The alcohol argument, applied consistently, supports the approach in Day 8. Not the ban as implemented.

## Day 10 — The children most at risk

The children most likely to need support through this policy are worth naming.

LGBTQ+ young people in rural and isolated communities. For many of them, social media was the first place they encountered people who understood their experience. The ban does not give them something safer. It gives them nothing. The support they need from trusted adults in schools and families matters now more than it did a week ago.

Children with pre-existing anxiety and depression. Research from Rideout and Fox (2018) found adolescents with moderate to severe depressive symptoms are twice as likely as their peers to turn to social media for emotional support. CAMHS waiting lists are 18 months. The alternative is not available. The question for parents and schools is what replaces that function.

Children in poverty. Smartphone-based social connection is often the only social infrastructure for families who have seen youth clubs, leisure facilities and after-school provision cut for a decade. These children will have less offline infrastructure to fall back on than children from well-resourced families.

These are not arguments against the ban. They are the populations that need the most proactive support in the months ahead. Knowing who they are is the first step.

## Day 11 — The 99.666% statistic, used honestly

The Monitoring the Future Study is one of the papers cited most frequently to justify this ban.

Twenge et al. (2018) found a correlation between social media use and depressive symptoms. That finding was communicated to the public as alarming. It is worth knowing what a re-analysis of the same data found.

Orben and Przybylski (2019) ran 3.2 billion analytical approaches on 350,000 adolescents. Technology use accounted for 0.4% of variation in wellbeing.

As Odgers and Jensen (2020) summarise: 99.666% of the variation in adolescent depressive symptoms was due to other factors.

I want to be precise about what this means and what it does not mean.

It does not mean social media is harmless. It means the effect is small on average across the population.

What Odgers' own research is clear about: effects concentrate in children who are already vulnerable. Pre-existing mental health difficulties. Unsupportive home environments. Limited trusted adult relationships. The child who is already struggling is the child most affected by platform design.

This is why the framework for protecting children has to start with the child, not the platform. The platform is a variable. The child's vulnerability and their relationships are the factors that determine outcome.

## Day 12 — What good policy looks like

I want to be clear about what I am arguing for.

Not the status quo. The concern about algorithmic design, about passive scrolling, about social comparison amplification, is real and documented.

What I am arguing for is precision.

Statutory design standards for under-18 content: the features listed on Day 8. No infinite scroll. No autoplay. No variable reward notifications. No like counts. These are regulatable. Platforms can comply technically.

Sleep protection legislation: no notifications to under-18 accounts between 10pm and 7am. Statutory, not voluntary.

CAMHS capacity: 600 additional therapists would cost approximately £135m over five years. The children in crisis need mental health support. The ban does not provide it.

Digital literacy from Year 3: a statutory curriculum from age 7 to 16 building understanding of how platforms work and how to navigate them. Education for a Connected World already defines this. Most schools do not deliver it.

Longitudinal outcome study: baseline data now, before Spring 2027, so we know whether the ban worked. Australia does not have this. The UK should.

Total cost of targeted package: approximately £305m. Saving versus the ban: £495m. Evidence base: considerably stronger.

## Day 13 — What your school needs to know

The ban handles access. Ofsted will ask about readiness.

KCSIE requires schools to support children's online safety. RSHE requires teaching about healthy relationships including online relationships. The Online Safety Act creates duties around education. DfE AI guidance is incoming.

There is a case that no one is making loudly enough: education is the constant that the ban does not change.

A child will encounter algorithmic amplification, social comparison mechanics, manipulation attempts and identity pressure somewhere in their digital life. The platform that delivers it may change. The mechanism does not. A school that has built genuine digital literacy in its students is ahead of this policy regardless of what happens to the ban or what replaces it.

The school that waited for the ban and called it done will face a very different set of Ofsted questions.

What the curriculum needs to do: not teach children to avoid the internet but to navigate it. Not build fear but build the specific skills that change outcomes. That is what Education for a Connected World is designed for. Most schools are not delivering it yet.

## Day 14 — What I want you to take from all of this

The ban has happened. Spring 2027 is the date.

Here is what I want you to hold onto.

The crisis is real. Children are struggling. The mental health data is genuine and alarming. Anyone who dismisses the concern is not engaging with what parents and teachers are actually seeing.

The platforms need to change. Infinite scroll, variable reward notifications and algorithmic amplification of harmful content to children are not defensible. We can all agree on that.

Education is the constant. Whatever platform a child is on at 11, or 14, or 17, the skills that protect them are the same. How algorithms work. How social comparison affects mood. What manipulation looks like. How to use technology rather than be used by it. No ban changes whether those skills have been built.

The ban is not the preparation. The preparation is the preparation.
