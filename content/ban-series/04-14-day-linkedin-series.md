## Day 1 — The announcement

The government banned social media for under-16s today.

Spring 2027 is the date.

I have three children. I run Guided Childhood. I have spent six months in the research literature on this.

Here is what two Cambridge professors said about it today, on camera.

Professor Amy Orben, who led the most-cited research informing this policy: "We wouldn't expect a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term."

Professor Sander van der Linden, who advised the government directly: "The evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution."

I have published the full three-evidence-base research article. Link in the comments. Every claim cites a primary source. Challenge anything.

What I want parents to take from today: the ban handles access. It does not handle your child's preparation for the digital world. That part is still yours.

## Day 2 — The Australia data

Six months ago, Australia did this.

Here is what happened.

4.7 million accounts removed. Two thirds of affected children on Discord and Telegram within eight weeks. No mental health outcome data published. "Major gaps in enforcement" confirmed by the eSafety Commissioner in March 2026.

The children did not stop being online. They moved somewhere with no one watching.

This is the comparator the government chose as its model.

The question to ask: if Australia is six months ahead, where is the evidence that children are better?

There is none yet. And that matters enormously for whether this policy is what it claims to be.

---

## Day 3 — The machine nobody is regulating

The ban is real. But there is something nobody is talking about.

The infinite scroll is still there.

The variable reward notification system is still there.

The algorithmic amplification of social comparison content is still there.

These are the specific design features that the peer-reviewed literature identifies as the actual harm mechanism. Professor Orben named them today: "those things that make it really hard for us to leave social media, even if we want to."

Every 16-year-old in the country will encounter those same machines in Spring 2027. The ban has not changed them. The ban has not required platforms to change them.

Canada's approach is different. Their Social Media Safety Act uses the ban as a lever: temporary restriction until platforms remove risky functionality. Infinite scroll gone. Autoplay gone. Messages from strangers gone. Then access restored.

The UK has not done this. The machines are unchanged.

## Day 4 — The children most at risk from this ban

The children most likely to be harmed by this policy are the ones it claims to protect.

LGBTQ+ young people in rural and isolated communities. For many of them, social media is the first place they encountered people who understood their experience. The ban does not give them something safer. It gives them nothing.

Children with pre-existing anxiety and depression. Research from Rideout and Fox (2018) found adolescents with moderate to severe depressive symptoms are twice as likely as their peers to turn to social media for emotional support. That support is now gone. CAMHS waiting lists are 18 months. The alternative is not available.

Children in poverty. Smartphone-based social connection is often the only social infrastructure. After-school clubs, youth centres and leisure facilities have been cut for a decade. Social media stayed. Now that is gone too.

Children with autism. Asynchronous text-based communication removes real-time social processing demands. For many autistic young people it is a practised, lower-anxiety social environment. Removal without replacement scaffolding is a loss of something that was working.

These children are not being given something better. They are being given nothing.

## Day 5 — The 99.666% statistic

The Monitoring the Future Study is the paper most frequently cited to justify this ban.

Twenge et al. (2018) found a correlation between social media use and depressive symptoms. That correlation was used to alarm parents, advise governments and ultimately pass legislation affecting 16 million children.

Here is what a re-analysis of the same data found.

That correlation accounted for less than 1% of the variance in adolescent depressive symptoms. As Odgers and Jensen (2020) summarise: "99.666% of the variation in adolescent depressive symptoms was due to other factors."

The same data. The same effect size. Communicated in dramatically different ways to the public.

That is not a minor methodological dispute. That is the difference between proportionate policy and a £800m enforcement programme built on a finding that explains less than one third of one percent of what is happening to children.

## Day 6 — What Haidt actually recommends

Jonathan Haidt wrote The Anxious Generation. His book is the intellectual foundation cited most frequently in support of this ban.

He did not recommend this ban.

His policy recommendations on page 284: remove smartphones from schools (already UK law), age verification for platforms, and redesign the machines. Quote: "changing the default settings of the phone, not removing the child."

The government used his framing to justify a policy he did not recommend. His concern was the design of the machines. The ban does not change the design of the machines.

If you have read Haidt and think this ban follows from his work, read page 284 again.

## Day 7 — The sleep finding

The strongest effect size in the entire social media and mental health literature is not about social media use.

It is about sleep.

Scott et al. (2019), published in the BMJ, found a correlation of r = 0.36 between late-night social media use and sleep disruption. Sleep disruption at adolescent developmental stages causes depression, anxiety, impaired prefrontal cortex development, reduced academic attainment and physical health deterioration.

The intervention with the strongest evidence base is: device out of the bedroom at 9pm.

This costs nothing. It can start tonight. It does not require a parliamentary act, an enforcement agency or £800m.

It is also not what was announced today.

## Day 8 — The type of use question

Not all social media use is the same. The research makes this distinction consistently and the ban ignores it entirely.

Passive scrolling: associated with negative wellbeing outcomes. You consume content. The algorithm feeds you more of what keeps you watching. Social comparison happens. This is where the harm concentrates.

Active communication: associated with neutral to positive outcomes. Teens who sent more text messages over a study period reported feeling less depressed than those who were texted less frequently (Odgers and Jensen, 2020). Peer connection, arrangement of offline meetups, emotional support between friends. These are the functions most used by the children who will lose the most from this ban.

The ban removes both. It does not distinguish. A uniform policy applied to a non-uniform harm profile produces non-uniform outcomes.

---

## Day 9 — The moral panic pattern

Every generation finds fault in the technology the next one adopts.

Video games cause violence. Television causes passivity. Comic books cause juvenile delinquency. Rock music causes moral corruption. Novels cause overactive imaginations.

None of those causal claims held up under rigorous examination. The concern was real. The mechanism was wrong. The intervention was disproportionate. A generation of children was treated as victims of a technology they were simply living alongside.

Odgers and Jensen (2020) acknowledge this pattern directly in their peer-reviewed paper: "moral panic around new technologies is an expected and well established cycle that plays out as new technologies are introduced."

This does not mean the concerns are wrong. It means the mechanism needs to be established before the intervention is built. The mechanism has not been established.

---

## Day 10 — The digital divide question

Children in lower-income families spend twice as much time passively consuming media as children in higher-income families (Odgers, 2018, Nature).

They are also more likely to receive negative spillover from online experiences into offline conflict, trouble at school and social difficulty. They are less likely to receive parental mediation of internet safety.

The ban removes the platform. It does not address the underlying inequality. Children from well-resourced families will have more offline social infrastructure to fall back on. Children in poverty will have less.

"Policies restricting adolescents' access to new technologies may be ill advised if new technologies are being used as a valuable source of social support" (Odgers and Jensen, 2020).

This is in the peer-reviewed literature. It was available before the bill passed.

## Day 11 — What a good policy would look like

I want to be clear about what I am arguing for.

Not the status quo. The status quo was not working. The concern about algorithmic design, about passive scrolling, about social comparison amplification, is real and documented.

What I am arguing for is precision.

Statutory design standards for under-18 content: no infinite scroll, no autoplay, no variable reward notification systems, no like counts. These are regulatable. Platforms can comply technically. The harm mechanism is addressed.

Sleep protection legislation: no notifications to under-18 accounts between 10pm and 7am. Statutory, not voluntary.

CAMHS capacity: 600 additional therapists would cost approximately £135m over five years. The children in crisis need mental health support. The ban does not provide it.

Digital literacy from Year 3: a developmental curriculum from age 7 to 16, statutory, building the understanding of how platforms work and how to navigate them.

Longitudinal outcome study: baseline data now, before Spring 2027, so we know whether the ban worked. Australia does not have this. The UK should.

Total cost of targeted package: approximately £305m. Saving versus the ban: £495m. Evidence base: considerably stronger.

## Day 12 — The conversation that prepares your child

The ban is April 2027. Your child is in front of you now.

The conversation that prepares them for the digital world cannot wait for a government timetable.

At 4 to 7: screens are a choice. We notice how we feel before, during and after. Some things on screens are real and some are made to make us watch more.

At 8 to 10: platforms are designed to keep us using them. Understanding the design does not mean we cannot use it. It means we are in charge of it.

At 11 to 13: social media shows us the best version of everyone else. You are comparing your inside to their outside. The images are edited. The life is curated. What does social comparison feel like in your body?

At 14 to 16: how does the algorithm decide what you see? What is engagement optimisation? How do you design your digital environment rather than be designed by it?

At 16: the ban lifts. The machines are the same machines. The literacy acquired in the years before is what makes the difference.

## Day 13 — What your school needs to know

The ban handles access. Ofsted will ask about readiness.

KCSIE requires schools to support children's online safety. RSHE requires teaching about healthy relationships including online relationships. The Online Safety Act creates duties around education. DfE AI guidance is incoming.

The school that can show it has equipped children with the knowledge, the language and the practised ability to navigate digital environments is ahead of this policy, not behind it.

The school that waited for the ban and called it done will face a very different set of Ofsted questions.
