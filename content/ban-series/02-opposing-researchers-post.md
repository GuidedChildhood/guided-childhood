# SERIES ARTICLE 2: The researchers the government cited. Here is their actual work.

*Justin Farrow, Guided Childhood. June 2026.*

---

Before you read this: I am not saying these researchers are wrong to be concerned. I am saying their methodology does not support the policy that was built on it. There is a difference. Read the work yourself. Links are below.

---

## Jonathan Haidt

**The book:** The Anxious Generation (2024). Not peer reviewed. Popular science.

**What it says:** The smartphone transition between 2012 and 2015 correlates with deterioration in adolescent wellbeing across multiple indicators. Girls are more affected than boys. Passive social media use involving social comparison is the proposed mechanism.

**The methodology problem:** Haidt uses ecological correlation. He observes country-level trends and proposes a mechanism. This is not the same as individual-level causal evidence. Candice Odgers at UC Irvine published a direct methodological challenge to this approach in the Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry (2020). She examined 40 studies. She found no consistent negative association for boys. She found weak, use-pattern-specific associations for girls.

**What Haidt actually recommends:** Not a full access ban. Page 284 of The Anxious Generation: he recommends removing smartphones from schools (already UK law), age verification for platforms, and redesigning the machines. The government used his framing to justify a policy he did not recommend.

---

## Jean Twenge

**The work:** iGen (2017) and Twenge et al. (2018) in Clinical Psychological Science.

**What it says:** Social media use correlates with depressive symptoms in the Monitoring the Future Study data.

**The methodology problem:** The same data was re-analysed by three independent teams. Kreski et al. found social media use was not a moderately strong or consistent risk factor. Orben and Przybylski found associations that were specification-dependent and explained less than 0.4% of the variance in wellbeing. As Odgers and Jensen (2020) summarise: "99.666% of the variation in adolescent depressive symptoms was due to other factors." The effect Twenge reported is real but tiny. The alarm she raised was not proportionate to the finding.

---

## Amy Orben

**The work:** Orben and Przybylski (2019) in Nature Human Behaviour. 3,221,225,472 analytical approaches across 350,000 adolescents. Technology use accounted for 0.4% of variation in wellbeing. Effect sizes r = 0.01 to r = 0.05.

**Her response to today's ban:** On camera, she said she would not expect "a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term." She called the government's announcement "really disappointing" because it signals they believe they cannot make social media safe through platform design. She argued the real problem is platform design features.

**What her research actually shows:** The smallest effect sizes in this literature. Her own work is the strongest methodological argument against this ban.

---

## Sander van der Linden

**The work:** Professor of Psychology at Cambridge. Studies misinformation, social influence and online harms.

**His response to today's ban:** On camera, directly: "The evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution, but it is not the nuanced one." He told the government this over the past year. They heard it.

**What he recommends instead:** The Canada model. Temporary ban as a strategic lever to force platforms to remove risky functionality. When platforms are safe, restore access. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from what the UK has implemented.

---

## The pattern

Each of these researchers identified real concerns about specific platform design features. Passive scrolling. Social comparison amplification. Notification anxiety. Algorithmic content optimisation for engagement over wellbeing.

None of them produced experimental evidence that access bans improve population-level mental health outcomes. None of them recommended this specific policy as their primary intervention. The government took their concerns, attached them to a simpler mechanism, and called it evidence-based.

It is not.
