# THE POST

A developmental psychologist has been tracking thousands of teenagers every single day since 2008.

Their sleep. Their step count. Their school records. Who they are with. How they feel. What they do online. And, crucially, what they say they need.

Her name is Candice Odgers, and she has spent 25 years analysing trends in teen mental health. I want to share what she found, because it is not the story any of us are being sold.

The story we hear every day is that teenagers are lost. Worse off than ever. That smartphones and social media destroyed them and their brains.

Odgers is blunt about it. That story does not match the data, and it does not match what the teenagers themselves say. There is a massive gap.

Her explanation for the gap is one line worth sitting with. Scary stories sell. And the more often you hear something, the more likely you are to believe it is true.

So here is the quieter, truer version.

Over the past 20 years, teen violence, alcohol use and pregnancy have fallen to historic lows. This is the most educated generation in history. At the same time, young people tell her they are sadder and more worried about the world, about safety at school, about climate, about their future. The stressors that predict their mental health day to day are not the ones we fixate on. They are conflict at home and pressure to do well at school.

Then the part that should stop us.

In her longitudinal studies, social media does not emerge as a major predictor of teen mental health. Other researchers reach the same place, calling it one of the least influential factors. For boys, no association. For girls, the data shows that girls who are already depressed go on to use social media more, not the other way around.

And on the solution everyone is converging on, banning social media for under 16s, she points out something I cannot stop thinking about. There is not one single study to date that has tested whether shutting it off actually improves their mental health. When the same thing is tested on adults, the effect is close to indistinguishable from zero. The National Academies of Sciences reached this conclusion too.

So what does predict how a teenager is doing? By far the strongest factor she names is the mental health of the adults around them. And we are in the middle of an adult mental health crisis. Between 2011 and 2021, overdose deaths among parents more than doubled. As she puts it, people ask what changed in that period besides social media. The answer is that adults were in distress and parents were dying.

Her conclusion is not that the online world is fine. She is clear that big tech needs a serious overhaul and that real harms exist online. Both things are true at once. But she argues that a ban punishes the victims. It removes the spaces teenagers go to find their friends and, sometimes, to escape harm in their own homes. It pushes them somewhere less safe and less regulated, and it lets the companies off the hook.

What she says would actually help:

Invest in the adults around children. In US middle schools there is one counsellor for every 500 students. Pouches to lock up phones will not fix that. Hire counsellors and teachers, and pay them well.

Build spaces, real and online, that are welcoming and safe, because young people go online more when they are anxious and we should be there to meet them.

And stop believing everything you hear about young people. They are not lost. They are resilient, often succeeding despite the struggles of the adults around them. Set high expectations, then support them to reach them. The same combination that has always worked in teaching, coaching and parenting.

Her closing line is the one I would put on the wall.

If all we see when we look at our kids is their phone, we will fail to see what they really need from us.

Worth twelve minutes of anyone's time. Her talk is in the comments.
