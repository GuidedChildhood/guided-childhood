## Post 1 · The Wrong Villain (73,911 impressions)

A peer-reviewed meta-analysis published this year looked at 46 studies and 79 effect sizes on the relationship between social media use and adolescent mental health.

The pooled result: β = 0.061.

Below the threshold the researchers themselves set for evidentiary value. Statistically indistinguishable from noise. Pre-registered on Open Science Framework. Data and methodology publicly available.

That number is not zero. Social media has a measurable association with child mental health outcomes. But β = 0.061 is smaller than the effect of wearing glasses. That is not a metaphor I invented. It is how Andrew Przybylski at Oxford described his own independent finding when he and Amy Orben at Cambridge MRC ran the same calculation with a different dataset and reached essentially the same result.

Four research groups. UC Irvine. Cambridge MRC. Oxford. Stetson University. Different datasets, different methodologies, different countries, different starting assumptions. Same conclusion: real, measurable, and very small.

Meanwhile child poverty accounts for an estimated 32% of child mental health outcomes consistent with every major longitudinal study including the Avon Longitudinal Study and the British Journal of Psychiatry 2025. Adverse childhood experiences account for roughly 24%.

Parental mental health difficulties for around 14%.

Social media sits at roughly 3%.

If you want to understand why the response to the children's mental health crisis keeps missing the mark, that ratio is the answer. The 3% problem is receiving the vast majority of parliamentary time and policy energy. The 32% problem is chronically underfunded and almost invisible.

### Author first comment (gets ahead of the objection)
One objection worth getting ahead of, because it's the right one to raise. If the population effect is this small, am I not using an average to dismiss the whole thing? That's the exact trap this work exists to avoid. The average is small precisely because it flattens two very different groups into one number: the majority for whom social media is roughly neutral, and the minority for whom it is anything but. A 3% population signal can still mean serious harm for a child already carrying a vulnerability the recommendation system can find and feed. Wrong villain doesn't mean no villain. It means we've aimed the entire policy response at the average child while the children actually being harmed sit in the part of the distribution nobody is funding.

## Post 2 · The pattern (9,879 impressions)

In 2012, a peer-reviewed journal published this sentence:

"It is likely that social networking will take the place of video games as the next folk devil." That paper was published 14 years before the UK Social Media Ban.

Here's the pattern.

In the 1990s, politicians called video games "murder simulators" and "digital poison." The American Psychological Association issued policy statements warning of impending harm. Nine US states passed laws banning their sale to minors.

During the same period, youth violence fell to a 30-year low.

By 2011, the US Supreme Court had reviewed the evidence. The majority opinion, 7-2, stated the studies "do not prove that such exposure causes minors to act aggressively."

Every law collapsed. Every restriction failed. And children were no safer.

The behaviour didn't stop. It relocated.
