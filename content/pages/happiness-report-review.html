The biggest claim, and the biggest dataset

The 2026 World Happiness Report made social media its theme. Its headline chapter makes one of the strongest claims in the field. Here is a fair, fully sourced look at whether the biggest evidence we actually have can carry it.

WHR 2026, Chapter 3 Haidt and Rausch Sources at the foot Steelman first
The claim

State it at full strength first

The 2026 World Happiness Report, from the Wellbeing Research Centre at Oxford with Gallup and the UN, devoted its theme to social media and young people. Its third chapter, by Jonathan Haidt and Zachary Rausch, is titled, word for word, "Social media is harming adolescents at a scale large enough to cause changes at the population level."

They frame it as a product safety trial. The question they pose is whether social media platforms are dangerous consumer products whose design has harmed young people, and they conclude that on the evidence these products are not reasonably safe for children and adolescents.

This deserves respect, not a reflex. Their case is not one weak study. It is seven independent lines of evidence they argue all point the same way:

  • 1. What young people say about their own experience
  • 2. What parents, teachers and clinicians report
  • 3. Internal corporate documents
  • 4. Cross sectional studies
  • 5. Longitudinal studies
  • 6. Social media reduction experiments
  • 7. Natural experiments

Their sharpest move is this. A critic can wave away any single method, but they have to explain away all seven at once. Their strongest single card is a genuine causal study: when Facebook rolled out college by college across the United States, researchers used that staggered timing as a natural experiment and found the arrival of Facebook caused a real worsening in student mental health, with the mechanism pointing at unfavourable social comparison (Braghieri, Levy and Makarin, American Economic Review, 2022). They add that the decline concentrates in girls and in anxiety and depression, that it tracks the early 2010s smartphone shift, and that under 25 life evaluations fell by nearly a full point on the 0 to 10 scale across the United States, Canada, Australia and New Zealand over a decade, even as youth wellbeing rose in much of the rest of the world.

That is a serious argument. Now the honest look at whether the title holds.

The problem at the centre of the claim

Population scale is exactly where the effect is smallest

The phrase in the title, population level, makes a specific promise. It says the effect is large enough to show up across a whole population. So look at the largest population level datasets we have.

0.4%

The largest study of this question assembled 355,358 adolescents. Analysed across every defensible method at once, digital technology use explained at most about 0.4 percent of the variation in wellbeing. The researchers put it next to eating potatoes and wearing glasses. Real, measurable, and tiny (Orben and Przybylski, Nature Human Behaviour, 2019).

It is not one study. When the same question is asked at true global scale, the signal does not appear. Across 2.4 million people in 168 countries, wellbeing was mostly positively associated with internet access as it spread, with no sign of a population wide harm tracking its arrival. Across 946,798 people in 72 countries, the spread of Facebook showed no population level harm either (Vuorre and Przybylski, 2023 and 2024).

The title promises a population scale effect. The best population scale data shows one of the smallest effects in the field. The confidence on top is doing work the evidence underneath cannot support.

Candice Odgers, who has tracked thousands of teenagers, put it plainly in her review of Haidt's book in Nature: there is no evidence that these platforms are rewiring children's brains or driving an epidemic of mental illness. Andrew Przybylski's line is shorter. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and right now that evidence is not there.

The honest pivot

Where Haidt and Rausch are right

This is not a takedown, and the lazy version of this argument is wrong too. Several of their points hold, and pretending otherwise would be its own dishonesty.

The Facebook rollout study is real and it is causal, not just a correlation, which is rare and valuable. Their honest limit is that it studied 2004 Facebook and college students, not a thirteen year old on an algorithmic video feed today, but the direction is real. They are also right that harm concentrates in girls and in early adolescence, and right that no single family can solve a collective action trap: a child cannot simply leave the platform their whole friendship group lives on. The report's economics chapter, by Cass Sunstein, makes this well. Even children who are not on a platform can be worse off because everyone else is.

And there is a genuine effect hiding in the averages. Which is the next point, and it is the one that matters most.

The reconciliation

Small on average, real in the tail

Two things that sound opposite are both true, and holding them together is the whole of an honest position.

The average effect is small. That is why "rewiring a generation" overclaims. An average blends a large majority of children who are basically fine with a smaller group who are genuinely hurt.

The harm concentrates. The same research group that found the small average also mapped windows of developmental sensitivity: the negative link is strongest around ages 11 to 13 for girls and 14 to 15 for boys (Orben, Przybylski, Blakemore and Kievit, Nature Communications, 2022). Odgers shows it lands hardest on children already vulnerable offline. A feed cannot tell distress from appetite, so it finds the struggling child fastest.

So the defensible claim is not the title. It is quieter and better supported: a modest effect, concentrated in a vulnerable minority and in specific developmental windows, plus real time trend worries that observational data cannot cleanly pin on any one cause. That is worth acting on. It is not a population scale epidemic.

The report checks itself

The World Happiness Report disagrees with its own headline

The most telling thing is that the editors did not lead with Chapter 3. They published the whole report under the banner of a complex global picture, and they paired the strong chapter with more cautious ones.

  • The report's own nuance: children using social media under an hour a day reported the highest wellbeing of all, higher even than non users. All use is not equal.
  • Its international evidence chapter found that raising a child's sense of belonging at school from low to high improved girls' life satisfaction around four times more in the UK and Ireland, and around six times more across 47 countries, than moving social media use from high to low.
  • Its finding on heavy use was that the association with lower wellbeing is real on average but highly dependent on the platform, the way it is used, gender and family circumstances.

Read the whole report and it does not say social media is the main character in the youth mental health story. It says the picture is mixed, the effect is uneven, and other things in a child's life matter more. That is the honest headline, and it is not the one the loudest chapter chose.

So what follows

Get the size right, then aim the response to match

If the effect is small on average and concentrated in the vulnerable, two things follow, and they are not the ban.

First, aim the serious protection where the harm actually is: the children already carrying the most, often the ones facing things at home that never make a headline. The factors that carry the largest share of the youth mental health burden, poverty, adverse childhood experiences and family mental health, sit far above platform use in the evidence, and they get a fraction of the attention. Getting the proportions right is not minimising social media. It is spending our effort where it changes the most lives.

Second, for every other child, the answer is capability, not eradication. Social media and AI are not leaving. A child will live their whole life with recommendation systems. So we teach them, in steps, age by age, to see the machine and steer it, the way we teach roads and swimming. Regulate the design that genuinely exploits children. Teach the literacy. And build the real world richness that gives a feed less to compete with.

Panic is the wrong response, not because children are safe, but because it aims at the wrong target. The evidence says prepare, in proportion.
