How this chart works, and why it is not a bribe

You are not paying your child to behave. You are making a deal visible. Screens are part of childhood now, and pretending otherwise starts a fight you cannot win. What children need is a clear, warm path: here is how screen time is earned, here is how much a token is worth, and here is how we end well. When the deal is on the fridge, you stop being the referee. The chart is.

Ages 5 to 7

Reward daily. Young children live in today. Count stars together at teatime, hand over tokens straight away, and let them spend them the same evening. Three stars earns one token. Six stars earns two.

Ages 8 to 11

Reward weekly. Older children can hold a bigger deal. Tally the week on Sunday, pay out screen bucks, and let them choose how to spend from the menu on their chart. Saving up is allowed and worth praising.

The five house rules of the chart

1. Set the rows together on Sunday. A chart done to a child gets ignored. A chart built with a child gets defended by them.
2. Earned tokens are never taken back. Consequences live elsewhere. The chart only ever pays out.
3. Tokens are minutes, and minutes are honoured. If a token says 15 minutes, the timer says 15 minutes.
4. Use the ending well card five minutes before time is up. Endings are where the battles live, so we rehearse them.
5. Keep it warm. The chart is a game the family wins together, not a surveillance system.

Print the charts on A4, laminate them if you can, and tick with a dry wipe pen so one print lasts all year. The token sheet prints best on card.

The ending well cards

Endings are where the battles live. These two cards rehearse them until they are boring, which is exactly what you want.

Five minutes left

BEEP BOOP! Five minutes to go!
Finish your level. Save your game.
No new episodes start now.

Parent: hand this card over silently. The card is the messenger, so you are not the baddie.

Time is up

Screen off. One big breath in... and out.
High five. You ended well!
That is a star for tomorrow's chart.

Ending well today earns the first star of tomorrow. The loop closes itself.

Why a breathing beat works

Switching off mid game feels to a child like being yanked out of a swimming pool. One slow breath is the towel. It gives the brain a beat to arrive back in the room, and it turns the ending into a small win you can praise instead of a fight you have to referee.
