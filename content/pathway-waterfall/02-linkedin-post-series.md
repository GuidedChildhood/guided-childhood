## POST 1 · The wall is not a plan

Hook before the fold:
The government banned social media for under 16s. Most parents feel relief. I understand it completely. But the relief is hiding a cliff edge.

---

The government banned social media for under 16s. Most parents feel relief. I understand it completely. But the relief is hiding a cliff edge.

Picture the plan as it actually is.

A child is blocked at 13. Three birthdays pass. On the morning they turn 16 the wall disappears. Full access overnight to the same platforms a government decided yesterday were too dangerous for a 15 year old. No test. No supervised hours. No licence. The only thing that changed is the number.

We do not do this with anything else that carries risk. A 17 year old does not sit in a classroom for two years and then get handed the car keys. They drive on real roads, supervised, making real mistakes at real speeds. By the time the licence arrives the road is familiar. Only the autonomy is new.

The ban builds a wall and sets a date. It does not build the bridge to the other side.

A child does not become digitally capable on their 16th birthday. They become unsupervised. Those are not the same thing.

I have written the full piece on how we build that bridge, from age 4 to 16. It is in the first comment.

The crisis is real. The wall is not the answer to it. The pathway is.

## POST 2 · What actually happens at 16

Hook before the fold:
A child banned from social media at 13 will turn 16 during a sleepover. Their phone will light up. Nobody will be there. That is the plan.

---

A child banned from social media at 13 will turn 16 during a sleepover. Their phone will light up. Nobody will be there.

That is the plan.

Here is the neuroscience the ban does not cite. The prefrontal cortex, the part of the brain responsible for impulse control and risk assessment, is not fully developed until around 25. Not 16. The number 16 was not chosen for a developmental reason. It was inherited from earlier legislation that traces back to a 1998 US data protection law. It was never designed as an age of digital competence.

So the threshold has no developmental science behind it. And behind the threshold there is no transition. No supervised hours. No framework for the morning a child gains full access to everything they were protected from the night before.

The cliff edge exists because every ban sets a date, and no ban builds a bridge.

A child does not become digitally literate at 16. They become unregulated.

## POST 3 · The pathway, stage by stage

Hook before the fold:
If the ban is a wall, here is the bridge. Five stages, age 4 to 16. This is the part no policy builds for you.

---

If the ban is a wall, here is the bridge. Five stages, from age 4 to 16. This is the part no policy builds for you.

Age 4 to 7. Before habits form. The work is the family norm, not the lecture. When the timer ends, screens go down. The device sleeps in the kitchen. We notice how we feel before, during and after.

Age 8 to 10. Habits before the algorithm learns them. The bedroom rule becomes the non negotiable, set as a family decision and not a prize. We start naming that platforms are built to keep us using them.

Age 11 to 13. The critical window. Cambridge research flags this as the highest risk period. The single most protective thing here is not a rule, it is a conversation. You open the feed together, with curiosity, no alarm, and ask why it showed them that.

Age 13 to 15. Identity and footprint, while the relationship holds. The protection is the open door. Your child has to know you are the first call and not the last, before anything goes wrong.

Age 16 and over. The wall lifts. If the years before were a pathway, this is a step, not a fall. The question is no longer get off your phone. It is what kind of digital life do you want to build.

Each stage is one conversation at a time. That is the whole method.

## POST 4 · The window nobody is teaching into

Hook before the fold:
There is one window where digital literacy either gets built or gets missed. It is age 11 to 13. Most parents do not know it exists.

---

There is one window where digital literacy either gets built or gets missed. It is age 11 to 13. Most parents do not know it exists.

This is the period Cambridge MRC research identifies as the highest risk. Identity is forming through comparison. The algorithm amplifies whatever vulnerability it finds. The mood drop after a Sunday evening scroll is not in your child's imagination. It is the mechanism working as designed.

Here is what most parents do. They say the app is rotting your brain, and the conversation ends before it starts.

Here is what works. You sit beside them and open the feed together. No judgment. Just curiosity. Why do you think it showed you that. How does this make you feel, not what do you think of it. Ten minutes.

The parent who can talk about the algorithm without alarm is the greatest single protective factor a child has. That is not my opinion. It is what the research keeps finding.

Social media is not an age you unlock. It is a readiness you build. And the window to build it is now, before the apps, not at 16 after the wall comes down.

## POST 5 · What the ban means for schools

Hook before the fold:
The ban handles access. Ofsted will ask about readiness. Those are two very different questions, and only one of them is your job.

---

The ban handles access. Ofsted will ask about readiness. Those are two very different questions, and only one of them is the school's job.

KCSIE requires schools to support online safety. RSHE requires teaching about healthy online relationships. The statutory curriculum is being updated from September 2026 to add algorithmic manipulation, deepfakes and AI generated imagery. The vehicle is being built. Most schools are not yet delivering it.

Here is the case no one is making loudly enough.

A child will meet algorithmic amplification, social comparison and manipulation somewhere in their digital life. The platform that delivers it will change. The mechanism will not. A school that has built genuine digital literacy is ahead of this policy whatever happens to the ban, and whatever replaces it.

The school that waited for the ban and called it done will face a very different set of questions.

Teaching children to avoid the internet is not the goal. Teaching them to navigate it is. That is what the updated curriculum is designed for, and it is what we built the schools pathway to deliver.

## POST 6 · The intervention that beats the ban and costs nothing

Hook before the fold:
The most powerful intervention for adolescent mental health is not a ban. It needs no legislation. It costs nothing. It is the phone charger in the hallway.

---

The most powerful intervention for adolescent mental health is not a ban. It needs no legislation. It costs nothing.

It is the phone charger in the hallway.

Swedish research followed 4,810 adolescents and found that sleep was the pathway through which social media affected mental health. Three independent sleep mediators. Remove them and the link between social media and poor mental health almost disappears.

The adolescent brain makes this worse. Puberty delays the body clock by around two hours. Blue light suppresses melatonin at the exact moment the brain is trying to sleep. The platforms are built around infinite scroll, designed to be used in bed, in the dark, at 11pm.

That is not a parenting failure. It is a design choice deployed against a developmental vulnerability.

A ban that arrives in 2027 does nothing for the phone charging next to a 14 year old's bed tonight.

The charger in the hallway does. One change. No legislation. Takes effect tonight.

## POST 7 · Four things you can do tonight

Hook before the fold:
You do not need the ban to protect your child. You do not need to wait for 2027. Here are four things that work, that you can do tonight.

---

You do not need the ban to protect your child. You do not need to wait for 2027. Here are four things that work, that you can start tonight.

Move the charger out of the bedroom. Sleep is the strongest single intervention in the research. The phone by the bed at 11pm is the thing working against it.

Turn autoplay off on every device in the house. Passive scrolling is where the mood effects concentrate. Removing the autopilot changes the equation.

Have one conversation about what your child is doing on their phone, not how long. Type of use matters more than time. Texting a friend and doom scrolling are not the same activity.

Tell your child what the plan is for 16. Not a wall that disappears, but a framework that arrives.

None of these need a parliamentary act. All of them target the actual mechanism the research identifies. And all of them are stronger than waiting.

## POST 9 · Does the ban change our approach? Not much.

*Strong candidate to lead the series or pin to the profile. It is the thesis in one post.*

Hook before the fold:
The biggest social media law in a generation just passed. Here is how much it changes the way we should build digital literacy in children. Not much. And that is the point.

---

The biggest social media law in a generation just passed. Here is how much it changes the way we should build digital literacy in children.

Not much. And that is the point.

A law this big feels like it should change everything. It does not. And that is not a weakness in the approach. It is the whole reason it works.

The skills that protect a child are the same whether the apps are banned, allowed, renamed or replaced. How an algorithm decides what you see. How comparison affects your mood. What manipulation looks like. How to use a device rather than be used by it. None of that is specific to Instagram, or to 2026, or to whether the wall is up or down.

The ban is a variable. The platform list will change. The date will move again. Another app will appear that nobody has named yet. If a child's protection depends on the exact rules of the day, it breaks every time the rules change. If it is built on understanding, it holds through all of it.

So we adjust exactly one conversation, the one at 13 to 15 about the workaround and what a child is actually looking for. Everything else in the pathway from 4 to 16 stands exactly as it was.

The people rewriting their whole approach around the ban were building around the platform. We built around the child.

That is why the ban changes our approach not much. And that is the strongest thing about it.
