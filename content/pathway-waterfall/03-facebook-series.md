## POST 1 · Should my child have Snapchat?

This is the question I get asked more than any other, so here is the honest answer after two years in the research.

The age on the box is 13. That is a data protection number, not a readiness number. The real question is not how old they are. It is whether they understand what the app is doing.

Snapchat is built around streaks, disappearing messages and a map that shows where friends are. Streaks create a daily obligation. Disappearing messages remove the pause that makes a child think twice. The map raises the question of who can see your child's location.

So before yes or no, I would do one thing. Sit down together and go through the settings as a team. Turn Ghost Mode on so the location is off. Look at who can contact them. Talk about the streak as a design choice, something built to keep them coming back, not a friendship.

A child who understands why the app wants their attention is far safer than a child who was simply told no until a birthday. Yes or no matters less than ready or not ready.

## POST 2 · What age is right for a first smartphone?

Short answer, there is no magic age. Longer answer, which is more useful.

The question parents ask is what age. The question that actually protects a child is what device, and what have we built first.

Between 8 and 11, a feature phone, calls and texts and no apps, does almost everything a worried parent wants. They can reach you. You can reach them. There is no algorithm learning their moods, and no feed to fall into at bedtime. It buys you years of independence without the part that does the harm.

The smartphone conversation is not really about the phone. It is about whether the groundwork is in place. Does the device sleep in the kitchen, not the bedroom. Have you had the conversation about how the algorithm decides what they see. Can they come to you when something online feels wrong, without fearing you will take the phone away.

If those three things are true, the smartphone is a step. If they are not, it is a cliff edge in your own house.

## POST 3 · How much screen time is too much?

I used to ask this question too. Then I read the research and changed the question.

The strongest finding in the whole literature is not about how much. It is about when. Late night use, in bed, in the dark, is where the harm concentrates, because it wrecks sleep, and sleep is the single biggest lever on a child's mood. One study of nearly 5,000 teenagers found that once you account for sleep, the link between social media and poor mental health almost disappears.

So the most powerful change is not a time limit. It is the charger in the hallway. The phone sleeps in the kitchen. The bedroom is for sleep. That one rule does more than any screen time app.

The second change is the type, not the total. Texting a friend, planning to meet up, a group chat about a shared hobby, that is active use and it is mostly fine. Scrolling a feed on autopilot is passive use and that is where the mood drops live. Turn autoplay off on every device and you have changed the equation without a single argument about minutes.

Ask what kind of screen time, not how much. It is a better question and it leads somewhere.

## POST 4 · Is Roblox or gaming safe for my child?

A fair worry, and the answer is more reassuring than the headlines, with one real caveat.

The game itself is rarely the problem. For most children gaming is social. It is where their friends are. The game is the venue, not the point. If your child is spending a lot of time there, especially with the social media ban pushing children off other apps, that is usually displacement, the social life moving somewhere, not addiction.

So the useful move is not to ban it. It is to get curious about it. Ask them to teach you their game for ten minutes. Ask who they play with. You will learn more in that ten minutes than in a month of worrying.

The real caveat is chat and strangers. The risk in these spaces is contact, not content. Turn on the parental controls, switch chat to friends only, and have the one conversation that matters more than any setting. If anyone online ever makes you uncomfortable, or asks you to keep a secret, you tell me, and I will help first and ask questions later.

That open door is the safeguard. The settings just buy you time.

## POST 5 · The ban is coming. So what do I actually do now?

A lot of parents feel relieved about the under 16 ban, and I understand it. But I want to be honest about what it does and does not do, because I do not want anyone to relax into a false sense of safety.

The ban handles access. It does not handle preparation. A child blocked at 13 still turns 16 one day and gets full access overnight, with no skills and no practice. In Australia, where they did this six months ago, two thirds of children were back on private apps within eight weeks. The wall has gaps, and behind it nothing was actually taught.

So here is what I would do in the window before it arrives, whatever age your child is.

Move the charger out of the bedroom tonight. Turn autoplay off on every device. Have one real conversation about how the apps are built to hold attention, with curiosity, not a lecture. And tell your child what the plan is for 16, so it is a framework arriving and not a wall vanishing.

The years between now and 16 are not a waiting room. They are the only preparation time you get.
