## CAROUSEL 2 · The 4 to 16 escalator

*Visual: a rising staircase, one step per stage, hand drawn. Each step a card. The wall at the far end with a gap, then a bridge drawn over it.*

Slide 1 (cover):
How to raise a digitally capable child. Five steps, age 4 to 16.

Slide 2:
Age 4 to 7. Foundation. Screens are a choice we make together. When the timer ends, screens go down. The device sleeps in the kitchen. Notice how we feel.

Slide 3:
Age 8 to 10. Builder. The bedroom rule is the non negotiable, set as a family decision. We start naming that apps are built to keep us using them.

Slide 4:
Age 11 to 13. Explorer. The critical window. Open the feed together with curiosity. Ask why it showed you that. The calm parent is the protective factor.

Slide 5:
Age 13 to 15. Shaper. The open door. Your child has to know you are the first call, not the last, before anything goes wrong.

Slide 6:
Age 16 plus. Independent. The wall lifts. If the years before were a pathway, this is a step. The question becomes what kind of digital life do you want to build.

## CAROUSEL 3 · Four things you can do tonight

*Visual: four bold cards, one tip each, mono numerals, then a save card. Warm and doable, no fear.*

Slide 1 (cover):
Four things that protect your child tonight. No ban required.

Slide 2:
One. Move the charger out of the bedroom. Sleep is the strongest single thing in the research. The phone by the bed at 11pm works against it.

Slide 3:
Two. Turn autoplay off on every device. Passive scrolling is where the mood drops live. Remove the autopilot.

Slide 4:
Three. Ask what they are doing on the phone, not how long. Texting a friend and doom scrolling are not the same thing.

Slide 5:
Four. Tell your child the plan for 16. Not a wall that vanishes. A framework that arrives.

## REEL 1 · The one thing that actually works

*Format: 45 to 60 seconds, to camera, one idea, spoken naturally, not read.*

Script beats:
If you do one thing after watching this, do this one. The most powerful thing for a teenager's mental health is not a screen time limit. It is where the phone sleeps. A study of nearly five thousand teenagers found that once you account for sleep, the link between social media and poor mental health almost disappears. The phone in the bedroom at 11pm is the problem. The charger in the hallway is the fix. It costs nothing. You can do it tonight. No law required.

## REEL 3 · The question I wish someone had asked me

*Format: 45 to 60 seconds, to camera.*

Script beats:
When my kids were small I kept asking how much screen time is too much. It is the wrong question. The right question is what kind. Texting a friend, planning to meet up, a group chat about football, that is active use and it is mostly fine. Scrolling a feed on autopilot is passive use, and that is where the mood drops live. So stop counting minutes. Turn autoplay off, and ask what they are actually doing on there. It changes everything, and it ends the fight about time.

On screen text: Not how much. What kind.
