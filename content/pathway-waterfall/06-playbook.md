## WHY THIS EXISTS

The government banned social media for under 16s, and the access date keeps moving. The ban handles access. It does not handle preparation. A child blocked at 13 still turns 16 and gains full access overnight, with no skills and no practice, unless somebody built the bridge first.

This playbook is the bridge. Five stages, age 4 to 16. At each stage there is the one thing that matters most, the words to say it with, the words to avoid, and the warning signs to watch for. You do not need to do all of it. Find your child's stage, and start there tonight.

The rule that runs through all of it: we never just allow or deny. We build readiness, one conversation at a time.

---

## THE FOUR THINGS THAT WORK AT EVERY AGE

If you read nothing else, do these. They target what the research actually blames, and none of them need a law.

1. **The charger lives in the hallway.** Sleep is the single strongest lever on a child's mood. The phone by the bed at 11pm is the thing working against it. The device sleeps in the kitchen. The bedroom is for sleep.
2. **Autoplay off, everywhere.** Passive scrolling is where the mood drops concentrate. Turning off autoplay removes the autopilot that keeps them there.
3. **Ask what, not how long.** Type of use matters more than total time. Texting a friend and doom scrolling are not the same activity. Get curious about which one is happening.
4. **Name the plan for 16.** Tell your child, before they get there, that 16 is not a wall vanishing. It is a framework arriving.

## STAGE 1 · FOUNDATION · AGE 4 TO 7

**The focus:** first relationships with technology, before habits form.
**The device:** a shared family device, full supervision, no exceptions.

**The one thing that matters:** the family norm. When the timer ends, screens go down, every time. The rule is not a punishment. It is the structure that makes screen time safe, and it builds the self control they will need at 13.

**Say this:** We have a family rule about screens. When the timer goes off, screens go down. Every time. And then we do something together.

**Not this:** Just five more minutes. Once you move the line, it moves every time.

**Tonight:** set up a charging station in the kitchen or hallway. Devices do not sleep in bedrooms in this house, starting now.

**Watch for:** real distress when a device is taken away, choosing screens over play every time, losing interest in things they used to love.

---

## STAGE 2 · BUILDER · AGE 8 TO 10

**The focus:** building habits before the algorithm learns them.
**The device:** a shared device moving toward a supervised personal one. A feature phone, calls and texts and no apps, is a strong first device here.

**The one thing that matters:** the bedroom rule, set as a family decision and not a prize to be earned. Sleep is the non negotiable, and one consistent rule removes the blue light, the notification sounds, and the 2am check all at once.

**Say this:** Where does your phone sleep at night? In our house, phones sleep in the kitchen. The bedroom is for sleep. That is our rule and it will not change.

**Not this:** You need to earn the right to have it in your room. That makes it a prize, not a family norm.

**Tonight:** have the bedroom rule conversation as a family decision, not an order. Where should our devices sleep?

**Watch for:** secretive use or hiding what they watch, the first signs of comparing themselves to what they see, mood changes around device removal that feel out of proportion.

---

## STAGE 3 · EXPLORER · AGE 11 TO 13

**The focus:** the algorithm conversation, before social media. This is the critical window.
**The device:** a first personal device. A feature phone is strongly recommended over a smartphone here.

**The one thing that matters:** the conversation, not a rule. Cambridge research flags this as the highest risk period, when identity forms through comparison and the algorithm amplifies whatever vulnerability it finds. The parent who can talk about this without alarm is the single greatest protective factor a child has.

**Say this:** Did you know your phone is learning what you like? Every video you watch, every post you pause on, it notes it. Open your feed and let us look at it together.

**Not this:** That app is rotting your brain. That shuts the conversation down before it starts.

**Tonight:** open their feed together, no judgment, just curiosity. Interesting, why do you think it showed you that? Stay for ten minutes.

**Watch for:** mood dropping noticeably after phone use, anxiety about being left out, checking the phone after lights out, withdrawing from family conversation.

---

## STAGE 4 · SHAPER · AGE 13 TO 15

**The focus:** identity and digital footprint, while the relationship still holds.
**The device:** a smartphone with agreed family guidelines in place.

**The one thing that matters:** the open door. A teenager has to know, before anything goes wrong, that you are the first call and not the last, and that you will help before you react. A child who fears losing their phone is a child who hides problems.

**Say this:** If anything ever goes wrong online, and things do go wrong, I am the first call you make, not the last. I will not overreact. I will help.

**Not this:** If I catch you doing that, I am taking your phone. That closes the door before anything happens.

**In a ban world, add this:** The government has said the apps are going. That does not fix the thing underneath them. If you ever find a way around the ban, I am not going to explode. I want to understand what you were looking for. That sentence is worth more than the ban itself, because the child who bypasses the wall and hits something harmful must not be alone with it.

**Tonight:** set up a no agenda weekly ten minutes. Not about screens, not about homework. Just checking in. Same day, same time.

**Watch for:** secretive use or panic when you pick up the device, extreme mood swings tied to online interactions, online relationships that feel more real than offline ones, withdrawal from things they enjoyed.

---

## STAGE 5 · INDEPENDENT · AGE 16 AND OVER

**The focus:** genuine digital independence before they leave home. This is where the ban lifts.
**The device:** a full smartphone. The conversation has shifted to literacy, not limits.

**The one thing that matters:** if the years before were a pathway, 16 is a step, not a fall. The goal is not compliance. It is a young person who can describe their own relationship with technology, what it gives them and what it costs.

**Say this:** You are at the age where you decide what kind of digital life you build. What does your online presence say about who you are? Not to me. To the world.

**Not this:** Get off your phone. That conversation is over. A different one has started.

**Tonight:** suggest a joint digital audit. Go through their online presence together, as peers, not parent and child. What does it say? What would they change?

**Name the real risks plainly:** sextortion, deepfakes and AI generated content are real at this age. Have a specific, matter of fact conversation about what to do if anything goes wrong. Not if. When.

**Watch for:** measuring self worth through likes and followers, struggling to be present in real life, real anxiety without phone access, no meaningful offline friendships or activities.

## FOR SCHOOLS

The ban handles access. Ofsted will ask about readiness. KCSIE requires you to support online safety. RSHE requires teaching about healthy online relationships, and the statutory curriculum is being updated from September 2026 to add algorithmic manipulation, deepfakes and AI generated imagery.

The case to make to your leadership: a child will meet algorithmic amplification, social comparison and manipulation somewhere in their digital life. The platform changes. The mechanism does not. A school that has built genuine digital literacy is ahead of this policy whatever happens to the ban. The school that waited for the ban and called it done will face a very different set of questions.

**Three moves for this term:**
1. Map the pathway above onto your key stages. Stage 1 to 2 is KS1 and KS2. Stage 3 is KS3, the critical window. Stage 4 is KS3 to KS4. Stage 5 is KS4 to KS5.
2. Run one assembly on the algorithm conversation in Year 7, the start of the critical window. We have a free assembly pack for this.
3. Send the four things that work home to every parent. The home and school messages should match, or they cancel each other out.
