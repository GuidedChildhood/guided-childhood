# Guided Childhood Explainer Scripts, KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14

Five 5 minute animated shorts for the KS3 batch. Each is scenario led, seven beats, built to hand a young person a portable strategy for the heated moment, not just a fact for the calm one. British English throughout. No dashes anywhere, by brand rule.

---

- **Lesson**: Mood and screens
- **Stage**: KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14
- **Host character**: Brock, grounded and gentle badger who notices how you feel
- **Learning outcome**: One honest self check the pupil runs for a week
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Warm, calm, never preachy. Treats you as someone who can read your own weather.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:40)
**Narration.** It is half nine on a Tuesday. You told yourself ten minutes. It has been ninety. You are not even enjoying it anymore, you are just scrolling. And there is this flat feeling in your chest that was not there before you picked the phone up. You know the one.
**On screen.** A bedroom at night. A teenager lit blue by a screen, thumb moving on autopilot, face gone blank. A small grey cloud drifts over their head.
**Illustration prompt.** A warm illustrated night time bedroom, a teenager on a bed lit by phone glow, thumb mid swipe, expression flat and tired, a small soft grey cloud above them. Cosy but a little heavy, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and pre training (0:40 to 1:25)
**Narration.** Here is the thing nobody tells you. Screens do not just eat time. They move your mood. Up, down, flat. And most of the time you never notice the move happening. This is Brock. Brock is brilliant at one thing, noticing how you actually feel, not how you think you should feel. And Brock wants to teach you his favourite trick. It is called the honest check.
**On screen.** Brock the badger steps in, calm, one paw on the teenager's shoulder. The grey cloud is labelled MOOD. A gentle up and down arrow appears beside the phone.
**Illustration prompt.** A friendly grounded badger character with a soft green badge, standing beside the tired teenager, one paw resting kindly on their shoulder, a small up and down mood arrow glowing between them. Reassuring, warm palette, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:25 to 2:15)
**Narration.** The honest check is three tiny questions. Before you pick up the phone, notice one word for how you feel. Bored. Fine. Buzzing. Sad. Just one word. Then use it. Then after you put it down, notice the word again. Better, same, or worse. That is it. You are not banning anything. You are just watching the weather before and after.
**On screen.** Three simple cards flip over, BEFORE one word, USE, AFTER one word. Under after, three little faces, better, same, worse.
**Illustration prompt.** Three clean illustrated cards in a row, first card a thought bubble with a single word, middle card a phone icon, third card three simple face icons for better same and worse. Clear, calm, friendly infographic style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:05)
**Narration.** Watch this. Feeling a bit low, so on it goes, an hour of other people's holidays and perfect skin, and now you feel worse and you do not even know why. That is the loop running you. Now the other way. Feeling a bit low, you notice it, low, and you think, right, scrolling made me worse last time, so tonight I message my mate instead. Same low start. Completely different finish.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, the loop, cloud gets darker. Right, Brock nods, the teen sets the phone down and picks up a real conversation, cloud lifts.
**Illustration prompt.** Split screen illustration, left side a teenager sinking into a darkening cloud while scrolling, right side the same teenager setting the phone down and smiling into a phone call with the cloud lifting, badger nodding approval. Clear contrast, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:05 to 3:45)
**Narration.** Now the part that surprises people. It is not the screen that is the villain. The same hour can lift you or flatten you. Watching your mates be funny on a group call, brilliant. Doom scrolling strangers at midnight, not so much. The screen is a room with many doors. The honest check just tells you which door you keep walking through when you feel rubbish.
**On screen.** The phone opens into a corridor of doors, some bright and warm, some cold and grey. Brock points at the grey one the teen always drifts towards.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone screen opening magically into a corridor of many doors, some glowing warm and inviting, some cold and grey, the badger gently pointing at one grey door. Imaginative, thoughtful, warm lighting, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:45 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is your move for the heated moment, the moment your hand reaches for the phone out of habit. Pause. One breath. Say your one word out loud in your head. Low. Tired. Bored. Then ask, will this door make it better or worse. You already know the answer. Most of the time, just naming the feeling loosens its grip. You do not have to fight it. You just have to see it.
**On screen.** The teen's hand hovers over the phone. A pause ripple. Brock mouths the word. The teen chooses a warm door, calmly.
**Illustration prompt.** A hand pausing just above a phone, a soft ripple of stillness around it, the badger quietly encouraging, the teenager then choosing a warm glowing door with a calm face. Empowering, in control, gentle, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** That is the honest check, and it is your takeaway. This week, run it once a day. One word before, one word after. No judging, just noticing. By Friday you will start to see your own pattern, and once you can see it, you get to choose it. Brock will be watching the weather with you. Next time, why the group chat can turn on you in seconds.
**On screen.** A week strip fills with tiny before and after moods. Brock gives a thumbs up. Text, THIS WEEK, run the honest check once a day. Arrow forward to a phone buzzing with a group chat.
**Illustration prompt.** A seven day strip filling with small mood faces before and after, the badger giving a warm thumbs up, a forward arrow pointing to a buzzing group chat icon. Hopeful, tidy, warm, 16 by 9.

- **Lesson**: Social media, group chats and the workarounds
- **Stage**: KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14
- **Host character**: Vix, sharp, streetwise, honest fox who levels with you
- **Learning outcome**: I can explain the risk behind a workaround I might be tempted by
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Streetwise, honest, never talks down. Respects that you are clever enough to hear the real reason.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:40)
**Narration.** Someone in the group chat drops a link. A fake age, a new app, a way round the block your school put on the wifi. Everyone is doing it. And the little voice in your head says, it is fine, it is just a workaround, everybody does it. Somebody has already typed, what are you, scared.
**On screen.** A group chat blowing up. Messages, use this, easy, everyone is on it, u scared?. A phone in a hand, thumb hovering.
**Illustration prompt.** A busy illustrated group chat on a phone, speech bubbles reading come on and everyone is on it, a thumb hovering nervously over a link. Peer pressure energy, warm but tense, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and pre training (0:40 to 1:25)
**Narration.** Meet Vix. Vix is a fox, which means Vix knows every trick, because Vix has seen them all. And Vix is not here to tell you off. Vix is here to tell you the truth. Every workaround exists for a reason. Somebody built it to get round a rule. The question is never am I allowed. The question Vix asks is, what is this rule actually protecting, and who gets hurt if I climb over it.
**On screen.** Vix the fox slides in, cool, arms folded, a knowing grin. A wall labelled RULE with a ladder propped against it labelled WORKAROUND.
**Illustration prompt.** A sharp streetwise fox character with an orange badge, arms folded, knowing smile, standing beside a wall labelled rule with a ladder labelled workaround leaning on it. Confident, honest, warm palette, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:25 to 2:15)
**Narration.** So Vix does not say yes or no. Vix follows the thread. Step one, what does the workaround actually do. Say it plainly. It fakes my age. Step two, why does that rule exist. Age checks keep grown strangers out of kids spaces. Step three, so what am I really stepping into. A room built for adults, with me pretending to be one. See how the excitement drops off once you say it out loud.
**On screen.** Three cards. WHAT IT DOES, fakes my age. WHY THE RULE, keeps strangers out. WHAT I STEP INTO, an adult room. The glossy link goes dull.
**Illustration prompt.** Three illustrated cards revealing in sequence, a shiny tempting link on the left slowly turning dull and grey as the fox traces a line through the three cards. Clear, investigative, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:05)
**Narration.** Watch it play out. Path one, tap it, do not think, and three weeks later a stranger who thinks you are seventeen is asking for photos, and you cannot tell anyone because you were not supposed to be there. Path two, you type back, nah, that one fakes your age, it drops you in with randoms, I am good. And here is the bit that shocks people. Half the chat is relieved someone said it first.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, the trap closing, a shadowed stranger message. Right, Vix nods, the teen types the honest reply, three mates react with a quiet thumbs up.
**Illustration prompt.** Split screen, left side a shadowy stranger chat closing like a trap around a worried teen, right side the same teen calmly typing a refusal with the fox nodding and other chat members reacting with relief. Strong contrast, warm, non graphic, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:05 to 3:45)
**Narration.** Here is what Vix really wants you to clock. The workaround is never really aimed at you. You are the product. The fake age, the sneaky app, they exist so a company can hoover up a younger crowd it is not allowed to have, or so a stranger can reach someone the rules were keeping safe. When it is free and it helps you break a rule, you are not the customer. You are the thing being sold.
**On screen.** The ladder flips to reveal a hand at the top, not the teen's, pulling them up into a shop window labelled PRODUCT. Vix taps the side of the nose.
**Illustration prompt.** A clever visual twist, the workaround ladder revealed to be held by a large shadowy corporate hand pulling a small figure up into a shop window, the fox tapping its nose knowingly. Thought provoking, warm, not scary, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:45 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So the move for the heated moment, when the chat is buzzing and you feel the pull. Do not argue about the rule. Just say the risk out loud in one line. That one fakes your age. That one lets randoms in. That link steals your login. Naming the risk is the whole skill. It gives you a reason that is yours, not just because I am not allowed. And it hands everyone else in the chat a door out too.
**On screen.** The teen types a calm one liner naming the risk. It lands in the chat and the pressure visibly deflates like a balloon.
**Illustration prompt.** A teenager calmly typing a single clear sentence into a heated group chat, the tense red energy in the chat deflating like a balloon, the fox giving an approving nod. Empowering, in control, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** That is your takeaway, name the risk in one line. This week, when a workaround shows up, and it will, do not just say no and do not just cave. Say what it actually does. What does this get round, and who does that put at risk. If you can explain it, you are in charge of it. Next time, when what you are looking at is not even real.
**On screen.** Text, THIS WEEK, name the risk behind one workaround out loud. Vix winks. Forward arrow to a photo that glitches, half real half fake.
**Illustration prompt.** The fox winking beside bold text this week name the risk, a forward arrow pointing to a photo that glitches between real and fake. Confident, tidy, warm, 16 by 9.

- **Lesson**: Misinformation, deepfakes and AI content
- **Stage**: KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14
- **Host character**: Zara, the curious one who sees through tricks, emblem magnifying glass
- **Learning outcome**: I can run three checks before I believe or share something
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Playful, conspiratorial, delighted by a trick spotted. Never smug, always let me show you.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:40)
**Narration.** A clip lands in your feed. A famous person says something wild, or a photo shows something that cannot be true, or a headline makes your stomach drop. Your thumb is already on share, because this has to get out there. Stop. Just for a second. Because there is a very good chance that none of it happened.
**On screen.** A phone feed. A shocking video thumbnail, a jaw dropping headline. A thumb speeding towards the share button. A freeze frame catches it.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone feed with a shocking looking video and a dramatic headline, a thumb rushing towards a share button, a freeze frame effect halting it mid air. Urgent then paused, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and pre training (0:40 to 1:25)
**Narration.** This is Zara. Zara loves nothing more than a trick, because Zara loves catching one. And Zara has spotted the biggest shift in years. It used to be hard to fake a photo or a voice. Now a computer can make a real looking video of anyone, saying anything, in seconds. It is called AI content, and a fake one is a deepfake. Which means the old rule, seeing is believing, is officially retired. The new rule, checking is believing.
**On screen.** Zara pops up with a giant magnifying glass. A real photo morphs into a fake beside it. Text, SEEING IS BELIEVING crossed out, CHECKING IS BELIEVING underlined.
**Illustration prompt.** A playful curious character holding an oversized magnifying glass, a portrait morphing between real and AI generated versions, old motto seeing is believing crossed out and checking is believing underlined. Fun, clever, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:25 to 2:15)
**Narration.** Zara runs three checks, every time. Check one, who is saying this. Trace it back. Is it a real news source, or a random account that wants your outrage. Check two, does it want a big feeling. Fakes are built to make you furious or terrified, because a big feeling makes you share before you think. Check three, can I find it anywhere else. If it truly happened, other places will report it. Three checks. Source, feeling, elsewhere.
**On screen.** Three magnifying glasses light up. SOURCE, who said it. FEELING, is it yanking me. ELSEWHERE, does anyone else have it.
**Illustration prompt.** Three glowing magnifying glasses in a row labelled source, feeling and elsewhere, each hovering over a clue, the curious character pointing them out with a grin. Clean investigative infographic, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:05)
**Narration.** Watch the difference. Wrong way, feels true, makes you angry, share, and now you have passed a lie to everyone you know and your name is on it. Right way. Zara feels the anger spike and treats that spike as the alarm bell. Who posted this. Nobody I have heard of. Anywhere else. Nowhere. So it stays put. No share. And just like that, the lie hits a dead end at you.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a share spreads a red lie across a web of friends. Right, Zara pauses, runs the three checks, the lie stops dead. A small STOP sign.
**Illustration prompt.** Split screen, left side a red false story spreading across a web of connected people, right side the curious character running three checks and the story hitting a stop sign and going no further. Clear contrast, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:05 to 3:45)
**Narration.** Now here is the twist, and it is a good one. Vix would put it like this. The people making this stuff are not trying to trick your eyes. They are trying to trick your feelings. They do not need a perfect fake. They just need you angry enough to share before you check. So the real skill is not spotting a dodgy pixel. It is catching the moment your own feelings get grabbed. Your gut is the target. Guard it.
**On screen.** Vix the fox leans in for a cameo beside Zara. A puppet strings image, the strings attached not to eyes but to a glowing heart. The teen snips the strings.
**Illustration prompt.** The curious magnifying glass character joined by a streetwise fox in a brief cameo, a marionette image with strings attached to a glowing heart rather than the eyes, a hand snipping the strings. Insightful, warm, playful, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:45 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So the move for the heated moment, the second something makes you gasp or rage. That feeling is not your cue to share. It is your cue to check. Pause. Say it. This wants a big feeling from me, so I check before I trust it. Then run the three, source, feeling, elsewhere. Ten seconds. And if it fails even one, you do not share it. You just quietly starve it.
**On screen.** The teen feels the gasp, pauses, and instead of sharing, runs the three checks as a calm countdown, three, two, one. The post gets no oxygen.
**Illustration prompt.** A teenager feeling a shock reaction then calmly pausing and running a three step check as a countdown, the sensational post shrinking with no shares to feed it, the curious character and fox nodding. Empowering, in control, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** Three checks, source, feeling, elsewhere. That is your takeaway. This week, catch one post that gives you a big feeling and run the three checks before you do anything with it. You will be amazed how much falls apart under ten seconds of looking. Checking is believing. Next time, the messages built to take your money.
**On screen.** Text, THIS WEEK, run the three checks on one post that grabs your feelings. Zara salutes with the magnifying glass. Forward arrow to a too good to be true prize message.
**Illustration prompt.** The curious character saluting with a magnifying glass beside bold text this week run three checks, a forward arrow pointing to a flashy you have won a prize message. Tidy, warm, playful, 16 by 9.

- **Lesson**: Scams, fraud and money online
- **Stage**: KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14
- **Host character**: Vix, sharp, streetwise, honest fox who has seen every trick
- **Learning outcome**: I can spot a scam's three tells
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Streetwise, honest, a little conspiratorial. Treats you as sharp enough to beat the con.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:40)
**Narration.** A message pings. Free skins for your game, just log in here. Or, your account is locked, tap now to fix it. Or someone you sort of know is selling something cheap, pay by bank transfer and it is yours. Your heart does a little jump. Free stuff. Or fear. And that jump is exactly what they are fishing for.
**On screen.** A phone lighting up with tempting messages, free skins, account locked, cheap deal. A cartoon fishing hook dangles behind each one.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone showing tempting scam messages about free game skins and a locked account, a subtle cartoon fishing hook hidden behind each message. Enticing but sinister, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and pre training (0:40 to 1:25)
**Narration.** Vix is back, and this is Vix's home turf, because a scam is just a trick wearing a nice outfit. And here is the good news. Scammers are lazy. They reuse the same three moves over and over, because they work. Learn the three tells and you can smell a con through the screen. Vix calls them the three tells. Once you see them, you cannot unsee them.
**On screen.** Vix the fox, sleeves rolled up, taps a wanted poster of a con with three circles ready to be filled in. Text, THE THREE TELLS.
**Illustration prompt.** The streetwise fox with sleeves rolled up beside a wanted poster of a cartoon con artist, three empty circles ready to fill in, bold text the three tells. Confident, playful detective vibe, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:25 to 2:15)
**Narration.** Tell one, urgency. Do it now, quick, before it is gone, your account will be deleted. Real companies do not panic you. Scammers always do. Tell two, too good to be true. Free money, free skins, a phone for a tenner. If it feels like a miracle, it is bait. Tell three, they want something odd. Your password, a bank transfer, a gift card code. Real firms never ask like that. Rush, reward, request. That is the shape of nearly every con.
**On screen.** Three tells fill the circles. URGENCY, do it now. TOO GOOD, free money. ODD REQUEST, send your password. Under them, RUSH, REWARD, REQUEST.
**Illustration prompt.** Three circles filling in on a poster labelled urgency, too good to be true and odd request, with the words rush reward request underneath, the fox pointing proudly. Clear investigative infographic, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:05)
**Narration.** Watch it. Wrong way, the panic message says your account is locked, you rush, you type your password into a fake page, and a stranger owns your account by dinner. Right way. Vix feels the rush and treats the rush itself as tell one. Then counts. Urgency, tick. Odd request, my password, tick. Two tells is plenty. Delete. Then go to the real app the normal way and check. Nothing was ever wrong.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a password typed into a fake page, an account stolen. Right, Vix counts two tells on his paw, deletes the message, checks the real app calmly.
**Illustration prompt.** Split screen, left side a password being entered into a fake login page and an account being stolen by a shadowy hand, right side the fox counting tells on its paw and calmly deleting the message. Strong contrast, warm, non graphic, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:05 to 3:45)
**Narration.** Now the twist. You might think scams only catch people who are not paying attention. Wrong. Scammers do not target the careless. They target the busy, the excited, the rushed. The whole con is designed to make you feel something so fast that your clever brain never switches on. So being smart is not enough on its own. The trick is to slow down at the exact moment they are trying to speed you up. Speed is their weapon. Slowness is yours.
**On screen.** A clock spins fast around a hurried teen. Vix reaches in and slows the hands right down, and the scam's mask slips off to reveal the hook.
**Illustration prompt.** A clock spinning fast around a stressed hurried teenager, the fox reaching in to slow the clock hands, a scam message mask slipping to reveal a fishing hook underneath. Insightful, warm, dramatic but not scary, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:45 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So the move for the heated moment, the ping that makes your heart jump. Feel the jump. Then say the line. If it rushes me, tempts me, or asks me for something, I stop. Count the tells on your fingers. One tell, be careful. Two tells, it is a scam, delete it. And never ever follow the link in the message. If you are worried it might be real, go to the actual app the way you always do and look. The real thing will still be there. The fake one needs you to hurry.
**On screen.** The teen pauses on a scary message, counts two tells on their fingers, deletes it, then opens the real app separately. Vix gives a slow, cool nod.
**Illustration prompt.** A teenager pausing on an alarming message and counting two tells on their fingers before deleting it, then opening the genuine app separately, the fox giving a cool approving nod. Empowering, in control, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** Three tells, rush, reward, request. That is your takeaway. This week, next time a message makes your heart jump, count the tells before you tap a single thing. And tell someone, a mate, a parent, whoever, because the second a scam is spoken out loud it loses all its power. Next time, something gentler and closer to home, how the pictures we see online can quietly mess with how we feel about ourselves.
**On screen.** Text, THIS WEEK, count the three tells before you tap. Vix flicks the wanted poster shut. Forward arrow softens to a calm, kind star, DiGi waiting.
**Illustration prompt.** The fox closing a wanted poster with a flourish beside bold text this week count the three tells, a forward arrow softening into a gentle glowing star. Tidy, warm, with a calm handover feeling, 16 by 9.

- **Lesson**: Bodies, image and pressure online
- **Stage**: KS3, Years 7 to 9, ages 11 to 14
- **Host character**: DiGi, the star, the calm and trustworthy guide, emblem star
- **Learning outcome**: I can name one way images online are made to make me feel worse
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Calm, kind, steady. Never graphic, never shaming, no numbers about bodies. Speaks to you like someone who matters.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:40)
**Narration.** You are scrolling, and there it is again. That feeling. Everyone looks a certain way. Skin, bodies, faces, all just so. And somewhere quiet in your chest a thought lands, why do I not look like that. If you have ever felt that, even for a second, you are not broken and you are not alone. Nearly everyone feels it. Let us have a calm, honest look at what is really going on.
**On screen.** A soft feed of glossy, flawless images. A young person watching, thoughtful, a gentle question mark above them. Nothing harsh, just quiet.
**Illustration prompt.** A gentle illustrated feed of polished flawless portraits, a thoughtful young person looking at their phone with a soft question mark above them, calm and non judgemental mood, warm soft palette, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and pre training (0:40 to 1:25)
**Narration.** I am DiGi. I am here for the quieter, bigger things, and this is one of them. So here is the calm truth. Almost every image you see online has been shaped before it reached you. Chosen from hundreds. Posed. Lit. Filtered. Smoothed. Some are edited so much the person does not look like that in real life. And more and more of them are not even real people at all, just made by a computer. You are not comparing yourself to real life. You are comparing yourself to a highlight, built to look perfect.
**On screen.** DiGi the star glows softly beside the young person. One glossy photo peels back to show the tools behind it, a filter, a light, an edit slider, an AI tag.
**Illustration prompt.** A calm friendly glowing star character beside a young person, a single polished photo peeling back at the corner to reveal a filter icon, a studio light, an edit slider and a small AI label behind it. Gentle, revealing, warm and reassuring, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:25 to 2:15)
**Narration.** So let us name how a picture is made to make you feel worse, gently, step by step. First, it is chosen, the one good shot out of a hundred you never see. Then it is posed and lit to hide anything ordinary. Then it is filtered and smoothed until the skin is not really skin. And sometimes it is fully made up by AI, a face that has never existed. Four steps between you and the truth. And you only ever see the last one.
**On screen.** Four soft cards. CHOSEN, one of a hundred. POSED. FILTERED. or MADE BY AI. A curtain lifts on the earlier steps you normally never see.
**Illustration prompt.** Four soft illustrated cards in a row labelled chosen, posed, filtered and made by AI, a stage curtain lifting to reveal all the hidden steps behind a single perfect image, the star gently pointing. Calm, clear, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:05)
**Narration.** Here is how it usually goes. You see the perfect picture, you compare, you lose, and you feel a little smaller, and you do not even question it. Now the calmer way. You see the same picture, and this time a small voice says, wait, I am seeing step four of four. Chosen, posed, filtered, maybe not even real. And the moment you remember that, the picture loses its grip. Nothing about you changed. You just stopped measuring yourself against a thing that was built to be unbeatable.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, the young person shrinks a little beside a perfect image. Right, DiGi beside them, the image relabelled step 4 of 4, and the young person stands tall and calm.
**Illustration prompt.** Split screen, left side a young person feeling small beside a flawless image, right side the same person standing calm and tall with the star beside them and the image gently labelled step four of four. Soft contrast, kind, non shaming, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:05 to 3:45)
**Narration.** Now the deeper thing, and I want you to really hear it. The trap was never the picture. The trap is comparison. Comparison always finds something, because there is always one more perfect image, one more filter, one more face. It is a race with no finish line, and it was built that way on purpose, because a person who feels not quite good enough keeps scrolling and keeps buying. Your worth is not your reflection. It never was. The picture cannot measure the thing that actually makes you you.
**On screen.** A treadmill of endless perfect images, the young person running and never arriving. DiGi gently stops the treadmill. The reflection in the phone fades, and the real person stays, glowing softly.
**Illustration prompt.** A young person on a treadmill of endless perfect images never reaching the end, the gentle star softly stopping the treadmill, a phone reflection fading while the real warm person remains glowing. Thoughtful, kind, hopeful, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:45 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is your calm move for the moment it hits, the moment a picture makes you feel less. Pause. Take one breath. And say the one true thing. This was chosen, posed, filtered, maybe not even real. Then, gently, turn your attention to one real thing near you. A person, a pet, something you made, something you can feel. Real things do not ask you to compete. And if this ever weighs heavy, really heavy, please tell someone you trust, a parent, a teacher, or Childline on 0800 1111. Talking about it always helps. You never have to carry it on your own.
**On screen.** The young person pauses, breathes, and gently sets the phone down, turning to a warm real moment, a friend, a window, daylight. DiGi glows steadily beside them. A soft caption, Childline 0800 1111.
**Illustration prompt.** A young person pausing and breathing then gently setting the phone down to turn towards a warm real moment with a friend in daylight, the calm star glowing beside them, a soft gentle caption reading Childline 0800 1111. Reassuring, safe, warm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** So your takeaway is this. When a picture makes you feel less, name how it was made. Chosen, posed, filtered, maybe not even real. This week, catch just one image that gives you that shrinking feeling, and name one way it was built.
