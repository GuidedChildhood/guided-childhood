# Guided Childhood · Explainer Video Scripts

## KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16

Five minute animated shorts for Justin to build. Seven beats each, runtime up to five minutes. British English, UK law, UK context. The under 16 social media age restriction is arriving, framed as the ban world, and readiness at 16 matters. Hosts are Vix, the sharp streetwise fox who levels with you, and DiGi, the calm trustworthy star who carries every delicate and legal topic in maximum calm. No dashes anywhere. This is the analyst's seat. We pull back the curtain, teach the mechanism, and let you judge it.

## 15. Manipulation and persuasion

- **Lesson**: Manipulation and persuasion
- **Stage**: KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
- **Host character**: Vix, sharp streetwise fox who levels with you and never talks down
- **Learning outcome**: I can name the technique being used on me
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Honest, quick, respectful. Vix hands you the analyst's seat and treats you as nearly adult.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:30)
**Narration.** You get a message. Everyone is going. You are the only one not coming. Say yes now or you miss it forever. Your stomach drops and your thumb is already moving. Stop. Nobody just told you a fact. Somebody just used a technique on you.
**On screen.** A phone screen with a message bubble. The words Everyone and Now and Forever glow slightly brighter than the rest. A thumb hovers over yes.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly phone held up close, one message bubble on screen with three words softly glowing, a hovering thumb, a small alert fox peering in from the corner of the frame. Warm illustrated style, clean, bright, one idea, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)
**Narration.** I am Vix. Here is the thing nobody tells you. Persuasion is not magic. It is a small set of moves, and every move has a name. Once you can name the move, it loses most of its power. So today you take the analyst's seat. You stop being the target and you start being the person who spots the trick.
**On screen.** Vix the fox pulls back a curtain to reveal a tidy wall of labelled cards. Big title card reads Name The Move.
**Illustration prompt.** A confident fox character pulling back a stage curtain to reveal a neat wall of labelled cards, a large heading card reading Name The Move, the fox gesturing like a guide. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The mechanism (1:10 to 2:10)
**Narration.** Here are four moves you will meet this week. Urgency. Now or never, so you cannot think. Social proof. Everyone is doing it, so you feel odd standing still. Scarcity. Only a few left, only for you, so you grab. And flattery. You are not like the others, which feels good and lowers your guard. Four moves. Four names. Watch how they always rush you past the pause.
**On screen.** Four cards flip over one at a time. Urgency with a ticking clock. Social proof with a crowd. Scarcity with a nearly empty shelf. Flattery with a shiny mirror.
**Illustration prompt.** Four flip cards in a row, each with a simple icon and a one word label, a clock for urgency, a small crowd for social proof, an almost empty shelf for scarcity, a mirror for flattery, the fox pointing along the row. Warm illustrated style, clean, bright, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** Let us dissect one. A seller messages you. This deal ends in ten minutes and I only offer it to serious people like you. Feel that. That is urgency and flattery stacked together. Wrong response, panic and pay. Right response, name it out loud. That is urgency. That is flattery. A real offer survives a night's sleep. A trick needs you to move before you think.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a panicked figure tapping fast, red. Right, the same figure calm, labelling the message Urgency plus Flattery, then putting the phone down.
**Illustration prompt.** A clean split scene, left side a hurried figure tapping a phone with a red rushed feeling, right side the same figure calm and steady placing two small labels reading Urgency and Flattery onto the message then setting the phone down. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Here is the part they do not advertise. Knowing the trick does not make you immune. That is the trap. You think, I would spot that, so you drop your guard, and the move lands anyway because it works on feeling, not on cleverness. So do not rely on being smart. Rely on the pause. The pause is the one thing every one of these moves is built to steal from you.
**On screen.** A person labelled I would never fall for that, while a small trick quietly slips past them. Then a big calm word appears. Pause.
**Illustration prompt.** A confident figure with a small thought label reading I would never fall for that while a tiny trick figure tiptoes past unnoticed, then a large gentle word Pause appearing in the centre with soft light. Warm illustrated style, bright, one idea, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is what you actually do. When your stomach drops and your thumb speeds up, that feeling is the alarm. Do three things. Name the move out loud. Wait one full hour before you reply. And if it is about money or something private, tell one real person you trust before you act. A good choice does not expire in ten minutes.
**On screen.** A simple three step card. One, name it. Two, wait an hour. Three, tell someone you trust. Vix taps each step.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly three step card headed Take The Wheel with steps reading Name it, Wait an hour, Tell someone you trust, the fox tapping the steps like a guide. Warm illustrated style, clean, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** You are not an easy target. You are the analyst now. This week, catch one message that tries to rush you and name the move out loud, even just to yourself. Next time we look at something bigger. When persuasion turns into pressure to share a photo, the law itself steps in on your side. That is next.
**On screen.** Vix gives a small nod. A calendar tag reads This week, name one move. A soft arrow points forward to a star, DiGi.
**Illustration prompt.** The fox giving a warm confident nod, a small calendar tag reading This week name one move, a gentle forward arrow pointing toward a soft glowing star in the distance. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

## 16. Consent, images and the law

- **Lesson**: Consent, images and the law
- **Stage**: KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
- **Host character**: DiGi, the calm trustworthy star who carries legal topics in maximum calm
- **Learning outcome**: I know the law and my options before anything is shared
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Calm, plain, steady. Never graphic, never a lecture. The law is explained as something on your side.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:30)
**Narration.** Someone asks you for a photo. Or someone shows you one of a person you both know and says, look at this. In that moment it can feel like there is only one polite thing to do. There is more going on here than politeness. There is a law, and it was written to protect you. Let us walk through it calmly, together.
**On screen.** A phone face down on a table. A gentle question mark hovers above it. No image is shown. Everything is soft and unhurried.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone lying face down on a calm table with a soft question mark floating above it, muted warm colours, no image on screen, a quiet unhurried mood, a small gentle glowing star waiting nearby. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)
**Narration.** I am DiGi. I will keep this calm and clear, because the calm bit matters. Here is the big idea. In the UK, a photo of a young person under eighteen that is sexual in nature is treated seriously by the law, even when the young person is the one who took it. That sounds heavy. Stay with me. The point of this law is not to catch you out. It is to give you a reason and a right to say no.
**On screen.** DiGi the star appears, steady and warm. A calm title card reads The Law Is On Your Side.
**Illustration prompt.** A gentle glowing star character with a kind steady expression beside a calm title card reading The Law Is On Your Side, soft reassuring colours, quiet and safe mood. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The mechanism (1:10 to 2:10)
**Narration.** Let us make the law simple. Three plain points. One. A private image of anyone under eighteen is not allowed to be made, kept, or shared. Not by them, not by anyone. Two. Consent does not fix this. Even if someone says yes, the image is still not allowed to exist. Three. Sharing an image of someone without their permission is a separate wrong on its own, at any age. So the law gives you clear ground to stand on. No is not rude here. No is right.
**On screen.** Three calm cards. Not made or kept or shared. Consent does not make it legal. Sharing without permission is its own wrong.
**Illustration prompt.** Three clean calm cards in a row with short plain text labels, soft icons of a shield, a stop hand, and a locked box, the star gently indicating each one. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · A real example dissected (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** Picture this. A boy sends a girl he likes a message. Just send one, everyone does, I would never show anyone. Let us dissect it calmly. Everyone does is social proof. I would never show anyone is a promise nobody can keep, because once an image leaves your phone it is not yours anymore. The kind answer and the legal answer are the same. No. Not because you do not like him. Because the law and your future both say no.
**On screen.** A message bubble with the words everyone and never gently underlined. An arrow shows a photo icon leaving a phone and multiplying, then a calm stop.
**Illustration prompt.** A single message bubble with two words softly underlined, a small photo icon leaving a phone and beginning to copy outward, then a gentle stop symbol, the star watching calmly. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, muted warm colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Here is the part that surprises people. If someone pressures you and it goes wrong, you are not the one in trouble. The law sees the person who pressured, the person who shared, as the one in the wrong. And if an image of you ever does get out, there is a real route to get it taken down. It is not hopeless and it is never the end of your story. Adults deal with this. That is their job, not yours.
**On screen.** A worried figure, then a warm shield lowers over them. A soft label reads You are not in trouble. A second label reads There is a way to take it down.
**Illustration prompt.** A young figure looking worried while a warm protective shield gently lowers over them, two soft labels reading You are not in trouble and There is a way to take it down, the star standing steady beside them. Warm illustrated style, calm, reassuring, non graphic, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here are your options, ready before you ever need them. You never have to send anything. That is always allowed. If someone asks, you can simply say no, or say nothing at all. If you are worried about an image, of you or anyone, tell a trusted adult, or call Childline free on 0800 1111, or use the report button on the Child Exploitation and Online Protection site, which is CEOP. Do not carry this alone. You never have to.
**On screen.** A calm options card. You can always say no. Tell a trusted adult. Childline 0800 1111. Report on the CEOP site.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly options card headed Your Options with clear plain lines reading You can always say no, Tell a trusted adult, Childline 0800 1111, Report on the CEOP site, the star resting calmly beside it. Warm illustrated style, calm, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** Remember this. The law here is not against you. It is standing next to you. Your body and your image are yours to keep private, always. This week, save the Childline number, 0800 1111, in your phone, just so it is there. And know that if the pressure ever turns into a threat, there is one more thing you need to understand, and we will face it together, calmly, next time.
**On screen.** A phone with a saved contact, Childline 0800 1111. DiGi gives a calm reassuring look. A soft forward arrow to the next star.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone showing a saved contact reading Childline 0800 1111, the gentle star with a calm reassuring expression, a soft forward arrow pointing onward. Warm illustrated style, calm, reassuring, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

## 17. Sextortion

- **Lesson**: Sextortion
- **Stage**: KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
- **Host character**: DiGi, the calm trustworthy star, maximum calm
- **Learning outcome**: I know exactly who to tell and that it is not my fault
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Maximum calm, warm, plain. Warmth over fear. Ends on support and agency, never on dread.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:30)
**Narration.** Someone online turns friendly, fast. Then something is shared, or they claim it is. And then the message changes. Pay me, or send more, or I will show everyone. If that ever happens, your heart will pound and your head will say you are trapped. You are not trapped. I am going to stay right here and walk you through exactly what to do.
**On screen.** A phone with a threatening message shown only as a grey blurred bubble, no words readable. A calm star steps into frame beside it. Nothing graphic.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone with a single greyed out blurred message bubble, unreadable, a gentle glowing star stepping calmly into the frame beside the worried space, soft muted colours, safe and steady mood. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)
**Narration.** I am DiGi, and I will keep my voice calm because you need calm right now, not panic. This has a name. It is called sextortion. It is a scam, and it is one of the oldest tricks there is, just moved online. The person is not powerful. They are a con artist running the same script on many people at once. The moment you see it for what it is, a scam, you take back the ground.
**On screen.** DiGi the star, steady and warm. A calm title card reads This Is A Scam. It Has A Name.
**Illustration prompt.** A gentle glowing star character with a calm steady expression next to a title card reading This Is A Scam, soft reassuring colours, a quiet unhurried feeling. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The mechanism (1:10 to 2:10)
**Narration.** Here is how the scam works, so it holds no mystery. First, they rush to seem close, a fast friend. Then they get something, or pretend they have. Then comes the threat and the demand, usually money, and usually with a clock. Send it now or else. That clock is the whole trick. It only works if you act alone and in a hurry. So the answer is the exact opposite. Slow down, and do not stay alone.
**On screen.** Three calm steps. Fast friend. The threat. The clock. Then a gentle line crosses out the clock.
**Illustration prompt.** Three simple calm cards reading Fast friend, The threat, The clock, with a gentle line drawn through the clock card, the star pointing steadily, soft muted colours. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, bright, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** So picture the message arrives. The wrong path is what they are counting on. Pay them, panic, and delete everything in fear. Here is why that fails. Paying tells them you are worth more, so they ask again. And deleting removes the proof adults need to help you. The right path is calmer and stronger. Stop. Do not pay. Do not delete anything. Tell someone now. Four small moves, and every one of them takes power back.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a red hurried path, pay and delete, leading nowhere. Right, a calm path. Stop. Do not pay. Do not delete. Tell someone.
**Illustration prompt.** A clean split scene, left a red rushed path with icons of paying and deleting leading to a dead end, right a calm steady path with four gentle steps reading Stop, Do not pay, Do not delete, Tell someone, the star beside the calm path. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Here is the part that changes everything, and I need you to really hear it. This is not your fault. It does not matter what was said or sent or how it started. A scammer chose to do this. The blame is entirely theirs. And the thing they are counting on most is your shame keeping you silent. So the bravest and smartest thing is simply to tell someone. The second you are not alone, the scam falls apart.
**On screen.** A figure carrying a heavy weight labelled shame, then a trusted adult gently lifts it away. A soft label reads Not your fault.
**Illustration prompt.** A young figure carrying a heavy bundle softly labelled shame, a kind adult figure gently helping lift it away, a warm label reading Not your fault, the star glowing steadily nearby. Warm illustrated style, calm, reassuring, non graphic, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is exactly what you do, in order. Stop. Do not pay a penny. Do not delete the messages, they are your evidence. And tell someone now. A parent, a teacher, any trusted adult. Or call Childline free on 0800 1111, any time, day or night. Or report it on the Child Exploitation and Online Protection site, CEOP. You will not be in trouble. They will help you make it stop.
**On screen.** A clear action card. Stop. Do not pay. Do not delete. Tell someone now. Below it, Childline 0800 1111 and Report on the CEOP site.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly action card headed What To Do Now with steps reading Stop, Do not pay, Do not delete, Tell someone now, and a lower line reading Childline 0800 1111 and Report on the CEOP site, the star resting calmly beside it. Warm illustrated style, calm, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** So hold on to this. It is not your fault. You are not in trouble. And you are never as alone as the scam wants you to feel. There is always an adult whose actual job is to help you with this. This week, do one small thing. Save Childline in your phone, 0800 1111, and pick in your mind the one adult you would tell. Just so you already know. You have got this, and you have got people.
**On screen.** A phone with saved contact Childline 0800 1111, and a small circled figure labelled my person. DiGi gives a warm steady look.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone showing a saved contact Childline 0800 1111 and a small softly circled trusted figure labelled my person, the gentle star with a warm steady reassuring expression. Warm illustrated style, calm, reassuring, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

## 18. Radicalisation and misogyny

- **Lesson**: Radicalisation and misogyny
- **Stage**: KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
- **Host character**: DiGi, the calm trustworthy star, carries the delicate topic in maximum calm
- **Learning outcome**: I can recognise when content is grooming my beliefs
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Calm, respectful, critical thinking led. Never platforms any extremist claim, never names influencers. Ends on agency.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:30)
**Narration.** You watch one video. It gets you. It says the thing you have been feeling, that you are overlooked, that something is unfair. It feels like someone finally understands. The next video is a little stronger. And the one after that. Nobody announced a plan. But a plan was running. Today we pull the curtain back on it.
**On screen.** A single video tile that quietly multiplies into a row, each one a shade more intense. A calm star watches from the side.
**Illustration prompt.** A single video tile softly multiplying into a row of tiles, each a slightly deeper shade, suggesting a slow slide, a gentle glowing star observing calmly from the side. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)
**Narration.** I am DiGi. Here is the calm truth. Some content is not trying to inform you. It is trying to recruit you. That is what radicalisation is. Not a shout, but a slow pull on what you believe. And it almost never starts with hate. It starts with something warmer. Belonging. A feeling that here, finally, are your people, and here, finally, is who to blame.
**On screen.** DiGi the star beside a title card reading Grooming Your Beliefs. Underneath, two soft words, Belonging and Blame.
**Illustration prompt.** A gentle glowing star character next to a calm title card reading Grooming Your Beliefs, with two soft supporting words Belonging and Blame beneath, muted warm colours, quiet serious but safe mood. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The mechanism (1:10 to 2:10)
**Narration.** Here is the pipeline, in plain steps, so you can spot it. Step one, belonging. You are welcomed, you feel understood. Step two, grievance. You are told your struggles are somebody else's fault, and very often that somebody is women and girls. Step three, the enemy. A whole group is turned into the problem. Step four, loyalty. Doubting the group starts to feel like betraying it. Belonging, grievance, enemy, loyalty. Same shape every time.
**On screen.** Four connected steps along a line. Belonging. Grievance. Enemy. Loyalty. Each one a step deeper.
**Illustration prompt.** Four connected stepping stones descending gently, labelled Belonging, Grievance, Enemy, Loyalty, the star standing at the top pointing calmly down the path. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · A real example dissected (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** Let us name one clearly, because it is everywhere. A lot of this content targets boys with a simple sell. Feel strong again, and here is who to resent. Women. Girls. It dresses contempt up as confidence and calls cruelty just honesty. Here is the tell. Real advice makes you bigger and kinder. This makes you angrier and smaller, and it needs an enemy to work. That need for an enemy is the giveaway every single time.
**On screen.** A card labelled Confidence peels back to reveal Contempt underneath. Beside it, a simple test. Bigger and kinder, or angrier and smaller.
**Illustration prompt.** A card labelled Confidence with a corner peeling back to reveal the word Contempt beneath, next to a simple two way sign reading Bigger and kinder versus Angrier and smaller, the star indicating the test calmly. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, muted colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Here is the part they hide. This is a business. Outrage keeps you watching, and watching is what they sell. Your anger is the product. The people selling you an enemy are usually just selling you, to advertisers, to their own fame, to their own pocket. It was never about helping you feel strong. You were the customer and the product at the same time. Once you see the till behind the message, the spell weakens.
**On screen.** Behind an angry video, a cash register quietly rings. A label reads Your attention is the product.
**Illustration prompt.** An intense looking video tile with a small cash register quietly ringing behind it and coins dropping, a calm label reading Your attention is the product, the star revealing the till from behind a curtain. Warm illustrated style, calm, non graphic, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel. Ask three questions of anything that stirs you up. Who benefits if I believe this. Who is this teaching me to hate. And does this make me bigger and kinder, or smaller and angrier. Those three questions are your shield. And if content ever frightens you, or someone is pushing you somewhere darker, tell a trusted adult, or call Childline free on 0800 1111. Thinking it through with someone is a strength, not a weakness.
**On screen.** A shield card with three questions. Who benefits. Who am I taught to hate. Bigger or smaller. Below, a trusted adult and Childline 0800 1111.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly shield shaped card headed Three Questions reading Who benefits, Who am I taught to hate, Bigger or smaller, with a lower line showing a trusted adult figure and Childline 0800 1111, the star beside it steady. Warm illustrated style, calm, bright, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** So remember. Your beliefs are yours, and they are worth guarding as carefully as anything you own. Anyone handing you an enemy wants something from you. This week, next time a video makes you angry, pause and ask just one question. Who profits if I believe this. That pause is you, taking your mind back. And your mind is absolutely worth keeping.
**On screen.** DiGi gives a calm steady nod. A tag reads This week, ask who profits. A soft forward arrow to a fox, Vix.
**Illustration prompt.** The gentle star with a calm steady nod, a small tag reading This week ask who profits, a soft forward arrow pointing toward an alert friendly fox in the distance. Warm illustrated style, calm, reassuring, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

## 19. Readiness at 16: the ban world

- **Lesson**: Readiness at 16, the ban world
- **Stage**: KS4, Years 10 to 11, ages 14 to 16
- **Host character**: Vix, sharp streetwise fox, with DiGi the calm star
- **Learning outcome**: I can plan how I will handle full access when it arrives
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Honest, respectful, forward looking. Treats the ban world as real and readiness as the task.

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:30)
**Narration.** Right now in the UK, the plan is simple. Under sixteen, the big social apps are off. Then one day, on your birthday, a door opens and it is all just there. Every app, all at once, no training wheels. Most people walk through that door with no plan at all. You are not going to be most people.
**On screen.** A large calm door marked 16. Behind it, a glowing wall of app icons. A fox stands in front, sizing it up.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly door with the number 16 on it, a soft glow of many app icons behind it, an alert confident fox standing in front looking at it thoughtfully, a gentle star nearby. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:30 to 1:10)
**Narration.** I am Vix, and DiGi is here too. Here is the honest bit. The ban is not the finish line. Sixteen is the start line. The apps do not get safer the day you turn sixteen. You just get let in. So the real question is not will you get access. You will. The question is, who do you want to be when you do. That part, you get to plan now.
**On screen.** Vix and DiGi together. A start line, not a finish line, with a runner ready. A title card reads Sixteen Is A Start Line.
**Illustration prompt.** A confident fox and a gentle glowing star standing together at a running start line, a title card reading Sixteen Is A Start Line, a ready hopeful mood. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The mechanism (1:10 to 2:10)
**Narration.** DiGi, tell them how it will actually feel. On day one, three things hit at once. The rush, everything is new and loud and exciting. The flood, more content than any human can hold. And the compare, everyone else looks like they already know the rules. That is normal. It is not a sign you are behind. It is just the door opening. Knowing it is coming is half of handling it.
**On screen.** Three waves roll toward a calm figure. The rush. The flood. The compare. The figure stands steady, braced and ready.
**Illustration prompt.** Three gentle labelled waves reading The rush, The flood, The compare rolling toward a calm braced figure who stands steady, the star explaining beside them. Warm illustrated style, calm, bright, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** So here are two ways through that door. The unready way. Download everything, follow everyone, let the apps set your bedtime and your mood, and drift. The ready way. Pick a couple of apps on purpose. Decide your own limits before the app decides them for you. And keep the offline life that is already yours. Same door. Two completely different years on the other side of it.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a figure buried under app icons, drifting. Right, a figure holding just two chosen apps, standing tall, a football and friends nearby.
**Illustration prompt.** A clean split scene, left a figure half buried under a pile of app icons looking adrift, right a calm figure holding just two chosen app icons standing tall with a football and friends nearby. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Here is what the apps will never tell you. They are not built for your sixteenth birthday. They are built for their shareholders. Every default, the endless feed, the notifications, the autoplay, is set to keep you, not to serve you. So readiness is really just this. Changing the settings they hoped you would never touch. The power was always in the settings menu. Almost nobody opens it. You will.
**On screen.** An app settings menu opens. Toggles flip from their default to your choice. A label reads Defaults are their choice, not yours.
**Illustration prompt.** A friendly app settings panel with several toggles being switched from a default position to a chosen position, a calm label reading Defaults are their choice not yours, the fox flipping a switch with a knowing look. Warm illustrated style, bright, clean, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So let us write your day one plan now, while it is calm. Three moves. One, choose two apps that actually add to your life, not twenty that drain it. Two, set your limits first, your bedtime, your notifications off, before you post a thing. Three, keep one real world anchor, a sport, a job, a friend group, that does not live on a screen. Write those three down today. Future you will be very glad you did.
**On screen.** A day one plan card. Two apps on purpose. Limits first. One offline anchor. Vix and DiGi both point to it.
**Illustration prompt.** A large friendly plan card headed My Day One Plan reading Two apps on purpose, Limits first, One offline anchor, the fox and the star both gesturing toward it warmly. Warm illustrated style, bright, calm, plenty of white space, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:30 to 5:00)
**Narration.** So here is the whole thing. The door will open. You cannot stop that, and you would not want to. But you can decide who walks through it. This week, write your day one plan, just three lines, and keep it somewhere safe for the birthday it is really for. You have spent years learning how this stuff works.
