# Guided Childhood KS2 Lesson Scripts (Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11)

Six explainer video scripts for Justin to turn into animated shorts. Each runs about three to four minutes, seven beats, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9. Concrete first, label second. British English throughout. No dashes anywhere, ever.

---

## Lesson 4

- **Lesson**: Screen routines that work
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: Oliver, the one who thinks ahead, emblem hourglass, calm and practical
- **Learning outcome**: One routine the child sets tonight
- **Runtime**: about 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, a steady friend who has your back

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** It is bath time. It is dinner time. It is bed time. And somehow, every single time, the screen wins. You look up and a whole hour has gone, and you did not choose to give it away. Sound familiar? You are not naughty and you are not weak. The screen is just very good at holding on.
**On screen.** A cosy kitchen at dusk. A child on the sofa glued to a tablet while a clock on the wall spins fast. A plate of dinner going cold.
**Illustration prompt.** Warm illustrated evening kitchen scene, a young child sitting cross legged on a sofa absorbed in a glowing tablet, a round wall clock with hands spinning quickly, an untouched dinner plate steaming less and less, soft lamp light, gentle and relatable not scary, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and the guide (0:35 to 1:10)
**Narration.** Meet Oliver. Oliver thinks ahead. He carries a little hourglass, because his whole job is time. And here is the one word Oliver wants you to know today. Routine. A routine is just a plan you make once, so you do not have to fight the same battle every night. Make the plan when you are calm, and it does the hard work for you later.
**On screen.** Oliver steps in, hourglass in hand, and writes the word ROUTINE in big friendly letters. Under it he sets down the tablet gently.
**Illustration prompt.** Friendly character Oliver holding a small glowing hourglass, calm and practical expression, standing beside a large hand lettered word ROUTINE, placing a tablet down calmly, warm colours, encouraging teacher energy, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:10 to 2:00)
**Narration.** A routine that works has three tiny parts. A start, so you know when screens begin. A stop, so you know when they end. And a next thing, something good that happens straight after, so stopping feels like moving on, not losing out. Watch. Screens on after homework. Screens off when the timer sings. Then the next thing, kicking a ball outside or reading in the den. Start. Stop. Next.
**On screen.** Oliver builds three stepping stones across a stream. Stone one says START. Stone two says STOP. Stone three shows a football and a book.
**Illustration prompt.** Oliver crossing a gentle stream on three big stepping stones labelled START, STOP and NEXT, the last stone showing a football and an open book, playful and clear, three step visual metaphor, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:00 to 2:50)
**Narration.** Here is where most plans fall over. The wrong way. Just one more level. Just one more video. Just five more minutes. Five more minutes never arrives, does it. Now the right way. Oliver picks the stop moment before he starts, not during. A timer he can hear. And when it sings, screens off, even mid level. It stings for about ten seconds. Then it is fine. Every time.
**On screen.** Split scene. Left, a child pleading just five more minutes as the clock melts. Right, Oliver setting a loud friendly timer first, then calmly closing the tablet when it rings.
**Illustration prompt.** Split illustration, left side a child begging with a melting droopy clock and the words five more minutes, right side Oliver confidently setting a cheerful ringing timer then closing a tablet with a calm smile, clear contrast between chaos and calm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (2:50 to 3:30)
**Narration.** Here is the part the apps hope you never work out. The screen has no stop built in. It is designed to roll on for ever, auto play, next up, keep going. If you do not bring a stop, there is not one. So your timer is not a punishment. Your timer is the missing piece the app left out on purpose.
**On screen.** A long ribbon of videos rolling on and on with no end. Oliver walks up and drops a bright timer onto it. The ribbon finally stops.
**Illustration prompt.** An endless ribbon of tiny auto playing video thumbnails stretching off into the distance with no end in sight, Oliver placing a glowing timer onto the ribbon which makes it come to a gentle stop, makes an invisible design trick visible, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:30 to 4:05)
**Narration.** So tonight, you are in charge. Pick your three parts with a grown up. When do screens start. When do they stop. And the best bit, what good thing happens next. Say it out loud together, then write it on one card and stick it where you both can see it. That card is your routine now. Not a battle. A plan.
**On screen.** Oliver and a child high five over a bright card on the fridge reading START, STOP, NEXT with their own words filled in.
**Illustration prompt.** Oliver and a happy child high fiving beside a fridge, a colourful hand written card stuck on it with three headings START, STOP and NEXT filled in with friendly writing, proud teamwork moment, warm kitchen, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:05 to 4:30)
**Narration.** One thing to remember. A screen has no stop, so you bring the stop. This week, make your one card and try it for three nights. Notice how the first few seconds sting and then melt away. Next time, Oliver has his eye on games, and the clever ways they try to get your pocket money. Bring your curiosity.
**On screen.** The finished routine card glows on the fridge. Oliver taps his hourglass and winks. Small preview of a game controller with a coin.
**Illustration prompt.** The completed routine card glowing warmly on a fridge, Oliver tapping his hourglass and giving a friendly wink, a small preview corner showing a game controller with a gold coin, hopeful and inviting close, 16 by 9.

## Lesson 5

- **Lesson**: Gaming, time, intensity and spend
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: Oliver, the one who thinks ahead, emblem hourglass, calm and practical
- **Learning outcome**: I can spot when a game is trying to get me to spend
- **Runtime**: about 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, on the child's side against the tricks

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** You are playing your favourite game. You are doing great. And then a shiny box pops up. A glowing skin. A special pet. A timer counting down, only twelve hours left. Your heart does a little jump. You want it. Right now. Here is a question worth asking. Why did that box show up exactly when you were having fun?
**On screen.** A child mid game, delighted, when a sparkling loot box bursts onto the screen with a ticking countdown and a big BUY NOW button.
**Illustration prompt.** A young child playing a colourful game on a tablet looking excited, a dazzling treasure chest popping up on screen glittering with sparkles, a red countdown timer and a bright BUY NOW button, tempting and shiny, gentle warning tone, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and the guide (0:35 to 1:10)
**Narration.** Oliver is back, hourglass in hand. He loves games, honestly he does. But Oliver reads the small print the rest of us skip. And here is his word for today. Design. Everything in a game was designed, drawn and placed there by a grown up who wanted something. Most of it wants you to have fun. Some of it wants your pocket money. Today we learn to tell the two apart.
**On screen.** Oliver holds up a game like an X ray, showing the fun parts glowing warm and the spend parts glowing a different colour. He writes DESIGN.
**Illustration prompt.** Oliver holding up a game screen like an X ray panel, parts of the game glowing warm friendly gold and other parts glowing a cooler colour, the word DESIGN hand lettered beside him, calm investigator feel, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:10 to 2:05)
**Narration.** A spending trap almost always uses three moves. One, a rush, a timer or a limited drop so you cannot stop and think. Two, a shine, something so pretty you feel you must have it. Three, a tiny price that hides a big one, because pounds turn into gems, and gems make you forget it is real money. Rush. Shine. Hidden cost. Once you see the three moves, you cannot unsee them.
**On screen.** Three cards flip over. Card one, a ticking clock. Card two, a glittering skin. Card three, a coin turning into gems then quietly back into a five pound note.
**Illustration prompt.** Three flip cards in a row, first a ticking clock labelled rush, second a glittering character skin labelled shine, third a gold coin morphing into gems then into a five pound note labelled hidden cost, clean and clear teaching layout, warm style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:05 to 2:55)
**Narration.** The wrong way feels like this. Ooh, only ten minutes left, buy it quick before it is gone. And later, a grown up finds a bill and nobody is happy. The right way is slower, and Oliver does it every time. He names the trick out loud. That is a rush. He puts the controller down for one minute. And he asks the real question. If the timer was not there, would I still want this tomorrow. Usually the answer is no.
**On screen.** Split scene. Left, a child tapping buy fast, coins flying away. Right, Oliver pausing, saying that is a rush, and waiting calmly as the timer runs out and nothing bad happens.
**Illustration prompt.** Split illustration, left side a child frantically tapping a buy button with gold coins flying out of a wallet, right side Oliver calmly setting the controller down saying that is a rush while a countdown expires harmlessly, strong calm versus panic contrast, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (2:55 to 3:35)
**Narration.** Here is the part they really do not say out loud. The game is often free, but it is not really free. The way it makes money is by getting you to spend a little, again and again, without noticing. That is the whole plan. So when a game is generous with shiny boxes, it is not being kind. It is fishing. And you get to decide whether to bite.
**On screen.** A cheerful free to play sign, then the camera pulls back to reveal a friendly looking fishing line dangling a shiny skin as bait over the water.
**Illustration prompt.** A bright free to play game banner, camera pulling back to reveal it is bait on a gentle fishing line, a glittering game skin dangling as the lure above calm water, a small fish looking up, makes the business model visible without being frightening, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:35 to 4:05)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel. Before you play, agree a spend rule with a grown up. Maybe it is zero. Maybe it is a set amount for a birthday. Either way, decide it before the shiny box appears, not during. And build the one minute pause habit. Rush, shine, hidden cost. Spot even one of the three, and put the controller down for sixty seconds.
**On screen.** Oliver and a child shake hands on a simple written spend rule taped near the console. A big friendly PAUSE button glows.
**Illustration prompt.** Oliver and a child shaking hands over a small taped note near a games console reading our spend rule, a large glowing friendly PAUSE button beside them, calm agreement and teamwork, warm colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:05 to 4:30)
**Narration.** One thing to remember. Free games still want your money, so spot the rush, the shine and the hidden cost. This week, agree your spend rule before you next play, and try naming a trick out loud when you see it. It feels great to catch one. Next time we go inside something even sneakier, the way your feed decides what to show you. DiGi will guide you through that one.
**On screen.** The spend rule note glows by the console. Oliver taps his hourglass. A small preview of a glowing star, DiGi, beside a scrolling feed.
**Illustration prompt.** The spend rule note glowing by a games console, Oliver tapping his hourglass with a knowing smile, a small preview corner showing a bright friendly star character beside a scrolling phone feed, hopeful handover, 16 by 9.

## Lesson 6

- **Lesson**: How algorithms work
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: DiGi, the star, the guide, emblem star, calm and trustworthy, never alarmist
- **Learning outcome**: I can explain why my feed keeps me watching
- **Runtime**: about 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, clear and calm, demystifying not scaring

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** You watched one video about puppies. Just one. And now your whole feed is puppies. Puppies falling asleep, puppies barking, puppies wearing tiny hats. You never asked for a hundred puppies. So how did the app know. And more strangely, how did it get it so right that you kept watching?
**On screen.** A phone feed filling up, one puppy video multiplying into a wall of puppy videos, a child watching wide eyed.
**Illustration prompt.** A child holding a phone with delighted surprise, the screen showing one puppy video multiplying into a whole grid of puppy videos, cosy and a little uncanny, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and the guide (0:35 to 1:15)
**Narration.** This is DiGi. The star. Your guide for the trickier stuff. DiGi is calm, because once you understand something it stops being scary. And here is the word for today. Algorithm. It sounds enormous. It is not. An algorithm is just a helper that watches what you do and tries to guess what you will like next. That is it. A guesser. A very, very fast guesser.
**On screen.** DiGi appears as a warm glowing star and writes ALGORITHM, then draws a simple friendly robot with a magnifying glass and a notepad.
**Illustration prompt.** Friendly glowing star character DiGi hand lettering the word ALGORITHM, beside a simple cute robot holding a magnifying glass and a notepad, calm reassuring mood, demystifying a big word, warm colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:15 to 2:05)
**Narration.** Here is how the guesser guesses, in three little steps. One, it watches. Not what you say you like, what you actually do. Two, it counts. How long you watched, what you tapped, what you skipped. Three, it serves. It brings you more of whatever kept you looking longest. Watch, count, serve. Then it does the whole thing again, and again, thousands of times a day, getting a tiny bit better at guessing you every time.
**On screen.** A three part conveyor belt. An eye labelled WATCH, a tally counter labelled COUNT, a plate labelled SERVE handing over more videos, looping round.
**Illustration prompt.** A simple looping conveyor belt with three stations, a friendly eye labelled watch, a clicking tally counter labelled count, a plate labelled serve handing a stack of videos back to a phone, the loop arrow circling round, clear machine diagram, warm and approachable, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:05 to 2:55)
**Narration.** Now, most people believe the wrong thing. They think, the app knows me, it must really get me. But look closer. The app does not know you. It only knows what makes you stop scrolling. Those are not the same thing. The right way to think about it is this. It is not a friend who understands you. It is a mirror that copies your habits. And a mirror cannot tell the difference between what is good for you and what just grabs you.
**On screen.** Split scene. Left, a child imagining the app as a warm friend who understands them. Right, DiGi turns it around to reveal a mirror simply copying the child's every scroll.
**Illustration prompt.** Split illustration, left side a child picturing the phone as a warm smiling friend with a heart, right side DiGi gently turning it to reveal the truth, a plain mirror reflecting the child's scrolling hand, thoughtful gentle reveal, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (2:55 to 3:35)
**Narration.** Here is the part they do not advertise. The guesser learns fastest from strong feelings. Things that make you laugh hard, or gasp, or feel a bit worried, keep you watching more than calm things do. So the loudest and most extreme stuff gets pushed to the front, not because it is true or good, but because it holds you. That is why your feed can feel exciting and exhausting at the same time.
**On screen.** Two videos race along a track. A calm gentle one and a loud shocking one. The loud one pulls ahead and gets boosted to the top of the feed.
**Illustration prompt.** Two short videos drawn as little race cars on a track, one calm and smiley, one loud and shocking with wide eyes, the loud one pulling ahead with a boost arrow lifting it to the top of a feed, playful but makes a serious point, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:35 to 4:05)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel back. The guesser learns from what you do, which means you can teach it on purpose. Watch the calm and kind things all the way through, and it serves you more of those. Skip fast past the stuff that leaves you feeling worse. And every so often, do the thing it never expects. Put the phone down and go and do something in the real world. That confuses the guesser in the best possible way.
**On screen.** DiGi guides a child's hand to linger on a kind video and swipe quickly past an angry one, then to set the phone down and step outside into sunshine.
**Illustration prompt.** DiGi gently guiding a child's hand to rest on a kind gentle video and swipe away from an angry one, then the child setting the phone down and stepping out into a sunny garden, empowering and calm, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:05 to 4:30)
**Narration.** One thing to remember. Your feed is a guesser that copies your habits, so your habits are the steering wheel. This week, teach your feed on purpose for three days. Watch what leaves you feeling good, skip what does not, and notice how it changes. Next time, Sofia takes over to talk about something you carry everywhere without seeing it, your digital reputation.
**On screen.** The feed subtly calms and brightens. DiGi glows and gives a small nod. A preview of Sofia with her shield.
**Illustration prompt.** A phone feed looking calmer and brighter than before, DiGi the star glowing warmly with a small proud nod, a preview corner showing a warm character Sofia holding a shield, hopeful handover, 16 by 9.

## Lesson 7

- **Lesson**: Privacy and digital reputation
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: Sofia, the protector, emblem shield, warm and steady and reassuring
- **Learning outcome**: I can decide what not to share
- **Runtime**: about 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, steady and safe, never frightening

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** Imagine you write your full name, your school, your street and your best friend's secret on a postcard. Then you pin it to the school gate for everyone walking past to read. You would never do that, would you. But online, it is easy to do exactly that without even noticing, one little share at a time.
**On screen.** A giant postcard pinned to a school gate covered in personal details, crowds of people strolling past reading it.
**Illustration prompt.** A large postcard pinned to a school gate filled with hand written personal details like name, school and address, a stream of everyday people walking past glancing at it, gentle and thought provoking not scary, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and the guide (0:35 to 1:15)
**Narration.** Meet Sofia. Sofia is the protector. She carries a shield, and she is warm, not worried. She is not here to scare you off the internet. She is here to help you keep the right things to yourself. And here is her word for today. Private. Private does not mean secret or naughty. Private just means it belongs to you, and you get to choose who sees it.
**On screen.** Sofia steps forward with her shield and writes PRIVATE, then tucks a small treasure chest safely behind the shield.
**Illustration prompt.** Warm character Sofia holding a sturdy friendly shield, hand lettering the word PRIVATE, tucking a small treasure chest safely behind the shield, calm and reassuring protector energy, warm colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:15 to 2:05)
**Narration.** Sofia sorts everything into two piles. The keep in pile and the okay to share pile. In the keep in pile go the things that help a stranger find you or fool you. Your full name, your address, your school, your passwords, and photos that show exactly where you are. In the okay to share pile go the things that are just fun and give nothing away. Your favourite colour, a drawing you made, a joke. Keep in. Okay to share. Simple.
**On screen.** Two labelled baskets. Sofia sorts cards into them. Address, school and password go into KEEP IN. A drawing, a joke and a favourite colour go into OKAY TO SHARE.
**Illustration prompt.** Two labelled baskets, one reading keep in and one reading okay to share, Sofia sorting little cards into them, a house address, a school badge and a padlock going into keep in, a crayon drawing, a smiley joke and a colour swatch going into okay to share, clear sorting activity, warm style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:05 to 2:55)
**Narration.** Here is a slip that happens all the time. A child films a fun video in their bedroom. Behind them, the window shows the street sign, and their school jumper is on the chair. Harmless, right. Not quite. Bit by bit, a stranger could piece together exactly where they live. The right way is easy once you know it. Before you post, Sofia does a quick scan of the background. What is in shot that I did not mean to share. Then she moves it, or she does not post. Ten seconds. Every time.
**On screen.** Split scene. Left, a cheerful video with a street sign and school logo accidentally visible in the background. Right, Sofia scanning the background with her shield and gently clearing those clues away.
**Illustration prompt.** Split illustration, left side a child filming a happy video with a street sign visible through the window and a school logo on a jumper in shot, right side Sofia scanning the background with her shield glowing over the accidental clues and clearing them, careful and calm, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (2:55 to 3:35)
**Narration.** Here is the part that surprises people most. The internet does not forget. When you share something, you do not really get it back. Others can save it, copy it and pass it on, long after you wish you had not. All those little shares add up into something with a name. Your reputation. It is the story the internet tells about you, and today, you start writing it.
**On screen.** A single shared photo copies itself into many hands and spreads outward. Then the copies gather into a book with the child's name on the cover, titled reputation.
**Illustration prompt.** One shared photo copying itself and passing between many hands spreading outward across a soft network, the copies then gathering to form the pages of a book with a child's name on the cover titled reputation, thoughtful and clear, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:35 to 4:05)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel. Before anything goes out, run Sofia's three questions. One, is it in the keep in pile. Two, what is in the background I did not mean to share. Three, would I be happy if my whole class and my nana saw this in a year. If any answer feels off, you keep it in. You are not missing out. You are in charge.
**On screen.** Sofia holds up three question cards. A child checks each one against a photo, then confidently chooses to keep it private with a calm smile.
**Illustration prompt.** Sofia holding up three numbered question cards, a child checking a photo against each one then confidently pressing keep private with a calm proud smile, empowering decision moment, warm and steady, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:05 to 4:30)
**Narration.** One thing to remember. The internet does not forget, so decide before you share, not after. This week, do one background scan on a photo before it goes anywhere, and sort three things into keep in or okay to share out loud with someone you trust. Next time, Sofia stays with us, and brings her friend Zara, to talk about being kind and staying safe when other people are online too.
**On screen.** The child pockets the phone happily, choice made. Sofia raises her shield. A preview of Zara peeking in with her magnifying glass.
**Illustration prompt.** A child happily pocketing a phone having made a good choice, Sofia raising her shield warmly, a preview corner showing curious character Zara peeking in with a magnifying glass, hopeful and safe close, 16 by 9.

## Lesson 8

- **Lesson**: Being kind and safe with others online
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: Sofia, the protector, emblem shield, with Zara, the curious one, emblem magnifying glass
- **Learning outcome**: I know three things to do if someone is unkind online
- **Runtime**: about 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, steady and kind, gives a child real tools

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** You are playing a game with other people, or reading messages, and someone says something that makes your tummy go tight. Mean words. A nasty comment. Being left out on purpose. It stings, and part of you wants to fire something mean straight back. Hold on a second. There is a better move, and it keeps you the winner.
**On screen.** A child at a screen, face falling as an unkind message pops up, a little knot appearing in their tummy shown as a squiggle.
**Illustration prompt.** A young child at a screen with their face falling as an unkind speech bubble appears, a small worried squiggle over their tummy to show the feeling, gentle and empathetic not distressing, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · Big idea and the guides (0:35 to 1:15)
**Narration.** Sofia is here, shield and all, because keeping you safe is her whole heart. And she has brought Zara, who sees straight through tricks. Here is their word for today. Bystander. That is a big word for a simple idea. A bystander is anyone who sees something unkind happen. And the amazing thing is, a bystander is never powerless. What you do next can change everything.
**On screen.** Sofia and Zara stand together. Sofia writes BYSTANDER. Zara peers at it through her magnifying glass and gives a knowing nod.
**Illustration prompt.** Sofia with her shield and Zara with her magnifying glass standing together as a team, Sofia hand lettering the word BYSTANDER, Zara inspecting it through her magnifying glass with a knowing nod, warm and empowering duo, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:15 to 2:10)
**Narration.** If someone is unkind to you online, Sofia has three things to do, and they go in order. One, stop. Do not reply, not even once. A mean message wants a reaction, so give it nothing. Two, save. Take a screenshot, so there is proof and you are not carrying it alone. Three, show. Show a grown up you trust. Not to tell tales. To hand over the weight. Stop. Save. Show. Three steps, and you are never doing it by yourself.
**On screen.** Three big numbered steps light up. STOP shows a hand not typing. SAVE shows a screenshot being taken. SHOW shows the child handing a phone to a trusted adult.
**Illustration prompt.** Three large numbered steps glowing in a row, step one stop showing a hand lifting away from a keyboard, step two save showing a phone taking a screenshot, step three show showing a child handing the phone to a warm trusted adult, clear and reassuring, warm style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:10 to 3:00)
**Narration.** The wrong move feels good for about two seconds. Someone is mean, so you fire back something meaner. Now there are two unkind people, and somehow you can end up in trouble too. Watch the right move instead. Zara spots the trick. See, a mean comment is bait. It is fishing for your reaction. So Sofia does not bite. Stop, save, show. The unkind person gets nothing, and you keep your calm and your good name.
**On screen.** Split scene. Left, two children hurling angry speech bubbles, both getting tangled. Right, Zara pointing out a fishing hook in the mean message while Sofia calmly does stop, save, show.
**Illustration prompt.** Split illustration, left side two children throwing angry speech bubbles and getting tangled together in them, right side Zara pointing out a small fishing hook hidden in a mean message while Sofia calmly follows stop save show, clear contrast, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:40)
**Narration.** Here is the deeper part, and it is important. Most people online are not bystanders who help. They scroll on past. That is exactly why the person who speaks up matters so much. You do not have to fight anyone. Just a small kind thing tips the whole scene. A message to the person who was hurt. Are you okay. That one sentence can matter more than anything the unkind person said.
**On screen.** A crowd of little figures scrolling past a hurt child without stopping. Then one figure stops, turns back and sends a small glowing message, are you okay, and the mood lifts.
**Illustration prompt.** A crowd of small figures scrolling past a hurt child looking away, then one single figure stopping and turning back to send a small glowing kind message reading are you okay, warmth spreading from that one act, gentle and moving, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:40 to 4:10)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel, whether it happens to you or to someone else. If it is you, stop, save, show. If it is someone else, be the one who turns back. Send them a kind word, and tell a grown up together. And a promise worth making now. Never send anything you would not say to a person's face, in front of your teacher. That one rule keeps you kind and keeps you safe.
**On screen.** Sofia and Zara stand either side of a child who confidently holds up a card reading stop, save, show, and turns to send a kind message to a friend.
**Illustration prompt.** Sofia and Zara standing proudly either side of a confident child holding a card reading stop save show, the child turning to send a warm kind message to a friend, teamwork and courage, warm colours, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · Close and the mission (4:10 to 4:35)
**Narration.** One thing to remember. A mean message is bait, so do not bite, stop, save, show. This week, learn your three steps by heart, and if you see someone having a hard time online, be the one who turns back and checks they are okay. Next time, Zara takes the lead on something you make all the time and might not know the rules for, your work, and other people's work.
**On screen.** The child pockets the phone, calm and proud. Sofia and Zara wave. A preview of Zara beside a paintbrush and a music note.
**Illustration prompt.** A child pocketing a phone looking calm and proud, Sofia and Zara waving warmly, a preview corner showing Zara with a magnifying glass beside a paintbrush and a music note, hopeful handover, warm illustrated style, 16 by 9.

## Lesson 9

- **Lesson**: My work and other people's work, copyright and ownership
- **Stage**: KS2, Years 3 to 6, ages 7 to 11
- **Host character**: Zara, the curious one who sees through tricks, emblem magnifying glass, playful and gently conspiratorial
- **Learning outcome**: I can credit work that is not mine
- **Runtime**: about 3 to 4 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Justin, warm and plain, second person, playful and fair, treats the child as a maker

### Beat 1 · Hook (0:00 to 0:35)
**Narration.** You spend all afternoon on a drawing. It is brilliant. You are proud. The next day, someone in your class hands it in as their own, with their name on it, and gets all the claps. How does that feel. Not great, right. Now here is the twist. Online, that exact thing happens all day long, and sometimes, without meaning to, we are the ones doing it.
