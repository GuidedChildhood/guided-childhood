# Guided Childhood, Lesson Scripts: KS5 (Years 12 to 13, ages 16 to 18)

Two five minute explainer scripts hosted by DiGi, written for sixth formers who are ready to be coached, not taught. Each runs about five minutes across seven beats. Turn each one into an animated short.

---

- **Lesson**: AI mastery and data rights
- **Stage**: KS5, Years 12 to 13, ages 16 to 18
- **Host character**: DiGi, the star, the guide, calm and clear
- **Learning outcome**: I can use an AI tool and defend where I checked its work.
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Peer to peer and adult, confident and respectful, a coach handing you the controls rather than a teacher marking your book.

### Beat 1 · The hook (0:00 to 0:45)
**Narration.** It is late. You have an essay due, a personal statement half written, and a tab open with an AI tool blinking at you. You could paste the question, take the answer, and go to bed. Loads of people your age do exactly that. But here is the thing nobody tells you. The people who will win with these tools are not the ones who use them the most. They are the ones who can prove they checked the work.
**On screen.** A sixth former at a desk at night, three tabs open, cursor blinking in a chat box. Two paths glow ahead: PASTE AND GO, and CHECK AND OWN.
**Illustration prompt.** A calm night time desk scene, one sixteen year old student lit by a laptop, an essay document and an AI chat window on screen, two softly glowing forward paths on the wall labelled with short words, friendly star guide DiGi hovering nearby, clean modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:45 to 1:30)
**Narration.** So let us name what is actually happening. This tool was built by feeding it a staggering amount of text from the internet, then training it to predict the next likely word. That process has a name. It is called pre training. It means the model is brilliant at sounding right and has no idea whether it is right. It is a confident guesser, not a knower. Once you hold that one word in your head, pre training, everything about using it well starts to make sense.
**On screen.** The word PRE TRAINING lands in the centre. Behind it, an ocean of text pours into a glowing model shape that then predicts one word at a time.
**Illustration prompt.** A large clear label reading PRE TRAINING, behind it a river of tiny text fragments flowing into a soft glowing brain like model that outputs single words in sequence, DiGi pointing at the label with a calm explaining gesture, modern illustrated style, warm and clear, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:30 to 2:15)
**Narration.** Using it well is a habit you can build in three moves. One, prompt with context. Tell it who you are, what you need, and what good looks like, because a vague ask gets a vague answer. Two, ask for its reasoning, not just its conclusion, so you can see how it got there. Three, treat every claim as a lead to check, never a fact to trust. Context in, reasoning out, claims verified. That is the whole loop.
**On screen.** Three numbered cards slide up in turn: CONTEXT IN, REASONING OUT, VERIFY EVERY CLAIM.
**Illustration prompt.** Three clean numbered cards rising in sequence with short labels, a student typing a detailed prompt on one, arrows looping between them to show a repeatable method, DiGi gesturing along the loop like a coach, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:00)
**Narration.** Watch the difference. The naive way. You ask for the causes of a war, you get four tidy paragraphs with two dates that are simply wrong, and you hand it in. The mastered way. You ask for the causes, then you ask where each claim comes from, you open a trusted source, and you catch that one date is off by a decade before it ever reaches your teacher. Same tool. Same two minutes. One of those is a grade, and a habit, that holds up.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, a student pasting an answer with two dates quietly flagged red. Right, the same student cross checking a source and correcting a date to green.
**Illustration prompt.** A clear split screen comparison, left side a student accepting an AI answer with two subtly incorrect dates, right side the same student checking a reliable source and correcting one date, red turning to green, DiGi nodding toward the right side, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Now the deeper part. These tools carry the bias of everything they were trained on, so they can be confidently unfair as well as confidently wrong. And soon they will not just answer, they will act. They will book, buy, and send on your behalf as agents. That raises the real question. Where does your data go, and who holds it. In the UK the law is on your side. You have the right to know what a company holds about you, the right to have it corrected, and the right to say no to how it is used. Your data is yours. Consent is not a checkbox you owe them, it is a choice you get to make.
**On screen.** A model reflects a skewed pattern, then an agent icon reaches out to book and send. Then three rights cards: KNOW, CORRECT, SAY NO.
**Illustration prompt.** A glowing model showing a subtly uneven pattern to signal bias, an AI agent figure reaching out to act on a calendar and a shopping cart, then three clear rights cards labelled with short words, a shield around a data icon held by the student, DiGi standing beside the shield, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is the method you carry out of this room. Prompt with context. Ask for reasoning. Verify at least one claim against a source you trust before you use anything. And before you sign up to any tool, read what it does with your data, and only give what you are happy to give. You are not asking permission to use these tools. You are choosing to use them like someone who knows exactly what they are holding.
**On screen.** A single checklist assembles: CONTEXT, REASONING, VERIFY ONE CLAIM, CHECK THE DATA TERMS. The student picks it up like a set of keys.
**Illustration prompt.** A confident sixteen year old holding a clean four point checklist that resolves into a set of keys, an AI tool open and under control on the desk, DiGi handing over the keys like a coach passing the wheel, empowering and calm, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · The close and the mission (4:30 to 5:15)
**Narration.** The tool is not your shortcut, and it is not your rival. It is a fast, confident guesser, and now you know how to check it. This week, use an AI tool for one real task, and write one line underneath. Here is the claim I verified, and here is where I checked it. Do that, and you can defend your work to anyone. That habit is the difference between using AI and being used by it, and it is the thing that will still matter when the tools change again.
**On screen.** DiGi gives a knowing nod. The student writes one line under their work: I checked this here. They close the laptop, calm and clear.
**Illustration prompt.** Friendly star guide DiGi giving a warm knowing nod, a sixteen year old writing a short verification note beneath their essay then closing the laptop with a small confident smile, resolved and hopeful, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

---

- **Lesson**: Digital identity and the future of work
- **Stage**: KS5, Years 12 to 13, ages 16 to 18
- **Host character**: DiGi, the star, the guide, calm and clear
- **Learning outcome**: I can name the human skills I am building that AI cannot replace.
- **Runtime**: about 5 minutes, seven beats
- **Voice**: Forward looking and a little inspiring, coach to soon to be adult, honest about the change and clear about your edge.

### Beat 1 · The hook (0:00 to 0:45)
**Narration.** Somewhere out there is a version of you at twenty five, walking into a first proper job. Here is what is strange. That job might not exist yet, and half of what you will do in it has not been invented. Meanwhile a headline tells you a machine will take it before you even get there. So let us be honest and useful for five minutes. Some work is changing fast. But the part that is truly yours is not the part that is going anywhere.
**On screen.** A young person a few years older stands at the door of a workplace that is half built, half glowing with possibility. A newspaper headline flickers: WILL AI TAKE YOUR JOB.
**Illustration prompt.** A hopeful near future scene, a young adult in their mid twenties stepping toward a workplace that is partly under construction and partly futuristic, a flickering headline about AI and jobs fading in the background, friendly star guide DiGi beside them, clean modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 2 · The big idea (0:45 to 1:30)
**Narration.** Here is the key word for today. Portfolio. Not a CV that lists what you were told to do, a portfolio that shows what you can actually make and how you think. Because the way we prove ourselves is shifting. Employers are starting to care less about the badge and more about the evidence. And your digital identity, the trail of what you build, share, and stand for online, is becoming that evidence whether you plan it or not. So you may as well plan it.
**On screen.** The word PORTFOLIO lands. A CV shrinks while a living collection of projects, writing, and made things grows around it.
**Illustration prompt.** A large clear label reading PORTFOLIO, a plain CV on one side shrinking as a vibrant collection of projects, sketches, code, and writing grows into a personal showcase, DiGi pointing at the growing collection, modern illustrated style, warm and clear, 16 by 9.

### Beat 3 · The idea in small steps (1:30 to 2:15)
**Narration.** Building that identity is simpler than it sounds, and it is three moves. One, make things, small real things, and keep them where people can see them. Two, show your thinking, not just the finished piece, because how you got there is what people trust. Three, be someone worth working with online, because your reputation now travels faster than your CV ever will. Make, show the thinking, be trustworthy. That is a portfolio and a name, built quietly, over time.
**On screen.** Three numbered cards rise: MAKE THINGS, SHOW YOUR THINKING, BE TRUSTWORTHY.
**Illustration prompt.** Three clean numbered cards rising in sequence with short labels, a young person adding a small project to an online profile, a thinking process shown as visible steps, a handshake icon for reputation, DiGi guiding along the three like a mentor, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 4 · Wrong then right (2:15 to 3:00)
**Narration.** See how this plays out. The naive way. You spend three years collecting grades, keep your head down, post nothing, and arrive in a crowded room where everyone has the same grades and no way to stand out. The mastered way. You get the grades and you build a small trail alongside them. A project you finished. A problem you explained clearly. A thing you cared enough to make well. Same three years. One of those walks into the room already known.
**On screen.** Split screen. Left, an identical crowd of blank profiles. Right, one profile with a few real, specific pieces of work that make it stand out.
**Illustration prompt.** A clear split screen comparison, left side a crowd of identical faceless CV cards, right side one distinctive profile showing a few specific real projects and a short written explanation, DiGi nodding toward the right side, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 5 · The twist (3:00 to 3:50)
**Narration.** Now the part worth remembering. AI is fast at patterns, drafts, and summaries. So the work that is only pattern is the work most at risk. But look at what it cannot do. It cannot truly judge when the answer feels wrong. It cannot sit with a frightened person and make them feel held. It cannot take real responsibility, or notice the idea nobody asked for, or read the room. Judgement. Communication. Care. Creativity. Working with people. Those are not soft skills. They are the durable ones, and they are the reason you are not replaceable.
**On screen.** Five glowing cards settle in a row: JUDGEMENT, COMMUNICATION, CARE, CREATIVITY, WORKING WITH PEOPLE.
**Illustration prompt.** Five warm glowing skill cards arranged in a steady row with short labels, small human vignettes on each, a person deciding, comforting, creating, and collaborating, an AI shape handling routine patterns to the side, DiGi presenting the five cards proudly, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 6 · Taking the wheel (3:50 to 4:30)
**Narration.** So here is how you take the wheel. Let AI carry the routine, the first draft and the boring bit, and put your energy into the five that last. Build one thing that shows your judgement. Explain it in a way only you would. Look after the people you work with. That is not fighting the machine. That is doing the deeply human part on purpose, and letting the tool clear the path so you can.
**On screen.** A young person hands the routine tasks to an AI helper and steps forward carrying the five durable skills like tools. A checklist forms: MAKE ONE THING, EXPLAIN IT, LOOK AFTER PEOPLE.
**Illustration prompt.** A confident young adult delegating routine tasks to a helpful AI assistant while stepping forward holding the five human skills as glowing tools, a short three point checklist forming beside them, DiGi handing over the wheel like a coach, empowering and calm, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.

### Beat 7 · The close and the mission (4:30 to 5:15)
**Narration.** The future of work is not a race against the machine. It is a question of what only you can bring. This week, make one small thing you are a little proud of, and put your name to it. A page, a project, a clear explanation of something you understand. That is the first stone in a path that is unmistakably yours. The tools will keep changing. The human you are becoming is the part that lasts.
**On screen.** DiGi gives a knowing nod. The young person publishes one small piece of work with their name on it, then looks up toward a bright, open horizon.
**Illustration prompt.** Friendly star guide DiGi giving a warm knowing nod, a young adult proudly publishing one small named project and looking toward a bright open horizon full of possibility, resolved and inspiring, modern illustrated style, 16 by 9.
