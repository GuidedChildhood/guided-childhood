THE PROTECTION CASE

Knowing the risks is not protection. Understanding the system is.

These are the six skills that change outcomes. Not as a school subject. As a way of thinking.

1
How algorithms work

Every platform is built by engineers whose job is to keep your child there as long as possible. A child who understands this is not immune but they have a frame. They can name what is happening to them.

2
Privacy as power

The product is free because the child is the product. Data literacy means understanding what apps collect, why the platform costs nothing, and what that means for the content they are shown.

3
Scam and phishing recognition

Online fraud is the UK's most common crime and young people are the most targeted group. Recognising a fake profile, a phishing link, and a manipulation attempt is protective knowledge that is now as important as road safety.

4
Image consent and digital footprint

Sending an image removes control of it permanently. Understanding what stays, what spreads, and what the law says is knowledge every child needs before they are asked to share something.

5
Verifying information

Being able to check a source, trace a claim, and recognise a manipulated image is the civic literacy of this century. Children who can do this are harder to radicalise and harder to mislead.

6
Recognising emotional targeting

Platforms and advertisers target emotional states algorithmically. Loneliness, boredom, and anxiety are exploited by design. A child who can name this is measurably more resilient to it.

WHAT IT ENABLES

Six young people who changed the world using the skills you are building.

These are not exceptional talents. They are the result of curiosity, access to information, and adults who did not restrict the tools.

BS
Boyan Slat, age 16
The Ocean Cleanup

At 16, while diving in Greece, Boyan found more plastic bags than fish. He spent the next two years using online research and digital design tools to model The Ocean Cleanup. His organisation has now removed more than 10 million kg of plastic from the world's oceans and rivers.

GR
Gitanjali Rao, age 11
Lead Detection Device

After watching the Flint water crisis unfold online, Gitanjali used YouTube and Khan Academy to teach herself carbon nanotube science. She built Tethys, a portable lead detection device, and won the 3M Young Scientist Challenge. TIME magazine named her Kid of the Year in 2020.

KD
Kelvin Doe, age 15
Community FM Radio Station

Growing up in Sierra Leone with no formal training, Kelvin taught himself electronics from online resources and parts from rubbish bins. At 15 he built a working FM radio station for his community. He became the youngest person ever invited to MIT's visiting practitioner programme.

AM
Ann Makosinski, age 16
Body Heat Flashlight

Ann's friend in the Philippines could not study at night because her family had no electricity. At 16 Ann entered the Google Science Fair with a flashlight powered entirely by the heat of a human hand. No batteries. No charging. She won.

SB
Shubham Banerjee, age 12
Low Cost Braille Printer

Shubham asked his parents why a Braille printer cost $2,000. Nobody had a good answer. At 12 he built one from a LEGO Mindstorms kit for $350. Intel invested in his company before he turned 14.

JA
Jack Andraka, age 15
Pancreatic Cancer Detection

After a family friend died of pancreatic cancer, Jack emailed 200 research scientists asking to use their lab. 199 said no. One said yes. At 15 he developed a test that is 168 times faster, 400 times more sensitive, and 26,000 times cheaper than existing methods.

THE BALANCE

The richest digital lives belong to children with the richest real lives.

The research is consistent. The children who navigate digital life best are not the ones with the strictest screen rules. They are the ones with the most compelling offline alternatives. Sport, cooking, music, building, reading, time outdoors without a goal. The algorithm has less to work with when a child already has somewhere better to be.

Digital literacy and real world richness are not in competition. They are the same project. A child who can build a radio station from salvaged parts and a child who can spend an afternoon outside with no agenda are both harder for the internet to own.
