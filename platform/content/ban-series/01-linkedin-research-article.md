# What the evidence actually says about the social media ban. Three evidence bases. One conclusion.

*Justin Farrow, Guided Childhood. June 2026.*
*Pre-print, not formally peer reviewed. Every claim links to a primary source. Corrections welcomed publicly.*

---

I am a parent of three and the founder of Guided Childhood, a platform that helps families navigate the digital world. I am not a researcher. I have spent six months reading the primary literature on children and social media.

I want to be direct about something before you read this. Two Cambridge University professors responded to today's ban announcement on camera. The first said she would not expect "a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term." The second said "the evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution."

Both are at Cambridge. Both have advised the government. Both said this today.

I am going to show you the three evidence bases that lead every serious researcher to the same conclusion. The crisis is real. The intervention does not match the mechanism.

---

## Introduction

Adolescent mental health has deteriorated. In the United States, suicide rates among girls aged 10 to 14 tripled between 1999 and 2017 (Hedegaard, Curtin and Warner, 2018). Increases in self-reported symptoms of anxiety and depression have been documented in countries including the UK, Germany, Sweden, Norway and New Zealand from the 1980s onwards (Collishaw, 2015).

These increases are real. They are alarming. They demand a serious response.

The question is not whether children are struggling. The question is whether this intervention matches the mechanism of what is harming them.

When two trend lines increase together, they appear to explain each other. Social media adoption and adolescent depression both increased across the 2012 to 2015 period. Odgers and Jensen (2020), in the most comprehensive review of this literature, note: "the fact that two trend lines increase together does not mean that one phenomenon causes the other."

The government has acted as though it does. The evidence says otherwise.

## Evidence Base 1: What the meta-analytic record actually shows

Six major systematic reviews and meta-analyses, published between 2014 and 2019, have examined the association between digital technology use and adolescent mental health. Their findings are consistent.

The associations are mixed between positive, negative and null findings. When present, they are too small to translate into practically or clinically meaningful effects. The direction of causation cannot be determined from the available data.

Huang (2017) performed a meta-analysis across 67 independent samples including 19,652 participants. The mean correlation between time spent on social networking sites and psychological wellbeing was r = 0.07. This includes depression, loneliness, life satisfaction and self-esteem combined. The finding did not vary significantly by age or gender.

McCrae, Gettings and Pursell (2017) reviewed 11 studies meeting strict eligibility criteria, total sample 12,646. They found r = 0.13 for the association between social media use and depressive symptoms, but noted the "difficulty in interpreting the clinical significance of the findings due to the wide variation observed in sample sizes, methods, and results."

Of 29 studies reviewed across all six major reviews, only 4 (14%) used longitudinal designs. Only 2 included experimental or quasi-experimental designs. The conclusion in the peer-reviewed literature is explicit: "the ability to make causal inferences is extremely limited and does not allow for conclusions regarding whether increased time online or engagement with social media use causes changes in young people's mental health" (Odgers and Jensen, 2020).

The parliamentary Digital Mental Health Group submitted evidence to Parliament citing zero experimental studies. This is on the public record (SMR0005). The committee noted it. The bill passed regardless.

## Evidence Base 2: The large-scale preregistered studies

The most rigorous studies in this field are preregistered, meaning their methodology is locked before data collection, preventing post-hoc adjustment to produce significant findings. These are the studies that carry the most weight. Their findings are the most damaging to the case for this ban.

Orben and Przybylski (2019), published in Nature Human Behaviour, ran specification curve analysis across three national datasets of adolescents, total N exceeding 350,000. Across 3,221,225,472 analytical approaches, digital technology use accounted for less than 1% (0.4%) of the variation in wellbeing.

Professor Amy Orben, who led this research at Cambridge, responded to today's announcement on camera: "We wouldn't expect a massive boost in well-being or mental health in the short term." She called it "really disappointing" that the government believes it cannot make social media safe through platform design. Her research is the most cited in the policy debate. Her own conclusion is that the ban alone is insufficient.

The Monitoring the Future Study is the paper most frequently cited to justify this ban. Twenge et al. (2018) used it to argue social media was driving increases in adolescent depression. A re-analysis of the same data found the correlation accounted for less than 1% of variance in depressive symptoms. As Odgers and Jensen summarise: "99.666% of the variation in adolescent depressive symptoms was due to other factors."

Przybylski and Weinstein (2017), in a preregistered study of over 120,000 English adolescents, found no robust associations between mental wellbeing and moderate digital technology use. A measurable but small negative association existed only for those with very high engagement levels.

In the Orben, Dienlin and Przybylski (2019) study using UK Household Longitudinal data (12,672 children aged 10 to 15), associations between social media use and life satisfaction were described by the authors themselves as "arguably trivial." In over half the models using stringent statistical controls, associations did not differ significantly from zero.

## Evidence Base 3: The real-world comparator

Australia implemented a comparable ban in November 2024. They are six months ahead of the UK. This is the closest available real-world test.

The eSafety Commissioner reported 4.7 million account removals in January 2026. Professor Sander van der Linden, Professor of Psychology at Cambridge and one of the government's own advisers, stated on camera today: "60% of kids are getting around the ban, and those kids who are deemed most influential in terms of popularity are still on the platform, which leads other kids to still be on the platform. So the social norms aren't changing."

He continued: "I've told the government this over the last year. I know they've heard it from other influential academics and charities that the evidence base just isn't there to support bans, but politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution, but it is not the nuanced one."

The eSafety Commissioner reported "major gaps in enforcement" in March 2026. No peer-reviewed mental health outcome data has been published. No evidence that the ban changed how Australian children feel has emerged in six months.

The displacement pattern matters. Two independent studies found two thirds of affected users migrated to Discord, Telegram and private messaging groups within eight weeks. These platforms have no content moderation for minors, no statutory safeguarding obligations and no age verification infrastructure.

Professor van der Linden points to Canada's approach as the more evidence-based alternative: a temporary ban structured as an injunction to force platform redesign. "We're going to ban social media temporarily as a strategic lever to give social media companies the opportunity to actually make their products safe for kids. Get rid of risky functionality. No infinite scroll. No autoplay. No messages from strangers. Clean and safe feeds for kids. And when you've done that, we're going to let kids back onto the platforms."

The UK has not structured the ban this way. There is no statutory requirement for platforms to redesign their products. The ban removes access. It does not change the machines.

## Four things that would actually work

**1. The harm mechanism is design, not access**

Odgers and Jensen (2020) are explicit: "Screen time is no longer a useful construct." The research consensus is that harm varies by the type of use, not the quantity. Passive scrolling and social comparison content produce negative associations. Active communication and peer support produce positive or neutral associations.

Orben identifies the specific features: "those things that make it really hard for us to leave social media, even if we want to." These are design choices: infinite scroll, autoplay, variable reward notification systems, like counts. They are not inherent to social media. They are business decisions. They are regulatable.

The Online Safety Act addresses some of this. It does not mandate the removal of infinite scroll, the elimination of like counts for under-18 content, or the prohibition of autoplay. These statutory design standards would target the actual mechanism of harm. The ban does not.

**2. Online risk mirrors offline vulnerability**

Odgers and Jensen's central finding: "adolescents at higher risk for depressive symptoms may selectively use social media more, or differently." Children struggling offline struggle online. Children thriving offline thrive online. The digital environment amplifies what is already present.

This is a fundamentally different policy implication. If online harm tracks offline vulnerability, then the intervention should address the offline vulnerability. Funding CAMHS, school counselling and social infrastructure for low-income children addresses the root. Removing platform access addresses the symptom and leaves the root.

**3. Digital technologies provide critical support infrastructure for vulnerable children**

This is the finding most absent from the public debate. Rideout and Fox (2018) found adolescents with moderate to severe depressive symptoms are twice as likely as their peers to turn to social media for emotional support. Odgers and Jensen conclude: "Policies restricting adolescents' access to new technologies are advocated, but may be ill advised if new technologies are being used as a valuable source of social support."

LGBTQ+ young people in rural and isolated communities use social media for identity support, peer connection and mental health community access at significantly higher rates than their peers. For children in geographic isolation, digital community is often the first available community. The ban removes this. No replacement is announced.

**4. Digital literacy from age 7, not age 16**

Children encounter screens at four. The curriculum begins engaging with digital behaviour at GCSE. That is a decade too late.

Van der Linden describes the goal: children who are empowered to express themselves online, gather with friends, and explore the digital world safely. Not permanent exclusion followed by sudden full access at 16 with no preparation.

The ban lifts in Spring 2027 for today's 15-year-olds. On that day, the machines are the same machines. The algorithmic design is unchanged. The content is the same. The only thing different is that the child has had no experience navigating it and no adults who helped them develop that navigation.

---

## Conclusions

The adolescent mental health crisis is real. The research confirms increasing rates of anxiety and depression, concentrated in girls, intensifying since approximately 2012.

The evidence for social media access as the cause of this crisis is weak. Three bodies of evidence converge on the same finding: associations are small, causation is not established, and population-level access restrictions have not produced measurable mental health improvements.

Cambridge University's two most prominent researchers in this field said so today. One told the government directly over the past year. The government heard it and acted anyway because, as Professor van der Linden stated, "politically it's popular because it seems like an easy solution."

The children most likely to be harmed by this ban are the children it claims to protect. LGBTQ+ young people who depended on digital communities for their first experience of peer support. Children with pre-existing anxiety who used social media to access mental health resources. Children in poverty for whom smartphone-based social connection was the primary infrastructure. Children with autism for whom asynchronous text-based communication reduced the demands of real-time social processing.

These children are not being given something safer. They are being given nothing.

The ban handles access. It does not handle the machines that cause the harm. It does not build the literacy that protects children after the ban lifts. It does not fund the mental health services children in crisis need today.

Professor Orben identified what a serious policy response would look like: address the features that make it hard to leave social media. Professor van der Linden identified what a serious legislative mechanism would look like: use the ban as a lever to force platform redesign, then restore access when platforms are safe.

Neither of those is what was announced today.

The ban has happened. Spring 2027 is the date. The question now is whether we use the time to build what the government has not: the literacy, the conversations and the preparation that make a child genuinely ready for the digital world.

That is what Guided Childhood is for.
