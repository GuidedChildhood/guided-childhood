A
Learning Objectives
Knowledge

Understand the variable reward mechanism and why social media platforms are engineered to hold attention.

Understanding

Recognise the comparison trap — the difference between curated online life and real life — and the research on why this matters particularly at ages 11–15.

Skill / Application

Choose and commit to one personal strategy before the end of the lesson. This is a decision, not homework.

C
Lesson Plan with Scripts
0–5 Starter

Before we begin

Display Slide 2. Distribute blank slips. Ask pupils to write their answer privately — hands up is also fine for those willing.

Script

"Before we start — think about the last time you spent time scrolling. Did you feel better, worse, or the same afterwards? Write it on your slip. You don't need to share it. This is just between you and the paper."

TIP

Hold the silence for a full ten seconds after asking. The discomfort you feel is shorter than the recognition pupils are processing. Do not fill it.

5–20 Main 1

The Variable Reward (Slides 3–4)

Teach the three concepts: pull to refresh, unpredictable reward, dopamine loop. Slide 3 displays each card in turn. Spend approximately 4 minutes on the teaching, then move to the activity.

Script — introducing the slot machine

"I want you to pull down on an imaginary feed right now. That gesture — the pull down. Now imagine you're at a slot machine and you pull the handle. The movement is identical. That is not a coincidence. The engineers who designed your app studied slot machines. They copied the hand movement deliberately."

Script — the key reframe

"Finding it hard to stop scrolling is not a personal weakness. It is the intended outcome of millions of pounds of engineering. You are not failing at willpower. You are succeeding exactly as the design intended."

Activity (Slide 4, 7 minutes): Pupils work individually on Section 1 of the worksheet, then compare with the person next to them. Circulate and listen for the pupils who have the 'oh' moment — ask them to share with the class.

TIP

The pupil who says "I do this with YouTube" is your teaching moment. Name the mechanism they described and thank them for it.

20–35 Main 2

The Comparison Machine (Slides 5–6)

Introduce Dr Amy Orben's Cambridge research. Deliver the three column points on Slide 5 without rushing. The research on peak sensitivity windows (11–13 for girls, 14–15 for boys) is the most important teaching content in the module.

Script — introducing the research

"A researcher at Cambridge University — Dr Amy Orben — tracked thousands of young people over time and looked at when social media had the most impact on wellbeing. She found that it wasn't the same for everyone. For girls, the period of highest impact is between 11 and 13. For boys, it's a little later — around 14 to 15. Some of you are right in the middle of that window. That doesn't mean you're damaged. It means we should be paying attention."

Script — the Tuesday line (use verbatim)

"Nobody posts their Tuesday. You scroll through a feed of everyone's best moments. Best holiday. Best angle. Best day. And your brain — automatically, without asking you — compares that to your behind-the-scenes reality. That comparison was never fair. It was never meant to be."

Activity (Slide 6, 10 minutes): Feed analysis activity. Pupils analyse the sample feed on their worksheet. Collect two or three sentence completions for whole-class share.

TIP

The sentence "What this feed does not show is..." produces powerful answers. Read two or three aloud without naming the authors. The ones that land hardest are worth spending thirty seconds on.

35–45 Plenary

Choose Your One Strategy (Slides 7–8)

If time allows, show Slide 7 (mental health signals) briefly — emphasise these are signals to notice, not diagnose. Then move to Slide 8 and the strategy choice.

Script — framing the strategy choice

"You cannot unknow what you now know. So the question is not whether this matters. The question is what you actually do. I am not asking you to delete your apps. I am asking you to choose one thing — one small action — and commit to it before you leave this room."

Pupils circle their chosen strategy on the worksheet. This is a commitment device — physical circling is more effective than a verbal instruction.

TIP

Acknowledge that the bedroom rule is the hardest one to stick to. If pupils laugh at it, that's a signal. Ask: "Why is that the funniest one?"

45–50 Exit

Exit Ticket and Collection (Slides 9)

Display Slide 9. Pupils complete the three exit ticket sentences at the bottom of their worksheet. Collect all worksheets before pupils leave — do not allow them to take the worksheets home before you have reviewed them. The parent take-home note is on the back and may be detached.

Safeguarding — read before the lesson

This lesson regularly surfaces disclosures. Exit ticket responses about mood, contacts, or content should be read before you leave the building. Any concern — complete Form GC-DSL-001 and submit to the DSL today. Do not investigate independently or contact parents without DSL guidance.

D
Discussion Questions — Use as Time Allows
"If the app gives you the same thing every time you open it, does it still hold your attention in the same way? Why not?"
This surfaces the unpredictable reward mechanic without you having to name it. Let pupils arrive there.
"What would a feed look like if it showed real life, not curated life? What would be different about how you felt afterwards?"
Opens discussion about design choices — platforms could show real life but choose not to.
"Is there a difference between the accounts that make you feel worse and the ones that don't? What's the difference between them?"
Encourages specific, account-level thinking rather than blanket statements about social media being good or bad.
"If you told a friend about the variable reward mechanic, do you think it would change how they use apps? Why or why not?"
Good for older groups or extension. Tests whether understanding the mechanism changes behaviour — a genuine research question.
E
Differentiation
Foundation

Focus on pull-to-refresh and the unpredictable reward only. Skip the dopamine science. The slot machine comparison is accessible without the neuroscience. For the feed analysis, reduce to two questions instead of four.

Core

As written. All activities at standard pace. Sentence completion is the key output to assess. Strategy choice is the measure of application.

Extension

Ask: who benefits from the current design? (platform owners, advertisers). Who designed the feed and why? Could feeds be designed differently? This moves from consumer literacy to systems thinking.

F
Statutory and Framework Links
Statutory RSE & Health Education (DfE 2019)

Online safety, managing relationships with technology, the impact of social media on mental health — all explicitly named requirements at secondary level.

Online Safety Act 2023

Duty of care for children's online experiences. Schools must provide education that builds capability to navigate online risks — this lesson directly meets that requirement.

Education for a Connected World (UKCIS)

Framework area 3 (Online Relationships), area 4 (Online Reputation), area 6 (Managing Online Information), area 8 (Health, Wellbeing and Lifestyle).
